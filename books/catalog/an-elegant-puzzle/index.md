## Classification
A how-to manual for engineering management. A guidebook of what the author has found works — and what doesn't — across the issues an engineering manager actually runs into.

## The Book in a Sentence
A how-to manual for managing software engineering systems.

## Structure
The book is organized issue by issue (very engineery). For each problem an engineering manager might face, Larson lays out what he's seen work and what he's seen fail. It's less a continuous argument than a reference book — a shelf of mental models you reach for when a specific situation comes up.

## The Problem
Larson wants to help others learn to be better managers. He succeeded. I won't use everything he's written today, but it's a great guidebook to return to whenever a specific issue or frame comes up.

## Key Terms
The book is less about introducing terms and more about giving mental models for problems engineering managers face. A few of the more durable framings:

- **Four states of a team** — falling behind, treading water, repaying debt, innovating. Each state needs a different intervention. Teams want to climb up; entropy drags them back.
- **Process vs. organizational design vs. culture** — three different forces operating at three different speeds. Process is fast and cheap. Org design sits in the middle. Culture is slow but permanent. Match the tool to the problem.
- **Strategies vs. visions** — strategies are grounded documents that explain trade-offs and actions for a specific challenge; visions are aspirational documents that let people who don't work closely together still make decisions that fit together.
- **De-risk → enable → finish** — the migration playbook. De-risk by shopping the design with the hardest teams first. Enable with self-service tooling and migrate the easy 90% programmatically. Finish by stopping the bleeding (ratchets in linters), tracking, and digging into the long tail yourself.
- **Stocks and flows** (from systems thinking) — projects fail slowly, one sprint at a time. Working frenetically for a few weeks rarely counteracts months of accumulated deficit.
- **Scope, not headcount** — growing as a manager means taking on more important and complex scope, not enumerating more direct reports. Less zero-sum competition that way.

## Key Ideas
- **Treating management as something that can be optimized.** Hiring, growing a team, running an interview loop, writing policy — Larson treats each of these as a system you can instrument and improve. It's a very engineery way to think about management, and it works.
- **Management is an ethical profession.** This resonated. You don't see who you are in the mirror — you see it in how you treat the team member who isn't succeeding, in your compensation philosophy, in whom you promote, in how you handle PTO requests and working hours. The impact you have on the people who work for you is the measure.
- **Cold sourcing.** Loved this. The simple, slightly uncomfortable practice of reaching out directly to people you don't know about a job opportunity. Most ignore you, some demur, a few engage — and nobody is offended. It's a high-leverage skill for hiring managers.
- **How to interview empathetically.** Worth re-reading. Be kind. Make sure interviewers agree on the role and on the signal each interview is checking for. Come prepared. Deliberately express interest in candidates (have every interviewer send a personal note when an offer goes out). Treat the loop as a conversion funnel and instrument it.
- **Solution validation.** Before jumping into execution, validate cheaply: write the launch announcement first, identify prior art, find reference users, prefer experimentation over analysis, justify switching costs. Cheap validation beats clever solution-picking.
- **How to write strategy and run migrations.** The strategy/vision distinction and the de-risk/enable/finish migration playbook are concrete enough to actually use.

There's a lot more in here. It's dense.

## Disagreements
I'm sure there's nuance Larson misses, but as I'm not currently a manager I'm not the best person to identify where.

## Takeaways
A foundational set of manager mental models that I'll try to build on in the years to come.

## Suggested Reading
From the author's recommendations (§7.2 "Books I've found very useful"). Roughly ordered most to least valuable, per Larson:

- Donella H. Meadows, *Thinking in Systems: A Primer* — for Larson, systems thinking has been the most effective universal tool for reasoning through complex problems; this book is a readable, powerful introduction.
- George Lakoff, *[[books/catalog/dont-think-of-an-elephant/index|Don't Think of an Elephant! Know Your Values and Frame the Debate]]* — completely changed how Larson thinks about presenting ideas. Briefer and more readable than Lakoff's academic writing.

- Richard Rumelt, *Good Strategy/Bad Strategy: The Difference and Why It Matters* — gave Larson permission to acknowledge that many strategies he's seen professionally aren't very good; a structured approach to writing good ones.

## Suggested Papers
From the author's recommendations (§7.3 "Papers I've found very useful"). Larson curated these for engineering paper-reading groups.

- **"Dynamo: Amazon's Highly Available Key-Value Store"** — "the" classic modern systems paper. Phenomenal introduction to eventual consistency, coordinating state across distributed storage, and reconciling data as it diverges across replicas.
- **"Hints for Computer System Design"** — Butler Lampson's concise summary of his ideas around systems design. Acknowledges it doesn't break new ground, but a phenomenal overview.
- **"Big Ball of Mud"** — labels the most frequent architectural pattern as a "Big Ball of Mud" and explores why elegant initial designs rarely remain intact. Humorous but correct that software design is remarkably poor.
- **"The Google File System"** — one of the early entries in Google's strategy of defining technical themes in Silicon Valley through papers; largely inspired the Hadoop File System.
- **"On Designing and Deploying Internet-Scale Services"** (James Hamilton) — a true checklist of how to design and evaluate large-scale systems, in the way the Twelve-Factor App serves as a checklist for operable applications.
- **"CAP Twelve Years Later: How the 'Rules' Have Changed"** (Eric Brewer) — overview and review of CAP from its original author. There's no "seminal CAP paper"; this article serves in its stead.
- **"Harvest, Yield, and Scalable Tolerant Systems"** — builds on CAP, introducing harvest and yield to add nuance to AP vs. CP. Self-evident concepts that are very rarely explicitly used.
- **"MapReduce: Simplified Data Processing on Large Clusters"** — an idea so successful it now seems self-evident; provoked a shift from data warehousing to a new paradigm. A major inspiration for Hadoop.
- **"Dapper, a Large-Scale Distributed Systems Tracing Infrastructure"** — Google's production tracing infrastructure for distributed services. Influenced Zipkin and OpenTracing.
- **"Kafka: a Distributed Messaging System for Log Processing"** — both a useful tool and a beautifully designed system. Kafka's partitions force application designers to make explicit decisions about trading off performance for predictable message ordering.
- **"Wormhole: Reliable Pub-Sub to Support Geo-Replicated Internet Services"** — Facebook's geo-replicated pub-sub system; transfers 35 GB/sec steady-state across deployments. Notable for supporting lagging consumers without sacrificing throughput.
- **"Borg, Omega, and Kubernetes"** — overview of Google's three container-orchestration systems and the lessons learned across them.
- **"Large-Scale Cluster Management at Google with Borg"** — Borg's centralized scheduling model, effective and efficient but increasingly hard to modify and scale. Inspired both Omega and Kubernetes.
- **"Omega: Flexible, Scalable Schedulers for Large Compute Clusters"** — an example of the second-system effect; an attempt to elegantly replace Borg that ended up more challenging than anticipated.
- **"Mesos: A Platform for Fine-Grained Resource Sharing in the Data Center"** — Apache Mesos's distinctive two-level scheduler. Heavily used at Twitter and Apple; was for some time the only open-source general scheduler with significant adoption.
- **"Design Patterns for Container-Based Distributed Systems"** — a survey of patterns (sidecars, adapters, etc.) that have evolved as microservices and containers became prominent.
- **"Raft: In Search of an Understandable Consensus Algorithm"** — equivalent to multi-Paxos but designed for understandability. Used by etcd and InfluxDB.
- **"Paxos Made Simple"** (Leslie Lamport) — explains the notoriously complex Paxos algorithm. Even at its simplest, Paxos isn't really that simple.
- **"SWIM: Scalable Weakly-Consistent Infection-Style Process Group Membership Protocol"** — focuses on availability rather than consistency during partition. Used in HashiCorp's software and Uber's Ringpop.
- **"The Byzantine Generals Problem"** (Leslie Lamport) — how to deal with distributed actors that intentionally or accidentally submit incorrect messages. A reminder that we still tend to assume components will behave reliably and honestly.

- **"The Chubby Lock Service for Loosely-Coupled Distributed Systems"**
- **"Bigtable: A Distributed Storage System for Structured Data"**
- **"Spanner: Google's Globally-Distributed Database"**
- **"Security Keys: Practical Cryptographic Second Factors for the Modern Web"**
- **"BeyondCorp: Design to Deployment at Google"**
