## Classification
A political theory and history book about why the United States struggles to build new things in the physical world — infrastructure, housing, clean energy — and how the progressive movement inadvertently created the paralysis.

## The Book in a Sentence
An exploration of why we struggle to build in government.

## Structure
1. **Overview** — introduces the problem through the decades-long failure to rebuild Penn Station
2. **Historical lens** — traces how American progressivism has flipped between Hamiltonian (centralize power to get things done) and Jeffersonian (diffuse power to protect the vulnerable) impulses from the early 1900s through the present
3. **Where we are today** — case studies showing the Jeffersonian overcorrection across domains: policing, housing, infrastructure, clean energy
4. **Ideas on what to do** — argues for "full-circle progressivism" that rebalances the two impulses

## The Problem
Why can't progressives build anymore? Dunkelman argues the movement has swung too far toward its Jeffersonian impulse — diffusing power, granting veto points, prioritizing voice over decision-making — to the point where government has been rendered incompetent. The diagnosis is compelling, though it may not be the right question: there was a reason the pendulum swung this far in the first place. Centralized power is not a stable equilibrium.

## Key Ideas
- **The Hamiltonian vs. Jeffersonian framing** is the book's central contribution. Progressivism contains two competing impulses: one that wants to concentrate power in competent institutions to solve problems (Hamiltonian), and one that wants to diffuse power to protect individuals from institutional abuse (Jeffersonian). These have always coexisted but the balance between them shifts.
- **The historical analysis is top notch.** Dunkelman traces the drivers for each frame — why progressives centralized power in the New Deal era, why they began dismantling it after the 1960s — in a way that makes the motivations on both sides legible.
- **Voice turned to veto.** The reforms designed to give affected communities a say in government decisions have, in aggregate, given nearly anyone the ability to block nearly anything. The crucial missing piece: who makes the final decision after everyone has spoken?
- **The pattern repeats across domains.** Policing (LEOBORs protecting bad cops), housing (NIMBYism), infrastructure (environmental review as obstruction), clean energy (progressive groups blocking solar and wind projects) — each follows the same arc of Hamiltonian overreach corrected by Jeffersonian reforms that themselves become oppressive.
- **Government incompetence fuels populism.** When progressives lionize government but ensure it can't deliver, voters turn to figures like Trump.

## Disagreements
The book diagnoses the symptom correctly but doesn't fully grapple with the fact that the Hamiltonian approach has its own failure mode. Power corrupts. The pendulum swung toward Jeffersonianism for real reasons — Robert Moses, midnight raids on welfare recipients, captured regulators. Proposing to re-concentrate authority without solving the corruption problem that caused the backlash feels incomplete.

## Takeaways
The Hamiltonian vs. Jeffersonian framing is a useful lens for understanding policy debates. The historical drivers for each frame were not well known prior to reading this.
