## Classification
A book about the Supreme Court of today. It goes in depth into each justice and what they believe. The author's hypothesis is that the Court is 3-3-3 instead of 6-3 — you need to take into account another axis beyond just progressive/conservative.

## The Book in a Sentence
An exploration of the current Supreme Court and a busting of typical stereotypes about it.

## Structure
The author first gives a history, then goes into each justice individually, then lays out her frame for how to view the justices, then talks about what to do to make the Supreme Court better.

## The Problem
Spread knowledge. Isgur wants to frame the Supreme Court properly to outside observers. In her opinion the popular 6-3 frame is wrong and one of the reasons pundits have such a tough time predicting votes. She says a better way to view it is 3-3-3, and goes into why that's a preferred approach. You need to take into account how "institutional" each justice is — by which she means a "we're here together as a team and need to make decisions together" type of frame.

## Key Terms
- **X-axis vs Y-axis** — the core frame. X = traditional left/right; Y = institutionalist/legitimacy axis.
- **3-3-3 Court** — Barrett/Kavanaugh/Roberts on the institutionalist end, Gorsuch/Thomas/Alito on the anti-institutionalist end, plus the three liberals.
- **Institutionalist** — a justice who considers consequences, precedent strength, the Court's legitimacy, and the idea of speaking as one Court.
- **"Peace through strength"** — Gorsuch's anti-institutionalist y-axis worldview. Don't appease critics; let the chips fall.
- **Edmund Burke conservatism** — the y-axis kind of conservatism, distinct from right-left politics. "Mistrusts a priori reasoning and revolution, preferring experience and gradual improvement of tried and tested arrangements."
- **Stare decisis** — "the ultimate y-axis question."
- **Questions, not cases / the QP** — the Court decides the question presented, not the case in front of it. Why hypotheticals dominate oral argument.
- **Defensive denial** — voting against cert when you don't think you have 5 votes to win.
- **The Great Professionalization Shift** — Isgur's theory that since the 1988 cert-discretion law, justices have become elite specialists with cookie-cutter résumés (Ivy League → SCOTUS clerk → circuit judge). Compare to the Brown v. Board Court, where five had degrees from public law schools and one had no law degree at all.
- **Originalism's "Loving problem"** — the methodology trap: a strict originalist read of the 14th Amendment doesn't obviously prohibit anti-miscegenation laws.
- **Interim/preliminary docket** — the "short-order counter" of the Court; fast-reaction rulings that wreak havoc on clerks, academics, and litigants alike.
- **Chevron deference / Loper Bright** — the rise and fall of courts deferring to agency interpretations of their own power.
- **Major questions doctrine / unitary legislative doctrine** — paired with the unitary executive theory: presidents get to be presidents, but Congress has to be Congress.
- **Negative partisan polarization** — voters trained to hate the other team rather than love their own.
- **Federalist Society** — the "second most successful political organization in modern American history" that reshaped the Court.
- **Fed Soc adjacent** — the social phenomenon of liberal law students refusing to socialize even with people who socialize with Federalist Society members.
- **"Pen and phone"** — Obama-era catchphrase for executive action as a substitute for legislation. Both parties have only escalated since.
- **End of the judicial filibuster** — Isgur's biggest single villain. Removed the incentive for nominees to be cross-aisle palatable.
- **Snailgate / the snails of justice** — Breyer's opening framing about how the Court interprets ambiguous text.
- **"I'm just a process girl living in an outcomes world"** — her catchphrase.
- Judicial methodologies catalog: living constitutionalism (Warren), formalism (Barrett), originalism (Thomas), textualism (Gorsuch), pluralism (Breyer), legal realism (Holmes), legal positivism (Raz), natural law (George), common good constitutionalism (Vermeule).

## Key Ideas
- **The 3-3-3 frame.** The biggest argument is that standard interpretations of the Supreme Court are incorrect. You need to take into account the justices' views on the institution of the Supreme Court too. This is a great frame and really opens up understanding the Court better.
- **The Court decides *who gets to decide*, not policy.** Most cases are about which branch or level of government has authority, not whether a policy is good. Misreading this is what makes pundits constantly wrong.
- **Process > outcomes.** Her recurring refrain. "The means are the ends in American law." The Court's worst decisions — Dred Scott, Plessy, Korematsu — all came when it bent to the political majority, not when it stood athwart it.
- **The Court is *supposed* to be unpopular.** If the majority wants something, the political process can deliver it. The Court's value is saying no.
- **Don't blame the Court — blame Congress, presidents, and voters.** The Court is getting dragged into everything because Congress stopped legislating and presidents govern by executive order. "Pen and phone" → courts as last resort.
- **End of the judicial filibuster has been catastrophic.** Nominees now compete to be the purest version of their side rather than to win cross-aisle votes. "Fed Soc adjacent" social ostracism is the canary.
- **The Court should hear *more* cases, not fewer.** Taking fewer cases concentrates the temperature on each one. Mixed outcomes across more cases would lower the stakes of any single ruling.
- **Term limits would backfire.** Justices would become ticket-mates campaigning with presidential candidates and worrying about post-Court careers. Reform ideas that sound clean often make things worse.
- **Her constructive proposals.** Bring back a modified filibuster (Harvey/Koenig two-track), an enforceable ethics code, lock reporters in a room with opinions before public release, random assignment for nationwide injunctions, and eight constitutional amendments — including requiring a 6-justice majority to strike down acts of Congress.

## Disagreements
Not really in a position to disagree — she's the expert. What I would have enjoyed is further expansion on ideas to help maintain the Court as a U.S. institution.

## Takeaways
This changed how I view the Court. I'll use her 3-3-3 frame in the future as a starting point, and I'll read coverage of the Court differently going forward.
