## Classification
An exploration of the history of fire and California. It demonstrates that California and fire have a deep, long relationship and that both have evolved together.

## The Book in a Sentence
Kaufmann explains the recent wildfires in California and demonstrates why fire has always been necessary for the state.

## Structure
Starts with a history of how fire shaped California, then moves through Native American cultivation of fire, then present-day wildfires.

## The Problem
California had a nasty run of wildfires in the last decade and Kaufmann wants to explain them to the lay reader: Why did they happen? What's the history of California with respect to fire? What can we do to lessen the impact of future wildfires?

He succeeds. The TL;DR of his argument: the U.S. has practiced a no-burn policy for the last 75–100 years, and this has resulted in the overgrowth of a lot of the forest. With so much fuel, fires get giant. He points out that California historically had many small yearly fires, and the benefits of those small fires on the ecosystem — they help spread seeds, clear the brush, and make room for new growth.

## Key Terms
No new vocabulary that stuck — more a way of conceptualizing the recent wildfires than a framework book.

## Key Ideas
U.S. policy of no burns has dramatically harmed the ecosystem and heightened our vulnerability to massive fires. We should do as much as we can to go back to the system before, where we have many many small yearly fires.

## Disagreements
The argument is great. It seems so obvious. And maybe that's the weakness — if it's so obvious, why do we find it so hard to make progress? He could've explored there.
