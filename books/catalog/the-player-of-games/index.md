## Classification
Science fiction, book 2 in Iain M. Banks' Culture series. Follows a master game player as a faction in the Culture uses him to interact with a nearby empire.

## The Book in a Sentence
A master game player named Gurgeh travels to the Empire of Azad to try their fabulous game — a game so complex, so like life itself, that the winner becomes emperor.

## Structure
A chronological novel that follows Gurgeh throughout. Setup in the Culture, travel to the Empire, then the tournament and its consequences.

## The Problem
Not an argument book per se, but Banks uses the story to hold up a mirror to our own civilization. The Empire of Azad is built on a game-as-meritocracy — anyone not successful in the game is left out of life's opportunities. It's an exaggerated version of our own systems, and that exaggeration is the point.

## Key Terms
No new vocabulary — Banks works in standard English. The central concept is the game of Azad itself: a game so complex it models life, used to justify the empire's hierarchy. "Meritocracy" as a game the winners insist is fair because they won.

## Key Ideas
Banks offers a frame for looking at our own civilization. Are we the Empire? Is meritocracy just a game? What happens to the people left out? The book makes you look at how our society is structured — the way systems of dominance are legitimized by the belief that the game is fair. It's exaggerated, and that's what makes it work.

## Disagreements
Not applicable — it's a novel, not an argument.

## Takeaways
A frame to keep in the back pocket. Not an immediate change in thinking, but a useful lens that may be important someday.
