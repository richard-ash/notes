## Classification
A series of eleven talks from Charlie Munger where he espouses many ideas on psychology and worldly wisdom. He uses each talk to build toward the last, where he details 25 of his mental models and how he uses them in everyday life. He tries to teach not directly but through stories and vignettes.

## The Book in a Sentence
Charlie Munger's worldview, in his own words.

## Structure
Eleven sequential talks.

## The Problem
Munger is trying to address the problem of how to think and why people are still so bad at it. He looks at the problem through many angles, using stories to probe the question. He comes up with many psychological mental models he uses as a checklist to help him think.

Whether he succeeds is hard to say. He definitely gets his opinion across, and it's unique.

## Key Terms
- **Mental models** — a latticework of frameworks pulled from many disciplines, used as a checklist to think.
- **The 25 standard causes of human misjudgment** — Munger's checklist of psychological tendencies that distort thinking. Full list in the highlights.
- **Lollapalooza effects** — extreme outcomes from confluences of psychological tendencies pushing in the same direction; Munger's most distinctive coinage.
- **Inversion** — "Invert, always invert." Solve forward and backward; figure out what *not* to do.
- **Circle of competence** — know what you know and what you don't.
- **Margin of safety** — incorporate one in every investment evaluation.
- **Sit-on-your-ass investing** — concentrated (~3 positions), low-turnover; Munger's view of "diversification."
- **The 10-principle investment framework** — Risk, Independence, Preparation, Intellectual humility, Analytic rigor, Allocation, Patience, Decisiveness, Change, Focus. Full text in the highlights.
- **The five Ws** — Carl Braun's rule: tell who is to do what, where, when, and *why*. Always say why.
- **Granny's rule** — eat your carrots before dessert; do the unpleasant, necessary tasks first, then reward yourself.
- **Man-with-a-hammer syndrome** — overweighing what can be measured because that's what your single tool handles; underweighing the hard-to-measure but more important factors.
- **Incentive-caused bias** — people drift into immoral behavior driven (consciously and subconsciously) by incentives, then rationalize. "Never seen a management consultant's report that didn't end with: 'This problem needs more management consulting services.'"

## Key Ideas
- The way of thinking really resonates. Using a latticework of mental models to go about one's day and figure out problems is the best mode of thinking I've come across.
- Lifelong learning.
- Checklists.
- **Bet heavily when the odds are in your favor.** The pari-mutuel framing: most of the time, do nothing. When you find a mispriced bet, load up.
- **Technology helps owners only sometimes.** Distinguish commodity businesses (where productivity gains flow to the customer) from franchise businesses (where they stick to the owner). The textile-loom story.
- **Ideology distorts cognition.** Even a genius like Chomsky bends to it. Don't pick up heavy ideology young.
- **Virtue and vice effects matter in economics.** Double-entry bookkeeping and the cash register did more for human morality than the congregational church.
- **Teach by indirection.** Make the listener reach for the conclusion — they hold it better via commitment-and-consistency. (This is Munger's whole pedagogical approach across the book; also, per the Disagreements section below, the part I find frustrating about it.)

## Disagreements
The method of teaching can be obtuse. Munger isn't telling you answers — he gives you stories and lets you figure out your own. More direct case-study detail would help.

## Takeaways
Already interior to what I've learned, and still good to reinforce.
