## Classification
Contrarian revisionist economic history with a policy agenda. Erdmann re-litigates the 2008 crisis to argue that housing *supply* — not credit excess — was the root cause, then turns that diagnosis into a present-day case for building more.

## The Book in a Sentence
The 2008 housing crash wasn't a credit bubble bursting — it was a supply shortage in a few un-buildable "Closed Access" cities, which we then turned into a catastrophe by cutting off mortgage credit to working families and running tight money.

## Structure
Introduction plus six parts, building from diagnosis → mechanism → indictment → prescription:

- **Introduction** — America has "made it illegal to just do stuff"; real pent-up potential strangled by rules.
- **Part 1 · A New History of the Great Recession** — the supply-shortage thesis; Closed Access vs. Contagion cities; prices tracked rents, not speculation.
- **Part 2 · Demand During the Housing Boom** — Fannie/Freddie/Ginnie and exotic mortgages were a *side effect* of rising prices, not the cause.
- **Part 3 · From Soft Landing to Suicide Pact** — policy abandons the soft landing; a Fed-induced demand shock collapses homebuilding forecasts by six million units.
- **Part 4 · Marching Hand in Hand to Crisis** — CDOs and fear as symptoms of an already-turned cycle, not drivers.
- **Part 5 · Adding Insult to Injury** — the twin blunders: tight monetary policy, then regulators locking working-class borrowers out of credit even after QE.
- **Part 6 · Where Do We Go from Here?** — a now-nationwide shortage; build more, restore credit access to the bottom half.

## The Problem
What actually caused the 2008 crisis, and what does getting the story wrong cost us? Erdmann made a convincing argument and laid out his ideas well — in that he succeeded. As for convincing the general public, that's still in progress and will likely be left for the historians.

## Key Terms
- **Closed Access cities** — superstar metros (NYC, SF, LA, Boston, San Diego) where building is effectively illegal, so a shortage shows up as price.
- **Contagion cities** — the Phoenix/Vegas/inland-CA/Florida metros that absorbed the out-migration and built real housing for it.
- **The canon** — the entrenched conventional wisdom (deliberately "hard to change") that Erdmann says got 2008 exactly backward.
- **Demand shock** — his reframe of the bust: a withdrawal of money and credit, not a supply correction.
- **Soft landing → suicide pact** — policymakers choosing to crush the market rather than let it settle.

## Key Ideas
The arguments that landed hardest (the first three are the ones worth repeating):

1. **Prices tracked rents, so it was a shortage, not a mania.** The empirical hinge: the places with the highest prices were the places with the highest rents. Real deprivation, not speculative profligacy.
2. **Mortgages were an effect, not a cause.** Exotic loans and rising debt were borrowers stretching to cope with supply-driven prices — a symptom downstream of the shortage, not the engine.
3. **The cure caused the disease.** Acting on the false "reckless lending" canon, regulators cut credit and the Fed tightened — *that* triggered the migration collapse, the building cancellations (which spiked in 2006, before prices fell), and the recession.
4. **The Fed ran tight, not loose.** The post-Lehman balance-sheet expansion was, on net, aggressively contractionary — the deflationary shock was policy-made.
5. **The recovery was rigged for the haves.** Even after QE, credit stayed shut to the bottom half, so only the top half of the housing market recovered — possibly "the most damaging policy decision of the entire affair."

## Disagreements
The weakest point is the underweighting of the default/financial-fragility explanation. To his credit, Erdmann addresses it. But it's weakest mostly because others have yet to move from the default position — the argument hasn't won the field, which itself signals its vulnerability.

## Takeaways
The alternative explanation for the 2008 recession is hugely insightful and forces you to look at the problem in a different way. Was the leverage in the system really the cause — or was that the gasoline, and the reduction in the ability to lend was the match?
