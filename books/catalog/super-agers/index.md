## Classification
A book describing recent advances in medicine and how they will aid human longevity. Topol works through chronic diseases — heart disease, neurodegenerative disease, and others — and looks at how recent tech advances could revolutionize medicine.

## The Book in a Sentence
Dr. Eric Topol lays out the longevity landscape and provides a doctor's overview for the lay person.

## Structure
Moves through distinct sections, focused on disease — for each one, showing the recent advances. The chronic-killer section is structured around the big three: cardiovascular disease, cancer, and neurodegeneration.

## The Problem
Topol sees a lot of bad information in the longevity space — the Bryan Johnson / unproven-supplement / hormesis-fad set — and wants to provide an authoritative guide for lay people, contrasting that noise with the boring evidence base (sleep, exercise, GLP-1s, statins, Mediterranean diet).

Did he succeed? Eh. There's a reason people listen to others — the book is too academic and dry, and that may cause people to overlook it.

## Key Terms
From the book (didn't personally stick, but worth naming):
- **Organ clocks** — epigenetic age measured per-organ via plasma proteins, not whole-body.
- **Polygenic risk scores** — multi-gene risk estimates for chronic disease.
- **p-tau-217** — blood biomarker for Alzheimer's.

## Key Ideas
The landscape is definitely changing. His ideas aren't wrong — they could just have been communicated in a better fashion.

From the book itself: GLP-1s are treated as the most underappreciated longevity intervention of the decade, well beyond their weight-loss application.

## Disagreements
Mostly a presentation critique rather than a substantive one: too academic, too dry. Nothing specific to push back on.

## Takeaways
Mostly interior to knowledge already had. Would recommend it to someone else wanting an overview.

One concrete idea did land — see the highlight note on Chapter 7: medical risk forecasting (multimodal AI integrating clinical factors, APOE genotype, polygenic risk scores, blood biomarkers, retinal imaging, speech analysis, etc.) could be a good model for an agent that surfaces a person's predicted long-term disease risk based on their biomarkers.
