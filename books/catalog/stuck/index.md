## Classification
Narrative history walking through eras of American mobility. It goes through the history and shows how local zoning grew into a thing and strangled mobility. It's also about how the rich used zoning to their advantage.

## The Book in a Sentence
How high-opportunity regions have priced out most of the population of the US — by refusing to build, they make it so only those who are rich or working in rich jobs can afford to be in the region.

## Structure
Two thematic parts forming a diagnostic-to-historical arc:

- **Part I — Diagnosis.** Chapter 1 ("A Nation of Migrants") frames the "drastic decline in geographic mobility" as a mobility crisis, not an affordability one. Moving toward opportunity is now a privilege of the economic elite.
- **Part II — Historical construction of the barriers**, roughly chronological:
  - Ch. 5 "Dirty Laundry" — invention of early zoning (the Modesto ordinance) as a facially neutral tool of land-use control.
  - Ch. 6 "Tenementophobia" — 1920s revolt against diversity; restrictive covenants and anti-apartment sentiment among the white, educated, and affluent.
  - Ch. 8 "The Housing Trap" — the 1933 HOLC, the invention of the long-term amortizing mortgage, and how it tied Americans to fixed locations.
  - Ch. 9 "A Plague of Localists" — postwar empowerment of local veto groups, creating a system where anyone with time and resources can block construction.

## The Problem
Appelbaum is trying to draw the narrative for why mobility has stalled in the US. He succeeded — zoning is a huge problem and definitely keeps people from moving to certain neighborhoods. Whether it was always *intentional* as an exclusion tool is murkier; maybe in some cases, but not always clear.

## Key Terms
- **Mobility crisis** — the central reframe: not an affordability crisis but a mobility one. Cheap housing exists (Flint); the elite-only good is *moving toward opportunity*.
- **Tenementophobia** — affluent reformers' passionate opposition to apartment buildings.
- **Zoning / districting** — originated in Modesto. "Control what can be done upon the land, and you control the prospects of the people who inhabit."
- **Restrictive covenants** — private property agreements used alongside zoning to exclude.
- **HOLC** — the 1933 New Deal agency that invented the modern mortgage (15-year terms, 80% LTV); entrenched federal control over housing markets and is still largely unique worldwide.
- **Community sovereignty** — local jurisdictions' power to "select their own members" through codes, zoning, and vetoes.
- **The reformers' dysfunctional system** — the cumulative result of progressive-era, New Deal, and postwar reforms granting veto power to anyone with time, education, and resources.

## Key Ideas
- **Framing zoning as the problem is the right call**, and identifying zoning as the *mechanism* by which others exert local control is a great point. Makes you think about systems like Japan's, where zoning is standardized and set at the national level.
- **The crisis is mislabeled as "affordable housing."** Cheap housing exists in places like Flint — that offers no escape. The real problem is moving toward opportunity, which has become an elite privilege.
- **A profound historical inversion.** For centuries, high-opportunity places drew the poor seeking "a foothold on the first rung." That pattern recently reversed: the places ordinary Americans most need to reach are priced beyond their grasp.
- **The modern mortgage was a mobility-suppressor.** The 1933 HOLC invented the long-term amortizing mortgage to stop Depression-era defaults and inadvertently tied Americans to fixed locations.
- **A multi-generational coalition built the cage** — Progressive-era scientific managers, New Deal bureaucrats, and postwar activists, "from a wide mix of motives, some laudable and others despicable."
- **Exclusion as a conscious "revolt against diversity."** 1920s affluent reformers explicitly sought tools to "defend their privilege" and keep "social inferiors someplace else," drawing on the New England tradition of autonomous towns as members-only clubs.
- **Prosperity used to trigger densification, not exclusion.** "Farms gave way to clusters of houses, which turned into town houses, which sprouted into apartment buildings." Modern restrictions froze that.
- **Immobility erodes community.** Counterintuitively, Americans expressed *more* trust in each other and *more* faith in institutions during the high-mobility postwar decades than during the static Depression years.

## Disagreements
- **The book is trying to do too much.** It's trying to give the back story *and* demonstrate why the argument is right. If you already agree, you just want the back story without the convincing. If you don't agree, you don't want the back story at all — you want the argument first.
- **Was the exclusion really intentional, or structural?** America has always been racist and exclusionary, but historically there was enough land that you could move somewhere else and start a new community. The issue now may be less about intentional exclusion and more about agglomeration effects: the places with the best opportunities have the least room for new people, and the returns to high-opportunity locations are higher than ever. It's not that people don't have freedom to move — it's that the high-opportunity locations are very, very crowded.

## Takeaways
Interior to my existing knowledge set — didn't shift my view, but reinforced the framing. Worth looking into Japan's nationally standardized zoning as a counterpoint.
