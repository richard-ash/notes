## Classification

A history of lobbying in DC. It looks at the major eras since the 1970s and tracks the three main phase changes K Street went through. A narrative history up until the present day.

## The Book in a Sentence

A narrative history of K Street's interaction and evolution with Washington DC.

## Structure

The book is told as a narrative, starting in the 1970s and moving to the present, organized around three phase changes in how corporate influence works:

1. **The Inside Game (1972–1999)** — the rise of the corporate lobbyist. In the 1970s, corporations realized they could win in the legislature by spending money on lobbyists to influence the way legislation was being written.
2. **The Outside Game (2000–2015)** — corporations realized they could go directly to the public with their pleas. The canonical case is net neutrality, where Google and other websites showed users their pleas and Congress was inundated with calls.
3. **The Reckoning (2015–present)** — the fragmentation of the lobbyist dynasties and the loss of their moral power. The rise of Trump and the populist movement around him. Corporations lost power in this era and will have to restrategize.

## The Problem

A narrative history detailing the rise and fall of the corporate lobbyist. They pulled it off.

## Key Terms

- **Inside game vs. outside game** — directly persuading lawmakers vs. mobilizing the public to pressure them.
- **Bundling** — lobbyists aggregating $1K–$2K checks from their networks of clients, colleagues, and friends into $100K+ hauls delivered to candidates. The workaround that emerged after McCain-Feingold cut off soft money.
- **Shadow lobbying** — post-Abramoff, influence work shifted into "public affairs, grassroots, polling, data analytics," none of which counts as lobbying under the legal definition and so doesn't have to be disclosed. Genentech is the datapoint: $50M advocacy budget, only $5M of it formally lobbying.
- **Black ops** — Evan Morris's term for planting sensationalized stories in hyper-partisan online outlets to whip up public emotion and pressure Congress.
- **"Odd couple" coalitions** — pairing corporate lobbyists with progressive activists who oppose the same bill for opposite reasons (Republican lobbyists + National People's Action against FHA reform).
- **The three dynasties** — Patton Boggs (Tommy Boggs), Black Manafort & Stone, and the Podesta Group. The cast that runs through the whole book.
- **Activist billionaires** — the post-Citizens United figure (Koch, Thiel) that displaced corporate PACs and tends to fund more ideologically extreme candidates than the corporate establishment ever did.
- **The pro-business consensus** — the 40-year bipartisan frame of reducing government authority and maximizing economic efficiency that these dynasties cemented in both parties.

## Key Ideas

The authors' overall frame is that corporate lobbying is bad. The narrative side is great — they do a great job getting into the weeds of the story and describing what actually happened.

The structural choice was well done: they show the rise of the dynasties but then also the moral corruption of the lobbyists at the top. Power corrupts; absolute power corrupts absolutely — again.

## Disagreements

The frame that all corporate lobbying is bad is oversold. Corporations should have a voice — their voice just shouldn't overshadow the voice of individual people.
