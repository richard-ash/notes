## Classification
A book arguing for why crypto will be important. It tries to showcase the vision and help people understand the why.

## The Book in a Sentence
An argument for a future where blockchains are at the center of society.

## Structure
The book is organized into three parts, framed around different eras in tech:

- **Introduction** — sets up the case: the internet went from permissionless to permissioned, and a handful of corporate networks now control everything.
- **Part One: Read. Write.** — the history of the internet through the read (web1) and read/write (web2) eras, and how corporate networks displaced protocol networks like RSS.
- **Part Two: Own.** — the case for blockchains as a new class of computer that enables user ownership of data, identity, and digital goods.
- **Part Three: A New Era** — what the future looks like if blockchain networks succeed: lower take rates, easy switching between front-ends, new governance models.

Dixon argues throughout that crypto will fix a lot of broken fundamentals in the current regime — most importantly, that we don't own our data.

## The Problem
Dixon is trying to convince others that this vision is a good vision to build for.

Did he succeed? Partially. The 3/5 rating is more about execution than the arguments themselves — I'm already convinced that blockchains will be fundamental to society at some point in the future. Just the way he describes it... I liked it but didn't love it.

## Key Terms
- **Read / Write / Own** — the three eras of the internet (web1, web2, web3)
- **Protocol networks vs. Corporate networks vs. Blockchain networks** — the central taxonomy
- **Take rates** — how much intermediaries extract from the people on their networks
- **Network effects** and **permission** — the gatekeeper problem
- **Federated networks** and the risk of **protocol coups**
- **Fungible vs. non-fungible tokens**

## Key Ideas
- The three eras framing (Read / Write / Own) is an interesting frame for thinking about where the internet has been and where it's going.
- The vision that we really should own our data, and that blockchains enable that.
- Blockchain networks enable easy switching between applications, which promotes competition and keeps take rates low. This is the structural reason they could be better than corporate networks rather than just a different flavor of them.

## Disagreements
As a book just describing what the vision could be, this is fine. But I want to see more emphasis on strategy and how we actually get there.

He also could've engaged more with the negative side of crypto and what to do about it — meme coins, easy profit for founders, the casino dynamics. There are a couple of places in my notes where this shows up: "He could back up this argument more," and on his treatment of critics, "This feels like he's just blowing them off and saying critics were wrong in the past so they still surely be wrong again."

## Takeaways
None really. The vision is already interior to my knowledge — I already thought this way. That's probably part of why the book is only 3/5 for me.

## Connections
Nothing strong from the rest of my reading. Most of what I've read recently has been in politics, history, and management — different territory.
