## Draft

### 1 — Setup: just finished the book

I recently finished Insecure Majorities (by Frances Lee) and had some thoughts on the problem she diagnoses in the book

### 2 — Diagnosis: majority competition drives partisan conflict

Lee's main argument is that competition for the majority is a big, underrated driver of partisan conflict in Congress. The gains from winning the majority are large enough that they poison the incentive to cooperate and govern

### 3 — Reframe: can't lower competition, so lower the prize

Near the end, Lee suggests a stable majority would govern better. But we can't engineer that. So instead, we should try to reduce the gains from winning. Said another way, we should give the minority a real stake in governance

### 4 — The fix: stop giving the majority all the chairs

For instance, all U.S. House committee chairs are awarded to the majority party. Why? What if instead we allocated them proportionally? Or even 2:1 in favor of the majority? Give the minority party something and watch the pressure to win the majority dissolve.

### 5 — Proof it's not utopian: Alaska

Alaska is attempting something in this vein. The majority is assigned to whichever group of legislators can assemble a floor majority, and they split the committee chairs across it. Currently, 14 Democrats, 5 Independents, and 2 Republicans make up this group even though Republicans won an outright majority of seats (21 of 40)

### 6 — Sign-off: read the book

So maybe the real question isn't how to make Congress less divided. It's why winning a bare majority should mean winning everything. For more, go read Insecure Majorities. It was an excellent and thought-provoking dive into why Congress is broken
