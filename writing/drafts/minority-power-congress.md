# Give the Minority Party Governing Responsibility

Status: outline
Source: [Insecure Majorities](../../books/catalog/insecure-majorities/index.md) by Frances E. Lee

## Core argument

Frances Lee argues that intense competition for majority control drives congressional polarization. Her implicit prescription — a less competitive Congress — is unrealistic. But there's another lever: reduce the spoils of winning. If majority control doesn't mean winning *everything*, the incentive to burn it all down weakens.

## Outline

### 1. Hook
Tom DeLay quote: "I am not among those here to govern. I am here to take over a majority from the Democrats." That's the whole problem in one sentence.

### 2. The diagnosis (Lee's argument, compressed)
Congress is polarized not just because of ideology but because majority control is worth too much. Neither party feels safe, so both invest in fighting rather than governing. The minority party has zero incentive to cooperate — a "glorious defeat" works better for base mobilization than a disappointing compromise. 2-3 paragraphs.

### 3. The twist (my insight)
Lee says competitiveness is the problem. But you can't make Congress less competitive — the country is closely divided and that's not changing. What you *can* do is reduce the prize. If winning the majority doesn't mean winning *everything* — all committee chairs, full agenda control, all the spoils for your lobbyists and donors — the incentive to obstruct weakens.

Key question to pose: if the Republicans held the House for 20 straight years, would Democrats settle into a governing minority role? Probably not in the current structure — because the structure gives the minority nothing.

### 4. The proposal — concrete mechanisms
- **Proportional committee chairs.** Instead of all chairs going to the majority, allocate proportionally. If you hold 45% of seats, you chair 45% of committees. The majority leader could still be elected by the majority, but committee chairs wouldn't be a winner-take-all prize.
- **Minority governing responsibilities.** Give the minority party a formal stake in outcomes — joint budget responsibilities, minority representation on the Rules Committee, requirement to address minority interests in legislation.
- **60-vote appropriations.** Force bipartisan coalitions on spending. Both parties own the budget.

For each: what changes in the incentive structure, and why.

### 5. Evidence it can work
- **Alaska** — bipartisan coalitions control both chambers, committee chairs distributed across party lines. The sustained American example.
- **US Senate 2001** — the 50-50 Lott-Daschle agreement gave both parties equal committee membership. Short-lived but proved it's operationally feasible.
- **European Parliament / German Bundestag** — proportional committee chair allocation is standard practice, not exotic.
- **Pre-Gingrich Congress** — Jonathan Rauch documents how informal power-sharing used to work. Minority Republicans "claimed a piece of the appropriations action in exchange for cooperation." Gingrich's revolution destroyed this.

### 6. The objection
"Won't this just produce gridlock?" No — it produces *negotiation*. The current system already produces gridlock, just with more finger-pointing. Bob Walker quote from the book: governing is hard, it requires compromise, and at the end of the day you only get part way to where you want to go — "You won, but you didn't feel really good about it." That's what governing is supposed to feel like.

### 7. Close
Connect back to the feedback loop. Congressional partisanship drives voter partisanship which drives further congressional partisanship. If you change the incentives at the congressional level — give the minority a reason to govern instead of obstruct — you might break the cycle. You don't need to change the electoral system. You can import the governance features of consensus democracy directly into the existing two-party Congress.
