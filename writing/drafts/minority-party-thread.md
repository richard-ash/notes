### 2 — Diagnosis: majority competition drives polarization

In the book, Lee's main argument is that majority party competition is a significant and understated contributor to polarization in Congress. The gains from winning the majority are large enough that they poison the incentive to cooperate and govern.

> **✏️ Suggested corrections**
> - ⚠️ Technical precision: "polarization" is the wrong word for Lee's *specific* claim. Her whole contribution is to separate her thesis from the standard **ideological polarization** story — she argues competition drives **partisan conflict / confrontation** (messaging, blame, obstruction) *independent of* ideology. Calling it a "contributor to polarization" quietly collapses her argument back into the thing she's distinguishing it from, and a knowledgeable reader (or Lee) would flag it. Use "partisanship" or "partisan conflict in Congress."
> - "they poison" agreement fix from last round is in. ✅

### 3 — Reframe: can't lower competition, so lower the prize

Near the end, Lee suggests a stable majority would govern better. But we can't engineer a stable majority. Instead we should try to reduce the gains from winning. Said another way we give the minority a real stake in governance.

> **✏️ Suggested corrections**
> - ✅ Verified: this is an accurate read of Lee — she does argue a *less competitive* Congress would be better governed, and calls her own prescription unrealistic. Your "we can't engineer a stable majority" lands the rebuttal cleanly.
> - Commas still missing: "Instead, we should..." and "Said another way, we give...".
> - Optional: "a less competitive Congress" mirrors Lee's actual term more closely than "a stable majority," but yours is a fair paraphrase.

### 4 — The fix: stop giving the majority all the chairs

For instance, all U.S. House committee chairs are awarded to the majority party. Why? What if instead we allocated them proportionally? Or even 2:1 in favor of the majority? Give the minority party something and watch the pressure to win the majority dissolve.

> **✏️ Suggested corrections**
> - ✅ Verified technically correct: every U.S. House standing committee chair comes from the majority party (the minority gets "ranking members"), and the majority holds a majority on every committee. Safe to state as fact.
> - "dissolve" slightly overstates (it'd *ease*, not vanish) — rhetorical, on-voice, leave it if you want the punch.

### 5 — Proof it's not utopian: Alaska

Alaska is attempting something in this vein. They determine the majority by whichever group of legislators can assemble and vote on a floor majority. The current majority consists of 14 Democrats, 5 Independents, and 2 Republicans even though Republicans won the most seats (21 of 40).

> **✏️ Suggested corrections**
> - ✅ Numbers verified against the 34th Alaska Legislature (2025): 40 House seats — 21 R, 14 D, 5 I. Coalition = 14 D + 5 I + 2 R (= 21); 19 Republicans sit in the minority. All correct.
> - ⚠️ Stronger *and* more accurate: 21 of 40 isn't just "the most seats," it's an **outright majority**. "won the most seats" reads as a plurality and undersells the twist. Say "even though Republicans won an outright majority of seats (21 of 40)" — that's the whole point: a real majority party, shut out.
> - Heads-up on a real coincidence: the coalition also sums to 21, so two different 21s are in play (21 R elected vs. 21 in the coalition). You avoided stating the coalition's total, which helps — but adding "two Republicans crossed over, leaving 19 in the minority" makes *why 21 ≠ control* explicit and removes any lingering confusion.

For more, go read Insecure Majorities. It was an excellent and thought provoking dive into why Congress is broken.
