## Please tell us about a time you most successfully hacked some (non-computer) system to your advantage.

I hacked big-tech hiring by figuring out what the interview actually measures, and by training it through a back door no one else uses. I studied physics and economics in college and taught myself to code at a startup for 2 years after graduating. As the startup wound down, I interviewed at Google. I bombed the interview even though I was crushing LeetCode. Instead of doubling down on LeetCode, I realized there was an adjacent skill I'd never trained: verbal fluency. I had to be able to talk about my work as fluently as I could do it. So I looked for other options. With no formal experience, I impressed the hiring manager at General Assembly in a live teaching demo and landed a job as their assistant iOS instructor. And when the lead dropped out, I stepped up and led the course. When I returned to big-tech interviews, it was different. Seven months after Google passed on me, I had competing offers from Facebook and Uber. The interview was never really testing whether I could code. It was testing whether I could think out loud. I found the one job that paid me to practice exactly that.

## Please tell us in one or two sentences about the most impressive thing that you have built or achieved.

In 2017, before Core ML existed, I shipped real-time credit-card scanning at Uber: I optimized a YOLO-based OCR model and bundled a TensorFlow runtime into the app to run the model on-device. It's still in production almost a decade later, scanning cards for millions of riders.
