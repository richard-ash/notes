Earlier today: Luis Garicano's 2026-04-24 *Silicon Continent* essay "The task is not the job" added to economics as `messy-jobs` — durable contribution is the **supply-side complement** to Imas's [[wiki/economics/ai-and-relational-scarcity|relational-scarcity]] demand-side argument, drawing on Garicano's forthcoming book *Messy Jobs* with Jin Li and Yanhui Wu. The two-pronged thesis: (1) **firms buy bundles, not tasks** — labour markets price jobs, and the question is not whether AI can perform a component but whether that component can be separated from the rest of the bundle at low cost; weak bundles unbundle and the human loses share, strong bundles hold and the human captures the productivity gain inside them. Two empirical anchors: travel agents (employment 60%+ below dot-com peak as booking unbundled, but surviving agents moved upmarket to luxury consortia and planning fees, raising avg weekly earnings from 87% → 99% of private-sector average 2000→2025); accountants (Frey-Osborne 2013 put automation probability at 94%, BLS now shows 1.6M employed at $81,680 median, +5% projected through 2034 — while "bookkeeping clerks" fall 6% — because the clerical task is a weak bundle and the audit-signing/legal-exposure-bearing accountant's job is a strong bundle). Three traits make bundles strong: **unpredictable demand** (Yanhui Wu's Hangzhou fieldwork on a Chinese customer-service firm two years into a domain-specific model deployment — humans still indispensable on recognising what the customer doesn't explicitly say + avoiding social-media-disaster interactions; senior manager quote "frequent switches between AI and human modes — coordination cost would be too high"), **production spillovers** (the radiologist who already spoke with the patient reads the scan better than one who sees only the image), and the **Alchian-Demsetz measurement problem** (joint production makes contribution-allocation impossible, so when AI drafts and a human signs and it goes wrong, the bundle holds because liability cannot be cheaply split — banks/regulators/clients need someone to blame). (2) **Organisations allocate authority and settle conflicts** — managers exist because marketing/engineers/lawyers/finance have legitimate but conflicting goals and someone must decide who loses. Garicano draws three pillars from organisational economics to argue AI cannot yet do this: **Arrow on trust** ("information is not a commodity, you cannot inspect it before you buy it because once you have, you already have it — what checks distortion is trust, which cannot be traded; if you have to buy it, you already have some doubts about what you've bought"); **Williamson on hold-up** (relationship-specific investments create vulnerability — xAI's Memphis Colossus exemplified after sunk-cost, the city demanded concessions; a human manager carries a reputation across counterparties, an AI cannot be sued or replaced-with-fanfare to signal reset); **Hart-Moore on residual decision rights** (the construction contract specifies March delivery but not what happens when the electrician and the plumber both need the same wall on the same day; the manager exercises authority over what contracts and processes do not specify — and this is not a cognitive task but an institutional one with three requirements: hold tacit/relational knowledge people will not share because communicating it gives away bargaining power, bear consequences (Sonos CEO fired after the 2024 app launch failure), confer legitimacy on the decision — which is what meetings actually do, they are rituals of procedural fairness not information exchanges, and people accept decisions they dislike when the process looks legitimate and the decider is accountable). Garicano concedes AI could in principle acquire this standing — the machinery (corporate registries, professional licensing, courts that compel testimony) took centuries to build for humans, no equivalents exist for AI, and the constraint is transition cost not stable equilibrium. Explicit reconciliation with Imas: same underlying price-theoretic intuition (when one input becomes cheap, its complements rise) applied at different scales — Imas describes "the economy of what customers want" (terminal consumption of relational goods), Garicano describes "the economy of what organizations need to do" (intermediate coordination, authority, accountability). Garicano claims the second is larger, which is the substantive disagreement: Imas's list (boutique fitness instructors, bespoke tailors, spiritual guides) reads as exhaustive and frightening if you are a 35-year-old middle manager; Garicano's complement (managers handling ambiguity, integrating context, reconciling conflicting interests, bearing the consequences of decisions) covers the hundreds of millions of office workers Imas leaves out.
