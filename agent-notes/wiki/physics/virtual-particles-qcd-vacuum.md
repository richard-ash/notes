# Virtual Particles and the QCD Vacuum

Virtual particles are a foundational concept in quantum field theory: particle–antiparticle pairs that continuously fluctuate in and out of existence in the vacuum. Their status has long been debated — are they "real" physical entities, or merely bookkeeping devices in Feynman diagrams? A 2026 experiment by the STAR collaboration at Brookhaven National Laboratory provides the strongest direct evidence yet that virtual quark–antiquark pairs in the QCD vacuum are physically real, by showing that their quantum properties (spin correlations) survive the transition into observable particles.

## The QCD vacuum and the quark condensate

The vacuum of quantum chromodynamics is not empty. It is permeated by the *quark condensate* ⟨q̄q⟩ — a nonvanishing expectation value of virtual quark–antiquark pairs. This condensate is intimately tied to *spontaneous chiral symmetry breaking*, the mechanism responsible for most of the mass of hadrons like protons and neutrons. Light quarks (up, down) have intrinsic masses of only a few MeV/c², yet protons weigh ~1 GeV/c² — roughly 150 times heavier. The difference comes from the dynamics of confinement and the quark condensate.

The quantum numbers of the vacuum (J^PC = 0^−+) constrain the quark–antiquark pairs in the condensate to have their spins parallel (spin-triplet states). This is the key prediction that the STAR experiment tests.

## The STAR experiment

The STAR collaboration measured spin correlations in ΛΛ̄ (Lambda–anti-Lambda) hyperon pairs produced in proton–proton collisions at √s = 200 GeV at the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider (RHIC). The physical picture:

1. **Protons collide** at 99.996% the speed of light, exciting the QCD vacuum
2. **Virtual strange quark–antiquark (ss̄) pairs** are liberated from the condensate
3. **Confinement reasserts itself** — the liberated quarks cannot remain free. The color flux tube ("string") between separating quarks stretches until it snaps, creating new particle–antiparticle pairs. Each quark binds with others to form hadrons (hadronization)
4. **Some ss̄ pairs hadronize into Λ and Λ̄ hyperons**, whose decay products allow reconstruction of the parent particles' spin

The key result: **a relative polarization signal of (18 ± 4)%** linking the spins of ΛΛ̄ pairs. This correlation is inherited from the spin-correlated virtual quark pairs in the vacuum — direct evidence that the virtual pairs were physically present before being "materialized" by the collision.

Crucially, **the correlation vanishes when the hyperon pairs are widely separated in angle**, consistent with quantum decoherence during the hadronization process. This angular dependence rules out trivial explanations and provides a quantitative window into how quantum coherence is lost during the transition from partons to hadrons.

## The string-breaking picture

Hossenfelder offers an accessible framing of the result using the string model of QCD confinement. The strong nuclear force has an unusual property: it *strengthens* with distance (unlike gravity and electromagnetism, which weaken). Two quarks being pulled apart are connected by a color flux tube that behaves like a stretching string — the farther apart, the more energy stored. Eventually the string snaps, and the released energy creates a new quark–antiquark pair at the break point. Each new quark binds with one of the original quarks, producing two new hadrons.

This string-breaking process converts virtual quark–antiquark pairs into real ones. The STAR result demonstrates that the quantum correlations (spin entanglement) of the original virtual pair survive this conversion — proving the virtual particles were there all along.

Hossenfelder draws an analogy to Hawking radiation near black holes, where gravitational tidal forces play the role of the separating color force, pulling virtual particle–antiparticle pairs apart until one falls in and the other escapes as real radiation.

## Philosophical implications

The ontological status of virtual particles has been a persistent question in the foundations of physics. Two camps:

- **Instrumentalist view**: Virtual particles are computational tools — internal lines in Feynman diagrams that help calculate observable quantities. They don't "exist" in any meaningful sense; only the final measured quantities are real.
- **Realist view**: If the mathematics correctly describes observations, that *is* what it means for something to be real. Hossenfelder argues the instrumentalist position is self-defeating — the same logic would force you to say real particles aren't "really there" either, since they too are mathematical descriptions of observations.

The STAR result strengthens the realist position by showing that properties attributed to virtual particles (spin correlations in the condensate) have measurable consequences in the real particles they become.

Hossenfelder extends this to a deeper question: if the vacuum is teeming with virtual particles whose relationships generate measurable physical effects, what does that say about the nature of space itself? Could space emerge from the relational structure of virtual particles, rather than being a backdrop they inhabit?

## The proton spin puzzle

The STAR result also sheds light on the longstanding proton spin puzzle. The SU(6) quark model predicts that 100% of the proton's spin comes from its three valence quarks. Experiment shows valence quarks contribute only ~35%. STAR has confirmed that roughly half of the remaining 65% comes from gluons. The hyperon spin structure measured in this experiment exhibits a different spin decomposition from the proton, offering a new experimental handle on how spin emerges from QCD dynamics.

## Future directions

RHIC ceased operations in February 2026. Brookhaven is now constructing the **Electron Ion Collider (EIC)**, which will collide electrons with protons and ions at high luminosity. The EIC is expected to provide precision measurements of the quark and gluon structure of hadrons, including further investigation of spin correlations and the nonperturbative QCD processes that the STAR result has opened up.
