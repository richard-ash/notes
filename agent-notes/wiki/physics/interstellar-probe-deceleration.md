# Interstellar probe deceleration as a Bayesian likelihood constraint

Samir Varma's 2026-05-17 essay "You Can't Quietly Park at 0.1c" argues that the standard Fermi-paradox debate over UAP-as-alien-probes (as advanced by Tyler Cowen and Robin Hanson) has been fought entirely at the level of the **prior** — whether interstellar probes plausibly exist in our solar system — while the **likelihood** — whether the actually-observed UAP behavior is what an alien probe would produce — has been ignored. Varma's contribution is to do the likelihood calculation from first-principles physics. The piece concedes the prior (probes are physically plausible) and crushes the posterior via the likelihood term.

The structural move is forensic Bayesian: the wins go to whoever finishes the derivation, not whoever has the most rhetorically defensible prior.

## The argument's structure

Varma sets the piece up as a charitable engagement with Cowen and Hanson, then a Bayesian decomposition:

1. **Concede the prior.** A civilization arising a few million years before ours — <0.1% of galactic history — could have launched 0.1c probes with self-replication (Tipler/Barlow), saturating the galaxy in 10⁶–10⁷ years. The naive speed-of-light and civilizational-coherence objections both fail. Score: Cowen 2, debunkers 0.
2. **Find the missing physics term.** The deceleration problem (and its slow-probe alternative, the archaeology problem) makes the likelihood of UAP-shaped evidence given the probe hypothesis vanish.
3. **Apply Bayes.** Prior × likelihood = posterior. Even a generous 5% prior multiplied by an illustrative 10⁻⁶ likelihood gives a posterior of ~5×10⁻⁸ — "noise," not "appreciably non-zero."

The form of the argument is what travels. The numerical likelihood pieces are explicitly illustrative.

## The deceleration constraint (fast probes)

**The rocket equation tax doubles.** Getting to 0.1c is hard. Stopping is at least as hard because Tsiolkovsky exponentiates: you must accelerate the fuel you will later use to decelerate. For a fusion drive at 0.05c exhaust velocity and a round-trip 0.2c delta-v, mass ratio = e⁴ ≈ 55 → 98% of launch mass is fuel. For chemical propulsion the number of zeros breaks notation.

**Kinetic energy is the binding constraint.** A 100 kg craft at 0.1c carries 4.5 × 10¹⁶ J ≈ 10 megatons TNT ≈ 700 Hiroshimas. This cannot be hand-waved away.

**Slow braking buys a runway, not silence.** Spreading 10 megatons over 10 years gives only 140 MW average power — a modest power plant. But constant deceleration from 0.1c over a decade requires roughly **half a light-year of braking distance** (tens of thousands of AU). This is a different ontological class of object from "thing hovering over an airport."

**External-momentum solutions are no help.** Solar radiation pressure at 1 AU delivers ~9 µN/m². Decelerating a 100 kg payload on decadal scales drives sail area toward ~1 km², and braking must begin where sunlight is dimmer still. Magsails and electric sails change the mechanism but preserve the moral: **every serious deceleration scheme requires a huge interaction cross-section, a long braking baseline, or both.**

## The flyby / rendezvous dichotomy

For atmospheric UAP, two structurally different probe modes exist and neither matches the data:

- **Flyby.** Physically easy — [Breakthrough Starshot](https://breakthroughinitiatives.org/initiative/3) is explicitly this design: gram-scale chips at 0.2c, past Alpha Centauri in hours. But a 0.1c flyby crosses the inner solar system in hours and is gone. **A flyby is a streak, not a sighting.** It does not loiter.
- **Rendezvous.** [Heller and Hippke (2017)](https://arxiv.org/abs/1701.08803) worked out a Starshot-style *rendezvous* at Proxima: ~76 g sail, 10 g payload, ~10⁵ m² sail area (hundreds of meters per side — "more stadium than spacecraft"), maximum capture speed only 4.6%c. The common pattern across every rendezvous design is **ultralight payload + huge apparatus + long baseline**. None of which is consistent with a compact object filmed over New Jersey.

"Atmospheric loitering" is neither a flyby signature nor a braking signature.

## The slow-probe escape and the archaeology problem

The natural counter — drop cruise speed from 0.1c to 0.01c or 0.001c — does reduce the deceleration bill. At 0.01c kinetic energy falls 100×; at 0.001c, 10000×. The rocket equation also relaxes. So yes, slow Von Neumann probes *could* arrive compact and quiet.

But the problem now shifts from mechanics to archaeology. Self-replication on million-to-billion-year timescales should produce **persistent infrastructure**: processed asteroids, factories, waste heat, communication nodes, spectral anomalies, orbital artifacts. Barlow's slow-probe model is explicitly an industrial system — robots, assemblers, factories — not a magic seed. The evidence required is structural, not anecdotal.

**The two cases bracket the hypothesis.** Fast probes should look like flybys or stadium-sized sails. Slow probes should look like Solar System archaeology. Neither looks like fuzzy orbs.

Two sub-escape-hatches:

- **The "lurker" hypothesis.** A probe parked in the asteroid belt since the Pliocene is physically allowed — but its expected signatures are orbital anomalies, thermal glints, spectral oddities. *Not* low-altitude videos. **The lurker hypothesis and the atmospheric-sightings hypothesis are not the same hypothesis**, and evidence for one is not evidence for the other.
- **"Probes build local factories."** This adds a *second* Fermi paradox inside the first — if they built factories, where are the factories? And large construction from asteroid materials should leave thermal/spectral/orbital signatures of its own. None observed.

## Superluminal shortcuts: wormholes and warp drives

The standard rejoinder — wormholes, Alcubierre drives — fails on the same energy-conditions wall. Every known traversable wormhole solution requires **violation of the null energy condition**: matter with negative energy density. Morris-Thorne-style constructions demanded planetary-to-stellar masses of exotic matter; later work (Visser, Kar, Dadhich) constructed geometries with arbitrarily small total violation, but Fewster-Roman analyses showed these are submicroscopic, severely curved, or otherwise constrained.

The Casimir effect proves negative energy *exists* at nanometer scales between parallel plates, but provides no path to the **macroscopic, stable, engineerable, geometrically configured** stress-energy a traversable wormhole requires. Varma's water analogy: observing a droplet of water tells you nothing about the engineering feasibility of a dam.

Alcubierre-style warp drives reduce the formal energy cost in later geometries but never eliminate the requirement for exotic stress-energy. The direction is invariant: these solutions need physics we have neither observed nor produced.

This isn't a technology gap. It's a physics gap. The Einstein field equations are among the most precisely tested theories in science (LIGO 2015, EHT 2019, daily GPS corrections); their constraint on what advanced civilizations can build is a binding one. Cowen explicitly acknowledges being "short usable wormholes." Varma agrees — and observes that the deceleration constraint already bites even granting that concession.

## The behavioral mismatch (separate diagnostic)

Even setting the physics aside, the actually-observed UAP behavior — glowing orbs, fast trajectories, atmospheric loitering — is inconsistent with *any* coherent probe model: not the dandelion model, not Hanson's distributed-civilization model, not even Cowen's "seek power sources and observe." Probes aren't doing the things probes would do (mining, replicating, building, broadcasting). They're "just there. Doing stuff that looks weird on camera."

Two diagnostic options, both unflattering:

- **A: they want to be seen.** Why? An interstellar civilization can build sensors that dominate Earth's atmosphere — visible hovering is a strange choice unless the purpose is theatre.
- **B: they don't want to be seen but are failing.** A civilization that solved interstellar navigation and self-replication cannot manage stealth against a 120-year-old aviation species? "Like someone who built a functioning warp drive being unable to turn their headlights off."

The Navy Tic Tac case — the UFO community's strongest single piece of evidence — is officially **unresolved**, per ODNI's public position and the [2024 AARO consolidated report](https://media.defense.gov/2024/Nov/14/2003583603/-1/-1/0/FY24-CONSOLIDATED-ANNUAL-REPORT-ON-UAP-508.PDF) and [NASA's public FAQ](https://science.nasa.gov/uap/faqs/), neither of which finds evidence of extraterrestrial activity. "Unresolved" is not evidence for any particular hypothesis. Misidentified terrestrial objects, sensor artifacts, classified military programs, and observer error remain consistent with all observations; advanced-alien-probe is not.

## The Bayesian formalization

Varma decomposes the likelihood into three roughly separable suppressions (illustrative, not measured):

- **L(size):** P(probe manifests as compact atmospheric craft | aliens). Fast-probe physics → ultralight sails, not compact craft. Slow-probe physics → infrastructure, not isolated objects.
- **L(loiter):** P(probe loiters visibly atmospherically vs. orbital remote sensing | aliens). Million-year-advanced tech choosing low hover is implausible.
- **L(ambiguity):** P(probe produces fuzzy video specifically vs. invisible-or-unambiguous | aliens). "The one thing million-year-old technology *wouldn't* produce is weird stuff on camera you can't quite explain."

With each factor at a generous 1%, the joint likelihood is 10⁻⁶. Even granting strong correlation between the factors (they are not independent), each would need to rise by orders of magnitude to change the answer. A 5% prior × 10⁻⁶ likelihood ≈ **5 × 10⁻⁸ posterior** — "not appreciably non-zero. Noise."

The structural lesson — the durable contribution Varma is exporting — is **"the prior is not the posterior."** Cowen on the Sullivan podcast openly described his 10% as an informed intuition shaped by Fermi-paradox reasoning, not a physics-derived number; Varma reads that as a prior, not a posterior, and observes that priors are where you start, not where you finish. The forensic move is to refuse to argue the prior and instead force the likelihood to be calculated.

## What the piece does *not* settle

Varma is explicit that the argument is against **inference from atmospheric UAP sightings to alien probes**, not against the broader Fermi-paradox prior. He concedes the Fermi paradox remains puzzling — the physics is permissive; sub-light self-replicating probes really could fill the galaxy in 10⁶–10⁷ years — and his closing claim is that the question worth deriving is "where are the probes that should be here?" not "are these particular videos probes?" The candidate answers (Great Filter, motivation problem, civilizational introversion, physics we don't yet understand) are left open.

The piece also does not engage:

- **Loeb's ʻOumuamua argument** specifically (Varma notes Loeb's UFO-adjacent work focuses on anomalous interstellar objects, not the rendezvous problem).
- **The detection-bias question** — what fraction of plausible probe activity would we be capable of observing with current instruments. The behavioral mismatch argument assumes our sensor coverage is broad enough that absence of evidence is evidence of absence; this is itself a contestable premise.
- **The Hanson "domestication" / "they're hiding from us deliberately and managing the visibility" theory** beyond noting that each added assumption narrows the parameter space. Hanson's full account is not engaged on its own terms.
