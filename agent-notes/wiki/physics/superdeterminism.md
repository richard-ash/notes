# Superdeterminism and the Speed of Light Limit

Superdeterminism is a class of hidden-variables interpretations of quantum mechanics in which all measurement settings and particle states share common causes stretching back to initial conditions. It is the only known way to construct a *local* hidden-variables theory that reproduces all quantum predictions, thereby avoiding the non-locality that Bell's theorem otherwise forces on any realist interpretation.

## The argument in brief

Hossenfelder argues that the physics community's treatment of the speed of light as a sacred, absolute limit rests on a circular chain of reasoning:

1. **Relativity says c is a barrier** — but no physicist expects general relativity to be the final theory, since it lacks quantum effects. The limitation might not survive in a theory of [[quantum-gravity|quantum gravity]].
2. **The causality objection is weaker than it appears.** The standard argument is that faster-than-light (FTL) signals allow closed timelike curves (time-travel paradoxes). Hossenfelder counters that this only follows if you ignore the empirical arrow of time. A cosmological "preferred slicing" — which already exists in standard cosmology, anchored to the cosmic microwave background — defines a universal forward-time direction. FTL signals constrained to travel forward along that slicing cannot form causal loops.
3. **Quantum non-locality is misunderstood.** Entanglement does not transmit information. Measuring one particle of an entangled pair does nothing to the other; the "non-locality" is purely epistemic — you learn something about a distant particle, but no signal travels. The apparent instantaneous "collapse" of the wavefunction has no direct experimental evidence as a physical process.
4. **The circularity.** Physicists assume the speed-of-light limit holds, therefore conclude wavefunction collapse is physical (since hidden variables would require superdeterminism, which most dismiss), therefore treat quantum mechanics as fundamental, therefore conclude the speed-of-light limit must persist even in quantum gravity — completing the circle.

## Why superdeterminism matters for FTL

If quantum mechanics is not fundamental but rather a *statistical* description of a deeper deterministic layer — analogous to how thermodynamics emerges from statistical mechanics — then deviations from quantum-mechanical predictions are in principle possible. Hossenfelder notes the structural similarity between the Dirac-von Neumann equation (equivalent to Schrodinger's equation) and the Liouville equation of classical statistical mechanics as suggestive evidence for this view.

In a superdeterministic theory, measurement outcomes can depend on the choice of measurement basis in ways that standard QM forbids. Such "out-of-distribution" results would break the no-signaling theorem: a measurement on one entangled particle could influence the statistics observed at the other, enabling FTL signaling — while remaining technically *local*, since all correlations were fixed at the point of entanglement.

## The EPR connection

Einstein, Podolsky, and Rosen's 1935 argument that quantum mechanics is "incomplete" is, in Hossenfelder's reading, essentially correct. The collapse postulate is a placeholder for an unknown physical process in the deeper theory. Bell's theorem only rules out local hidden variables if you assume "measurement independence" (that detector settings are uncorrelated with hidden variables) — superdeterminism relaxes exactly that assumption.

## Reception and controversy

Hossenfelder acknowledges that superdeterminism is a minority position. She attributes its dismissal largely to social reinforcement within physics rather than to substantive technical refutation — most physicists, she claims, have never engaged with how superdeterminism actually works. This is a contested claim; critics argue that superdeterminism undermines the very possibility of experimental science by allowing hidden correlations between experimenters' choices and particle states.

## Prediction

Hossenfelder predicts that anomalous observations in quantum computing and quantum technology experiments will emerge within the coming decade (~by mid-2030s), and that AI systems analyzing the data will identify hidden-variables models as natural explanations — eventually opening a path to FTL communication.
