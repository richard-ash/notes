# Reverse Searing — A Three-Phase Approach

The reverse sear is a two-stage technique for cooking thick steaks: a low-and-slow cook to bring the interior to target doneness, followed by a high-heat sear for crust. Chris Young — co-author of *Modernist Cuisine* and founder of Combustion Inc. — argues that the standard one-temperature approach (225–250 °F throughout) is a convenience compromise, and that understanding the three hidden phases of the low-and-slow stage unlocks sous-vide-level precision with a superior crust.

## Why grill temperature is misleading

A steak is mostly water (~3 cups in a 34 oz rib-eye). About 60% of the heat energy during cooking goes to evaporating water from the surface, not raising the meat's temperature. This evaporative cooling means the meat's effective cooking temperature is significantly lower than what the grill's air thermometer reads — the same principle behind why sweating cools you down.

## The three phases

Young identifies three distinct phases during the low-and-slow stage, each requiring a different grill temperature:

### 1. Warming (start at ~300 °F)

The meat is cold and can absorb heat quickly without risk of overcooking. Starting hot gets the surface temperature up to the target doneness range fast. Young monitors surface temperature (not just core) using a multi-sensor thermometer inserted parallel to the eventual slicing direction.

### 2. Stalling (drop to ~175 °F)

Once the surface temperature approaches target doneness, evaporative cooling and incoming heat reach equilibrium — the surface temperature stalls. Dropping the grill to ~175 °F holds this balance while the interior continues to warm gradually.

### 3. Rising (drop to target + 10 °F)

As the surface dries, moisture from deeper in the steak can't reach the surface fast enough to maintain cooling. The stall breaks and surface temperature climbs rapidly — the danger zone for overcooking. Young drops the grill to just ~10 °F above the target doneness (e.g., 150 °F for a 140 °F target). The core steadily climbs to match the surface, producing edge-to-edge evenness rivaling sous vide.

## The cooling step before searing

Young recommends letting the steak cool for 15–20 minutes before searing — not for juice retention (he has argued elsewhere that [[resting-meat|resting doesn't meaningfully reduce juice loss]]), but to create a temperature buffer. A cooler interior means more time under the sear before the core overcooks.

- **Thick steaks:** let core drop ~10 °F before searing
- **Thin steaks:** let core drop ~20 °F before searing

This cooling window doubles as prep time for getting the grill searing-hot and finishing the rest of the meal.

## The sear

Young's searing method: ~30 seconds per side, flipping frequently (4 flips over 2 minutes). Each flip lets the hot side shed excess heat through evaporative cooling before returning to the grill. This prevents the surface from driving heat too deep into the meat.

## Finishing

Young finishes with beef fat, butter, fresh-ground pepper, finishing salt, and a squeeze of lemon juice to cut through the richness.

## Young's target temperature for rib-eye

Young advocates 137 °F for rib-eye — slightly above traditional medium-rare — arguing the intramuscular fat in rib-eye renders better at this temperature, producing a more tender and less chewy bite than the standard 130–135 °F.

## Quick reference

| Phase | Grill temp | Trigger to move on |
|---|---|---|
| Warming | ~300 °F | Surface nears target doneness |
| Stalling | ~175 °F | Surface temp begins rising (stall breaks) |
| Rising | Target + 10 °F | Core reaches target doneness |
| Cooling | Off heat | Core drops 10–20 °F (15–20 min) |
| Searing | Max heat | 4 flips, ~2 min total |

## Comparison with constant-temperature reverse sear

The standard 225–250 °F approach works but is a compromise: it pushes through the stall faster at the cost of overcooking meat near the surface, and requires accounting for [[resting-meat|carryover heating]]. Young's three-phase method trades two extra temperature adjustments for edge-to-edge precision and eliminates guesswork about carryover.
