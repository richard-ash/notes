# Jasmine Rice

Jasmine rice is a long-grain Thai variety that cooks softer and more fragrant than standard white rice. It needs less water than most white rice — the typical 1:1.5 ratio used for other varieties will make it mushy.

## Water-to-Rice Ratio

| Method | Rice | Water |
|--------|------|-------|
| Unrinsed | 1 cup | 1¼ cups |
| Rinsed | 1 cup | 1¼ cups minus 2 tbsp |

The reduction for rinsed rice accounts for moisture absorbed during rinsing. One cup of dry jasmine rice yields approximately 3 cups cooked (3–4 servings).

## Stovetop Method

1. Combine rice and cold tap water in a medium saucepan with a tight-fitting lid.
2. Bring to a rapid simmer over medium-high heat, **uncovered**.
3. Reduce heat to low/medium-low, cover, and cook 12 minutes.
4. **Do not lift the lid** during cooking — steam does the work.
5. Remove from heat and rest, still covered, for 10 minutes.
6. Fluff with a rubber spatula or rice paddle.

The 10-minute rest is non-negotiable — it redistributes moisture so the grains firm up evenly rather than being wet on top and dry on the bottom.

## Rinsing

Rinsing is optional for jasmine rice. Nagi notes it "barely makes a difference to fluffiness," unlike starchier short-grain varieties where rinsing prevents clumping. If you do rinse (swish and drain 2–3 times until water runs relatively clear), reduce cooking water by 2 tablespoons per cup of rice.
