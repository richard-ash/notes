# Resting Meat — The Science of Carryover Cooking and Juice Retention

Resting meat after cooking is one of the most universal pieces of cooking advice. Chris Young — co-author of *Modernist Cuisine* and founder of Combustion Inc. — argues it's also one of the most misunderstood, and that the standard explanations for why resting "keeps meat juicy" are pseudoscientific.

## The carryover cooking problem

When meat is removed from a heat source, its core temperature continues rising as heat flows inward from the hotter surface. The conventional rule of thumb — "expect 5–10 °F of carryover" — is dangerously imprecise. Young's controlled experiments (pan at a constant 350 °F, Breville Control Freak) found:

| Steak thickness | Flipping frequency | Pull temp | Peak core temp | Carryover |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1¾ in | Every 30 sec | 120 °F | 147 °F | **27 °F** |
| 1¾ in | Once (halfway) | 120 °F | 143 °F | **23 °F** |
| 1 in | Every 30 sec | 120 °F | 141 °F | **21 °F** |
| 1 in | Once (halfway) | 120 °F | 138 °F | **18 °F** |

Carryover depends on the thermal gradient between surface and center, which is influenced by meat thickness, shape, fat content, cooking method, and flipping frequency. There is no reliable rule of thumb.

## Resting does not reduce juice loss

Young's key experiment: two identical steaks both brought to 130 °F final core temperature — one fully rested, one sliced immediately.

- **Rested steak:** 8.4 g of juice lost (~6% of post-cooking weight)
- **Unrested steak:** 8.8 g of juice lost (~6% of post-cooking weight)

No meaningful difference. Young reports repeating this experiment multiple times with consistent results.

## Why the "sponge" explanation is wrong

The popular explanation — that heating compresses muscle fibers like a sponge, and resting lets them relax and reabsorb juices — does not hold up. Water is incompressible, and there are no open tunnels within meat for juices to flow freely. Only juices within a few millimeters of the surface can percolate out during cooking. Juices lost to the pan are gone permanently; they are not "reabsorbed" during resting.

Young also retracts his own earlier hypothesis from *Modernist Cuisine*, where he proposed that cooling causes dissolved proteins to gel, thickening juices and preventing leakage. While juices do become more viscous as they cool (the same reason stock gels in the fridge), this effect is irrelevant at the temperatures of a warm steak — gelatin doesn't gel until the meat is essentially cold.

## The real mechanism: vapor pressure

At any temperature above ambient, water in muscle fibers is constantly evaporating and producing vapor pressure. When you slice into meat — rested or not — you create new escape routes for juices near the cut surface to be pushed out by this steam pressure. The only variable is whether the juices end up on the cutting board or the plate. Unless the meat is cold enough to eliminate meaningful vapor pressure, juice loss upon slicing is unavoidable.

## Why other experiments show a difference

Young acknowledges that other careful experimenters (Kenji López-Alt, Dan Souza at America's Test Kitchen, Dr. Greg Blonder at Amazing Ribs) have found rested meat retains slightly more juice. He attributes the discrepancy to confounding variables: small differences in pan temperature or flipping frequency between test runs cause different amounts of overcooking beneath the crust, which affects juice retention. A rested steak that appears juicier may simply be more evenly cooked and less overdone rather than benefiting from resting per se.

## Young's recommended method

Instead of relying on a fixed rest time and hoping carryover lands in the right place:

1. **Use a thermometer** to track core temperature continuously.
2. **Pull from heat 10–15 °F below target doneness** to allow brief resting time for plating.
3. **Monitor rising temperature** during the rest.
4. **Slice when it hits target doneness** — slicing dramatically increases surface area, dumping heat as steam and halting carryover. Even cutting a steak in half slows carryover significantly.

This approach treats slicing as a deliberate thermal control tool rather than something to avoid.
