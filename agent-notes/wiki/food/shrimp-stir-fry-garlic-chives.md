# Shrimp Stir-Fry With Garlic Chives and Chiles

A fast weeknight stir-fry built around the pairing of shrimp and garlic chives (Chinese leeks), with jalapeños for heat and a light mirin-soy glaze. Total time is about 30 minutes, most of it hands-off salting. Serves 4.

## Why it works

The cornstarch coating on the shrimp serves double duty: it creates a golden crust during the sear and helps the thin sauce cling. The technique of salting, resting, then blotting the shrimp before coating mirrors the dry-brining logic used in other protein preps — drawing surface moisture out so the Maillard reaction can start faster in the hot oil.

Garlic chives go in at the very end (30-60 seconds) so they wilt without going limp. This is the standard approach for delicate Chinese greens in wok cooking — the residual heat finishes them on the way to the plate.

## Key ingredients

- **Garlic chives (Chinese leeks):** Flat-leafed, thicker than standard chives, with a mellow garlic flavor. Sold in 6-12 oz bundles at Asian groceries. Use the whole bunch — they wilt down dramatically. Scallions substitute well; halve the whites lengthwise and add 30-60 seconds more cook time.
- **Jalapeños:** Sliced thin and cornstarch-coated with the shrimp so they fry alongside. 1-2 depending on heat preference.
- **Mirin + soy sauce + sesame oil:** The glaze is minimal — 1 Tbsp mirin, 2 tsp soy, ¼ tsp sesame oil — just enough to unify the dish without masking the chives.

## Method summary

1. Salt shrimp (¼ tsp kosher salt), rest 10 min, blot dry
2. Season with pepper, toss with sliced jalapeños and 2 Tbsp cornstarch
3. Sear in ¼ cup vegetable oil over medium-high, single layer, ~4 min total (flip halfway)
4. Crank to high, add garlic chives (2" pieces) and ginger matchsticks, toss 30-60 sec
5. Add mirin and soy sauce, toss, remove from heat, finish with sesame oil
6. Serve over [[jasmine-rice]] if desired

## Notes

- The recipe calls for a skillet rather than a wok — either works, but a flat-bottomed skillet gives better contact for the cornstarch crust on a Western burner.
- Ginger is cut into matchsticks rather than minced, keeping it as a textural element rather than dissolving into the sauce.
