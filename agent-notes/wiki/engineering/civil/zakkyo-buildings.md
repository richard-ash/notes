# Zakkyo Buildings

Zakkyo buildings (雑居ビル, literally "miscellaneous-use buildings") are a distinctive Japanese urban building typology that Noah Smith argues is a key reason Japanese cities feel fundamentally different from other dense cities worldwide. The term literally means "mixed-use," but in practice refers to 2–8 story buildings where multiple small shops or offices occupy different floors.

## Western mixed-use vs. zakkyo

In most of the world's dense cities — New York, Paris, San Francisco, Hong Kong, Singapore — "mixed-use development" follows a predictable pattern Smith calls "shop-top housing": retail on the ground floor, residential or offices above. The mixing is essentially binary — commercial bottom, residential top. This pattern holds from Little Italy in NYC to the Marais in Paris to Tsim Sha Tsui in Hong Kong.

Zakkyo buildings take a radically different approach. Rather than a two-layer sandwich, each floor hosts a completely different use — a ramen shop on the second floor, a dental clinic on the third, a bar on the fourth. The mixing is **vertical and granular**, not just a ground-floor/upper-floor split.

## Two key design features

Smith identifies two characteristics that make zakkyo work beyond simple multi-floor occupancy:

1. **Exterior signage on upper floors.** The stacked signs give Japanese downtowns their distinctive "forest of lights" aesthetic, but they also serve a functional purpose: passersby can discover upper-floor businesses from the street.
2. **Direct street access via stairs and elevators.** Upper floors open directly to the sidewalk, not through a lobby or shared entrance. The see-it-then-walk-in-immediately loop is critical — without frictionless access, upper-floor visibility alone wouldn't drive foot traffic.

## Serendipity

Smith argues the core benefit is **serendipity per unit of walkable street**. By stacking businesses vertically rather than spreading them horizontally, a pedestrian encounters more shops and restaurants per meter of walking. This benefits small businesses (easier customer discovery) and customers (more variety and novelty).

## The residential proximity effect

Smith considers this zakkyo's most remarkable consequence: by concentrating commercial activity vertically into a small land area, zakkyo buildings allow quiet residential neighborhoods to exist very close to dense commercial centers. His central example: Shoto, a quiet leafy residential neighborhood in Shibuya, is an **eight-minute walk** from Dogenzaka, one of the most packed commercial streets in Tokyo.

The math is straightforward: if 1,000 shops all occupy ground floors (shop-top model), the commercial district must spread three times wider than if those shops are distributed across three floors. This spatial compression means commercial noise and foot traffic stay concentrated rather than bleeding into surrounding residential areas — which, Smith notes, is one reason people resist mixed-use development in the first place.

## Zakkyo vs. indoor vertical malls

Indoor vertical malls (common in China, Singapore, and increasingly Japan) also concentrate retail vertically and can create some serendipity. Their advantage is climate control. But Smith argues they're geometrically inferior: internal walking spaces can't match the throughput and density of zakkyo-lined streets, where the street itself is the corridor.

## Zakkyo outside Japan

Smith notes that zakkyo-style buildings already exist in pockets of American cities — Koreatown in NYC has a few, and St. Mark's Place shows upper-floor retail signage. He sees no fundamental barrier to American cities adopting the form more broadly. In Taipei, he observes zakkyo, shop-top, and single-store buildings side by side, with pedestrians clustering around the zakkyo buildings.

For further reading, Smith recommends *Emergent Tokyo* by Jorge Almazán (@McReynoldsJoe et al.).
