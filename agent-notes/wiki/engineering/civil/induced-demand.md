# Induced Demand

Induced demand is the phenomenon whereby increasing the supply of a resource (such as road capacity) generates new consumption of that resource, rather than simply relieving existing pressure. In transportation, this means that widening roads in high-demand areas tends to produce more traffic rather than less congestion — the new capacity fills up.

## The biological argument

Jarrett Walker argues that induced demand is not merely an empirical regularity but an **axiom derivable from the definition of an organism**. The reasoning proceeds from first principles:

1. An organism must consume resources from its environment to survive.
2. It must expend less energy acquiring resources than the resources provide — a positive energy balance sheet.
3. Therefore, organisms will systematically prefer lower-cost paths to resources.

Humans, as mobile organisms, minimize both time and energy (including money) when pursuing resources. When a new road or lane reduces the cost of driving relative to alternatives, organisms predictably shift toward the cheaper option. Walker compares this to the value of pi — you don't need to keep measuring circles to confirm it, because it follows from definitions.

## The sprawl feedback loop

Walker traces a second-order consequence: induced demand doesn't just fill lanes, it reshapes cities. When road capacity increases:

- Developers build in more distant locations where land is cheaper (lower energy cost for them).
- The urban area sprawls, increasing average travel distances.
- Congestion returns as the new capacity fills.
- Residents end up spending *more* time and energy reaching the same resources (jobs, groceries, services) they previously accessed at lower cost.

The net effect is that the average person's "energy balance sheet" — the ratio of resources gained to effort expended — deteriorates. The intervention meant to help organisms reach resources more efficiently ends up doing the opposite.

## Implications

The axiomatic framing has a rhetorical purpose: if induced demand follows from biological first principles rather than just empirical observation, then debating individual studies or methodologies is beside the point. The burden shifts to explaining why a particular case would be an *exception* to the pattern, rather than requiring proof that the pattern holds.

This connects to broader [[systems-thinking]] themes — interventions in complex systems often produce counterintuitive results because they trigger compensating feedback loops. Road widening is a textbook case of a "fix that fails" archetype, where the solution reinforces the problem it was meant to solve.
