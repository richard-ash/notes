# Tokyo's Microspaces

Tokyo is widely considered one of the world's best-functioning metropolises, but its most distinctive feature may be its profusion of intimate, idiosyncratic *microspaces* — tiny commercial, public, and residential spaces as small as 5 square meters. McReynolds argues that these microspaces are not cultural accidents but the product of specific, identifiable forces: postwar history, hierarchical zoning, progressive taxation, strong property rights, and informal community governance.

## Historical origins

Tokyo was almost entirely destroyed twice in the 20th century — by the 1923 Great Kantō Earthquake and by Allied firebombing in WWII. The postwar government drafted but abandoned a comprehensive reconstruction plan for lack of budget. Funds went to restoring rail and road infrastructure; everything else was citizen-led, small-scale redevelopment out of necessity.

Family businesses and individual residents scraped together resources to build small structures on modest plots. The government regularized their property rights after the fact, producing an unusually large share of individually owned urban land. These neighborhoods *emerged* rather than being planned — narrow labyrinthine streets, no public space, but exceptional flexibility and adaptability.

Simultaneously, roughly 60,000 black-market (*yami-ichi*) stalls sprang up around major train stations during postwar rationing. When the government cleared them in 1949, it treated merchants not as criminals but as citizens deserving accommodation — relocating them to new purpose-built stalls on tiny plots they now formally owned. These gradually became bars and restaurants, birthing the *yokochō* alleyway culture that defines much of Tokyo's nightlife today.

## Four types of microspace

McReynolds identifies four main varieties, each with its own history but sharing a degree of fungibility between commercial uses:

### Yokochō alleyways

Warrens of microscale bars and restaurants centered on tiny alleys, often in the shadow of major commercial districts. A yokochō bar may have as few as two or three seats. Once stigmatized for their black-market origins, yokochō are now widely beloved as settings of informal public life and "Shōwa retro" atmosphere. Surviving yokochō have developed layers of economic, regulatory, and practical insulation against redevelopment — from nonprofit land-pooling to filing *kenchiku kyōtei* (binding nondevelopment pacts) to lobbying for "disaster-prone area" designation that automatically limits development.

### Dual-use residences (*heiyō jūtaku*)

Ordinary row houses with ground floors given over to shops, restaurants, bars, or light manufacturing. This is enabled by Japan's zoning system, which permits any residential building to engage in commercial activity on its ground floor by right. Disputes are mediated informally by local *jijikai* (neighborhood self-governance associations), with police (*kōban*) as a reluctant last resort. The combination of ubiquitous transit access and regulatory disincentivizing of car culture means these businesses have minimal parking/traffic impact — removing the NIMBY objection that blocks mixed-use in most American residential neighborhoods.

### Undertrack infills

Spaces under Tokyo's extensive elevated railways, repurposed as commercial spaces. The oldest date to pre-WWII elevated sections of the Yamanote Line (1910–1914). Many were occupied by black markets after the war. Today they are magnets of human activity, managed at scale by private conglomerates that control both the railways and real estate. Cities like Berlin are attempting similar projects, but Tokyo's scale is unmatched because private rail companies manage vast stretches of undertrack space into the suburbs.

### [[zakkyo-buildings|Zakkyo buildings]]

Densely packed multistory buildings where each floor hosts different microbusinesses — up to 80 in a single narrow building. The phenomenon has no English name (*zakkyo* translates roughly as "coexisting miscellany"). Many began as generic office buildings and became zakkyo through gradual "vertical colonization" by commercial enterprises. See the dedicated article for more detail.

## The policy framework

### Hierarchical zoning

Japan's zoning is *hierarchical* (also called inclusionary): 12 nuisance tiers where any activity below a zone's nuisance threshold is permitted by right. This contrasts with American *flat* zoning, where you must meet precise qualifications for each zone. Even Japan's most residential zone allows "houses with other small-scale functions" — ground-floor commercial use by right with minimal permitting. This permissiveness naturally shifts governance power from planning departments to local neighborhood committees and corporate redevelopers.

### Inheritance taxes and plot subdivision

Japan's inheritance taxes have historically been far higher than Western nations (up to 50%), payable as a lump sum. To pay, heirs often subdivide property and sell a portion. Tokyo has no minimum dwelling size, so plots can be subdivided almost without limit. This produces a bifurcated landscape: plot consolidation on superblock peripheries (where building height is tied to road width) and ever-smaller subdivisions in neighborhood interiors. The resulting hyperfragmented ownership, combined with strong property rights enshrined in postwar law, functions as unofficial historical preservation — making large-scale redevelopment prohibitively difficult. The Roppongi Hills development required decades of land acquisition by the Mori Corporation.

### Disaster regulation and "pocket neighborhoods"

Tokyo sits at the meeting point of several tectonic plates, and nearly all building regulation prioritizes disaster prevention. The postwar government's attempt to widen narrow alleys through setback obligations largely failed — perversely incentivizing owners to keep old wooden structures rather than rebuild smaller. The solution has been "superblocks": wide arterial roads lined with fire-resistant buildings that serve as "antifire blockade belts." This creates *pocket neighborhoods* — blocks whose interiors preserve older, low-rise residential fabric, ringed by taller commercial buildings that serve as both fire barriers and sources of daytime foot traffic for interior mom-and-pop businesses.

### Micro-friendly tax and regulation

Japan's tax system treats small businesses preferentially (e.g., proprietors keep a portion of collected sales tax, pay reduced income taxes). Alcohol licenses are simple and nearly free. Health inspections occur roughly every 5–7 years in practice. A tiny bar can launch with as little as several thousand dollars in capital — ludicrous by American standards. Many operate from owner-occupied housing (live upstairs, business below), insulating them from rent pressure.

## Implications for American urbanism

McReynolds argues that some aspects of Tokyo's approach could be transplanted to American cities: permissive ground-floor commercial use in residential zones, easier licensing, elimination of minimum dwelling sizes. Others would be nearly impossible to replicate: the postwar historical accident of fragmented ownership, the deep cultural infrastructure of *jijikai* governance.

The growing "yes in my back yard" (YIMBY) movement in American cities is pushing for changes that, while not explicitly aimed at creating microspaces, could enable their emergence — particularly relaxed zoning, reduced minimum lot sizes, and streamlined permitting. McReynolds notes that Golden Gai, perhaps Tokyo's most iconic microspace district, was *not* an organic accident but was strictly publicly planned with ownership determined via lottery — suggesting that deliberate policy can create the conditions for emergent urbanism.

## Temporal notes

The paper was published in November 2022. Since then, several of the trends McReynolds describes have intensified: Tokyo's ongoing redevelopment boom (accelerated after the 2020 Olympics postponement) continues to put pressure on surviving yokochō and older zakkyo buildings. In the US, YIMBY reforms have gained further traction in states like California and Montana, moving some of McReynolds's "pipe dream" policy changes closer to reality.
