# AI judgment atrophy

The thesis that routinely delegating cognitive work to AI erodes the friction-built "muscle" of human judgment — not because AI gives bad answers, but because good answers, delivered without effort, deprive us of the reps that develop instinct.

## The core argument

Ashley Heron argues that the most consequential effect of AI is not the apocalyptic scenario (mass replacement) or the utopian one (boundless creativity), but something mundane enough that nobody writes LinkedIn posts about it: **we are outsourcing the reps that build judgment, and we are doing it because it works.**

The mechanism is simple. Judgment develops through friction — sitting with a problem, weighing counsel against your own instinct, and slowly developing a position through contact with difficulty. When AI removes that friction (because its answer is fast, usually correct, and unburdened by emotion), the muscle that friction was building begins to atrophy.

Heron frames this not as anti-AI polemic but as an observation he implicates himself in. His own example: when a third-party integration broke and he needed to draft a diplomatic incident message for users, he went to Claude instead of writing it himself. The result was "probably better than what I would have written under pressure" — but he also didn't *think about it*, didn't sit with the problem, didn't sharpen his instinct for the next version of the same situation. He traded friction for speed, and the trade was rational but possibly unwise.

## The parenting case

Heron extends the pattern to parenting. Friends consulting ChatGPT about sleep schedules and developmental milestones are likely making better-informed decisions than previous generations who relied on instinct and dog-eared paperbacks. The information is more accurate, comprehensive, and current. If you optimize for outcomes, the machine wins.

But Heron argues we aren't only optimizing for outcomes. We are also, without meaning to, "optimizing away the experience of sitting with a problem long enough to develop our own position on it." The cost of that trade is real but invisible — it doesn't announce itself, arriving later as a dulled reflex and an atrophied capacity.

## The horse and the locomotive

Heron uses an analogy from the AI capabilities debate: "We are training a horse to run faster and faster, expecting it, one day, to give birth to a locomotive." He is clear that the horse is extraordinary — at his company Comma Eight, AI has compressed three-day tasks into forty-five minutes. But the horse is not a locomotive, and the billions spent trying to make it one "might be better spent learning how to ride it."

The question of whether AI thinks, Heron argues, is fascinating but operationally irrelevant. The horse doesn't need to understand the field to pull the plow. The question that actually matters is what happens to the farmer who never learns to plow because the horse has always been there.

## The 95/96 problem

Heron cites the "40-foot car wash" test: tell an AI the car wash is 40 feet away and ask whether to walk or drive. The AI says walk — missing that you need your car at the car wash. A six-year-old grasps this; the model pattern-matches "short distance" to "walking."

But the real insight isn't the AI's failure. It's what the ninety-five correct answers out of a hundred do to your vigilance on the ninety-sixth. When the machine is right often enough, the habit of checking erodes — and with it, the capacity to catch the failures that matter.

## The prescription

Heron's position is pragmatic, not Luddite. Use AI to compress everything that can be compressed: the distance between ideas and execution, between raw material and finished product, between the question you already know the answer to and the output you need. But don't use it to compress the distance between a question and an answer "when the question deserves to sit with you for a while."

His closing line captures the stance: "Check it anyway. Not because the machine is likely to be wrong, but because checking is the act of remaining the kind of person who checks."

## Connections

This argument complements but stands in tension with [[ai-and-the-future-of-work]], where Andreessen advocates using AI as a training tool to build E-shaped skills across adjacent domains. Heron would likely agree on the potential but warn that the default mode of AI use — reaching for a fast answer rather than a teaching interaction — is the one that atrophies judgment. The distinction is between AI-as-tutor (you do the reps, AI evaluates) and AI-as-oracle (you skip the reps, AI provides the answer).

The vigilance decay Heron describes also connects to [[the-bitter-lesson]] from the opposite direction: Sutton argues general computation always beats hand-crafted knowledge in AI systems, but Heron is concerned about the mirror image in humans — that general AI counsel is replacing the hand-crafted knowledge (judgment, instinct) that humans build through effortful practice.
