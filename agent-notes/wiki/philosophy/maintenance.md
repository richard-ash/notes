# Maintenance

Stewart Brand — creator of the Whole Earth Catalog and one of the philosophical architects of the early internet — argues in *Maintenance: Of Everything, Part One* that the upkeep of things, bodies, relationships, and civilizations is the essential and undervalued art. The book builds a philosophy of repair against a culture optimized for novelty and disposability. Pete Seeger: "the essential art of civilization is maintenance."

## Maintenance as biological default

Brand, a biologist by training, frames maintenance as what life *is*. A beaver cuts trees to maintain its dam, plants tend the soil that feeds them, animals constantly rebuild their bodies. Humans extend the same pattern outward through layers — body, vehicle, home, city, civilization, planet. "We've been terraforming badly, and we need to terraform well." The constancy of maintenance is a given for any living system; only the layers and tools differ.

## Ownership = knowing how to fix it

Brand redefines ownership: it is not legal possession but **possession of the knowledge** of how a thing functions, how to diagnose problems, and how to fix them. Maintenance is therefore the act through which you take real ownership of an object — physically, mentally, and socially. This reframes right-to-repair from a consumer-protection issue into an ontological one: technologies you cannot maintain are not really yours.

## The Ford / Rolls-Royce parable

Both began in 1908. Ford built the Model T as a rugged platform with interchangeable parts that the owner *had* to maintain to keep running — and which therefore became a generative space for kits, modifications, and creativity. Rolls-Royce built a car so precisely tuned that maintenance was impossible outside the factory: extreme reliability purchased at the cost of agency. Brand uses this contrast as the foundational design choice every technology faces. The hacker ethos of early Silicon Valley descended from the Model T side; the iPhone-must-be-jailbroken regime, from the Rolls-Royce side.

## Right to repair as policy implication

The legibility argument has a legislative corollary. The John Deere case study (covered in part two of the book) illustrates how a beloved company became a poster child for right-to-repair backlash by forcing farmers to route every fix through dealerships. Brand endorses legislation: marketplace pressure works for some companies (Tesla, Patagonia), but where a firm has structural dominance and no competitive threat, government must require that things be left maintainable. Massachusetts and Colorado have begun.

## Maintenance as contemplative practice

Brand reframes tedium as ritual. He cites Japanese street-lamp crews who turn lightbulb changes into a small ceremony — uniforms, choreographed mounts and dismounts — converting a simple task into a complex dance. Ritual makes repetition bearable and centers the maintainer's mind. He quotes a passage in *Zen and the Art of Motorcycle Maintenance* and the sailor Bernard Moitessier, who at sea with a bent bowsprit forced himself to think for two days before acting "because I did not want to crystallize my thinking prematurely."

The repair principle that emerges:

> Don't just jump at a solution because you might make the problem worse. Think through the solution. Disrupt the system minimally in the process of figuring out what needs to be fixed, fixing only that, and then back carefully out so the rest of the system doesn't get disrupted.

Notre Dame's burned steeple and Chernobyl are Brand's cautionary cases: both started as routine maintenance done carelessly. Repair is itself a trauma to the system being fixed.

## Maintenance, care, and the things that care back

Klein observes that maintenance is what we call care when applied to things rather than people. Brand pulls the symmetry the other way through Albert Borgmann's passage on the horse: a 1,000-pound animal that demands constant feeding, shoeing, and protection from infection — and that, when it nuzzles your chest, answers the question of where you want to be. Brand wonders aloud whether AI may eventually give us "vehicles that can care back." This connects maintenance to Martin Buber's *I and Thou*: the discipline of having a relationship with your things that resembles the relationship you have with a child or pet.

## Maintainers vs. optimists

Brand identifies a constitutional asymmetry. Optimism is "fatal for maintainers." Real maintainers are realists; the best lean pessimist, scanning for what could go wrong and getting ahead of it. Brand confesses his own shortcoming: he is an optimist. The same temperament that produced the Whole Earth Catalog and the campaign for the picture of Earth from space is poorly suited to the daily work of upkeep.

## The bathtub curve

A maintenance-engineering concept Brand applies to his own life at 87: failure rates over a system's lifetime are bathtub-shaped — high during break-in (new buildings have lots of bugs), low through middle life if you stay ahead of upkeep, then rising again as the system ages. "By the time you're in your 80s, just being old is a half-time job."

## Tension with the AI moment

Brand sits oddly with current AI development, which his own intellectual lineage helped seed. The Whole Earth Catalog's mission was making the world *intelligible* — conferring agency through half-open doors. Klein notes the irony of seeing one in the OpenAI offices, where the staff openly admit they do not understand the fundamental center of how their systems work. Brand's metaphor: humans relating to AIs are "redwood trees trying to communicate with the hummingbird" — coexisting on different pace layers, the redwood unable to track who is in its branches.

He concedes both poles. AIs can automate the *toil* of maintenance (dependency upkeep, predictive repair) — that is the upside. The downside is that we will spend more of our lives "arguing with robots" we cannot model, and engineering at scales we cannot understand. The Whole Earth Catalog conferred agency; algorithmic platforms (Cory Doctorow's "enshittification") transfer it the other way, so that "the volition has shifted" — users are acted upon rather than acting.

## Brand's prescription

- Get the best tools you can; respect for the tool produces care for the tool.
- Treat boring tasks as ritual rather than tax.
- Disrupt minimally when fixing.
- Recognize maintainers — the best ones — as pillars of civilization on par with librarians, and pay them accordingly.
- The deepest maintenance object is the Enlightenment itself: open discourse, science, peaceful political succession, comfort with the fact that solving problems generates new problems.
