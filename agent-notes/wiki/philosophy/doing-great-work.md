# Doing Great Work

Paul Graham's attempt to extract the cross-domain recipe for doing great work — the intersection of techniques that apply regardless of field. The essay is unusually comprehensive, covering problem selection, the mechanics of working, originality, morale, and the role of colleagues.

## The four-step framework

Graham argues that virtually everyone who has done great work followed the same basic path:

1. **Choose a field** — find something you have natural aptitude for and deep interest in. The third criterion (scope for great work) is rarely the binding constraint; ambitious people are already too conservative about it.
2. **Learn enough to reach the frontier** — knowledge expands fractally; from a distance its edges look smooth, but up close they're full of gaps.
3. **Notice the gaps** — this requires skill because your brain actively wants to smooth over anomalies to maintain a simpler world-model. Many discoveries come from questioning what everyone else takes for granted.
4. **Explore promising gaps** — the "fractal bud" metaphor: you notice a crack in the surface of knowledge, pry it open, and find a whole world inside.

The engine driving all four steps is curiosity. Graham contends that if an oracle could name the single secret to great work in one word, it would be "curiosity."

## Problem selection matters more than problem-solving

Graham argues — echoing [[you-and-your-research|Hamming]] — that originality in *choosing* problems matters even more than originality in solving them. This is what distinguishes people who discover whole new fields. Several forces conspire to make problem selection conservative even among smart people:

- **Fashion:** people get sucked into working on fashionable problems, undervaluing unfashionable ones.
- **Risk aversion:** problems are bigger bets than solutions (years vs. days), so people hedge.
- **Misunderstanding questions:** people think big ideas are answers, but often the real insight was in the question. "A really good question is a partial discovery."

The most overlooked problems are not explicitly unfashionable — they just don't seem to matter as much as they actually do. The test: "If you were going to take a break from 'serious' work to work on something just because it would be really interesting, what would you do?" The answer is probably more important than it seems.

## The "staying upwind" strategy

Graham advocates against heavy planning for creative work. Planning only works for achievements you can describe in advance — you can win a gold medal that way, but you can't discover natural selection. Instead, he recommends "staying upwind": at each stage, do whatever seems most interesting and gives you the best options for the future.

This connects to his advice on iterative development: great things are almost always made in successive versions. Start with the simplest thing that could possibly work, evolve it based on feedback. An early version dismissed as a "toy" is a good sign — it has everything a new idea needs except scale.

## The mechanics of working

Several practical observations on the craft of working itself:

- **Activation energy:** it's harder to start than to continue. Permissible self-deception — "I'll just read over what I've got so far" — is a legitimate tool for getting past the threshold.
- **Contiguous blocks:** the hardest types of deep work may only be sustainable for four or five hours a day, but those hours should be uninterrupted.
- **Two kinds of procrastination:** per-day procrastination is visible and manageable. Per-project procrastination is far more dangerous because it camouflages itself as productive work on something else. The antidote: periodically ask "Am I working on what I most want to work on?"
- **Compounding:** work that compounds yields exponential growth, but the curve feels flat at the start. People underrate this, which is why they underinvest in learning and audience-building early on.
- **Diffuse thinking:** undirected thinking (walking, showering) can solve problems that resist frontal attack — but only when interleaved with deliberate work that feeds it questions. This parallels [[creative-thinking-claude-shannon|Shannon's]] observation about letting problems "simmer."

## Originality and independent-mindedness

Graham argues originality is not a "creative process" but a habit of mind — original thinkers throw off new ideas about whatever they focus on, like sparks from an angle grinder. You're more likely to have original ideas when working on something slightly too difficult, not when trying to have original ideas.

Two requirements for seeing new ideas:

1. **Strictness** — being willing to see where your model of the world clashes with reality, instead of looking away. Einstein saw the wild implications of Maxwell's equations because he was stricter, not because he was looking for novelty.
2. **Rule-breaking** — from the point of view of the old model, the new model always breaks implicit rules. Graham distinguishes *aggressively* independent-minded people (who enjoy breaking rules) from *passively* independent-minded ones (who are indifferent to them — novices, outsiders, and sometimes autistic people).

The paradox: strictness + rule-breaking sound opposed, but only in trivial matters. In questions that really matter, only rule-breakers can be truly strict.

## Earnestness as the meta-virtue

Graham identifies earnestness — intellectual honesty, informality, optimism, and the absence of affectation — as the unifying character trait behind great work. Affectation wastes energy on seeming rather than being. "Nerds have a kind of innocent boldness that's exactly what you need in doing great work."

This connects to [[solitude-and-leadership|Deresiewicz's]] argument that independent thought requires insulation from social pressure, and to [[self-renewal|Gardner's]] observation that self-renewing individuals maintain "tough-minded optimism."

## Morale management

Graham treats morale as a fragile biological system that compounds in both directions: good work raises morale which enables better work, but the cycle also runs in reverse. Key principles:

- **Inoculate against catastrophic morale collapse** by explicitly considering setbacks part of the process. "Doing great work is a depth-first search whose root node is the desire to. Never abandon the root node."
- **Distinguish good pain from bad:** struggle is not necessarily a bad sign, but damage is.
- **Audience sustains morale** — even a small dedicated one. Avoid intermediaries between you and your audience.
- **Choose colleagues and companions carefully:** work with people you want to become like. Don't marry someone who sees your work as competition.

## Connections to the broader "how to do significant work" canon

Graham's essay covers much of the same territory as [[you-and-your-research|Hamming's "You and Your Research"]] but is broader in scope and more prescriptive about the creative process itself. Where Hamming emphasizes courage and "important problems," Graham emphasizes curiosity and interest as more reliable guides. Both agree that per-project procrastination is the great career killer, and both stress the value of working with great colleagues.

The essay also overlaps with [[common-denominator-of-success|Gray's thesis]] that success comes from habitually doing uncomfortable things — though Graham frames it less as discipline and more as finding work so interesting that the discomfort doesn't register as cost.

Graham's advice to "make something you yourself want" echoes a central [[make-good-art|Gaiman]] theme, and his framework for distributing attention across topics according to a power law connects to [[multidisciplinary-approach-to-thinking|Kaufman's]] argument for cross-disciplinary thinking.

This essay grew directly out of a paragraph in Graham's earlier [[superlinear-returns|"Superlinear Returns"]] — the companion piece that provides the structural argument for *why* great work matters so disproportionately (the reward curve is superlinear, so incremental effort at the far end is an extraordinary bargain).
