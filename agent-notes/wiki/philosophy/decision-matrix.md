# Decision Matrix

The Decision Matrix is a 2x2 framework for triaging decisions based on two axes: **reversibility** (can you undo it?) and **consequentiality** (does it matter much?). Parrish describes it as a decision-making adaptation of the Eisenhower Matrix (urgent/important), designed specifically to answer: which decisions should I make personally, and which should I delegate?

## The four quadrants

| | Inconsequential | Consequential |
|---|---|---|
| **Reversible** | Delegate freely | Experiment — run cheap tests |
| **Irreversible** | Delegate (low stakes) | Focus here — your highest-value work |

The key insight: most decisions that feel pressing are actually reversible and inconsequential. They become "urgent" only because no one is making them, creating a backlog that looks like a crisis but isn't.

## Delegation logic

Parrish argues both types of inconsequential decisions should be delegated, regardless of reversibility. The reasoning:

1. **Time asymmetry.** The decision itself often takes seconds; the presentation and approval ceremony around it takes 30 minutes. Delegating inconsequential decisions saved Parrish 5–7 hours per week.
2. **Judgment development.** Inconsequential decisions are the ideal training ground for developing judgment in team members, precisely because mistakes are cheap. This connects to [[circle-of-competence]]: people expand their circle by making real decisions in low-stakes domains.
3. **Quality improvement.** The people closest to the problem often make better inconsequential decisions than a distracted leader rushing through a queue. Parrish was making worse choices than his team would have, simply because he was overloaded.

## Reversible + consequential: the experimentation zone

Parrish identifies this quadrant as the most commonly misjudged. Reversible-but-consequential decisions "trick you into thinking they are one big important decision" when they're actually ideal candidates for cheap experiments. The approach: define the experiment, specify what results would confirm the right path, assign ownership, then review findings.

This reframes big-seeming decisions as iterative learning rather than one-shot bets — closely related to [[tradeoffs]], where the cost of experimentation is itself a deliberate tradeoff against the cost of deciding with less information.

## Irreversible + consequential: the leader's real job

Everything else in the matrix exists to protect capacity for this quadrant. Parrish's supplementary rule: unless the decision is operationally time-critical, take a 30-minute walk before deciding. The walk isn't procrastination — it's ensuring that the one category of decision you can't undo and can't afford to get wrong receives genuine deliberation rather than residual attention.

## Implementation requirements

The framework only works if the team shares definitions of "consequential" and "reversible." Parrish notes this required an initial calibration period — people checked with him at first, but eventually internalized the boundaries and began deciding autonomously. The result: 75% fewer decisions made personally by Parrish, with improved quality across all quadrants, higher team engagement, and fewer sick days.

## Connections

- [[tradeoffs]] — the Decision Matrix is itself a tradeoff tool: you trade personal control for capacity, betting that delegation quality exceeds distracted-leader quality
- [[circle-of-competence]] — delegating inconsequential decisions is how team members expand their circles through low-cost experience
