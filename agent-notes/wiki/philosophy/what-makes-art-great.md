# What Makes Art Great?

Nabeel Qureshi's 2026 essay sketches a three-principle theory of aesthetic greatness, explicitly framed as an answer to the question "why is AI writing dull?" The principles are **surprise**, **echoes**, and **depth** — each operating at multiple levels of a work, each individually achievable but jointly very hard to satisfy. The fourth and final move of the essay concedes that even all three together fail to fully capture greatness; there is a residual that comes from the artist's lived experience.

Confidence is `medium`: this is an opinionated aesthetics essay synthesizing well-known critics (Christopher Alexander, Stephen Booth, Nabokov, Auerbach), not an empirical claim, and many of the demonstrations are interpretive rather than proven.

## The three principles

### 1. Surprise (high information content)

Qureshi reframes "great art" in information-theoretic terms: **slop is compressible; greatness is not**. A cliché vase-of-flowers can be paraphrased as "imagine a New Yorker cartoon of a vase of flowers" — the image carries little information beyond its category. A great painting cannot be substituted by its description. This is why AI-generated images of "a cup on a table" feel arbitrary: the cup is green, but it could have been blue. The details don't *matter*.

The mechanism is the [predictive processing model of the brain](https://slatestarcodex.com/2017/09/05/book-review-surfing-uncertainty/): perception is constant prediction, and pleasure comes from satisfying violation of expectation. Hence Henrik Karlsson's example of bad prose — "an interesting and exciting finding," where "exciting" adds no information beyond "interesting" — versus *Macbeth*'s "the **multitudinous** seas **incarnadine**," where both bolded words are unguessable from context.

Qureshi's most striking move is to argue that **surprise operates at every level simultaneously**, not just word choice:

- **Word level.** Larkin's "hothouse flashed uniquely" — students with the rest of the stanza in front of them never guess either word.
- **Visual detail.** Christopher Alexander's Fra Angelico exercise: cover up the black stripe and ask if you'd have painted it there. Most painters wouldn't. The surprise is what makes the image haunting.
- **Defamiliarization.** Tolstoy describes whipping by stripping the action of its name, forcing the reader to feel it as if for the first time. (Cf. the Russian Formalists' *ostranenie*.)
- **Grammar.** Shakespeare constantly produces *garden-path sentences* — "To be, or not to be... whether 'tis nobler in the mind to suffer / The slings and arrows..." — where the brain commits to one parse and is forced mid-line into another. Qureshi argues the grammatical rug-pull is a *general* feature of great writing, not a quirk of Elizabethan prose, and that readers feel it unconsciously even when they can't name it.
- **Form.** *Moby-Dick* opens with sixteen pages of whale quotations.

The corollary: **there is no recipe**. A characteristic of greatness is that the next great work doesn't look like the last one.

### 2. Echoes (multi-layered coherence)

A purely random sequence of words is maximally surprising but is not great art. Surprise alone is noise. Greatness requires that the surprises **cohere** — that details rhyme with each other across multiple independent dimensions.

Qureshi borrows the term *echoes* from Christopher Alexander's *The Nature of Order*: "a deep underlying similarity — a family resemblance — among the elements, so deep that everything seems to be related, and yet one doesn't quite know why, or what causes it." Rose windows at Chartres echo each other in non-obvious ways. Beethoven's Fifth grows the entire first movement out of a four-note seed.

In language, this layering is denser than most readers consciously notice. Qureshi works through Shakespeare's *Sonnet 15* to show six independent systems running through fourteen lines: rhyme scheme, the When/When/Then syntactic overlay (8+6) cross-cutting the sonnet form (4+4+4+2), the plant metaphor culminating in "ingraft," a vowel-plus-n sonic thread, k/g and r consonant patterns that reunite in the final word, and bridging sounds (Whereon → wear → Where → war) that stitch across the formal divisions.

He extends this to longer works:

- **Nabokov on *Anna Karenina*.** Trains and railway imagery saturate the novel beyond just the plot pivots that occur at stations.
- **Nabokov on *Metamorphosis*.** The number three is everywhere: three parts, three doors, three family members, three servants, three lodgers with three beards.
- ***Macbeth*** is full of birds — ravens, crows, bats, owls, the Thane of *Caw*dor.
- **Stephen Booth on *Hamlet*.** "Ear" and its variants (hear, heard, ears) appear 84 times — Claudius murdering by pouring poison in the ear, Polonius placed "in the ear" of conferences, Hamlet calling Claudius a "mildew'd ear" of corn, etc. Qureshi reports verifying this with an AI analysis across the plays, and notes the same pattern with "hair/heir/airy/Ariel" in *The Tempest*.

The crucial move: **it doesn't matter whether the patterns were intentional**. Whether or not Shakespeare planned the ear motif, its density creates "an electric charge of coherence" while the reader is processing the text. This is why echoes give great work the same feeling of *rightness* one gets from a tree or a grassy field — and why random word sequences, however surprising, never produce that feeling.

The constraint: echoes can't be too obvious (childish rhyme is worse than no rhyme). They must be both surprising *and* numerous, which is what makes the joint problem hard.

### 3. Depth (more meaning than can be paraphrased)

The third principle — that great art "contains more meaning than can be paraphrased" — is what makes great work survive rereading. Qureshi identifies three facets:

**Stacked meanings.** Stephen Booth's analysis of one line of *Sonnet 40*, "Then if for my love thou my love receivest," shows it carries at least seven distinct readings simultaneously (out of affection for me / because of my affection for you / in place of my affection / understanding my mistress to be my true love / etc.). The reader holds none of them consciously but registers the bottomless density.

**Diametrically opposed interpretations.** Some of the most powerful art contains a surface meaning and a hidden contrary one — the work *contains its own contradiction*. Examples Qureshi cites:

- Milton's *Paradise Lost* — the poem is meant to condemn Satan, but Satan gets the best poetry. (Blake: "Milton... was of the Devil's party without knowing it.")
- *Parasite* — ostensibly about structural oppression of the poor, but [the film systematically makes the poor look morally worse and the rich more sympathetic](https://marginalrevolution.com/marginalrevolution/2020/06/the-gaslighting-of-parasite.html), supporting the opposite reading.
- Plato's *Apology* — readable as a condemnation of Socrates rather than a defense.
- Thiel's *Zero to One* — Qureshi reports Thiel's claim that the hidden message is "perhaps you should not be an entrepreneur."

This is the [[straussian-reading|Straussian]] move generalized to art: the hypothesis is asserted and silently undermined in the same text. (Compare also the [[danger-of-a-single-story|single-story]] critique — great art refuses to be one story.)

**Mystery.** Good art is deliberately under-explained. Auerbach's reading of the Abraham/Isaac story emphasizes how little the narrative tells you — it just gives bare facts, leaving Kierkegaard an entire book (*Fear and Trembling*) to fail to explain Abraham's motivation. We never know if Hamlet is really mad. Many works include details that are *fundamentally inexplicable*, and the unresolvable residue is part of the work's charge.

## The "and yet" — why the formal account is incomplete

Qureshi closes by conceding the recipe is not enough. There is a residual quality he calls **the link between art and life**: the weight a piece of art has by virtue of being made by someone who actually lived through (or imagined themselves through) the experience the art is about. The same words written by an algorithm and by a person who lost a child are not the same words. This is the analogue of why a grandparent's story carries weight beyond its narrative content.

This is the explicit point on which Qureshi rests the argument that **human art will continue and even improve in the AI era**: not because AI can't produce surprising, echoing, deep texts (he thinks it eventually can), but because it cannot have the experiences that art is in part *about*.

## Implications for AI and creative work

Although the essay is framed as "what makes art good?" (question 1), question 2 — why AI struggles — runs underneath. Qureshi's diagnosis:

1. **LLMs default to obvious word choice.** His test prompt — "write a descriptive paragraph about a day in the park" — gets back "sunlight filters through the canopy of old oak trees and dapples the ground" from the leading model (Claude Opus 4.6). Every word is the modal completion. This is structural: next-token prediction selects high-probability tokens by definition. Surprise is the *opposite* of what the loss function rewards.
2. **You can't fix this by asking for surprise.** The harder constraint is that the surprises must *cohere*. Local randomness produces noise, not echoes. Producing a 14-line text with six independently coherent layers is qualitatively harder than producing one surprising sentence — and the joint problem is the one greatness solves.
3. **The depth requirement compounds again.** Generating something that survives rereading because new patterns keep emerging is a constraint over the *entire reception* of a work, not just its surface.
4. **The experiential residual is unreachable in principle**, not just in practice.

This connects directly to [[generative-ai-as-pattern-generation]] (Evans's framing of generative AI as remix-from-patterns rather than creation, with humans needed in the loop) and [[ai-judgment-atrophy]] (the risk that delegating creative work to AI erodes the muscle that does the surprising in the first place). Qureshi's principles supply the *aesthetic* explanation for why the pattern-generation framing is tight: the modal token is the pattern, and great art is the deviation from it.
