# Solitude and Leadership

William Deresiewicz's 2009 address to the plebe class at West Point — originally published in *The American Scholar* — argues that solitude is not merely compatible with leadership but is its essential prerequisite. The speech has become one of the most widely circulated modern essays on leadership, particularly within military and institutional reform circles.

## Core thesis

Deresiewicz contends that American institutions have produced a generation of "excellent sheep" — people superbly trained to jump through hoops, ace tests, and climb hierarchies, but who cannot think for themselves and therefore cannot truly lead. True leadership requires the ability to formulate a new direction, not merely execute an existing one. That ability, he argues, can only be developed through solitude: sustained, focused time alone with your own thoughts.

## The bureaucracy problem

Drawing on Conrad's *Heart of Darkness*, Deresiewicz offers a diagnosis of why mediocrity rises in large organizations. The manager of the Central Station — "commonplace," "ordinary," with "no genius for organizing, for initiative, or for order even" — is his archetype of the bureaucratic leader. Such people advance not through excellence but through a talent for maneuvering: pleasing superiors, conforming to expectations, never taking the risk of questioning how or why things are done. The result is a system that selects for compliance and punishes independent thought.

This is a structural observation, not merely a moral one. Deresiewicz argues the problem is national in scope — visible in the decline of American corporations, the Wall Street collapse, and (most pointedly for his audience) the strategic paralysis of the first four years of the Iraq War, which he attributes to senior leadership failures.

## What solitude actually means

Deresiewicz defines solitude more broadly than simple isolation. He identifies four forms:

1. **Introspection** — confronting the fundamental questions of your life (Am I doing the right thing? What do I actually believe?) rather than suppressing them with busyness and distraction.

2. **Concentrated work** — what Marlow finds in fixing his steamboat. The solitude of sustained attention on a single task, which Deresiewicz equates with thinking itself. He cites a Stanford study showing that heavy multitaskers perform worse at every cognitive task tested — including multitasking — than low multitaskers. "Thinking means concentrating on one thing long enough to develop an idea about it."

3. **Sustained reading** — especially of old books, which disrupt contemporary conventional wisdom simply by offering a different frame. Great books are "revolutionary in their own time, and they are still revolutionary today."

4. **Deep friendship** — the apparent paradox. Long, uninterrupted conversation with one trusted person is a form of solitude because it allows you to think out loud, "discovering what you believe in the course of articulating it." This is distinct from the shallow connectivity of having 968 "friends" you never actually talk to.

## Thinking as a slow process

One of the speech's most memorable passages concerns the slowness of genuine thought. Deresiewicz observes that his first thought is always someone else's — "always what I've already heard about the subject, always the conventional wisdom." Original ideas arrive only through patience, concentration, and the willingness to sit with discomfort. He invokes Thomas Mann ("a writer is someone for whom writing is more difficult than it is for other people"), James Joyce (100 words a day for seven years on *Ulysses*), and T. S. Eliot (150 pages of poetry across a 25-year career) to make the case that excellence in any form of thought requires radical slowness.

This connects directly to the argument in [[creative-thinking-claude-shannon]], where Shannon identifies prolonged focused attention as the medium in which creative breakthroughs occur. It also complements [[you-and-your-research]], where Hamming stresses the importance of sustained work on important problems over long time horizons. Where Hamming focuses on *problem selection* and Shannon on *cognitive tactics*, Deresiewicz targets the prior condition that makes both possible: the willingness to be alone with unresolved questions long enough for your own thinking to emerge.

## Moral courage

Deresiewicz distinguishes physical courage from moral courage — the courage to stand up for what you believe even when it displeases superiors. His example is General David Petraeus, who developed his counterinsurgency strategy while running Mosul in 2003, was sidelined for being ahead of the institutional consensus, and was ultimately vindicated. The point is not that Petraeus was clever but that he had the inner resources — developed through solitude and independent thought — to persist against institutional pressure.

This is the practical payoff of the entire argument: solitude builds the self-knowledge that enables moral courage. If you don't know what you believe — because you've never taken the time to think it through for yourself — you will default to institutional consensus at exactly the moments when independent judgment matters most.

## Connection to self-renewal

Deresiewicz's argument has significant overlap with [[self-renewal|Gardner's self-renewal thesis]]. Both diagnose a form of institutional calcification: Gardner sees individuals who stop growing, Deresiewicz sees institutions that select against growth. Both identify the root cause as a failure of inner life — Gardner's "barnacle" who cements in place, Deresiewicz's "excellent sheep" who outsource their thinking to the hierarchy. The prescriptions differ in emphasis — Gardner stresses emotional renewal and releasing the past, Deresiewicz stresses intellectual autonomy and the discipline of concentration — but they converge on the same fundamental claim: you cannot lead or grow without regularly confronting yourself in solitude.
