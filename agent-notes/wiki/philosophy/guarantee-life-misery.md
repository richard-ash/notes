# How to Guarantee a Life of Misery

Charlie Munger's 1986 commencement address at the Harvard School — his earliest major public speech — is a masterclass in inversion. Rather than tell graduates how to be happy, Munger catalogs seven prescriptions for guaranteed misery, building on a framework Johnny Carson had used in an earlier graduation speech at the same school. The method is pure Jacobi: "Invert, always invert." By studying how to reliably produce misery, the listener backs into a practical guide for a good life.

The speech is also an early showcase of the [[multidisciplinary-approach-to-thinking|multidisciplinary thinking]] and [[mental-models|mental model]] accumulation that Munger would later develop fully in his 1994 [[elementary-worldly-wisdom|Elementary Worldly Wisdom]] lecture. Where the 1994 talk applies the latticework to investing, this 1986 talk applies it to life.

## Carson's three prescriptions

Munger begins by expanding three prescriptions he attributes to Johnny Carson:

1. **Ingest chemicals to alter mood or perception.** Munger adds personal testimony: of his four closest childhood friends — all intelligent, ethical, well-backgrounded — two died with alcohol as a contributing factor, and a third became a living alcoholic. He notes that "the bonds of degradation are too light to be felt until they are too strong to be broken," and that he has never met anyone whose life was worsened by *over*-avoidance of substance abuse.

2. **Envy.** Munger recommends never reading any biography of Samuel Johnson if you wish to keep envy alive, because Johnson's life demonstrates that transcending envy is both possible and advantageous.

3. **Resentment.** Quoting Johnson again: "Life is hard enough to swallow without squeezing in the bitter rind of resentment." For those who cannot quit resentment cold turkey, Munger offers the Disraeli compromise: Disraeli stopped acting on vengeance but kept a drawer of names of those who had wronged him, periodically reviewing the list to observe how the world had brought his enemies low without his help.

## Munger's four additions

### 1. Be unreliable

Unreliability, Munger argues, is the single most effective destroyer of a life. It "more than counterbalances the combined effect of all your virtues, howsoever great." If you master unreliability, you will be "outrun by hordes and hordes of mediocre turtles and even by some mediocre turtles on crutches."

Conversely, reliability is so powerful that it can overcome severe handicaps. Munger tells of a college roommate who was severely dyslexic but became "perhaps the most reliable man I have ever known" and went on to run a multibillion-dollar corporation. The implication: if you persist in being reliable, your other disadvantages simply cannot hold you back.

This connects to what [[common-denominator-of-success|Gray's "Common Denominator of Success"]] identifies as the core habit that separates achievers from the rest — doing what you've committed to, whether you feel like it or not.

### 2. Learn only from personal experience

Munger's second prescription is to refuse vicarious wisdom — learning only from your own mistakes rather than from the accumulated experience of others, living and dead. He catalogs the "common disasters of mankind" — drunk driving, cults, business failures repeating predecessors' mistakes — and notes how unoriginal these disasters are. Most are preventable by studying others' errors.

The positive inversion is Newton's famous line: "If I have seen a little farther than other men it is because I stood on the shoulders of giants." Munger's [[elementary-worldly-wisdom|latticework of mental models]] is the systematic version of this insight — a deliberate program of learning from the best work done before yours.

### 3. Give up after adversity

The third prescription: when life deals you a severe reverse, go down and stay down. Since adversity is inevitable even for the lucky and wise, this guarantees permanent misery. Munger's counter-example is the Stoic slave Epictetus, whose self-written epitaph read: "Here lies Epictetus, a slave, maimed in body, the ultimate in poverty, and favoured by Gods." The lesson from [[self-renewal|Gardner's "Self-Renewal"]] parallels this — the capacity to recover and rebuild is itself a learnable discipline.

### 4. Avoid inversion and objectivity

Munger's final prescription — and the conceptual heart of the speech — is to ignore the rustic who said, "I wish I knew where I was going to die, and then I'd never go there." This is the method of inversion: studying how to create non-X as the surest route to understanding how to create X.

Munger draws a line from Jacobi's "Invert, always invert" through Einstein (who reversed Newton's laws to fit Maxwell's rather than the other way around) to Darwin, whose working method "always gave priority attention to evidence tending to disconfirm whatever cherished and hard-won theory he already had." This is the same disconfirmation discipline that [[you-and-your-research|Hamming identifies]] as essential to great research — the willingness to test and destroy your own ideas.

Munger quotes Philip Wylie on the alternative: people who refuse to update become those of whom "you couldn't squeeze a dime between what they already know and what they will never learn."

The appeal to objectivity here also echoes [[creative-thinking-claude-shannon|Shannon's creative-thinking heuristics]], particularly the tactic of structural analogy and reformulation — approaching the same problem from multiple angles until one yields insight.

## The inversion method

The speech's deepest contribution is not any individual prescription but the method itself. Inversion — defining what you want by carefully studying its opposite — is a [[mental-models|mental model]] that appears across domains:

- **Mathematics** (Jacobi): many hard problems yield only when addressed backward
- **Physics** (Einstein): special relativity emerged from inverting the assumed relationship between Newton and Maxwell
- **Biology** (Darwin): systematic disconfirmation of cherished hypotheses
- **Life design** (Carson/Munger): catalog what guarantees misery, then avoid those things

Munger would go on to use inversion as one of his core tools in the [[psychology-of-human-misjudgment|Psychology of Human Misjudgment]] — defining rationality by cataloging the tendencies that produce irrationality.
