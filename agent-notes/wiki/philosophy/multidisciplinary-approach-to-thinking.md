# The Multidisciplinary Approach to Thinking

Peter Kaufman's talk to the Cal Poly Pomona Economics Club distills a lifetime of Munger-influenced multidisciplinary thinking into a single actionable formula: **"Go positive and go first, and be constant in doing it."** Kaufman — CEO of Glenair and editor of *Poor Charlie's Almanack* — demonstrates the method by deriving two "parabolic" big ideas across three independent evidence bases, then combining them into a unified life strategy.

The talk is a direct application of Charlie Munger's [[elementary-worldly-wisdom|latticework of mental models]] philosophy, but where Munger focuses on investing, Kaufman turns the lens on human relationships and leadership.

## The three buckets

Kaufman's validation method is to check every candidate principle against three nested sample sizes:

1. **Bucket 1: 13.7 billion years** — the inorganic universe (physics, geology, chemistry)
2. **Bucket 2: 3.5 billion years** — biology on Earth
3. **Bucket 3: 20,000 years** — recorded human history

The logic: a principle that holds across all three buckets is derived from the largest, most relevant sample sizes that exist. If all three confirm the same pattern, confidence is effectively 100%. This is a practical heuristic for cross-domain validation — the same idea behind Munger's argument that models drawn from multiple disciplines are more reliable than any single-discipline insight.

Kaufman's shortcut for acquiring the models themselves: rather than reading textbooks, he read 144 interviews from twelve years of *Discover* magazine — "index fund style," including every topic, not just ones that seemed interesting. The best ideas came from the most obscure domains (nanoparticles, etc.), precisely because nobody else reads those.

## Big Idea 1: Mirrored reciprocation

Kaufman asks: is there a two-word description of how everything works?

- **Bucket 1 (physics):** Newton's Third Law — every action has an equal and opposite reaction. The table pushes back with exactly the force you push down. This has held for 13.7 billion years.
- **Bucket 2 (biology):** Pick up a cat by its tail and it scratches you. Stroke it gently and it licks your hand. The response mirrors the input, proportionally.
- **Bucket 3 (human history):** Every human interaction is mirrored reciprocation. Smile at a stranger in an elevator — 98% of the time they smile back. Scowl — they scowl back. Do nothing — you get nothing.

The principle is not just reciprocation but *perfectly mirrored* reciprocation: the magnitude and valence of the response match the input.

Kaufman attributes the insight that this is "simple" rather than "simplistic" to Einstein's five ascending levels of cognitive prowess: smart < intelligent < brilliant < genius < **simple**. The right kind of simplicity transcends genius because it can be understood and acted upon.

## Big Idea 2: Compound interest (constancy)

Kaufman asks: what is the most powerful force we can harness?

- **Bucket 1 (physics):** Compound interest — "dogged incremental constant progress over a very long time frame"
- **Bucket 2 (biology):** Evolution — the same mechanism operating over 3.5 billion years
- **Bucket 3 (human experience):** Olympic gold medals, musical mastery, Berkshire Hathaway — all built the same way

The critical variable is **constancy**. Kaufman introduces "variance drain" (his geometric metaphor): whenever you interrupt the constant increase, you fall off the logarithmic curve back to a linear curve or worse. Buffett and Munger got rich because they were constant. Most people are intermittent — they push the boulder up halfway, get distracted, and start over.

The puppy analogy illustrates this: a new puppy needs a calm, reassuring, safe environment — and constancy is the thread stitching it all together. You can't skip feeding the puppy one day or it becomes neurotic. Be constant for about seven days and the puppy "goes all in" — attaches to you for life. Crucially, going all-in is the *puppy's* idea, not yours. You created the conditions; the commitment was freely given.

## The combined formula: leadership and life

Kaufman combines the two ideas: go positive, go first, and be constant.

### The universal search

Every human being, Kaufman argues, is searching for someone who is: trustworthy, principled, courageous, competent, kind, loyal, understanding, forgiving, and unselfish. When someone encounters a candidate, they probe and test — not for weakness but for strength. The worst day is discovering weakness where you expected strength, because you must restart the search.

The "22-second course in leadership": take that list and **be the list** in every interaction. People will go all-in on you — not because you asked them to, but because you are what they've been looking for. This mirrors the puppy dynamic at scale.

### The five universal needs

All humans want the same five things: to be (1) paid attention to, (2) listened to, (3) respected, (4) to feel they matter, and (5) to be loved. The only variation is the strategy people employ to get them. If you provide these five things to others first, you get mirrored reciprocation — everything comes back.

### The obstacle: loss aversion

Why doesn't everyone do this? Kaufman invokes Kahneman's [[psychology-of-human-misjudgment|loss aversion]]: the asymmetry between the desire for gain and the desire to avoid loss. Going positive first carries a ~2% risk of rejection and a ~98% chance of positive reciprocation. Yet most people spend 98% of their emotional budget protecting against the 2% risk. Kaufman frames this through the insurance metaphor: "You're supposed to spend 2% to protect 98%. You're doing the opposite."

Lou Brock: "Show me a man who is afraid of appearing foolish and I'll show you a man who can be beat every time."

## Win-win and the six counterparties

Kaufman extends mirrored reciprocation to business strategy. The three hallmarks of a great investment — superior returns, low risk, and long duration — should be treated as co-priorities, not traded off. Win-win relationships produce all three; win-lose produces suboptimal outcomes in any game-theoretic scenario.

The prerequisite is what Kaufman calls the "basic axiom of clinical psychology": *If you could see the world the way I see it, you'd understand why I behave the way I do.* Two corollaries follow:

1. To understand someone's behavior, see the world as they see it.
2. To change someone's behavior, change how they see the world.

The practical application: leaders must see through the eyes of all six counterparty groups — customers, suppliers, employees, owners, regulators, and communities. Understanding their needs, aspirations, insecurities, and time horizons eliminates blind spots. "To understand is to know what to do" — Wittgenstein's line, which anchors the entire talk.

## Connections

Kaufman's framework is a direct descendant of Munger's [[elementary-worldly-wisdom|elementary worldly wisdom]] — the latticework of [[mental-models|mental models]] drawn from multiple disciplines. Where Munger's 1994 USC talk focuses on investment management, Kaufman applies the identical method to human relationships and leadership.

The "reciprocity" entry in the [[mental-models|mental models]] catalog captures the same principle Kaufman calls "mirrored reciprocation": "give what you want to receive; go positive and go first."

The compound-interest-as-constancy model connects to [[self-renewal|Gardner's concept of self-renewal]] — the ongoing effort required to maintain growth against entropy and complacency. Gardner calls it "tough-minded optimism"; Kaufman calls it "being constant." Both identify intermittency as the enemy of compounding.

Kaufman's loss-aversion obstacle is the behavioral economics foundation of Munger's [[psychology-of-human-misjudgment|25 tendencies of misjudgment]], particularly the "deprival super-reaction tendency" — the outsized pain people feel from losses compared to equivalent gains.

The "22-second course in leadership" — be the list of virtues people are searching for — echoes [[solitude-and-leadership|Deresiewicz's argument]] that leadership requires the inner conviction to act on principle rather than following the crowd.
