# Straussian Reading and Mansfield on Machiavelli

Leo Strauss's method of reading the great books — and Harvey Mansfield's extension of it to Machiavelli as the founder of modernity. The central claim: great books speak "downward" to their immediate audience while encoding a second layer of argument meant for attentive readers, and that layer is only reachable through irony and careful attention to *where* and *how* a thing is said.

Mansfield, interviewed by Tyler Cowen at age 93 after 61 years teaching at Harvard, is one of the last direct transmitters of Strauss's method. This article synthesizes his account of the method and his reading of Machiavelli as its most important application.

## Logographic necessity

The operational core of Straussian reading is what Strauss called **logographic necessity**: in a great book, nothing is accidental. Every argument is placed where it is for a reason — which chapter, which dialogue, which interlocutor, which position in the sequence. Mansfield: "There are no accidents in a great book. Everything is as it should be."

This cuts against analytic philosophy, which Mansfield treats as a rival method rather than a complement. Analytic philosophers abstract arguments out of context and compare them in an array. Strauss looks at the argument as if it were in a play with plot and background. An argument presented by Socrates to a particular interlocutor may be deliberately *inferior* — tailored down to what that interlocutor can accept — and the reader's job is to raise it back to the level the author intended.

## Irony as the distinguishing mark

Mansfield's sharpest criterion: **what distinguishes philosophy from non-philosophy is irony.** Great books speak down to their audience, presenting truth "in an inferior but still attractive way." The reader must notice the downward shift and climb back up.

This is why Mansfield is dismissive of John Rawls's notion of **public reason** — the idea that the reasons that convince the philosopher should be the same reasons offered to the public. For Strauss, reason is never universal in that sense; it must always take account of the character of the audience, which is typically less reasonable than the author.

Karl Popper reading Plato's *Republic* as totalitarianism is Mansfield's paradigm of a smart reader failing on exactly this axis: mistaking an argument pitched downward for the author's own position.

## Mansfield on Machiavelli: the invention of "fact"

Mansfield's lifelong claim, elaborated in *The Rise and Fall of Rational Control*, is that Machiavelli is the founder of modernity — and did it by inventing the concept of fact.

- **Effectual truth** (*verità effettuale*): the upshot of a statement or action, independent of what anyone intended or wished. "If you say 'I love you,' the effectual truth is I want something from you."
- The word *effettuale* was a Machiavelli neologism, from Latin *facere*. Modern usage of "fact" — a thing as it is, stripped of wish and speech — traces from here.
- This is the seed of modern empirical science. "Galileo didn't go around asking people, 'Does the earth move or not?' He looked for the fact." Plato and Aristotle grounded knowledge in speech and wish; Machiavelli grounded it in cause and effect.

A telling paradox in Mansfield's view: Machiavelli is both the origin of empirical method and a relentless exaggerator. Asked what percentage of Machiavelli's claims would survive empirical testing, Mansfield says "a small percentage" — but insists **exaggeration is a requirement of empirical analysis, not its enemy**. The exaggeration is how you reduce the realm of fortune and predict what will happen.

## Conspiracy and secrecy

Book 3, Chapter 6 of the *Discourses on Livy* — on conspiracy — is the longest chapter in Machiavelli's two great works, and no previous thinker had written anything like it. Earlier philosophers had asked whether killing a tyrant is just; Machiavelli instead explains *how to do it*: before, during, and after. Three stages, with warnings at each.

Mansfield is ambivalent about Machiavelli's conspiratorial picture of politics:
- **Against it:** America's 20th-century wars were not conspiracies. They were defensive, and the country saved the world from "three great invasions" without intending to.
- **For it:** Execution requires secrecy. Even a babysitter can't say everything to a baby. Mansfield's own work on executive power rests on the thesis that you can't speak without holding something back. "All politics has that kind of need for equivocation."

On the recent uptick in political assassinations (Trump attempts, Charlie Kirk, the healthcare CEO, Israel's jihadist-targeted strikes), Mansfield doesn't think people are reading political theory — they're copying each other. The mimetic channel, not the textual one.

## Spontaneous order is rational control in disguise

Mansfield is dismissive of the Hayekian tradition of [[../systems-thinking/index|spontaneous order]]. His argument: spontaneous order isn't an alternative to rational control — it's a form of it, and Machiavelli was the original author. Machiavelli wanted to "take the leash off humanity, especially the Christian leash, and let the nobles and plebs fight it out." The modern order came from *liberating* human energies, but the liberation itself required imposition. "Spontaneous order always presents itself as not spontaneous, as covered over in sludge." Hayek omits the imposition step. "That doesn't happen spontaneously."

This is a distinctly Straussian move: a contemporary theory (Hayek's) is treated as a downstream consequence of a founding philosophical project (Machiavelli's) that contains more than its later inheritors realize.

## The future of Straussianism

Straussian professors at prestigious universities are dying and not being replaced. Mansfield is nevertheless sanguine: the method rides on the great books themselves, which are self-sustaining. "Why is it we still read Plato's *Republic* 2,500 years later?"

Two explicit disavowals:
- **Bronze Age Pervert (Costin Alamariu)** is not the model for what Straussianism is becoming. Mansfield, who read Alamariu's Yale dissertation, says calling him a Straussian "is not correct." He picked things out of Strauss, mostly Nietzsche.
- **AI as a substitute tutor.** Cowen suggests LLMs for learning Straussian reading; Mansfield is unimpressed: "You want the best, and the best is in the original text."

Mansfield's recommended entry points are Strauss's *Natural Right and History* and *Persecution and the Art of Writing* (the latter being Strauss's explanation of esoteric writing).

## Related Mansfield positions worth noting

- **Trump as Shakespearean vulgarian.** Not a gentleman, works at "discordant impulse," says things to strike rather than persuade. In his way *more democratic* than the rest of us — a point Mansfield connects to Falstaff and "the vulgarity of democracy."
- **Manliness is in eclipse, not decline.** The danger is "unemployed manliness," which Mansfield thinks partly accounts for the rise in political violence. He attributes the eclipse partly to feminism.
- **Churchill** understood "the character of liberal democracy" and guided Britain from aristocracy into democracy without losing dignity. Mansfield saw Churchill speak at Margate in 1953.
- **Great books in the 20th century.** Only two, per Mansfield: Heidegger and Strauss himself. Philosophy has been "historicized" since the early 19th century — thinkers stopped believing it was possible to write for other times, and so stopped trying.
