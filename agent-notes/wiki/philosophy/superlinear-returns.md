# Superlinear Returns

Paul Graham argues that most people fundamentally misunderstand the shape of returns for performance. The folk wisdom — "you get out what you put in" — implies linearity, but in nearly every domain that matters, returns are superlinear: small differences in performance produce enormous differences in outcome. This isn't an artifact of capitalism or any other set of rules; it appears in fame, power, knowledge, military conquest, and scientific discovery alike.

Graham contends that all superlinear returns reduce to two fundamental causes, which often combine and reinforce each other.

## Cause 1: Exponential growth

Whenever how well you do depends on how well you've done, you get exponential growth. The key mathematical insight: if growth rate is proportional to performance *p* over time *t*, the reward is proportional to *p^t*. Even Graham finds this "startling" after decades of thinking about it.

Examples span from bacterial cultures (tricky to grow, but when they grow they grow exponentially) to startups (YC's focus on growth rate rather than absolute numbers steers founders toward exponential trajectories) to scholarship (the more you know, the easier it is to learn).

Graham observes that humans have almost no cultural customs for dealing with exponential growth because there have been so few historical instances of it. Herding was limited by grazing land. Conquest compounded territory but affected too few people to generate widespread lessons. Scholarship compounded knowledge but had little practical effect before the Industrial Revolution. We are still in the early stages of assimilating a world where "emperors of ideas can design bombs that defeat emperors of territory."

## Cause 2: Thresholds (step functions)

The "winner take all" pattern: a step function where crossing the threshold produces a discrete jump in returns. The source is not competition itself but the existence of thresholds in outcomes — proving a theorem, hitting a target, winning a battle. You don't need competitors to encounter a step function.

Graham is careful to note an asymmetry between the two causes: exponential growth guarantees both the *shape* and *magnitude* of the return curve (anything growing fast enough will get big regardless of starting size), while thresholds only guarantee the shape. A threshold heuristic therefore needs an additional test for whether the game is worth playing.

## How the two causes combine

Graham argues the two sources frequently co-occur and amplify each other:

- **Battle victories:** the winning side suffers less damage, making future wins more likely (threshold → exponential growth).
- **Network effects:** a company growing fast enough can lock out competitors (exponential growth → crossing thresholds).
- **Fame:** grows exponentially (fans bring new fans) but concentrates due to thresholds (limited mental A-list slots).
- **Learning:** the most important combined case. Knowledge grows exponentially, and key thresholds (learning to read, discovering a new field) act as "machine tools" that accelerate all subsequent learning. Knowledge is fractal — pushing at the boundary of one area sometimes reveals an entire new field, giving you first crack at its discoveries. Newton, Dürer, and Darwin all did this.

## Heuristics for finding superlinear returns

Graham distills several practical rules:

1. **Seek work that compounds** — either directly (infrastructure, audience, brand) or through learning. The learning channel is underappreciated because it can feel like failure while it's happening, even though you're accumulating exponential growth. This is why Silicon Valley tolerates failure *from people who are learning*.

2. **Always be learning** — if you're not learning, you're probably not on a path to superlinear returns. But don't over-optimize *what* you learn; overly strict filtering will "lop off the outliers."

3. **Replace mediocre-but-popular things** — if something is mediocre yet still popular, that's a signal that a better alternative would capture demand. (Graham cautions: sometimes monopoly, regulation, or broken buyer behavior explains the persistence of mediocrity.)

4. **Follow curiosity over careerism** — the research problems that lead to new discoveries tend to seem "mystifying but perhaps unimportant," not "legit but boring." Curiosity is a better guide than career strategy for finding intellectual thresholds.

5. **Look for fields with extreme concentration** — where a few big winners vastly outperform everyone else. Graham notes a striking pattern: once you remove sports (externally imposed thresholds) and politics/fame effects, the fields with superlinear returns are exactly the fields requiring [[doing-great-work|independent-mindedness]] — where ideas must be not just correct, but novel.

6. **Don't equate work with a job** — for people doing work with superlinear returns, "your work" is a portable portfolio carried across jobs, not the job itself.

## The expanding territory

Graham argues superlinear returns are becoming both more common and larger, driven by two intertwined forces: progress in technology and the declining importance of organizations.

In 1970, you needed an organization for resources, colleagues, and distribution. Organizations dampened variation in returns. That world has eroded: ambitious projects now require less initial funding, and new channels for learning, earning, collaborating, and distributing have emerged. The result is greater variation in outcomes — those who do well do even better, but those who do badly do worse.

This "great unlocking" of ambition democratizes opportunity more authentically than any institutional diversity program could. But it's not for everyone. Graham identifies two groups who should shoot for superlinear returns: those who know they're good enough to be net ahead in a higher-variance world, and the young who can afford the risk of finding out.

## The initial effort problem

Graham emphasizes that in all fields with superlinear returns, the early phase feels discouraging. Returns seem small at first — "at this rate, I'll never get anywhere." But because the curve rises so steeply at the far end, extraordinary early effort is a bargain. In the startup world this is called "do things that don't scale." The same applies to learning: the initial confusion is worth pushing through because compounding will take over. This connects to [[common-denominator-of-success|Gray's thesis]] that success requires habitually doing things that feel costly in the moment.

## Connection to luck

Graham acknowledges that luck is always a factor, and more so when working independently than inside organizations. Beyond the "prepared mind meets opportunity" adage, there's irreducible true chance. His solution: take multiple shots, which is another reason to start taking risks early. This maps directly onto [[four-kinds-of-luck|Austin's luck taxonomy]], where Chance III and IV can be systematically cultivated through action and distinctive personal style.

## Relationship to "How to Do Great Work"

This essay is the direct precursor to Graham's later [[doing-great-work|"How to Do Great Work."]] A recipe paragraph in this essay grew into that full essay. The two are complementary: "Superlinear Returns" provides the *why* (the shape of the reward curve makes exceptional effort a bargain), while [[doing-great-work|"How to Do Great Work"]] provides the *how* (the cross-domain recipe for actually producing great work).

The essay also complements [[you-and-your-research|Hamming's "You and Your Research"]], which frames the same territory from a scientist's perspective. Where Hamming emphasizes courage and "important problems," Graham emphasizes curiosity and the structure of returns. Both agree that the initial slog is worth enduring and that choosing the right problem matters more than raw effort.
