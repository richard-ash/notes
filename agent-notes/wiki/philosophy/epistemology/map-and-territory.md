# Map and Territory

All models are wrong; some are useful. This maxim, coined by statistician George Box in a 1976 paper, captures a foundational epistemological insight: every model is a simplification of reality, and the right question is never "is this model true?" but "is this model useful enough?"

The idea is closely related to Alfred Korzybski's earlier formulation: "The map is not the territory it represents, but, if correct, it has a similar structure to the territory, which accounts for its usefulness."

## Models as useful simplifications

A model is a simplified description of a system or process that fosters understanding. Parrish frames models as analogous to minimum viable products — imperfect by design, but functional enough to be iterated on. The alternative — demanding perfection before acting — would paralyze every domain. Medicine would halt because no procedure is flawless; science would stall because no result is 100% accurate; technology would freeze because every product has defects.

Georg Rasch put it clearly in 1960: "Models should not be true, but it is important that they are applicable, and whether they are applicable for any given purpose must, of course, be investigated. This also means that a model is never accepted finally, only on trial."

The practical corollary: the world runs on the functional, not the perfect. A kitchen knife that cuts vegetables is useful even if it could be sharper. A weather forecast that's often right enables planning even when it's occasionally wrong.

## The map metaphor

Korzybski's map-territory distinction illustrates the same principle spatially. A map of Paris will contain errors — renamed streets, demolished buildings, a new Metro line — yet it remains far more useful than raw trivia about distances. The map's value lies in structural correspondence with reality, not in perfect fidelity.

Joan Robinson extends this to its logical limit: "A model which took account of all the variation of reality would be of no more use than a map at the scale of one to one." Borges dramatized the same idea in *Partial Enchantments of the Quixote*, imagining a perfect 1:1 map of England that must contain a map of itself, recursing to infinity — a reductio ad absurdum of the desire for total accuracy.

Gregory Bateson argues in *Steps to an Ecology of Mind* that a map's purpose is not to be true, but to have a structure that represents truth within its current context. Maps improve over time — from flat-earth portrayals to satellite imagery — just as mental models are continually revised without ever reaching perfection.

## The latticework: breadth over depth

Parrish argues that models are most powerful when held as a large, diverse collection — a "latticework" — rather than mastered one at a time. A person with only one or two models will misapply them; a person with many can select the right tool for each situation. This echoes Charlie Munger's observation that "to a man with only a hammer, every problem looks like a nail."

The models that tend to be most durable are those rooted in fundamental domains (physics, mathematics, biology), applicable across multiple areas, and based on first principles rather than infinite regress. Time acts as a curator: models surviving from ancient Greece and Rome have endured millennia of scrutiny.

## Evaluating model usefulness

Parrish proposes six questions for assessing a model:

1. **Longevity** — how long has it survived scrutiny?
2. **Reality correspondence** — does it reflect the real world or just abstractions?
3. **Cross-domain applicability** — does it transfer across fields?
4. **Origin** — does it derive from fundamental domains?
5. **First principles** — is it grounded in foundational, non-derivable concepts?
6. **Self-sufficiency** — does it avoid infinite regress (needing unbounded justification chains)?

The key discipline is knowing when *not* to apply a model. As Feynman warned: "You must not fool yourself, and you're the easiest person to fool." No model is universally applicable — rigidity in application is as dangerous as having no model at all.

## The map as decision-making heuristic

Farnam Street's [[mental-models]] compendium positions map-and-territory as the foundational model in a latticework of ~100 mental models. Where Box and Parrish's earlier treatment (above) focuses on evaluating model *quality*, the compendium framing adds a pragmatic emphasis: updating our maps is difficult because it requires reconciling what we *want* to be true with what *is* true.

Parrish also highlights the social dimension — in many areas of life, we rely on maps provided by experts, pundits, and teachers. When you don't make the map yourself, the key discipline is choosing your cartographer wisely: seek out those who are rigorous, transparent, and open to revision.

## When models fail

Carlson illustrates two distinct failure modes of mental models through vivid examples.

**Expertise as vulnerability.** In 1989, champion rock climber Lynn Hill threaded her rope through her harness but was distracted by an untied shoe. She forgot to knot the rope and fell 85 feet. Her expertise didn't cause the mistake directly, but it enabled it — years of practice had automated the knotting sequence so thoroughly that one interruption in the routine left her unprotected. The model ("I always tie the knot") was correct in the abstract but fragile to any perturbation in the execution context. This is a concrete instance of the map-territory gap: the model of "what I do before climbing" diverged from what actually happened, and Hill's confidence in the model prevented her from noticing.

**Model obsolescence.** In investing, Carlson traces how once-reliable heuristics decay as conditions change. From 1900 to the late 1950s, comparing stock dividend yields to government bond yields was a dependable sell signal — when dividend yields dropped below bond yields, it was time to exit equities. The model worked until structural changes in capital markets permanently inverted the relationship, and the signal never worked again. Similarly, value investing guru Bill Nygren observes that screening for low P/E and low price-to-book stocks — once a reliable edge — now produces results any intern can replicate in minutes. As information becomes commoditized, the model's predictive value collapses. The simple, obvious stocks that look cheap generally *deserve* to be cheap.

Carlson's diagnosis is that **relative skill**, not absolute skill, determines model effectiveness. When more participants adopt the same model, the edge it once conferred disappears — a dynamic closely related to the Red Queen Effect in the [[mental-models]] compendium. The investors who continue to outperform are those who detect structural shifts and update their models accordingly, not those who execute the old model more diligently.

The prescription Carlson endorses is **strong opinions, weakly held** — commit to a framework for how the world works, but maintain the ability to revise it when evidence of structural change accumulates. The purpose of a mental model framework is not to calcify behavior but to provide a starting point that can be updated.

## Connections

This framework connects to several epistemological threads:

- Deutsch's concept of [[beginning-of-infinity|good explanations]] provides a more rigorous criterion than "usefulness" — a good explanation is hard to vary while still accounting for what it explains. Box's maxim operates at a lower bar: a model need not be a good explanation to be useful, but the best models tend to be both.
- The [[surprising-detail]] principle (Salvatier) explains *why* all models are wrong: reality contains vastly more meaningful detail than any model can capture. The question is whether the detail left out matters for the purpose at hand.
- Deutsch's argument that [[beginning-of-infinity|simplicity is posterior to physics]] challenges the assumption that simpler models are inherently better — what counts as "simple" depends on the physical substrate and the current theory, not on an a priori measure.
