# Reality Has a Surprising Amount of Detail

John Salvatier argues that reality contains far more meaningful detail than we typically expect, and that this is not a quirk of particular domains but a near-universal property of the world. The essay has become a widely cited articulation of why hands-on experience consistently surprises even intelligent people.

## The core claim

Every activity — from building basement stairs to boiling water to launching rockets — decomposes into layers of subtasks, each containing details with material consequences. Salvatier illustrates this through stair carpentry: what seems like a five-step job (cut boards, attach brackets, screw in stairs) rapidly expands into dozens of micro-decisions about angles, lumber warp, screw lengths, and drill technique. Each detail is trivial in isolation but consequential in aggregate.

This is not limited to craft domains. Salvatier extends the principle to physics itself: "water boils at 100°C" turns out to be a dramatic oversimplification once you account for nucleation sites, container material, superheating, and the multiple visible phases of the boiling process. Even the "simple and elegant" physical laws manifest in complex, counterintuitive ways.

## The epistemological problem

The essay's deeper argument is about how detail density creates intellectual stuckness:

1. **Before you notice a detail, it is invisible.** You don't know what you're looking for, so you can't direct attention toward it.
2. **After you notice it, it becomes transparent.** The insight integrates so seamlessly into your mental model that you can no longer see it as a distinct piece of knowledge — you "see right through it."
3. **This asymmetry makes it hard to imagine you're missing something.** Your current frame feels complete because you can't perceive its gaps.

Salvatier observes that we routinely mistake this structural property of the universe for a personal failing — "it was so simple all along, I don't know why I had so much trouble." Programmers, he notes, may think fiddliness is unique to software, when really "everything is fiddly, but you only notice the fiddliness when you're new, and in programming you do new things more often."

## Different people notice different details

A key illustration: Salvatier and his father disagree about how to cut stair boards. Salvatier wants to compute the angle trigonometrically; his father wants to trace it directly. Each sees a real aspect of the problem the other misses — mathematical precision vs. material reality (wood warps, so calculated angles won't perfectly match the actual lumber). The inability to communicate which details each found salient kept them stuck in a three-hour argument.

This generalizes: disagreements often stem not from different values or reasoning ability, but from attending to different subsets of the available detail. When Salvatier asks what would convince someone they're wrong, people demand extreme evidence ("all the data was faked") rather than the more likely scenario of "numerous important mistakes in the details."

## The prescription

Salvatier's recommendation is a practice of deliberate noticing:

- On walks, attend to details you'd normally skip — flower structures, road seams and what they imply about construction.
- In disagreements, figure out which details the other person finds important and why.
- In work, notice which specific contributions actually moved things forward.
- In learning, track which details changed how you think.

"If you wish to not get stuck, seek to perceive what you have not yet perceived."

## Connections

This essay connects to several adjacent ideas:

- [[leverage-points]] — Meadows's framework is itself an argument that most people attend to the wrong details (parameters) rather than the high-leverage ones (paradigms, system goals). Salvatier's essay explains *why* the high-leverage details are so hard to see.
- The concept relates to tacit knowledge (Polanyi) — the "transparent" details Salvatier describes are essentially knowledge that has become tacit and can no longer be easily articulated.
- The "fiddliness of programming" observation connects to Fred Brooks's distinction between essential and accidental complexity, though Salvatier would argue much of what Brooks calls "accidental" is actually essential detail of the materials.
