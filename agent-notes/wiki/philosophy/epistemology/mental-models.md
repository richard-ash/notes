# Mental Models

A mental model is a simplified explanation of how something works — a tool for compressing complexity into manageable chunks. The concept draws from Charlie Munger's insight that effective thinking requires a "latticework" of models drawn from many disciplines, not mastery of any single framework. As Munger puts it: "You don't have to know it all. Just take in the best big ideas from all these disciplines." For Munger's own detailed taxonomy of 25 psychological tendencies that cause systematic misjudgment — the canonical application of latticework thinking to psychology — see [[psychology-of-human-misjudgment]]. For the full latticework argument applied to investing and business — including the pari-mutuel metaphor, advantages of scale, and the Berkshire system of concentrated bets — see [[elementary-worldly-wisdom]].

This article catalogs roughly 100 models organized by their source discipline, as presented by Farnam Street. The framing throughout is Parrish's — these are popularizations of ideas from physics, biology, economics, psychology, and other fields, repackaged as decision-making heuristics.

## The latticework principle

The core argument is that models are most powerful when held as a large, diverse collection rather than mastered individually. A person with only one or two models will misapply them ("to a man with only a hammer, every problem looks like a nail"). A person with many can select the right tool for each situation. This connects directly to the [[map-and-territory]] distinction — every model is a simplified map, and the key discipline is knowing which map to use and when any map is inadequate.

## General thinking tools

Nine foundational models for reasoning across any domain:

- **[[map-and-territory|The Map is Not the Territory]]** — our abstractions are not reality itself; choose your cartographers wisely and update your maps frequently
- **Circle of Competence** — know the boundaries of what you know; the size of your circle matters less than knowing where it ends
- **First Principles Thinking** — decompose problems to fundamental truths rather than reasoning by analogy; almost no one does this, which is why it confers competitive advantage
- **Thought Experiment** — strip away confounding factors to test ideas in a simplified mental sandbox; the source of many profound innovations ("What if?")
- **Second-Order Thinking** — ask "And then what?" to anticipate ripple effects; first-order thinking optimizes for now, second-order thinking plays the long game
- **Probabilistic Thinking** — navigate uncertainty by thinking in shades of probability and constantly updating beliefs with new data
- **Inversion** — instead of asking "How do I succeed?", ask "What would guarantee failure?" — then avoid those things; much of success comes from avoiding common failure modes
- **Occam's Razor** — prefer the simplest explanation that fits the facts, but recognize that sometimes the truth is genuinely complex (contrast with Deutsch's argument in [[beginning-of-infinity]] that simplicity is posterior to physics, not prior)
- **Hanlon's Razor** — never attribute to malice what is adequately explained by incompetence; shifts perspective from defensive to empathetic

## Physics, chemistry, and biology

Models drawn from natural science, applied metaphorically to human systems:

- **Relativity** — perceptions are shaped by frames of reference; understanding this fosters empathy and reveals blind spots
- **Reciprocity** — give what you want to receive; go positive and go first without expectation; "the best way to achieve success is to deserve success"
- **Thermodynamics** — energy is conserved (first law) but entropy always increases in closed systems (second law); maintaining order requires constant energy input
- **Inertia** — objects at rest stay at rest; the status quo has mass; getting started is the hardest part, but once momentum builds, it carries you forward (the "flywheel" concept)
- **Friction and Viscosity** — resistance slows everything; often reducing friction is more effective than adding force; friction can also be weaponized against competitors
- **Velocity** — direction matters as much as speed; high speed in circles goes nowhere
- **Leverage** — small force, strategically applied, yields outsized results; but leverage amplifies downside risk too
- **Activation Energy** — the upfront cost of change is not a permanent obstacle; once things are moving, momentum takes over
- **Catalysts** — forces that accelerate change without being consumed; mentors, technologies, and transformative experiences all serve as catalysts
- **Alloying** — combining different skills/elements creates something greater than the sum of parts; rare skill combinations are disproportionately valuable
- **Natural Selection and Extinction** — the unfit are winnowed; there are no permanent victories, only ceaseless adaptation
- **The Red Queen Effect** — you must keep running just to stay in place; complacency is the fastest path to extinction (see also [[leverage-points]] on the paradigm level)
- **Ecosystems** — nothing exists in isolation; be slow to intervene because well-intentioned actions often cause unintended consequences; "First, do no harm"
- **Niches** — specialists thrive in stability but are fragile to change; generalists face more daily competition but adapt better
- **Self-Preservation** — a core instinct that can become limiting when it prevents risk-taking
- **Replication** — imitate first to establish a baseline, then innovate; but unchecked replication can become cancerous
- **Cooperation** — the model that most explains humanity; requires repeated interactions, shared benefits, and mechanisms to prevent cheating
- **Hierarchical Organization** — allows specialization and scale, but too much leads to power struggles that override organizational goals
- **Incentives** — understand the incentive, predict the outcome; poorly designed incentives encourage short-term thinking and perverse behavior
- **Tendency to Minimize Energy Output** — the path of least resistance is the default; experience doesn't become learning without reflection

## Systems thinking

Models for understanding complex, dynamic systems (see also [[leverage-points]] for Meadows's complementary framework):

- **Feedback Loops** — outputs influence inputs; understanding these is key to directing system change
- **Equilibrium** — systems continuously adjust toward balance but rarely stay there; stagnation and stability are two sides of the same coin
- **Bottlenecks** — the constraint that limits the whole system; focus all effort on the bottleneck, not the parts already moving fast (theory of constraints)
- **Scale** — what works small often breaks large; scaling requires re-engineering processes, not just multiplying inputs
- **Margin of Safety** — buffers look like waste in the short term but enable survival in the long term; the unsung hero of durability
- **Churn** — the silent killer; some turnover is healthy renewal, too much is fatal
- **Algorithms** — if-then recipes for consistent results; thinking algorithmically means searching for reliable processes
- **Critical Mass** — things happen slowly, then all at once; the tipping point where a system becomes self-sustaining
- **Emergence** — wholes that are more than the sum of parts; you cannot predict emergent properties from components alone
- **Irreducibility** — some things cannot be broken down without losing their essence; sometimes you must zoom out rather than decompose
- **Diminishing Returns** — easy wins come first; know when to stop optimizing and move on

## Mathematics

Models from mathematical thinking applied to everyday reasoning:

- **Sampling** — larger samples yield better conclusions; beware drawing conclusions from too few data points
- **Randomness** — humans are terrible at recognizing true randomness; we see patterns where none exist
- **Regression to the Mean** — extreme outcomes are rarely repeated; the exceptional tends to revert toward the ordinary
- **Multiply by Zero** — one critical failure negates all other effort; reliability matters because one zero wipes out the whole equation
- **Equivalence** — different inputs can produce identical results; there is usually more than one way to solve a problem
- **Surface Area** — more exposure means more interaction (both opportunity and vulnerability); different situations require different surface areas
- **Global and Local Maxima** — the first peak you find may not be the highest; sometimes you must descend to climb higher

## Economics

Models from economic thinking:

- **Scarcity** — infinite wants, finite resources; rare skill combinations are disproportionately rewarded
- **Supply and Demand** — the push-pull determining price and allocation; economic cycles are driven by human nature as much as by resources
- **Optimization** — making the most of what you have; works until it doesn't
- **Trade-offs and Opportunity Cost** — every yes is an implicit no; wisdom is anticipating the consequences of choices
- **Specialization** — go deep to broaden impact, but the most exciting discoveries happen at the edges between fields
- **Interdependence** — no one is self-made; this is both a vulnerability and a strength
- **Efficiency** — the more perfectly efficient a system, the more fragile it becomes; short-term inefficiency is often long-term resilience (see also Margin of Safety)
- **Debt** — gives leverage but takes freedom; makes you fragile to any disruption
- **Monopoly and Competition** — we need both; competition keeps firms honest, monopoly funds moonshots
- **Creative Destruction** — new innovations replace old ones; the only constant is change
- **Gresham's Law** — bad drives out good without consequences; but in the long run, true value wins
- **Bubbles** — collective enthusiasm outruns fundamental value; bubbles fund the infrastructure for future revolutions even as they destroy present wealth

## Art and narrative

Models drawn from artistic practice — less commonly cited in decision-making literature but illuminating for communication and product thinking:

- **Audience** — the invisible co-creator; never forget them, but never let them dictate your creation
- **Genre** — conventions create expectations that can be honored or subverted; the sweet spot is honoring expectations while bringing something new
- **Contrast** — our brains are wired to notice change and difference; without contrast, the world is bland
- **Framing** — how information is presented shapes how it's received; works on an emotional, subconscious level
- **Rhythm** — the organizing principle bringing order to chaos; routines, habits, and cycles of work/rest give structure to life
- **Plot** — stories are engines driven by conflict; the most powerful story is the one you tell yourself about your own obstacles
- **Character** — obstacles reveal character; character is not static but a journey of repeated choices
- **Setting** — environment shapes behavior; to change your behavior, change your environment

## Military and strategy

- **Seeing the Front** — personally verify conditions rather than relying solely on reports (connects to [[map-and-territory]])
- **Asymmetric Warfare** — the weaker side plays by different rules; limited resources drive creative tactics
- **Two-Front War** — splitting focus weakens both fronts; avoid fighting on multiple fronts simultaneously
- **Counterinsurgency** — tit-for-tat competition creates feedback loops demanding escalation
- **Mutually Assured Destruction** — strength paradoxically prevents conflict, but pushes catastrophic risk into the tails

## Human nature and judgment

Twenty-three cognitive biases and instincts, heavily influenced by Kahneman, Tversky, and Munger:

**Instincts and drives:** Trust (the foundation of civilization), Reciprocity bias, Pavlovian association, Envy and jealousy, Liking/disliking distortion, Denial, Self-preservation, Curiosity instinct, Language instinct, Narrative instinct, Social proof, Tendency to want to do something (action bias).

**Cognitive biases:** Availability heuristic (we recall what is salient, recent, and frequent), Representativeness heuristic (base rate neglect, stereotyping, false conjunctions), First-conclusion bias (the mind shuts after the first idea lands), Overgeneralization from small samples, Commitment and consistency bias, Hindsight bias ("I knew it all along"), Confirmation bias / falsification, Survivorship bias (we study winners and ignore the silent graveyard of losers), Fundamental attribution error (over-ascribing behavior to character vs. situation), Sensitivity to fairness, Relative satisfaction (happiness is relative to peers and past, not absolute).

**Amplifier:** Stress amplifies all other biases — "In the thick of battle, you will not rise to the level of your expectations, but fall to the level of your training."

## Synthesis

Several meta-patterns emerge across these domains:

1. **Models are maps, not territory.** Every model in this compendium is a simplification. The discipline is knowing which simplification to apply and when none is adequate.

2. **Short-term and long-term incentives almost always conflict.** Efficiency vs. margin of safety, optimization vs. adaptability, first-order vs. second-order thinking — the recurring tension is between what looks good now and what survives over time.

3. **Systems resist change but reward those who overcome resistance.** Inertia, activation energy, friction, the status quo — all point to the same principle: getting started is the hardest part, but momentum compounds.

4. **Adaptation is continuous, not terminal.** The Red Queen effect, creative destruction, evolution — there is no finish line, only an endless race against entropy and competition.

5. **Bad drives out good without effort; good requires constant cultivation.** Gresham's Law, entropy, cognitive biases — the default trajectory is decay, and maintaining quality requires sustained energy.
