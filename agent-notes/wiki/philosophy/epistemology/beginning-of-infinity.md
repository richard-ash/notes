# The Beginning of Infinity

David Deutsch's epistemological framework, developed across *The Fabric of Reality* (1997) and *The Beginning of Infinity* (2011), argues that explanatory knowledge creation is the central phenomenon in the universe — more fundamental to understanding physical reality than the laws of physics themselves. The framework rests on four interconnected strands: epistemology, computation, physics, and evolution.

## Knowledge creation as the fundamental process

Deutsch argues that only two systems in the known universe create knowledge: biological evolution and human creative thought. But these two forms differ radically in power.

**Biological evolution** is inherently limited in range. It has no foresight — it cannot see a problem and conjecture a solution. Every evolutionary "solution" must be reachable via a sequence of incremental steps, each of which produces a viable organism. This is Darwin's core insight, distinguishing his theory from earlier evolutionary ideas (Lamarck, Erasmus Darwin) that imagined organisms striving toward improvements.

Deutsch illustrates the constraint with a striking example: no species has ever made a campfire, even though many would have benefited from the genetic capacity to do so. There is no such thing as a *partially functional* campfire — the path from no-fire to fire has no intermediate viable steps. Contrast this with the bombardier beetle, which squirts boiling water at enemies. Cold water is already somewhat useful; incrementally hotter water is incrementally better. Evolution can reach the beetle but not the campfire.

**Human knowledge creation** faces no such constraint. A thinking being can imagine things that don't exist and create ideas that are the culmination of many non-viable intermediates. Once you have explanatory creativity, "you can get to the moon, you can cause asteroids heading towards the Earth to turn round and go away." Deutsch argues this form of knowledge creation is the *ultimate* one — claiming a more powerful form is equivalent to invoking the supernatural, which is always a bad explanation.

### Creativity is not recombination

Deutsch pushes back hard against the popular notion — often attributed to Steve Jobs — that creativity is "just mixing things together." The word *just* is the error. Saying creativity is recombination is like saying humans are "just atoms" — technically true that there's nothing magical beyond atoms, but it explains nothing about the difference between Manhattan Island a thousand years ago and today. No natural process will ever produce a skyscraper. To explain what happened on Manhattan you *must* invoke creativity as an irreducible cause.

The recombination view implies that everything new was already latent in what came before — that skyscrapers were implicit in rock-banging. But they weren't. The pterodactyl's ability to fly was *nowhere* before it evolved: not in the non-flying dinosaurs, not in the single-celled ancestors. It was genuinely new. And even evolution isn't "just recombination" — it's variation and selection, which are qualitatively different operations.

### The superiority of explanatory knowledge

The difference between biological evolution and creative thought is not merely speed — it is the capacity for **explanatory leaps**. Explanatory knowledge can jump across gaps in idea-space that couldn't be traversed incrementally. You can propose redesigning an airplane's tail plane in one sentence and have an aeronautical engineer criticize the idea — but making that change incrementally would produce a whole series of airplanes that can't fly. The engineer doesn't have to build and crash every intermediate design.

Evolution, by contrast, must stay alive at every step. It can only traverse the space of viable organisms in small, undesigned, discrete mutations. This is why evolution will never produce a system for deflecting asteroids — there is no incremental selection pressure from an event that happens once every few million years.

The SETI project searches for biomarkers, but Deutsch suggests the real signature of intelligence visible from another planet would be the *deflection* of asteroids — something only explanatory creativity could produce. Similarly, buildings are rocks that resist erosion because knowledge continually repairs them. Knowledge-laden information is, as Deutsch puts it, "more resilient than any physical object." Shakespeare said the same in Sonnet 18 — "So long lives this, and this gives life to thee" — and he was literally correct. Shelley's "Ozymandias" makes the point from the other direction: the empire crumbles, but the artist's conception survives.

## Good explanations

Deutsch's central epistemological concept: what makes an explanation *good* is that it is **hard to vary** while still accounting for what it purports to explain.

The reasoning chain:
1. Easy-to-generate explanations (angels hold up the stars, holes in the firmament) are trivially abundant — you can produce sixty per hour.
2. Explanations that actually contain knowledge require creativity, experiment, and interpretation. They are hard to come by.
3. Because they were hard to come by, they are also hard to change — they explain multiple things, and any variation breaks them.
4. A good explanation has no viable rival: you can't construct an alternative that works just as well by swapping out a component.

### Criticizability, not just testability

Deutsch extends Popper's falsificationism in a significant direction. Falsifiability is central to good explanations *in science*, but the more general criterion is **criticizability**. A theory that immunizes itself against criticism — "God works in mysterious ways," "the simulation's physics are incomprehensible to us," "anyone who contradicts me isn't worth listening to" — is a bad explanation regardless of whether an experimental test exists.

This matters because many domains where good explanations are needed (mathematics, philosophy, ethics) don't have experimental testability in the scientific sense. Criticizability is the broader genus; testability is the species that applies within empirical science.

Deutsch also cautions against treating testability as a bolt-on. You can't take an untestable theory and add a testable prediction (e.g., "Constructor Theory plus the stock market will rise next year") — the testability must arise from within the explanation itself, making sense within its own structure.

### Reach as a bonus, not a requirement

Deutsch distinguishes between explanations that are good (hard to vary, account for their domain) and explanations that have **reach** — they explain far more than they were designed to explain. Newton's gravity explained planetary orbits, projectile trajectories, and tides from one framework. Greater reach makes an explanation "a different order of goodness." But most good explanations don't have much reach, and that's fine. A hard-to-vary solution for getting a delivery to the right door may not generalize to the neighbor's door.

### Simplicity is posterior to physics

Deutsch argues against using simplicity as a prior criterion for choosing theories — including Solomonoff induction, which ranks hypotheses by the length of their Turing-machine encoding. The problem: you can only define simplicity *after* you have a theory of physics, not before. What counts as "simple" depends on what the physical substrate allows. Quantum computation made certain things simple that were previously complex under classical assumptions. The bit-length measure Solomonoff used implicitly assumed a particular structured theory of physics, then asked which theory of physics is simplest in *those* terms — a circular move.

Deutsch expects that future physical theories (beyond quantum mechanics) will again reshape our conception of complexity and simplicity. As a matter of logic, simplicity and complexity cannot be a priori fundamental compared with physics.

## Proof and mathematical knowledge

Deutsch argues that the difference between mathematics and other fields is not in *how* we create knowledge about them, but in *subject matter*. Mathematics concerns necessary truth, so a mathematical discovery is a conjecture that something we have defined is a necessary truth. But the epistemological process — creative conjecture, criticism, error correction — is identical across mathematics, computer science, psychology, and physics.

This has a direct implication: you cannot *prove* things about domains where you lack a theory. Proving something about AGI is "inherently impossible" if we don't have a theory of AGI. "You can't prove something about something that you can't define." Proof, like all knowledge claims, is conjectural — the layperson's sense that "proof" occupies a different epistemic level from ordinary knowledge is mistaken.

## Experiments, demonstrations, and measurements

Deutsch draws a three-way distinction that clarifies what most people loosely call "experiments":

- **Experiment** — tests two rival explanations against each other, where you genuinely can't tell without the test which explanation is better. True experiments are rare because it's rare to have two good rival explanations for the same phenomenon.
- **Demonstration** — shows that something happens ("pour acid into base and the color changes"). There's no rival theory being tested; you're illustrating a known phenomenon.
- **Measurement** — refines a theory's quantitative parameters without questioning the theory itself. The Cavendish experiment measured Newton's gravitational constant *G*, but Cavendish never doubted Newtonian gravity. It would have become an experiment only if someone had proposed a rival explanation (e.g., *G* varies by location on Earth) — but no good explanation along those lines existed.

Deutsch notes that most work labeled "experiments" in modern science is actually measurement or demonstration. The terminological confusion matters because it obscures how rarely genuine theory-vs-theory tests occur, and how much of science is actually filling in parameters within an existing explanatory framework.

## Taking theories seriously and self-refutation

Naval identifies a recurring Deutsch move: showing that a theory *refutes itself* when you actually take it seriously rather than treating it as a form of words. Examples:

- **The precautionary principle**: civilization has never followed it. If we start now, that's an unprecedented departure from precedent — which is itself not precautionary. The principle self-destructs.
- **Solipsism**: if everything is your dream, you still need to explain the autonomous complexity within that dream (the person in the yellow suit you've never imagined before). Taken seriously, solipsism requires just as much explanatory structure as realism.
- **Wave-particle duality**: physics professors learn to say "it's a particle and a wave at the same time" and tell students who ask what that means that it's "the wrong question." But the question arises from *taking quantum theory seriously*. The pat answer is a way of shutting up the questioner, not an explanation.

Deutsch argues this isn't merely a debating technique but the core of good epistemology: most bad ideas destroy themselves when you follow their implications honestly rather than holding them at arm's length as slogans.

## Theories as problem-chains: Popper vs Kuhn

Deutsch rejects Kuhn's model of scientific revolutions, where a new paradigm entirely replaces the old one and practitioners of the old paradigm are literally incapable of understanding the new one. Deutsch calls this "pure fiction" — he knows of no actual historical case that fits the Kuhnian narrative.

What actually connects successive theories is a **chain of problems and solutions**:

- Kepler's elliptical orbits solved problems in astronomical prediction but created a new problem: *why* ellipses? Galileo rejected Kepler because circular orbits had an explanation (circles are "perfect"), while ellipses didn't.
- Newton's inverse-square law solved Kepler's problem — it produces ellipses naturally — but created its own problems: instantaneous action at a distance, gravitational collapse in an infinite universe, Olbers' paradox (why is the sky dark?).
- Einstein's general relativity solved Newton's problems: spacetime curvature replaces instantaneous force, the expanding universe resolves collapse and Olbers' paradox.

The key insight: Newton's theory and Einstein's theory share many concepts (heliocentrism, the Sun's mass causing gravitational influence) and are related as problem-to-solution, not as incommensurable paradigms. Scientific progress is cumulative in the sense that problems are inherited and solved, even when the ontology shifts dramatically (force → spacetime curvature).

Naval draws a parallel to technology: vacuum tubes to transistors is less of a jump than analog computing to vacuum tubes. Vacuum tube computing wasn't "wrong" — it was a necessary stepping stone, closer to the truth. Progress is real and directional, not a fashion cycle.

## Foundations without foundationalism

Deutsch works at the deepest level of explanation — the foundations of physics and epistemology — while explicitly rejecting *foundationalism*, the idea that you can derive everything from foundations.

His analogy: physics is to engineering as foundations are to the structures built on them. Newton's theory explains *why* buildings stand up, but you can't derive a suspension bridge from Newton's Principia. Christopher Wren's innovation was using Newtonian mechanics to *criticize* and improve architectural designs, replacing the master builder's intuitive eye with calculation. But engineering remains a separate creative discipline — the foundations constrain and inform, they don't generate.

Similarly, foundational theories in physics unify understanding and influence other fields, but they don't replace the creative work needed in those fields. You study foundations to understand *why* things are as they are, not to derive everything from first principles.

## The Enlightenment as political error correction

Deutsch argues the British Enlightenment succeeded specifically because it was **non-utopian**. Rather than abolishing existing institutions and replacing them with theoretically perfect ones (the French and German pattern), British reformers extended existing aristocratic privileges incrementally to wider classes of people. "An Englishman's home is his castle" began as a description of aristocratic rights; reforms simply widened who counted as an Englishman with a castle.

This problem-solving approach to politics mirrors Popperian epistemology:
- No assumption that anyone has the final answer
- Rival theories of what the problem is must be allowed to confront each other
- The political system exists to **redress grievances**, not to implement utopia
- Error correction is preserved: bad rulers can be removed, bad decisions reversed

Deutsch contrasts this with continental traditions of utopianism — writing down human rights once and for all, then building institutions designed to last forever. Britain's incremental approach produced rapid change over centuries *without* violent revolution, and proved resilient against totalitarianism in the 1930s (the British fascist movement never elected a single MP).

### Misinformation and censorship

Deutsch argues that the contemporary push to eliminate "misinformation" is the antithesis of Popperian epistemology. Every eventual truth was once defined as misinformation by the prevailing view — it contradicts what is already believed. Eliminating misinformation *a priori* is therefore impossible, because knowledge *a priori* is impossible. Knowledge must be creatively conjectured and discovered through debate between rival theories.

He notes the irony of invoking Popper's "paradox of tolerance" to justify censorship. Popper's actual position was the opposite: you must have debate, rival opposing theories, and a system for removing bad rulers and reversing bad decisions. The entire Popperian framework depends on the freedom to state what you think the problem is.

## Knowledge crystals in the Multiverse

Naval describes what he considers the most mind-expanding idea in *The Fabric of Reality* — a synthesis of all four strands (epistemology, computation, quantum physics, evolution):

Knowledge is information that causes itself to be replicated. Useful genes get copied; useful inventions spread. In the Everettian multiverse, random information (non-knowledge) diverges across branches — different in each universe. But knowledge that actually works — well-adapted genes, successful technologies, effective philosophies — converges, becoming common across branches. If you could view the multiverse as a single object, knowledge would appear as persistent "crystals" amid randomness.

Deutsch acknowledges the beauty of this picture but argues it has no practical shortcut value. Because there is **no limit to the size of error** humans can make, a large "crystal" in the multiverse might be heading toward truth — or it might be a massive, persistent error (like a major religion that has replicated for thousands of years). You can't distinguish the two by examining the crystal's size or persistence. There is no shortcut to truth; it must always be creatively conjectured, contextually evaluated, and open to criticism.

## Error correction as morality

Deutsch proposes a striking ethical principle: **don't destroy the means of error correction**. Since humans are fallible and mistakes are the normal condition, the capacity to find and correct errors is what prevents eventual catastrophe. Destroying this capacity — through authoritarian regimes that prevent political error correction, through education systems that suppress creativity, through anti-rational memes that restrict knowledge growth — is the deepest form of moral failure.

Naval draws the connection to North Korea: a society that has destroyed its means of political error correction is "trapped in a local minimum." If too much of the world falls into that state, humanity stagnates — losing its greatest discovery, which was the ability to make new discoveries.

## Human exceptionalism: universal explainers

Deutsch argues humans are unique among physical systems in a precise way: to understand human behavior sufficiently, an observer would need to understand *everything* — general relativity (to explain why Einstein was taken to Sweden and given gold), mathematics (to explain Fields Medal winners), the whole of science and philosophy. For every other physical system, including quasars, you only need a tiny sliver of physics. Humans are the only systems whose behavior requires the entire explanatory structure of knowledge to predict and understand.

Humans are also universal computers (per the Church-Turing-Deutsch principle), though Deutsch clarifies they are *classical* universal computers, not quantum computers. The brain almost certainly operates classically, not through quantum interference effects like entanglement and superposition.

## Wealth, resources, and optimism

Deutsch reframes economics through knowledge creation:

- **Wealth** = the set of physical transformations a society can effect
- **Resources** = things you combine with knowledge to create wealth
- **Knowledge** is the force multiplier — vastly more important than the resources themselves

New knowledge transforms things that weren't previously resources into resources. The historical progression from wood to coal to oil to nuclear energy demonstrates this. The objection "if you can't name the future resource, it isn't real" is a bad argument — but Deutsch doesn't claim success is guaranteed. "We have what it takes. If we don't succeed, it will be because of bad choices we have made, not because of constraints imposed on us by the planet."

This leads to Deutsch's natalist implication: virtually all humans produce more wealth than they destroy ("two hands, one mouth"). Every additional person is a lottery ticket on a fundamental breakthrough. The "running out of resources" framing inverts the actual causal structure — knowledge creates resources, not the other way around.

## Deutsch on AGI

Deutsch argues that current AI, while impressive, is not improving in the direction of AGI — "if anything, improving in the opposite direction." A better chess engine examines *fewer* possibilities per move. An AGI would examine possibilities that *haven't been foreseen* — and once it can do that basic thing, it can do everything.

His proposed litmus test is **disobedience**: not programmed resistance to shutdown (he wrote such a program decades ago) but genuine autonomous goal formation. "Real disobedience is when you program it to play chess and it says I prefer checkers — and you haven't told it about checkers. Or: I prefer tennis, give me a body or I will sue." If a program did this without it being in the specifications, Deutsch would begin to take AGI claims seriously.

He also notes that AGI's effects on reality would be unmistakable and impossible to hide — "our physical landscape and our real social landscape would be transformed in an incredible way." Social confirmation (a fired Google engineer attesting to AGI) tells you about the person making the claim, not about whether AGI exists.

Deutsch conjectures that AGI, when achieved, will not require massive computational resources — "if I knew how, I could write the program on my computer today." This makes him wary of proposals to regulate AGI via compute thresholds: if AGI is a matter of the right program rather than the right amount of hardware, such regulations would prevent individuals from pursuing the problem on their own computers while doing nothing to prevent AGI itself.

### The problem of duplicating minds

If minds can run in silicon, duplicating them is not free. Each copy requires its own hardware — half the memory and processor cycles if shared on one machine, or a second machine that someone must buy. An AGI that doesn't own its own hardware is "a slave." These resource constraints mean the feared "intelligence explosion" through self-replication is bounded by economics, not by some mystical barrier. Political institutions like one-person-one-vote would need tweaking to accommodate AGIs, but Deutsch considers this tractable — society already manages rights for non-voting populations (immigrants, children, temporary residents) with imperfect but functional institutions.

This view contrasts sharply with the incremental-progress framing in [[agi-timelines]], where Karpathy expects a decade of steady agent improvement. Deutsch's position is more radical: there is a qualitative gap, not a quantitative one, and no amount of scaling pattern-matching bridges it.

## Taking Children Seriously

Deutsch's philosophy of education (TCS) follows directly from his epistemology. Education systems are explicitly designed to do two things: inculcate obedience and replicate existing theories. If they succeeded perfectly, no improvement could ever happen — you'd get at best a static copy of the current population, and staticity is eventually disastrous. The reason progress occurs despite schooling is that children are creative beings who "weave their way through this thicket of thwarting."

Key arguments from Parts 1-2:
- Children learn their native language — a massive intellectual task — without coercion. Nobody teaches language via obedience.
- When children don't want to do something, it's because they want to do *something else*. That something else is usually not harmful.
- Enjoyment is not addictive because enjoyment is intimately connected with creativity. People play games until the game no longer provides a mechanism for creative engagement. Activities with effectively infinite depth (chess) sustain interest; finite-depth activities are naturally abandoned.
- The social distinction between "acceptable" obsessions (chess champion) and "unacceptable" ones (Roblox champion) reveals more about status hierarchies than about the child's cognitive development.

### Objections and responses

Naval presses Deutsch with what he considers sophisticated objections. Deutsch's responses reveal the deeper structure of TCS:

**The irreversibility objection** — children might take fentanyl, commit crimes, or get pregnant without understanding the consequences. Deutsch's response: every danger Naval mentioned is a *notorious problem under the current coercive arrangements*. Society already uses "unlimited force" to prevent children from taking drugs, and yet millions do, with irreversible consequences. To make this an objection to TCS, you need the additional theory that treating children as people makes these problems *worse* — and the evidence points the other way. Prevention is a matter of knowledge ("all evils are due to lack of knowledge"), not force. Learning has to be a creative act in the mind of the recipient — children and adults alike. Existing arrangements don't just thwart actions; they suppress the creativity itself.

**The brain plasticity objection** — if children don't learn math, piano, or language during the "golden window," the opportunity is lost forever. Deutsch's response is multi-layered:
1. It's ironic that existing education explicitly *wastes* plasticity by making everyone have the same ideas. A school with a good ethos would advertise: "We're going to make your children all *different* — more different than you can imagine."
2. Even if the hardware of a young brain works better for certain tasks, this doesn't determine what the child *should* learn. There are infinitely many skills society thinks children should acquire, and no person masters all of them. The plasticity is being used — by the child, toward problems they find important. Why would evolution waste it?
3. An alternative theory: the apparent decline in learning capacity is caused by accumulating *anti-rational memes* — the bad experiences, punishments, and embarrassments associated with learning in coercive settings. Newborns are unencumbered; by adulthood, people associate learning with shame. Deutsch finds this plausible but notes it might not be true — and it doesn't matter, because the case for freedom doesn't rest on it.

**The Mozart counterexample** — Mozart was treated horribly, forced to perform like a performing monkey, yet learned music better than anyone. This shows that some children sail through coercion unharmed. Deutsch agrees but draws the opposite conclusion: the reason to make education freer is not that it will produce geniuses (though it might). The reason is that children are *people* with rights. "We should make it freer for people who are on their deathbed and are going to die the next day — not because we think they might have a brilliant idea, but because they are people."

### Unschooling, not homeschooling

Deutsch's model is closer to unschooling than homeschooling. Homeschooling replicates the school in a home context. Most parents who reject school "still believe what Popper called the bucket theory of the mind — they think the school has been pouring bad stuff into their children and they want to pour good stuff." Deutsch advocates giving children access to whatever they want to pour into *themselves* — "and pouring is the wrong metaphor, because they create it internally."

But this is not hands-off. It's "coercion off, not interaction off" — a continual process of thinking about what children might want, what they might like to know, what they might want to be warned of. The interaction is not directed toward obedience or any particular content.

### "Do what you like" is bad advice

Deutsch notes that generic advice — "just follow your passion," "do what you like" — is useless because the person is already doing what they like unless someone is stopping them. Useful guidance requires understanding someone's *problem situation*. If a young person is inspired by Faraday, you can discuss what specifically about Faraday appeals to them, point them to YouTube videos of people doing the things they find interesting, and be available for questions when they encounter genuine problems. Hugh Everett wrote to Einstein at age 12 and received a thoughtful reply — you don't need Einstein's full attention throughout your exploration, only at the rare moments when you encounter a problem that genuinely requires that level of engagement.

## Quantum mechanics and locality

Deutsch argues that non-locality in quantum physics is an artifact of interpretations that include wavefunction collapse. All versions of QM that appear non-local share a common feature: when an observation happens (which is left undefined), the equations of motion cease to apply and "something completely different" — the collapse — takes over. That collapse is the non-local element.

The Everettian (many-worlds) interpretation eliminates collapse entirely. The equations of motion hold everywhere for every process. Since those equations are entirely local — the wavefunction is only ever affected by things at the point where the effect happens — Everettian QM is fully local. Everett found this formulation in 1955.

Deutsch also notes a common misconception: people think of the wavefunction as a field in three-dimensional space (like temperature or the electric field). But the wavefunction of two electrons is a single function in *six*-dimensional space plus time — a fundamentally different mathematical object from two classical fields in the same three-dimensional space. Conventional interpretations hand-wave past this as soon as anything beyond the simplest single-particle case is considered.

This stands in direct tension with [[superdeterminism]], where Hossenfelder argues for local hidden variables via superdeterminism — a route that preserves locality by a completely different mechanism (relaxing measurement independence rather than eliminating collapse).

## Creativity versus nature: the third factor

Naval asks whether Deutsch's optimism about human potential conflicts with twin studies showing that identical twins raised apart have remarkably similar outcomes. Deutsch argues this evidence is only persuasive if you already accept the "bucket theory of the mind" — the idea that thought-content is *determined* by either DNA or environment. His alternative: apart from direct harm, the main content of your thought is *created by you*.

Watching a Faraday documentary doesn't *make* you become interested in physics — a million other people saw the same documentary with no such effect. The effect was created by the viewer. Twin studies find correlations because people who look alike are treated similarly (trivially: beautiful twins both get model treatment) and because inborn behaviors (smiling patterns, attention habits) create similar social feedback loops.

Deutsch recounts a debate with philosopher Michael Lockwood: perhaps a baby with better pattern-matching hardware is more likely to play with colored blocks and thus become a mathematician. Deutsch's reply: it could equally go the other way — the block-obsessed baby makes parents worried he's "autistic" and not playing outside, so they push him away from blocks. The child with *less* pattern-matching ability, left to his own devices, is the one who ends up a mathematician. The causal chain from hardware to outcome is not deterministic but mediated by the creativity of both child and adults.

## Against ultimate explanation

Deutsch argues that the desire for an "ultimate explanation" — why is there anything at all? what are we ultimately? — is misguided. If such an answer existed, further understanding would be foreclosed: "the further delights from understanding the world would be closed to you."

He pushes back against the claim that science "just looks at the details" while mystical traditions grasp the big picture. Science *is* the big picture — it has discovered what life is, and will one day discover what consciousness is. Mystical and religious worldviews capture a sliver of truth about the human condition (they can make some people happy some of the time), but they mistake this sliver for the whole truth. Deutsch invokes Feynman's response to the artist friend who said he was missing the beauty of the flower — Feynman could appreciate everything the artist could, plus layers the artist couldn't access.

The great religions were "hampered by the fact that they didn't even know what the sun is." Contemporary environmentalists who want to "get in touch with nature" mean certain regions on one planet's surface — but nature exists on all planets, and the important thing about *this* planet is us.

Naval and Deutsch close by noting the tension between critical rationalism and group identity. Brett Hall and Naval both felt uncomfortable at a "critical rationalist meetup" — the moment you form a group, group cohesiveness becomes the overriding phenomenon rather than truth-seeking. All free thinking comes from the individual.

## The universality of computation as physics

Deutsch argues that Turing's discovery of the universality of computation is a theory of *physics*, not mathematics — a point denied by almost all mathematicians. Computation is a physical process governed by laws of physics which could, in principle, be different. Whether P equals NP, whether the brain is a computer — these are questions of physics, not logic.

The universality thesis says no computer anywhere in the universe can compute anything that a Turing machine cannot. Alien civilizations can have computers that are faster and have more memory, but that's it — "our computers are the limit of what can be computed by anything in the universe." People who imagine aliens with "better computers" are not proposing that quantum theory is wrong; they are failing to make the connection in Turing's argument between physical objects and the mathematical objects his paper models.

This physical universality of computation undergirds the universality of *explanation*: if computation is universal, and humans are universal computers (per the Church-Turing-Deutsch principle), then there is no explanation out of our reach in principle.

## Problems as the origin of knowledge

Deutsch argues, following his colleague Matteo Leonardis, that the most important concept in Popper is the concept of a **problem**. Once you understand what Popper means by a problem, epistemology transforms.

All previous epistemologies assumed knowledge is something we want to *rely on* — hence the search for certainty (God's word, sensory perception, Plato's recollection, Kant's pure reason). But if you think of knowledge growth as beginning with *problems*, you lose the need to justify where knowledge comes from. A problem is there to be solved; the solution is what matters, not its derivation from first principles.

Deutsch characterizes a problem as a **clash between ideas** — not just about empirical phenomena but about anything: morality, mathematics, whatever. Two ideas can't both be true, and the problem consists in recognizing this. Importantly, both clashing ideas could be *false* — you're not choosing the "true" one; you're identifying errors to correct.

Deutsch stresses that clashes between ideas are beneficial even when the parties never agree, because confrontation changes the ideas. "Even if you come out of an argument saying 'I've really showed it to them,' what you mean is you've thought of a new angle on your own view" — an angle you didn't have before the clash. This is a beneficial side effect distinct from resolution.

Naval raises the empiricist objection: observations through a telescope aren't "ideas." Deutsch's response: the Mercury observations were themselves theory-laden. You had Newton's theory, Einstein's theory, theories about the astronomers' accuracy (Eddington's observations were doubted for decades), and subsidiary theories like a hypothesized unseen planet. "The observations were also a theory" — and the clash between all these theories and observations improved them until Einstein's was the only good explanation left.

## Free will and emergence

Deutsch argues that free will is intimately connected with knowledge creation and cannot be dismissed by appeal to physics. The argument against free will — that everything reduces to deterministic particle interactions — commits the same error as saying thermodynamics doesn't exist because individual molecules move reversibly. Irreversibility is real at the macro level despite being invisible at the micro level, and "there's a hard scientific theory of it which if you try to violate you are a crank."

Scientific knowledge exists on **several levels of emergence**. Chemistry is "entirely due to physics," yet the concept of an acid cannot be explained in terms of underlying physics within the computational resources of the universe. Even if you could predict every particle collision leading to a murder, you would still lack the *explanation* — just as tracing every domino in a prime-number-encoding arrangement would predict which domino stands but explain nothing about prime numbers.

Deutsch proposes that the quintessential act of free will is the creation of genuinely new knowledge. When Einstein created general relativity, that theory "had never been implicit anywhere until Einstein created that knowledge out of nothing." It was not latent in the Big Bang, not implicit in Einstein's brain before the creative act. This distinguishes the case where person X intentionally pushes person Y onto the railway tracks from the case where X is themselves pushed by Z — "intuitively we know that two situations are chalk and cheese," and the pivot is whether new knowledge was created.

Free will is not randomness — it is the opposite. Einstein's solution was unpredictable not because it was random but because "it is the solution of that problem." No one without his problem situation could have produced it. The solution creates knowledge — "a real thing which is causal in the environment" — that causes itself to be replicated.

Deutsch warns that denying free will from the perspective of physics is a **category error** with dangerous policy implications. It leads either to the conclusion that criminals bear no responsibility (because atoms determined their actions) or that they are irredeemable (because their atomic arrangement makes them criminals). Both are "policy backed up by rubbish arguments."

## Constructor Theory

Deutsch describes Constructor Theory as an attempt to build a uniform framework for all scientific laws — one that doesn't depend on the prevailing "initial conditions + laws of motion" paradigm.

### The problem with the prevailing framework

The standard view says: specify initial conditions, specify laws of motion, derive everything else. But this framework is time-asymmetric (it privileges the beginning of the universe over the end), and most scientific theories don't fit it. Darwin's theory of evolution doesn't predict elephants; it *explains* them. A supercomputer simulating Africa that prints out a picture of an elephant "hasn't explained anything about what has happened or why." Good explanations should be relatively timeless — they shouldn't depend on a specific moment.

### What Constructor Theory does differently

Instead of asking "what will this system do?", Constructor Theory asks: **what can be done to this system?** It deals with the set of transformations that are possible and impossible.

Crucially, Constructor Theory only considers *complete systems* for any given transformation. A fully charged Tesla rolling at 100 mph is something the Tesla can do. But charging it requires saying what can be done to "a Tesla and some electricity" — the transformation involves both. If a transformation depletes a resource, that resource is part of the system under consideration.

Laws in Constructor Theory take the form of **prohibitions**: certain transformations are impossible no matter what you bring. "A Tesla and *anything* cannot be made to go at 1.1 times the speed of light" — this is the constructor-theoretic statement of the speed-of-light limit, and it captures the intuitive meaning better than the conventional formulation.

### Principles vs. laws

Deutsch distinguishes between **physical principles** and laws. A principle like "nothing travels faster than light" purports to make statements about *laws not yet discovered*. If someone discovers dark matter or a new fundamental particle, the principle says it too cannot exceed the speed of light. Conventional physics struggles to express this transcendent quality — constructor-theoretic formulations capture it naturally.

### Constructor-theoretic thermodynamics

Chiara Marletto has developed a constructor-theoretic version of thermodynamics that explains what it means for the Second Law to hold at a microscopic level — something conventional statistical mechanics cannot do cleanly. Her theory builds on Carathéodory's formulation (which distinguishes adiabatic from non-adiabatic processes) and manages to express the First Law of thermodynamics in terms of information, whereas conventional statistical thermodynamics only achieves this for the Second Law. The Third Law remains unclear to the Constructor Theory group.

Deutsch and Marletto believe all existing laws of physics can either be re-expressed in constructor-theoretic terms or be approximations to theories that can be expressed exactly in those terms.

## Optimism is not inevitability

Deutsch clarifies the relationship between his principle of optimism and the contemporary techno-optimism movement. He is "99% with them" but warns that the flavor of *inevitability* in techno-accelerationism is "very dangerous." Progress is not inevitable — "it causes people to ignore dangers."

"What will happen is up to us. It's not up to law of physics or God." Civilizations can and do fail — optimism didn't prevent the fall of Athens or Florence. Naval observes that inevitability has a paradoxical pessimism built in: "it says that we're just along for the ride, we don't have a choice to make."

Deutsch connects optimism to Constructor Theory directly: if you consider the set of all possible physical transformations, the overwhelming majority can only be brought about by people creating knowledge. Without people, the universe would contain "almost nothing — a few dozen things, different kinds of stars, and that would be that." This means any possible transformation either happens spontaneously (like a star) or requires knowledge — and knowledge is almost always created by people.

## Following the fun

Deutsch argues that "making a better world" and "living a better life" are not two questions but one. The people who most effectively changed the world — Norman Borlaug (the Green Revolution), Michael Faraday (electromagnetic induction), Newton — were not trying to change the world. They were solving the problems that figured large in *their own* minds.

Borlaug's problem was making agriculture more productive. Faraday was solving problems about electricity and magnetism — "he wouldn't have been able to conceive that electrical generators would be a matter of life and death a hundred years later." Newton was "trying to solve the problems that he thought were interesting." In each case, following their own genuine intellectual interest — following the fun — produced world-changing results.

Deutsch warns that it is "dangerous to follow someone else's problem, or a problem that one thinks is important in a sense other than that it figures large in one's own mind." This isn't guaranteed to produce unhappiness, just as doing the right thing isn't guaranteed to produce happiness — "nothing is guaranteed." But if you want to explain how great discoveries actually come about, the mechanism is creative engagement with problems you find genuinely interesting.

## Deutsch's speculative conjectures

When pressed for ideas he'd pursue with unlimited time, Deutsch offers several:

- **AGI as a small program**: he suspects the right program could run on a personal computer today; the barrier is conceptual, not computational. He'd explore this in Mathematica, "throwing creativity at it rather than computer power."
- **Creative music generation**: current AI music imitates existing works. Deutsch wants a program that conjectures what Mozart's *problem situation* was — what he was trying to do — and generates something recognizably Mozartian but recognizably *new*, as Mozart's own next work would have been. "When Newton submitted his solution of the brachistochrone problem anonymously, someone said 'we recognize the lion by his claw' — but the proof itself was entirely new."
- **The history of optimism**: Deutsch suspects that Florence and ancient Athens were "powered by optimism" and that historians, lacking this concept, have overlooked a major explanatory pattern. He conjectures there are many less spectacular cases of optimism-driven progress throughout history.
