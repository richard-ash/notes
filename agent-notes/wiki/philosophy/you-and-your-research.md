# You and Your Research

Richard Hamming's 1986 talk at Bellcore (and the corresponding final chapter of his book *The Art of Doing Science and Engineering*) distills decades of observing first-class scientists at Bell Labs into a practical philosophy for doing significant work. The talk is one of the most widely cited pieces on research productivity and career strategy in science and engineering.

## Core thesis

The essence of the talk is **style** — not what you work on, but *how* you work, choose problems, present results, and manage yourself. Hamming argues that doing great work is largely within individual control, not a matter of luck or raw intelligence, and that most people fail to do significant work not because they lack ability but because they make systematic errors in problem selection, courage, and self-management.

## Key ideas

### Luck vs. preparation

Hamming opens by dismantling the "it's all luck" objection, citing Pasteur's dictum: **"Luck favors the prepared mind."** His main evidence: if luck were dominant, great things would not cluster around the same individuals. Shannon, Einstein, and Newton each produced multiple breakthroughs — they were *prepared* to find what was in the air. Preparation creates the surface area for luck to land on.

### Work on important problems

The talk's most famous passage: Hamming would ask colleagues at the Bell Labs lunch tables, "If what you are working on is not important and not likely to lead to important things, then why are you working on it?" Most people, he observes, spend most of their time on problems they themselves believe are unimportant. The remedy is to keep a mental list of 10–20 important unsolved problems and to pounce when a clue appears for attacking one. Crucially, "important" means *tractable* importance — a problem must have a possible angle of attack, not just inherent significance.

### Courage and confidence

Hamming emphasizes courage as a developable trait, not an innate one. He copied Shannon's chess-table bravado ("I ain't scared of nothing") as a deliberate self-management technique. Confidence compounds: looking at your successes — rather than dwelling on failures — builds the momentum to attempt the next hard thing.

### The drunken sailor vs. directed walk

Without a vision of excellence, effort resembles a random walk — expected displacement proportional to the square root of steps taken. With a clear goal, steps accumulate linearly. Over a career, the difference is enormous. This is Hamming's mathematical metaphor for why having a [[nabeel-qureshi-principles|strong set of principles]] and a plan matters more than raw hours worked.

### Intellectual compound interest

Effort compounds. Hamming's boss told him about John Tukey: "You would be surprised how much you would know if you had worked as hard as he has for as many years." One extra hour per day, sustained over a career, more than doubles total output. But the *direction* of effort matters as much as its quantity — hard work on the wrong problem is wasted.

### "Great Thoughts" time

Hamming reserved Friday afternoons for thinking about the big picture: where computing was heading, what Bell Labs' role should be, which problems were becoming ripe. He found this 10% time investment essential for staying on the right problems and avoiding the trap of perpetual detail immersion.

### Open doors vs. closed doors

An empirical observation, not a proven causal claim: researchers who kept their office doors open tended to work on the *right* problems (even if they got less done per day), while closed-door researchers drifted toward slightly wrong problems. Hamming suspects the open door both reflects and reinforces an open mind — exposure to serendipitous inputs keeps your problem selection calibrated.

### Inverting the problem

When constraints seem to block you, Hamming advises reframing the constraint as a feature. His own example: Bell Labs wouldn't give him an "acre of programmers," so he asked, "Why not get the machine to do the details?" — a direct path to the frontier of automatic programming. Constraints, properly inverted, can push you toward more significant discoveries than unconstrained resources would.

### Tolerating ambiguity

Great researchers simultaneously believe their field is the best *and* that it has enormous room for improvement. Too much belief prevents you from seeing opportunities; too little fills you with paralyzing doubt. Hamming admits he has no idea how to teach this, only that great people seem to do it naturally.

### Selling your ideas

Hamming insists that presentation is not optional overhead — it is at least 50% of the job. You must master formal talks, written reports, and spontaneous informal presentations. Many good ideas have been lost because they were poorly presented the first time. "Change does not mean progress, but progress requires change."

### The seven-year rotation

To avoid stagnation and the trap of fame, Hamming recommends shifting your research area roughly every seven years. Shannon, he argues, "ruined himself" by staying on information theory after leaving Bell Labs. When you enter a new subfield, you start as a beginner — which forces you to plant new acorns rather than coasting on old oaks. This echoes the principle of [[nabeel-qureshi-principles|deliberate self-management]].

### Research vs. management

When your vision is something you can execute single-handedly, stay an individual contributor. When your vision of what needs to happen exceeds what one person can do, you must move toward management. Hamming's boss Bode chose to become department head because he had a vision for mathematics at Bell Labs that required organizational power to realize. The key is to choose consciously and not try to do both.

### Surround yourself with great people

Hamming actively sought out stimulating colleagues and deliberately left lunch tables when the best people departed. He distinguishes between "critical mass" collaborators — who echo and amplify ideas — and "sound absorbers" — nice people who merely say "yes" and let ideas die. Seek the former; avoid structuring your time around the latter.

## Temporal notes

The talk was delivered in 1986 at Bellcore; the version compiled here is from Hamming's later book, which expanded the original talk into a full chapter. Hamming's anecdotes about Bell Labs describe the institution roughly from the late 1940s through the early 1980s — a period of unusual institutional freedom and concentration of talent that has few modern parallels. His claim that UNIX arose because researchers were given a *small* machine is a charming simplification of a more complex history, though it captures the spirit of constraint-driven innovation.

The advice on reading — "read to find the problems, not the solutions" — has become more relevant, not less, in the age of instant access to every paper. His warning about knowledge doubling every 17 years now feels conservative; the core insight that specialization alone cannot cope with exponential knowledge growth anticipates modern concerns about AI as a knowledge-management tool.
