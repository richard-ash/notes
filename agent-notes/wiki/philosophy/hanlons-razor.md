# Hanlon's Razor

*"Never attribute to malice that which can be adequately explained by neglect."*

Hanlon's razor is a philosophical heuristic — a mental shortcut for interpreting others' behavior. Like [[occams-razor|Occam's razor]] (which favors the simplest explanation), Hanlon's razor favors the most charitable one: before concluding someone acted with ill intent, first consider whether incompetence, ignorance, or simple oversight explains the outcome equally well.

## The core idea

People make mistakes constantly. They forget things, speak carelessly, prioritize short-term wins, act on incomplete information, and fall prey to cognitive biases. From the outside, these behaviors can look like deliberate attacks — but the mundane explanation is almost always correct.

The razor's power is behavioral, not just epistemic: when you assume stupidity rather than malice, you respond with empathy and problem-solving instead of defensiveness and retaliation. This is what makes it practically useful in relationships, management, and public life.

## Origins

The principle predates its name by centuries:

- **Goethe** (1774) in *The Sorrows of Young Werther*: "Misunderstandings and neglect create more confusion in this world than trickery and malice. At any rate, the last two are certainly much less frequent."
- **Napoleon Bonaparte**: "Never ascribe to malice that which is adequately explained by incompetence."
- **Robert J. Hanlon** coined the modern phrasing for a 1980 compilation of Murphy's Law variants.

## Interactions with cognitive biases

Parrish identifies several biases that make Hanlon's razor hard to apply consistently — biases that Munger would recognize from his [[psychology-of-human-misjudgment|catalog of human misjudgment]]:

- **Availability heuristic** — vivid memories of past wrongs inflate our perception of how often we're being wronged, making malice feel more plausible than it is.
- **Confirmation bias** — once we suspect malice, we selectively notice evidence that confirms it and ignore evidence of simple error.
- **Disliking/hating bias** — the more we dislike someone, the more likely we are to interpret their mistakes as intentional.
- **Projection** — we attribute our own flaws to others to protect our self-image, making us see aggression where there is only clumsiness.

These biases compound: a single bad experience (availability) with someone you already dislike (hate bias) becomes "proof" of a pattern (confirmation bias) that says more about your psychology than their intent.

## Hubbard's extension

Douglas Hubbard offers a more systemic corollary: *"Never attribute to malice or stupidity that which can be explained by moderately rational individuals following incentives in a complex system of interactions."* This is valuable because many outcomes that look like conspiracy or malice are simply emergent effects of decentralized agents acting in self-interest — no coordination required. This connects Hanlon's razor to [[systems-thinking|systems thinking]]: the system, not the individual, is often the right unit of analysis.

## Limits and the "forgive everything except" doctrine

Hanlon's razor is a default, not a universal law. Genuine malice exists, and the razor can become a vulnerability if applied reflexively — it can blind you to patterns of deliberate harm.

Parrish's position: the default should be charitable interpretation and forgiveness. But once malice is confirmed — with actual evidence, not pattern-matching from bias — respond decisively. The asymmetry is intentional: defaulting to charity costs little when you're wrong (you were too kind), while defaulting to suspicion when you're wrong is corrosive.

This resonates with the game-theoretic insight from tit-for-tat strategies: start cooperative, retaliate only when defection is confirmed, and return to cooperation when the other party does.
