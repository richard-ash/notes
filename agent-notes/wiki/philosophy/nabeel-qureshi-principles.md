# Nabeel Qureshi's Principles

A collection of 63 hard-won life principles from Nabeel Qureshi — researcher, writer, and builder. Originally compiled as a private Google Doc, later published on Substack. The principles span agency, epistemology, creative work, career strategy, and personal philosophy. Confidence is `medium` because these are one person's opinionated life lessons, not empirically verified claims.

## Agency and action

Qureshi's strongest throughline is radical bias toward action:

- **Speed as strategy.** "Slow is fake" — most delays come from procrastination, not actual work time. Moving fast also forces you to strip ideas to essentials (#6, #7). This echoes the [[surprising-detail|"reality has surprising detail"]] observation: engaging quickly with reality reveals what armchair planning misses.
- **Make reality say no.** Once you're comfortable hearing "no," you can ask for anything (#2). The corollary: stop seeking approval — plant the "this is happening" flag and people follow (#46).
- **Multiple bets with high execution.** Any single bet is likely to fail; success comes from volume × quality of execution. Fast cycle time is key (#52). This is a direct application of power law thinking (#45).
- **Zero-output days are the killers.** Daily progress on your primary focus, first thing, no exceptions — attributed to Tyler Cowen (#12).

## Epistemology and thinking

Several principles address how to form and test beliefs:

- **Fallibilism as method.** Form opinions, then seek the strongest critique. If you really can't disprove something, it has a chance of being right (#25, #26).
- **Empiricism over a priori reasoning.** Qureshi warns against confident deductive arguments about empirical matters (e.g., "AI will certainly kill everyone"), citing Knightian uncertainty. Reality is endlessly surprising (#58).
- **Think in writing.** Externalizing thought extends working memory and lets ideas compound. "The difference between a Turing machine and a finite state machine is the tape" (#53).
- **Seek uncorrelated inputs.** Original thinking requires reading minor works, older things, obscure journals — not just what's trending (#63).
- **Distinguish learning from entertainment.** Real learning is effortful; podcasts and pop science books are closer to entertainment (#51).

## Creative work and craft

- **Fun is the root.** The best creative work comes from joy and excitement, not grim obligation (#3).
- **Separate creator from editor.** When writing, first draft freely without quality concerns; edit later (#50).
- **"Aim for Chartres."** Borrowed from Christopher Alexander: aim to be the best there ever was, because your natural bias is toward mediocrity (#34).
- **Production over consumption.** Monitor your production/consumption balance; if you're only consuming, fix it (#13). Scrolling drowns out your inner voice (#57).

## Environment and relationships

- **Move to where the action is.** Agglomeration effects are powerful — geographic clustering matters for opportunity (#4, #38).
- **Make friends, not a network.** Writing online brings interesting people to you. A wide circle of genuine friends creates more opportunities than transactional networking (#48).
- **Seek energizing people.** Some rare people leave you wanting to conquer the world after talking to them; find them (#37).
- **Work with people who intimidate you.** The best collaborators are the ones you secretly worry are much better than you (#33).

## Ethics and values

- **High integrity, always.** Even when it costs you, shortcuts aren't worth it (#11).
- **Moral portfolio:** Qureshi suggests an 80/15/5 split — utilitarian, deontologist, virtue ethicist (#55). This is a pragmatic acknowledgment that no single ethical framework covers every situation.
- **Be careful rationalizing.** If something doesn't feel right, the feeling may be wiser than the argument (#29).
- **Status is fake.** Focus on substance and doing valuable work; beware the inner ring fallacy (#39).

## Risk and luck

- **You're probably too risk-averse.** Write out worst-case scenarios; they're usually not that bad (#21).
- **Luck has surface area.** It increases with exposure: conversations, being in the right places, asking for what you want (#61).
- **Cold emails work.** People respond more than you'd expect. Assume everyone's your friend (#35).

## Implications

Qureshi's principles form a coherent philosophy: lean into your idiosyncrasies, move fast, produce more than you consume, test your beliefs empirically, aim impossibly high, and expand your surface area for luck. The emphasis on speed and volume-of-bets aligns with a power-law worldview where most value concentrates in outlier outcomes.

Notable tension: the bias toward speed (#6, #7) can conflict with the call for deep, effortful learning (#51) and first-principles reasoning (#54). Qureshi doesn't resolve this explicitly, but the implied answer is fast *execution* paired with slow *thinking* — separate the two modes, much like separating creator and editor.
