# Circle of Competence

The circle of competence is a mental model popularized by Warren Buffett and Charlie Munger that describes the boundary between what you genuinely understand through earned knowledge and what lies beyond your expertise. The core insight is deceptively simple: know where your edge is, operate inside it, and never confuse confidence for competence.

## The model

Everyone has domains where accumulated experience gives them an informational advantage — where they can see patterns, spot risks, and make judgments faster and more accurately than outsiders. This is the interior of the circle. The perimeter is where that advantage fades. Beyond the perimeter, you're playing someone else's game.

Three key dynamics:

1. **Confidence decays slower than competence.** As you cross your perimeter, you don't immediately feel less capable — you just become less capable. The most dangerous zone is just outside the boundary, where you still feel expert but aren't.

2. **Extreme skill in one area often creates blind spots elsewhere.** Parrish notes that a chess Grandmaster's board mastery doesn't transfer to cooking. Specialization is a trade-off, not a universal multiplier.

3. **The perimeter matters more than the area.** Munger and Buffett emphasize that a small circle operated within is vastly more valuable than a large circle whose edges you can't identify. "It's better to have an IQ of 160 and think it's 150 than an IQ of 160 and think it's 200."

## Practical application

Buffett illustrates the model with Rose Blumkin (Mrs. B), who built Nebraska Furniture Mart into a powerhouse despite limited English and no formal education. She understood furniture, carpet, cash, and real estate — and categorically refused to touch stocks. Her narrowness was her strength: it produced focus and eliminated the risk of overreach.

Munger generalizes the principle beyond investing: figure out your aptitudes, accept that some games are unwinnable for you, and build mastery in an attainable domain. His example — becoming the best plumbing contractor in Bemidji — illustrates that circle of competence doesn't require genius, just disciplined focus and honest self-assessment.

## Expanding the circle

The model isn't static. Parrish argues you should work to expand your circle over time — but with honest awareness of where the current perimeter lies. The discipline is in the expansion being genuine (deep learning, not surface familiarity) and in never mistaking the aspiration for the reality.
