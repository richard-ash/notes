# Consumption as identity

The argument that personal identity is shaped more by what we *consume* than by what we *produce* — and that the conventional cultural emphasis has it backwards.

## The inversion

Noah Smith argues that culture across the political spectrum valorizes production as the source of identity: Americans introduce themselves by occupation, narratives center on careers, and words like "lazy" and "shiftless" stigmatize consumption. Left and right rarely agree, but both treat production as virtuous and consumption as suspect — leftists decry "consumer society," conservatives praise hard work, and the degrowth movement frames overconsumption as moral failure.

Smith's counter: consumption is actually more *individuating* than production. The asymmetry comes down to whose preferences drive each activity:

- **Consumption** asks: "What do *I* want?" Every purchase, hobby, or cultural choice forces self-examination — interrogating your own preferences to decide among alternatives.
- **Production** asks: "What do *other people* want me to do?" Career choices are heavily shaped by market demand. High salaries in law, engineering, and medicine reflect society's preferences, not necessarily the worker's.

This makes production a reflection of collective demand, while consumption reflects individual taste.

## Why society praises production

Smith offers several explanations for the cultural emphasis on work:

1. **Status as wage suppression.** Research shows people accept lower salaries for jobs perceived as socially valuable. Praising production effectively pays workers in status rather than money — which may explain both nonprofit wage gaps and why tech companies frame ad sales as "making the world a better place."
2. **Pre-market cultural inheritance.** Before markets, families and villages had to motivate labor through social pressure and compliments rather than wages. That norm persisted into the market era.
3. **Independence and leverage.** Productive power gives you leverage over society; the more the world needs your output, the less you depend on others' generosity.

## The psychology of choice

Smith cites two studies supporting the individuation thesis:

- **Cheek et al. (2022)** found that choosing from larger assortments made people feel their choices were more self-expressive — regardless of whether larger sets actually enabled better preference satisfaction.
- **Nanakdewa et al. (2021)** found that merely framing actions as "choices" made people feel larger, stronger, more independent, and more empowered to voice opinions.

The implication: consumer choice develops a *habit* of self-examination that extends to more complex life decisions — relationships, self-expression, personality. Economists would classify social interaction, romance, and self-expression as forms of non-market consumption.

## Creative work as bundled consumption

Smith handles the apparent counterexample of artists and writers with an elegant reframe: self-expressive jobs are bundles of production and consumption. The income an artist *forgoes* by not "selling out" (optimizing for audience demand) represents the consumption component — the premium paid for self-expression. Artists who do sell out tend to enjoy their craft less, which is exactly what the model predicts.

## AI and the meaning crisis

The essay's motivating context is AI's disruption of career-based identity. Smith agrees with the widespread concern that AI will trigger a crisis of meaning — an economist whose algebra is now automated, a software engineer whose job becomes reviewing Claude's output, may lose the pride that came from rare skills even if income is preserved.

But Smith argues this crisis is *optional*. If identity can shift from production to consumption, meaning survives the automation of work. His model for this is **college** — a period where identity and social position are defined by consumption (music taste, friendships, self-expression, intellectual exploration for its own sake) rather than production.

The utopian vision: AI abundance + redistribution could extend the "college experience" — self-discovery, leisure, intellectual play — to everyone, permanently. Smith frames this not as empty hedonism but as "technology freeing us to become more like ourselves." He acknowledges this outcome isn't inevitable but argues it's being ignored in most discussions of AI's future.

## Connections

The consumption-identity thesis interacts with several adjacent ideas:

- [[ai-and-the-future-of-work]] discusses how career identity adapts when AI reshapes task bundles — Smith's argument offers a more radical resolution: *stop anchoring identity to work at all*.
- [[ai-judgment-atrophy]] raises an interesting tension: if consumption involves self-examination through friction (choosing, evaluating, developing taste), and AI removes friction from decision-making, does AI-assisted consumption still individuate? Smith doesn't address this.
- The World Values Survey data Smith cites — that richer countries value self-expression more — connects consumption-identity to broader theories linking affluence and individualism (Greenfield's research on cultural change).
