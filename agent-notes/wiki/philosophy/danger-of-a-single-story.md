# The Danger of a Single Story

Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie's 2009 TED talk argues that when we hear only one narrative about a person, group, or place, that narrative becomes a flattening lens — not because it's false, but because it is incomplete. "The single story creates stereotypes, and the problem with stereotypes is not that they are untrue, but that they are incomplete. They make one story become the only story."

The talk is structured as a series of personal anecdotes that demonstrate the mechanism from multiple angles — as both victim and perpetrator — before distilling the underlying theory about narrative, power, and dignity.

## The mechanism: how single stories form

Adichie identifies a simple recipe: **show a people as one thing, as only one thing, over and over again, and that is what they become.** The mechanism operates through narrative saturation — when all available stories about a subject converge on a single theme, the listener loses the capacity to imagine alternatives.

She illustrates this with three autobiographical examples:

1. **Fide's family.** As a child in Nigeria, Adichie's mother described their house boy's family only as "very poor." When Adichie visited their village and saw a beautifully crafted raffia basket, she was startled — "it had not occurred to me that anybody in his family could actually make something." Poverty had become her single story of them.

2. **The American roommate.** At university in the U.S., her roommate assumed Adichie couldn't use a stove, asked to hear her "tribal music," and was disappointed when Adichie produced a Mariah Carey tape. The roommate's default position was "a kind of patronizing, well-meaning pity" — the only story she had of Africa was catastrophe.

3. **Adichie's own single story of Mexico.** After absorbing American media coverage of immigration, Adichie visited Guadalajara and was overwhelmed with shame at her own surprise that Mexicans were ordinary people — "rolling up tortillas in the marketplace, smoking, laughing." She had reduced an entire nation to "the abject immigrant."

The symmetry matters: Adichie is not pointing fingers but demonstrating that the single story is a universal cognitive failure. Everyone is susceptible, including the person making the argument.

## The role of power

Adichie introduces the Igbo concept of *nkali* — loosely, "to be greater than another" — to explain why certain single stories dominate while others don't take hold. Narrative asymmetry tracks power asymmetry:

- **Who tells stories** is determined by economic and cultural power. America's cultural dominance means the world has "many stories" of America, making it hard to reduce to a single narrative. Africa, lacking equivalent cultural exports, is easily flattened.
- **Where you start the story** determines its meaning. The Palestinian poet Mourid Barghouti's insight: "if you want to dispossess a people, the simplest way to do it is to tell their story and to start with, 'secondly.'" Start with the arrows of the Native Americans rather than the arrival of the British; start with the failure of the African state rather than its colonial creation — each produces a completely different understanding.
- **Power determines whether fiction becomes metonym.** When a student told Adichie it was "such a shame that Nigerian men were physical abusers" based on a character in her novel, she replied that she'd just read *American Psycho* — was it "such a shame that young Americans were serial murderers"? The absurdity of the second claim but plausibility of the first reveals the asymmetry: cultural power grants immunity from synecdoche.

## The antidote: a "balance of stories"

Adichie borrows Chinua Achebe's phrase — "a balance of stories" — as the remedy. The prescription is not to replace one story with another but to multiply the number of stories available. She demonstrates this with a cascade of "what if my roommate knew..." examples: Nigerian publishers, TV hosts, lawyers challenging discriminatory laws, Nollywood filmmakers, ambitious hair braiders — none of which contradict the stories of poverty and dysfunction, but which make it impossible to reduce Nigeria to any single one.

The closing thesis: "When we reject the single story, when we realize that there is never a single story about any place, we regain a kind of paradise."

## Connections to other frameworks

The single story operates through several mechanisms cataloged elsewhere in this wiki:

- **[[mental-models]]**: The latticework principle argues that a person with only one model will misapply it everywhere ("to a man with only a hammer, every problem looks like a nail"). Adichie's argument is the narrative equivalent — a person with only one story about a group will see only that story confirmed. The antidote in both cases is multiplicity.
- **[[psychology-of-human-misjudgment]]**: Several of Munger's twenty-five tendencies are at work in single-story formation — particularly *first-conclusion bias* (the first narrative you hear about a group anchors all subsequent perception), *social proof* (when everyone around you tells the same story, it feels unquestionable), and *disliking/hating tendency* (a single negative story triggers a cascade of further negative attribution).
- **[[multidisciplinary-approach-to-thinking]]**: Kaufman's "three buckets" method validates principles by checking them against multiple independent domains. Adichie's argument is structurally identical: understanding a person or place requires checking against multiple independent stories. Both reject single-source conclusions.
- **[[solitude-and-leadership]]**: Deresiewicz argues that independent thought requires solitude — the ability to step outside the received consensus. Adichie's single story is what happens when an entire culture lacks that solitude: the consensus narrative goes unquestioned because no one pauses to ask whether there might be other stories.
