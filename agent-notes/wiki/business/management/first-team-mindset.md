# First Team Mindset

The First Team mindset is a leadership principle: leaders should prioritize supporting their peer group of fellow leaders over supporting their direct reports. The concept, popularized by Patrick Lencioni and practiced here by engineering leader Jason Wong, reframes a manager's "team" — your first team is not the people who report to you, but the people you report alongside.

Wong argues this is transformational because it shifts leaders from faction-guarding to collaborative problem-solving. When a leadership group trusts each other, change management, vulnerability, and cross-team decision-making become dramatically easier.

## Why it's counterintuitive

Most new managers instinctively identify "my team" as their direct reports. The First Team mindset requires a deliberate reorientation: your peer leaders are your primary obligation, and serving them well is how you ultimately serve your reports better. Wong notes he took time to come around to this himself.

The underlying logic: if the leadership layer is fractured — each leader optimizing for their silo — the whole organization suffers from coordination failures that trickle down to every team. A unified leadership cohort makes better decisions, which benefits everyone downstream.

## Building the mindset in practice

Wong outlines four mechanisms:

### 1. Be proactive and explicit

State the expectation directly. Wong writes it into job descriptions, interviews for it, discusses it during IC-to-manager transitions, and encodes it in the career ladder. The principle: leave as little to interpretation as possible. If it's important enough to expect, it's important enough to write down.

This echoes [[how-to-operate|Rabois's insistence]] on making implicit expectations explicit — editing for clarity rather than hoping people infer what you want.

### 2. Treat leaders like a cohort

If you don't treat your leadership team as a team, they won't become one. Wong gives them the same team-building infrastructure as any other team: shared channels, mailing lists, social events, regular meetings. "Information and trust are the currencies of leadership, and demonstrating an equal distribution of them through shared experiences is a powerful tool."

### 3. Normalize interdependence

Actively refer managers to each other. When one manager faces a difficult conversation, pair them with a peer to role-play it. When someone has a problem another has solved before, send them to that person. Wong also recommends *Manager Roundtables* (from Lara Hogan / Where Withall) — facilitated sessions where managers share and solve problems collaboratively. He prefers a third-party facilitator to avoid power dynamics.

This connects to the broader theme in [[its-not-magic|Amaechi's leadership philosophy]] — genuine care for the people around you, expressed through deliberate rituals and structures rather than left to chance.

### 4. Invite leaders into your problems

Show vulnerability by asking your leadership team to help solve *your* challenges. Wong has his managers co-develop the organization's VMSO (Vision, Mission, Strategy, Objectives), participate in compensation calibration, and shape reorgs. Benefits:

- **Better outcomes** — more perspectives on hard problems
- **Development opportunity** — managers get exposure to next-level challenges
- **Buy-in** — people who shaped a reorg are more likely to execute it

Wong's most striking exercise: asking managers to develop a VMSO *for another manager's team*. This forces cross-team understanding and breaks silo thinking.

## Compensation calibration as a test case

Wong specifically calls out compensation and promotion calibration as a place where First Team mindset matters most. With a finite pool of money, leaders without peer trust devolve into "bickering and taker behaviors." A First Team approach means managers advocate fairly, get different perspectives on their reports, and build confidence that decisions are equitable. (See also Lara Hogan's work on inclusion math for the mechanics.)

## Connection to [[manager-anti-patterns|manager failure modes]]

Several of Larson's anti-patterns — particularly "only managing down" and failing to build peer relationships — are precisely what First Team mindset is designed to prevent. A manager stuck in their silo, over-identifying with their team's interests against the broader organization, is exhibiting the faction behavior Wong describes in his opening.

## Temporal notes

The article is from 2018. The concept has only gained traction since; Lencioni's *The Five Dysfunctions of a Team* (which introduced "First Team") remains widely referenced in management literature. Manager Roundtables, which Wong recommends, are now a well-established practice in engineering management circles.
