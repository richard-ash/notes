# Founder mode in investment firms

The "founder mode" discourse from tech (Brian Chesky / Paul Graham) is usually treated as a thesis about product companies — keep the founder close to the work, resist manager-mode delegation that hollows out culture. Gaurav Kapadia argues the frame applies just as much to investment organizations, and that the absence of founder mode is the reason most of his industry is structurally mediocre.

## The core claim

There is, Kapadia says, "something in the water" that makes people assume investing firms aren't entrepreneurial. The reality is the opposite — the *best* investment organizations (Sequoia, Andreessen Horowitz, Blackstone are his examples) have enormous entrepreneurial energy. But because barriers to entry in investing are low, the mediocre majority can survive without it. The result is a long tail of firms that won't innovate, won't adopt new tooling, and won't sustain the obsession needed to be the best at anything.

The transferable framing: founder mode isn't about product, it's about an organizational refusal to let the top get lazy.

## "Keep the rope tight"

Kapadia's cultural mantra for XN is "rigor and kindness," and the operational discipline he stresses is keeping the rope tight at the top. The mechanism he describes is direct:

> "If I ever slow down, everyone will see it. The number one thing I do is surround myself with people who will tell me the truth to make sure that if I am slowing down, they will say, 'Hey, you have to remember the commitment we all made to each other.'"

This is [[truth-telling-leadership|truth-telling leadership]] reframed as an anti-decay mechanism specifically for the founder seat. The downstream argument is that lazy-top cultures don't stay localized — they propagate through the whole org and seep into anything that's a "professional services" shape (law firms, investment firms, consultancies).

## Hiring: "good enough plus something special"

Kapadia attributes the framing to chef David Chang (Momofuku):

- **Good enough** — the standard credentials. 4.0 from Wharton or Harvard, time at Blackstone, technically capable. This is the table-stakes that "the average investment firm" treats as the *whole* bar.
- **Something special on top** — the extra spark, extra gear, extra curiosity, extra ingenuity that the credentialist filter actively selects against.

The "fine" candidate XN's industry hires by default has all of (1) and none of (2), which produces an org full of competent, undifferentiated talent that won't generate the kind of [[concentrated-investing-philosophy|concentrated, asymmetric ideas]] the strategy needs. This shares lineage with [[hiring-for-curiosity]] and the broader [[deliberate-want]] frame — knowing what specifically you're trying to acquire beyond raw quality.

The "extra spark" test is applied at *every position*, not just the investment seats. XN has ~50 people total, of whom only 10–12 are investment professionals. Kapadia argues most firms make the mistake of lowering the bar for non-investment hires — and that doing so kills the energy and culture they're trying to build. The whole org has to be at the bar; you can't carve out a back office that's allowed to be mediocre.

## The open-plan defense as a connectivity bet

Kapadia's defense of XN's open-plan office isn't ergonomic, it's epistemic: the world is *way* more interconnected than it used to be. His example is the modern AI-infrastructure investment conversation, which requires reasoning about software, power, and semiconductors simultaneously — a conversation that doesn't naturally happen across closed-door offices.

The open plan, in his framing, runs the Socratic method on the organization continuously. Curiosity sparks travel; threads get pulled in real time; analytical silos don't form. This is consistent with the [[multidisciplinary-approach-to-thinking|multidisciplinary]] / connection-density school of thought — the cost of siloed seating compounds over time in a way that's invisible day-to-day but lethal in a world where the durable investment theses cross sector boundaries.

## Founder mode as the AI-adoption test

Kapadia is bullish on AI's impact inside investment organizations but blunt about why most won't capture it. SEC-regulated investment firms carry "a ton of detritus" — compliance, legal, operations, tax — and integrating AI through that requires forcing the change through resistance the org structurally produces. Founder mode is what lets you push through.

He's also openly skeptical of headline-driven AI spending. When Cowen brings up J.P. Morgan's announced $2B AI investment for $2B in savings, Kapadia's reaction is "nothing":

> "A lot of people are in the headline-generating business. As we talked prior, we're in the results-generating business. In periods of technological change, that separates a great deal."

The implication is that most large-bank AI spend is announced faster than it's deployed, because the integration work is genuinely early and the headline economics don't yet exist. Founder-mode firms — XN and "a lot of organizations that have embraced this" — will both move first and avoid the announcement-for-its-own-sake trap.

## What this connects to

The argument runs parallel to [[elon-operating-philosophy|Musk's operating philosophy]] (maniacal urgency, demos over decks, single-metric focus) but applied to a non-product domain. Where Musk's frame is "treat every constraint as removable until proven otherwise," Kapadia's is "treat every easy lapse — easy hires, easy adoption, easy delegation — as the first crack in the culture."

It's also the structural complement to [[concentrated-investing-philosophy]]: a 10–15-position concentrated book *requires* an org that can sustain the curatorial discipline. Without founder mode, the natural drift is toward more positions, more diversification, more "fine" hires — which is why most firms end up running the diversified-mediocrity strategy whether they intended to or not.
