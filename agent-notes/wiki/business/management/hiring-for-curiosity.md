# Hiring for Curiosity

Julian Shapiro argues that curiosity — not passion for the mission, not raw experience — is the trait that most reliably predicts strong employees in early-stage startups and creative roles. The thread is a hiring philosophy, not a methodology: index on a single character trait, then customize the role around what that person wants to learn.

## Why curiosity beats passion

Shapiro's central claim is counter-intuitive: **curiosity outperforms mission-passion as a hiring filter.** His reasoning is that passion fails when the day-to-day grinds. A candidate who joined for the mission can still find themselves "bored out of your mind" once the work becomes routine — at which point they quietly check out and watch others pursue the mission "from afar."

Curiosity, by contrast, is durable because it is *self-directed*. A curious employee doesn't depend on the company's circumstances to stay engaged. They are running their own learning agenda in parallel with the company's work, and the role is the substrate. This produces several second-order effects:

- **Slump resilience.** When the company struggles, curious employees keep showing up because the learning hasn't stopped, even if the stock options are underwater.
- **Compounding growth curve.** Curious people "leapfrog non-curious ones pretty quickly" — they read industry blogs, test new tools, study competitors on their own time, all of which compounds into better judgment and capability.
- **Cultural infection.** Curiosity, Shapiro claims, is contagious. One curious hire raises the ambient level of self-driven learning around them.

The framing aligns with [[scaling-people]]'s argument that management is fundamentally about people decisions: the trait you select for at hiring time is the multiplier on everything else.

## Selfish self-growth as a feature, not a bug

The most heterodox move in the thread is Shapiro's explicit endorsement of employees treating their job "selfishly" as a vehicle for personal mastery. He frames this as alignment, not extraction: getting "2-4 years of a curious person's most enthusiastic effort is a huge win" and "they don't owe you their lifelong journey."

This is a pragmatic answer to retention anxiety. Most employers act as if every hire should ideally stay forever, and design incentives accordingly. Shapiro inverts: assume they'll leave, optimize for the intensity of the years you have. If they leave to start their own (non-competing) company, that's a clean exit and a future ally — not a betrayal.

The corollary is a hiring tactic: **customize the role to the candidate's curiosity.** Shapiro asks what skills they want to develop and reshapes responsibilities around that. Examples from the thread:
- Candidate wants senior responsibilities → mix in senior duties they can test
- Candidate wants to break into startups from corporate → hire them despite the experience mismatch
- Candidate wants to master a startup discipline (product, growth) → carve out ~⅓ of their time for it

This makes the role compelling to candidates a "standard" job posting could never attract — a flavor of [[deliberate-want]] applied at the role-design layer rather than the communication layer.

## Two interview questions

To assess curiosity, Shapiro proposes two questions:

1. **"In your current job, what have you done to further your skills?"** — looking for proactive work, not curriculum-style training the company assigned them.
2. **"What are the interesting tools you've been playing with to make your job easier?"** — curious people tinker constantly; uncurious ones don't have an answer.

Both questions test for *behavior*, not stated values. Anyone can claim to be curious; only curious people have a stack of recent examples.

## When this philosophy doesn't apply

Shapiro is careful to bound the claim. Curiosity-first hiring works for early software startups and creative roles, where the work is novel and the multiplier on self-directed learning is enormous. It does *not* apply to roles where reliability under known conditions matters more than learning velocity — his example is a pilot, where you want the most experienced person, not the most curious. Same logic would apply to surgeons, regulated-compliance roles, and most operational positions where the work is well-understood and the cost of error is high.

His underlying argument for *why* it fits startups: "startups and creativity are driven by innovation, experimentation, and resourcefulness, and that requires a team of curious people who are sponges. Curiosity compels mastery." The trait predicts performance specifically in regimes where the right answer isn't known in advance and has to be discovered by people who keep poking at things.

## A more general principle: curiosity before critique

Shapiro closes the thread with a tangent he frames as "related": many relationship troubles emerge from assuming bad intent, and one of the best things to teach children is to "lead with curiosity before critique" — to ask questions before making assumptions.

This generalizes the hiring argument into a broader posture. The hiring framing says: when you don't know what someone will turn into, bet on their drive to find out. The relational framing says: when you don't know why someone did what they did, ask before you judge. Both are bets that **inquiry beats prediction** under uncertainty — a stance that recurs in [[truth-telling-leadership]]'s argument for honest dialogue over comfortable narratives, and contrasts with hiring philosophies that try to credential-screen for outcomes.

## Implications

The thread is short on methodology but suggests a posture: hire fewer, hire weirder, redesign roles around the person you want. This trades short-term predictability (the role you posted is not the role they'll do) for long-term capability density. It works best when the manager has the autonomy to reshape work — which is itself a feature of small companies and a reason the philosophy doesn't transplant cleanly into large orgs with rigid job ladders.
