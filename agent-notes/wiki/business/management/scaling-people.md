# Scaling People (Psmith review)

*Claire Hughes Johnson, Stripe Press, 2023.* This article summarizes John Psmith's August 2023 critical review. It is *about* the book as read through Psmith's lens — the review is sharply opinionated, and the arguments below are the reviewer's, not a consensus view of the book.

## The book's framing

Johnson, former COO of Stripe, pitches the book in the introduction as "management turpentine" — practical, repeatable techniques for organizing and motivating people, independent of what the team is actually doing. The turpentine metaphor is deliberate: sourcing turpentine is identical whether you paint cats or boats.

## The central critique: "management turpentine" is a myth

Psmith's core argument: the turpentine framing conceals a third possibility that Johnson leaves unspoken — a book about *how to make great art*. No such book can exist; you cannot learn to paint masterpieces from a book. Psmith argues the same is true of management.

**Managing a team requires deep domain knowledge of that team's work.** Two concrete arguments:

1. **Evaluation.** When a subordinate claims a task is harder than expected, a manager who can't do the job themselves has no way to assess that claim.
2. **Morale and dominance display.** Subordinates need to see superiors who can do the work. Chefs take over a line cook's station when performance slips — a practical act (relieves a bottleneck) and a symbolic one (demonstrates earned authority). Engineering managers at good tech companies are former engineers, often the best. "Nothing motivates a soldier like seeing his commander leading the charge, weapon in hand."

Psmith traces the domain-agnostic-management ideology to Frederick Taylor's late-19th-century "scientific management" — the conceptual breakthrough that a nail factory and a car factory share the same abstract laws (time and motion, concurrency, bottlenecks), and a master of those laws could optimize either without knowing anything about nails or cars.

## Even "pure people management" isn't generic

If we grant that managing *computer programmers* is a narrower skill than managing everyone, are even those techniques generic? Psmith draws an analogy to parenting: every parent of more than one child knows that techniques that work for one kid fail for another. One child needs discipline, another encouragement; one needs reassurance, another boundaries. The same is true of managing adults, who additionally carry temperaments, life histories, and scar tissue.

Any generic advice needs heavy per-person customization — **and mastering that customization *is* management itself.** You can't learn it from a book of generic advice because the thing that needs mastering is the application layer, not the advice.

## Management as process knowledge

Psmith argues that management is a form of **process knowledge** — the kind of knowing that cannot be fully written down. He invokes the Zhuangzi's Wheelwright Pian parable: a wheelwright of 70 years who can feel when to chisel fast or slow but "cannot teach it to my son." Management books capture surface behaviors the way a cargo cult captures the shape of radar dishes — steps without the underlying why.

## Why credentialist civilization resists this

This is the review's sharpest turn. Psmith argues that society resists the idea of unwritable knowledge because it would threaten the legitimacy of the ruling class:

> If management is a domain-agnostic set of skills that can be learned at Harvard, this is extremely convenient for the class of interchangeable, domain-agnostic meritocrats who went to Harvard.

Un-credentialable knowledge would undermine authority that rests on educational credentials. Psmith notes Johnson presents her CV on practically the first page of the book "like a 17th century aristocrat rattling off family titles."

He characterizes meritocrats as selected for agreeableness, conscientiousness, and fluid intelligence — good at following orders and transmitting directives, but weak at autonomously generating objectives. A class well-suited to management-by-PR and poorly-suited to producing things.

## Management vs. leadership

Johnson explicitly distinguishes management from leadership and says the book is about management. Psmith credits her honesty but sharply criticizes her definition of leadership:

> "Leadership is disappointing people at a rate they can absorb."

Psmith reads this as: **lie to people or conceal the truth if you don't think they can handle it.** He calls this a fundamental tenet of the ruling ideology — that leaders' job is to manipulate subordinates (for their own good). He links this to U.S. public health messaging during COVID and to Western framing of the Ukraine war: strategies of perception-management substituting for engagement with physical reality.

## Counter-thesis: leadership is genuine caring

Psmith's own definition, offered as contrast:

> The greatest leadership superpower is actually caring about your subordinates.

Not performing care — *genuinely* caring. Human bullshit detectors are finely tuned, especially for people in power. Acting-like-you-care fails; caring works.

His model is **Napoleon's obsession with shoes**: the published correspondence with his generals returns repeatedly to the logistics of foot coverings for infantry. Psmith reads this alongside Napoleon remembering individual soldiers' names years later, sharing food and wine with sentries, and weeping after battles — as evidence of genuine concern for subordinates' lives.

Psmith argues that meritocracy strips leaders of structural reasons to care:
- **Aristocratic civilizations** had *noblesse oblige*.
- **Christian civilizations** had the theology of the servant-lord.
- **Credentialed meritocracy** gives none — in fact produces structural contempt ("don't these lazy idiots know I went to Harvard?").

## Illustrations and tangents

The review extends the "perception management" critique with two contemporary examples:

- **U.S. COVID response:** consistent science underneath, policy reversals driven by messaging objectives rather than epidemiology — masks, vaccines, and public gatherings toggling between mandatory and forbidden based on political convenience.
- **Ukraine war (as of 2023):** disproportionate focus on cinematic operations and memeable moments, hand-wringing over the *appearance* of failure rather than actual military progress.

Both are framed as the same class of credentialed meritocrats applying their core competency (verbal/suasion) to problems where physical reality dominates.
