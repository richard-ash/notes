# Manager Anti-Patterns

A taxonomy of ways engineering managers get stuck, organized by experience level. Will Larson wrote this as a companion piece to Camille Fournier's "How do Individual Contributors Get Stuck?" — extending the same diagnostic framework to the management track.

The piece is notable for how several anti-patterns exist in tension with each other: hiring solves problems *and* creates them; relationships are essential *and* can become a time sink; delegation is critical *and* can become abdication. Larson doesn't resolve these tensions — the point is that management is navigating contradictions, not following rules.

## New managers (first couple of years)

**Directional imbalances:**
- **Only managing down** — building what the team wants rather than what the company or customers need. The team is happy but disconnected from business value.
- **Only managing up** — chasing management's wishes until the team evaporates. Larson quotes Pearl Buck: "All power comes from the Earth." In management, all power comes from having a functioning team.
- **Never managing up** — excellent work goes unrecognized because it was never communicated upward. Team success depends significantly on the manager's relationship with the chain above them.

**Scope and relationship failures:**
- **Optimizing locally** — choosing technologies the company can't support, or building products that compete with sibling teams.
- **Not building relationships** — a team's impact depends on alignment with what other teams or customers want. Hard to ship without a supportive internal network.
- **Defining the role too narrowly** — effective managers become glue, filling gaps even when the work isn't glamorous. See also [[how-to-operate|Rabois's framework]], where the CEO sometimes acts as a "$10/hour TaskRabbit" removing friction.
- **Neglecting hiring** — firefighting crowds out recruiting, but in a growing business you eventually hire or burn out.
- **Forgetting your manager is human** — they'll commit your team to things without consulting you, forget to share information, and put you in awkward spots. Almost always with good intentions. The relationship requires room for mistakes on both sides.

## Experienced managers

- **Importing the previous company's playbook** — arriving at a new role and "fixing" everything before understanding what's actually broken. The tools and problems may be entirely different.
- **Over-investing in relationships** — common when moving from a large company to a smaller one, where the expectation shifts toward execution. Creates a perception of not contributing tangible value.
- **Believing hiring solves everything** — the inverse of the new-manager mistake. Over-hiring dilutes culture and creates unclear roles. This mirrors [[how-to-operate|Rabois's "barrels and ammunition" concept]]: headcount doesn't linearly increase throughput.
- **Absconding vs. delegating** — delegation means staying connected to what you've handed off. Abdication means ignoring it until disaster arrives. See also [[scaling-people|Psmith's argument]] that managing requires domain knowledge — you can't monitor what you've delegated if you don't understand the work.
- **Disconnecting from ground truth** — at larger companies, decisions increasingly appear to float free of physical and organizational reality.

## Universal (all experience levels)

**Identity traps:**
- **Mistaking team size for impact** — a larger team is a *different* job, not a better one. Larson argues this fixation is worth actively unlearning.
- **Mistaking title for impact** — titles are context-dependent social constructs that don't translate across companies. Letting them become the goal distorts priorities.

**Power and control failures:**
- **Confusing authority with truth** — authority lets you push weak arguments through, but the cost is high: people disengage and follow orders passively (or leave). Related to [[scaling-people|Psmith's critique of "management turpentine"]] — authority without domain credibility corrodes trust.
- **Not trusting enough to delegate** — bottlenecking on approvals is a proxy for insufficient trust. Scaling requires letting people do things differently than you would.

**Attention failures:**
- **Letting others manage your time** — the amount of work available will always exceed capacity. Default to proactive prioritization rather than reactive calendar-filling.
- **Only seeing the problems** — forgetting to celebrate successes leads to frustration and burnout. A purely problem-focused lens is corrosive over time.

## Temporal notes

Written in 2017 at a time when Larson was blogging regularly on engineering management (the site later became the basis for his book *An Elegant Puzzle*). The anti-patterns remain highly relevant — they describe structural tensions in the management role rather than anything time-bound.
