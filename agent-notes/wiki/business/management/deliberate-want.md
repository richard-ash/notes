# Deliberate Want

A hiring strategy built on two core principles: (1) treat open requisitions as perishable assets and move with urgency, and (2) consistently, explicitly communicate to candidates that they are wanted — from first contact through their first day in the building.

## The requisition problem

Rands frames hiring urgency around a simple observation: **reqs vanish randomly, often without notice, without reason, and at the least convenient time.** Budget freezes, reorgs, a boss who "borrows" your headcount for a candidate who's ready right now — any of these can eliminate an approved req before you fill it. His estimate: only about 50% of approved reqs he's managed in his career actually resulted in a hire.

The implication: from the moment a req exists, a manager should spend an hour a day working to fill it. Not because the work is that voluminous, but because every day the req sits open is a day someone can take it away. The industry average for filling a req is 90 days — three months of exposure to random cancellation.

Daily hiring activities Rands recommends:
- Source candidates directly (web, past contacts, network)
- Ping people who turned you down before
- Push your recruiter for pipeline updates
- Revisit the job description to sharpen your picture of who you need

A recruiter is valuable for screening, scheduling, compensation intel, and negotiation leverage — but the manager must own the relationship and the urgency.

## The "deliberate want" strategy

The article's central concept: **candidates who feel genuinely, consistently wanted are far less likely to walk.** This isn't flattery or ego-stroking — it's providing a stable signal amid the psychological chaos of a career change.

Rands argues that job transitions produce intense mental turmoil. Candidates oscillate between excitement about a fresh start and 3am panic about leaving something familiar. In that environment, a hiring manager who maintains a clear, unwavering message — "You are the best person for this gig. We want you" — becomes an anchor.

The strategy as demonstrated by "Michele," a manager in the article:
1. **Before interviews:** Drive to candidates in person, explain the gig, open with "we want you"
2. **After interviews:** Reinforce fit — "See, this is perfect for you"
3. **Offer construction:** Pre-research what matters to each candidate (base salary for one, stock for another) and build offers designed to be accepted, not negotiated
4. **Post-acceptance:** Don't go silent. Introduce the team, plant seeds about future projects, keep reinforcing the message through the gap between acceptance and start date

The post-acceptance period is particularly dangerous — Rands calls it "post-courtship purgatory." Counter-offers appear. Doubt creeps in. The easiest time to lose someone is after they've said yes but before they've started.

## The "Jesse walked" failure mode

The article opens and closes with a cautionary story. Jesse accepted an offer, resigned from his current job, then accepted a last-minute counter-offer at 4:45pm on his final day — without telling the hiring team until he no-showed on his start date Monday morning.

Rands's self-diagnosis: he had delegated too much of the candidate relationship to the recruiter. He didn't do the phone screen, didn't build trust in the interview, didn't pick up on Jesse drifting during offer negotiation. The signals were there; he just wasn't close enough to read them.

The lesson: **until a hire is sitting in the building, badge on belt, you haven't hired anyone.** Verbal acceptance is not a finish line.

## Hire for your career

Rands extends the argument beyond any single hire. The people you bring onto your team will eventually leave and carry your reputation with them. The care you demonstrate during hiring — the deliberate want — is the first chapter of that story. Hiring well isn't just about filling seats; it's about building a network of people who will speak well of working with you for the rest of your career.
