# How to Operate

Keith Rabois's framework for what a startup leader actually does day-to-day, drawn from his experience as VP Product at LinkedIn and COO of Square. Delivered as Lecture 14 of Sam Altman's 2014 Stanford "How to Start a Startup" series. The ideas below are Rabois's; where he attributes them to others (Grove, Thiel, Walsh), that's noted.

## The core metaphor: CEO as editor

Rabois argues the best mental model for a leader's job is **editor, not writer**. Writers (employees) produce most of the output. The editor's role is to:

1. **Simplify.** Strike things out. Distill to one, two, or three priorities. Reject the excuse of complexity — "you can change the world in 140 characters."
2. **Clarify.** Ask questions that resolve ambiguity: "Did you mean X or Y?" "Where's our competitive advantage here?" Every step you eliminate, Andy Grove estimated, improves performance 30-50%.
3. **Allocate resources.** Move people toward what matters, like an editor reassigning reporters from one beat to another. This can be top-down or bottom-up (employees propose initiatives for editorial approval).
4. **Ensure consistent voice.** Everything the company produces — website, PR, packaging, recruiting pages, support — should feel like one person wrote it. The Economist is Rabois's model. Apple under Jobs achieved it externally but not for internal tools. You'll never reach 100%, but track and close the gap.
5. **Measure red ink.** The amount of editing you need to do should decrease over time. If it's increasing, your communication about what matters isn't landing.

## Delegation: neither abdicate nor micro-manage

Both over-delegating and micro-managing are sins. Rabois offers two frameworks for navigating the tension:

**Task-relevant maturity** (from [[scaling-people|Grove's *High Output Management*]]): the more someone has done the exact same task before, the more rope you give them. The radical implication: a good leader has no single management style — you micro-manage the person who's doing something for the first time, and fully delegate to the person with deep relevant experience. Mixed reference checks ("half say micro-manager, half say hands-off") are a feature, not a bug.

**The conviction-consequence matrix** (from Peter Thiel): plot your own conviction level against the consequence of being wrong.

|  | Low consequence | High consequence |
|---|---|---|
| **Low conviction** | Delegate fully — let people learn from mistakes | Discuss, but lean toward delegating |
| **High conviction** | Let it go — learning opportunity | You must intervene, but explain your reasoning |

The critical caveat: when you override, explain the *why*. Rabois recalls a LinkedIn colleague whose response to being overruled was "okay, you're the boss" — and notes this was a signal he was burning social capital he couldn't afford to keep spending.

The Inner-Square example: Square's first marketing hire wanted a program to distribute free readers via existing merchants. Rabois's decade of experience said it wouldn't move metrics. But consequences were low (cheap per unit, on-brand), so he let it run. It didn't scale, but the employee learned to filter future ideas — worth the cost.

## Barrels and ammunition

Rabois argues this is possibly the most important concept in company-building. Adding headcount doesn't linearly increase throughput — sometimes it decreases it. The reason: most people, even great ones, are **ammunition**. The scarce resource is **barrels** — people who can take an idea from inception through shipping and bring others along.

Company velocity = number of barrels. You can only shoot through the barrels you have. An engineer ratio of roughly 1 barrel per 10-20 engineers is about right.

**How to identify barrels:**
- Give small tasks and expand scope until it breaks. The smoothie test: at Square, nobody could reliably get cold smoothies delivered to engineers at 9pm — office manager failed, assistant failed. A second-day intern nailed it. That intern was a barrel; keep expanding their responsibility.
- Watch who people go to for help, especially people outside that person's reporting line. That's an organic barrel signal.

**Growth rate matching:** every company has a growth rate and every individual has a learning curve. You can only keep someone in their role if their personal growth rate exceeds the company's. LinkedIn grew linearly (27 to 57 employees in 2.5 years). Square grew exponentially (20 to 300 in 2.5 years). The faster the company grows, the more likely you'll need to hire above existing people.

## Insist on focus (Thiel's rule)

Thiel insisted every person at PayPal do exactly one thing. Everyone rebelled. But the insight holds: without radical focus, people solve B+ problems (familiar, comfortable) instead of A+ problems (high-impact but hard). The A+ problem sits at the top of the to-do list but never gets done because it has no obvious solution. Across hundreds of people, this compounds into a company that grows steadily but never breaks through.

Rabois recommends at minimum tracking what would happen if you only gave everyone one priority.

## Metrics and transparency

**Dashboards:** The founder should draft the dashboard — not build it, but define what success looks like and the key inputs. The meta-metric: what fraction of employees use the dashboard daily? If it's not close to 100%, the dashboard isn't intuitive enough.

**Pairing metrics:** measuring one thing in isolation invites Goodhart's Law. Examples:
- Risk team measured on fraud rate alone will treat every user as a suspect. Pair fraud rate with false-positive rate to force innovation.
- Recruiting measured on hiring volume alone yields quantity without quality. Pair with quality-of-hire metrics.

**Look for anomalies, not expected behavior:**
- PayPal: 54 eBay Power Sellers hand-wrote "pay me with PayPal" into listings. The exec team's first instinct was to kick them out. David Sacks recognized this as the market. That anomaly became PayPal's success.
- LinkedIn: 25-30% of homepage clicks went to the user's own profile. Not editing — just looking. Max Levchin diagnosed it as vanity. This insight (users want to see themselves in the mirror) explained engagement patterns the product team had missed.

**Transparency tactics** Rabois deployed at Square:
- Share full board decks (minus compensation) with all employees after each board meeting
- Email notes from every meeting with 2+ people to the entire company
- Glass-walled conference rooms to eliminate speculation about who's meeting with whom
- Rabois is sympathetic to compensation transparency (citing sports: all salaries are public, and teams still collaborate). Steve Jobs tried it at NeXT with a simple two-band system.

## Details matter (Bill Walsh)

Rabois draws heavily on Bill Walsh's *The Score Takes Care of Itself*. Walsh took over the 49ers at 2-14 and won three Super Bowls in a decade. His first move: a three-page memo on how the receptionist should answer the phone. The philosophy: if the whole organization does everything the right way, receivers will run routes at exactly 7.5 yards, not 7 or 8 — and that precision compounds into championship performance.

Translating to startups: the details that seem invisible (food quality, laptop quality, office environment) have outsized effects. Bad cafeteria food means lunch is spent gossiping and complaining instead of brainstorming. Poor tools slow down otherwise excellent people. The CEO's job is often to be "a $10/hour TaskRabbit" — removing friction so high-performers can stay in flow.

Rabois is emphatic that the CEO should personally choose the office space. The physical environment dictates culture, decision-making, and work intensity more than almost any other factor.

## On management practices (Q&A)

- **One-on-ones:** weekly (ideally) to biweekly. Agenda set by the employee, not the manager. 5-7 direct reports max (derived from fitting weekly 1:1s into your calendar).
- **Promote from IC excellence:** At PayPal, Thiel mandated: VP Engineering = best engineer, VP Design = best designer. People aren't demoralized when their boss is genuinely better at the craft. Management techniques can be learned on the job; domain excellence cannot be faked.
- **Calendar audit:** Have CEOs write down their top three priorities, then compare against their actual calendar. It never matches. Recruiting is most commonly cited as #1 priority and most commonly under-allocated in practice. If recruiting is truly #1, ~25% of CEO time is a reasonable allocation.
