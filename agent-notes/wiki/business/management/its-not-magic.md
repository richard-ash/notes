# It's Not Magic (Amaechi on leadership)

*John Amaechi, Wiley, 2025.* This article compiles Amaechi's leadership philosophy as articulated in his September 2025 *Conversations with Tyler* interview about the book. Amaechi is a former NBA center (Orlando Magic, Utah Jazz, Cleveland, Houston), chartered psychologist, and professor of leadership at Exeter Business School. The views below are Amaechi's unless otherwise noted.

## The central claim: leadership is earned, not bestowed

Amaechi's thesis is a direct rebuke of both the "great man" and the "personality type" schools of leadership:

> It's not innate and it's not bestowed. Leadership is earned. It's not something that you need to understand what combination of personalities or cognitive styles you have. It's a set of skills that you need to choose to develop.

The ordinary framing is deliberate. Excellent leadership requires no special school, accent, language, or innate temperament — only a willingness to do the work. The skills themselves "are not complex. They're just energy-expensive." Anyone can choose them if they decide the people around them are consequential enough to warrant the effort.

This lands as a counterpoint to [[scaling-people|Claire Hughes Johnson's framing]] of management as domain-agnostic "turpentine" and to Psmith's reply that management is un-teachable process knowledge. Amaechi carves a third path: the teachable core isn't technique and isn't charisma, it's a set of *chosen behaviors* around presence, care, and eloquence.

## Culture is defined by the worst behavior tolerated

A one-line diagnostic Amaechi repeats: walk into a company and look for the poor behaviors nobody challenges — those define the culture, regardless of what the pension brochure says. His example: a firm where a senior leader ten years ago had started punctuating sentences by banging the table, and every ambitious man in the building now did the same. Benefits and pensions didn't define that place; the "discursive violence" of fists on desks did.

Cowen pushes back with the Jordan / Jobs / McCartney archetype — dysfunctional cultures that produced historically great output. Amaechi's reply has two parts:

1. **Don't generalize from Adamantium.** Companies built around once-in-a-generation talent get to break the rules. Most organizations contain mostly average people and cannot. Modeling your leadership on Steve Jobs is survivorship bias wearing a turtleneck.
2. **Fear is physically incompatible with peak performance.** "Anyone whose idea of being a great leader is that their superpower is somehow intrinsically linked with their ability to be an asshole has misunderstood the human condition, because nobody performs at their best when they're frightened, nobody."

## Rituals: connection, direction, humanity

Asked what rituals leadership requires, Amaechi names three functions:

- **Connective rituals** — signal that people are not just transactions.
- **Direction rituals** — give orientation in times of external and internal upheaval.
- **Humanity rituals** — show the leader is not "a frightening monster all the time" (a 6'9", 150-kg Black man has an acute interest in not being seen as one).

His own example: "Yorkshire Golden Hour" — a recurring meeting in his mostly-virtual team where work talk is explicitly banned and the only agenda is catching up on each other's lives.

The NBA analogue is Doc Rivers in Orlando, who began each pre-season with a **midnight practice** centered on a width-of-the-court sprint that every player had to complete in a set time. Amaechi reads this as a double-purpose ritual: it enforced pre-season conditioning ("day zero" fitness) and it set the tone for how the year would be run. Doc Rivers is the one active American leader Amaechi names as impressing him.

## Jerry Sloan: being a dick is not synonymous with being a leader

The counter-model is Utah's Jerry Sloan, who Amaechi says called him names "that would make your toes curl," labeled his doctoral studies a betrayal of basketball, and questioned his commitment while Amaechi's mother was dying of cancer in Britain. The lesson Amaechi draws is categorical — beloved does not equal effective, and cruelty is not a legitimate leadership instrument.

Karl Malone, by contrast, gets praised not for kindness but for self-imposed standards: when Sloan punished the younger players with endless sprints, Stockton read *USA Today* on the bench and Malone, at 30-odd years old and the best player in franchise history, rode a stationary bike at full tilt on the sideline. Amaechi's read: veteran status is earned by visibly out-working the people you could permissibly coast past.

Michael Cage gets the most affectionate story: as a veteran in Cleveland, he took rookie Bobby Sura's newly-purchased second car back to the dealership and made them unwind the sale, then lectured Amaechi about not buying too many suits because tax. "That's what you do as a veteran."

## Mentorship usually fails the mentee

Asked what *Star Wars* teaches about mentorship:

> It's very often mostly useful for one party and not the other, and often the party it's supposed to help most, it doesn't. The party that's supposed to be learning doesn't always do the learning, and the party that should be teaching is often the one that gets taught.

He extends this to corporate reverse-mentoring programs. Jedi also get a damning review as an institution: at the height of the Old Republic they had a chance to evolve and instead "doubled down" into monasticism and became the galaxy's police — "which was never going to lead to ingratiation."

## The $17 million principle

The biographical centerpiece of the interview: Amaechi turned down a six-year, $17 million Lakers offer (behind Shaquille O'Neal, likely four championships "without lifting a finger") to return to Orlando for $600,000 on a non-guaranteed one-year deal. He explicitly rejects loyalty as a concept — "it feels very quid pro quo" — and frames the decision in terms of *principle*: Doc Rivers and the Magic had kept him when they didn't have to, and the end of the year was his one chance to reciprocate.

He foresaw the likely consequence (being cut the following season when "sexier players came along") and it happened. But the story has a load-bearing function in his current work: "Nowadays, I work with very influential people in business and large organizations and they often ask me if I can be trusted. They often ask me how much my word is worth. And there's very few people in this world who can say about $17 million."

The post-Orlando period also illustrates a psychological cost: Amaechi developed a mental block on shooting ("couldn't hit the side of a barn"), which he says required therapy and someone to talk to — not theoretical knowledge (he was studying for his doctorate at the time, which didn't help). The specific point is that elite performance skills can be disassembled by emotional weight that willpower cannot lift.

## You are not what you do

His advice to young players who could plausibly reach the NBA is never "don't" — it's:

> Any role, any job, anywhere where your occupation is your definition will lead you to harm… I never was a basketball player. I just played basketball.

The grammatical distinction (noun vs. verb) did the psychological work of preserving a self that could outlive the career. For second-tier players whose careers fizzle in Europe, his prognosis is unexpectedly optimistic: anyone who's grinded through professional sport has "learned to embrace and even love mundanity, the boring, the repetitive. And if you can embrace the boring and the repetitive, there is no job you can't be good at."

## Personality testing is "absolute bollocks"

Asked the most overrated idea in current psychology, Amaechi picks the Five-Factor Model and Hogan-style personality inventories without hesitation:

> It is not that these factors don't exist, it's just that the constellations of these factors don't tell you who you are. Your personality is mutable. It changes moment to moment, nevermind under stress versus not… you are not a bloody primary color.

The practical objection is gatekeeping: personality tests sort candidates into fixed categories ("you're not extroverted enough to speak to shareholders") and have "been holding down the same groups of people for the last 50 years." Amaechi offers himself as a counter-example — "an extreme introvert" who finds humans energy-expensive but worth it, and who makes his living speaking to boards.

This dovetails with the book's central claim: if personality is mutable and leadership is a set of chosen behaviors, then no test result should be allowed to disqualify anyone from learning the skills.

## The practical psychologist stance

Amaechi distinguishes himself from academic psychology on two axes:

1. **Accessibility over cleverness.** Many academic psychologists "want to write in a way that makes what they know seem completely inaccessible, if not magic." He writes to make the knowledge feel ordinary and gainable.
2. **Pragmatic over theoretical.** "For me, psychology is not theoretical; it's pragmatic." The famous academics, in his view, write for a theoretical audience rather than for people living in a real and challenging world.

The title *It's Not Magic* is a direct product of stance (1).

## Scattered but load-bearing asides

- **The worst leadership failure he saw early** was college athletic programs protecting athletes from having to attend class — "one of the most obvious abdications of leadership I saw at a very early age."
- **Consultants who hide in bureaucracy.** If you're not interested in the craft of leadership, bureaucracy is a useful place to hide. The good leadership-minded consultants he's met tend to have had a prior profession (NHS doctor, physics teacher) — i.e., domain they were good at before they became advisors.
- **Homophobia correlates with religiosity, not race.** Asked about the stereotype of African American male communities being especially homophobic: the predictive factor in the data is religiosity. Black Caribbean and Black African homophobia in the UK he traces to the specific fact that British imperial rule exported the homophobic legal codes those societies still carry.
- **Nostalgia is a political tool.** "Make X great again" movements depend on a delusion that is never borne out: every generation believes its predecessor era was morally better. The only people for whom the past was actually better are aristocrats — not "rich today," but actually-aristocratic. "If you aren't part of the aristocracy now, you go back a couple of generations and you are working for somebody in the most abhorrent conditions imaginable."
- **Injury isn't bad luck; it's the schedule.** "You cannot be 350 pounds and play three or four times a week for an entire season and imagine that it's anything but dumb luck that you don't get seriously hurt… if you care about the players so much, then demand fewer games a week." He's had two back surgeries and two epidural anaesthetic injections "just so I can walk" in the year before the interview.
