# Tech Influencer (Content as Career Strategy)

Will Larson's framework for thinking about content creation as career advancement in tech. The core argument: most career benefits of writing online come from a tiny number of well-distributed pieces, not from prolific output, and the prestige content manufactures matters more than the influence it confers.

## Two distinct goals, two distinct strategies

Larson splits content creators into two camps with very different optimal playbooks:

**Career augmentation** (the modal case): "Create a small number of meaningful pieces, develop a distribution plan for each piece, keep doing it until a piece gets lucky with distribution, then stop." That's enough to make job searches and hiring smoother. No book, no Substack treadmill required.

**Industry influence** (the rare case): trying to shift industry practice on a topic — Tanya Reilly on glue work, Cindy Sridharan on testing in production, Dan Luu on hiring. This requires a decade-plus commitment, clear measurement, and tolerance for the reality that most of your output will not land.

The mistake Larson sees most often is people in camp one running a camp-two playbook — pattern-matching on prolific creators when their actual goal would be served by three good pieces.

## The case against being well-known online

Larson pushes back on the assumption that online visibility correlates with success:

- The most successful people he knows are largely *not* well-known online. Many don't write, speak at conferences, stream, or maintain a professional social presence at all.
- Online communities create a reality-distortion field where intra-community fame feels like professional credibility, but the mapping is weak.
- Visibility is asymmetric: harassment and damaged professional prospects are more common than career wins. "Visibility can go wrong in more ways than it can go right."
- Long-lived semi-private communities (learning circles, meetups) often deliver more career value per unit of effort than public output.

## Prestige is the real lever

The deeper mechanism Larson points to is prestige (linking to his [forty-year career](http://lethain.com/forty-year-career/) framing). Prestige is "a universal lubricant" — it makes you a more compelling candidate, builds a willing network, opens senior-role doors. Everything is easier with more of it.

The most efficient way to manufacture prestige is to spend time at a couple of prestigious companies. Content creation is the accessible alternative for people who can't or won't take that path — Larson notes you can write from rural Kentucky or a small town in Japan. Content isn't the goal; the prestige byproduct is.

This reframes the whole exercise: you're not trying to become a media personality, you're trying to manufacture portable signal. A handful of well-placed pieces does this; a weekly Substack mostly doesn't add to it after the first few.

## Measuring influence (most metrics are bad)

Larson rates common influence proxies skeptically:

- **Pageviews** — bad. Rewards being controversial over being influential, and incentivizes targeting large audiences (early-career engineers) over influential ones (executives).
- **Social media followers** — useful for reach, which is part of distribution, but the audiences people accumulate often aren't the ones they later hope to influence.
- **Book/dollar sales** — quantifiable but doesn't track who reads it or whether their behavior changes.
- **Volume of writing** — easy to game. "There are a lot of LiveJournals out there to testify to that fact."
- **Inbound conversations** — Larson's preferred signal. People give up on confusing writing; they only reach out about things that move them (or that they strongly disagree with).
- **Conference/podcast invitations and similar requests** (book proposals, angel invitations, mentorship asks) — a useful signal because hosts are good at picking guests who'll engage their audience.

The honest move, Larson argues, is to name what you're measuring out loud — even if the answer is "I don't know yet."

## A little goes a long way

Larson's stark self-report: of his hundreds of posts, ~20 are flagged as influential by his own generous definition; only four (Migrations, A forty-year career, Sizing engineering teams, Productivity in the age of hypergrowth) make a stricter cut. Roughly a 1:150 ratio. Patrick McKenzie's [Salary Negotiation](https://www.kalzumeus.com/2012/01/23/salary-negotiation/) piece is the canonical example: most readers can name that one piece and no others, yet that one piece has shaped industry practice.

Practical implication: distinguish writing for practice from writing for influence and don't conflate them. You can't predict which posts will hit, but you can usually tell which ones have no chance — self-edit those out when the goal is influence.

## Distribution is a co-equal problem

A recurring theme: "Books are *sold* rather than *bought*." Even excellent content fails without distribution.

- The simplest effective distribution plan: coordinate with ~10 visible friends to share a piece around the same time. That's enough for snappy pieces.
- Building your own distribution channels (mailing lists, social, RSS) requires consistent volume — which is why people fixated on influence often default to grinding output.
- An entire ecosystem exists (private chat rooms, voting rings, weekly link mailing lists) but as a primary creator your interests are largely aligned with it; investing in *operating* that ecosystem pulls energy away from creating.
- **Borrow distribution when you can**: write on someone else's blog, publish to their list, speak at their conference. Prolific creators won't let you do this repeatedly, but for occasional high-quality work they're often glad to (it solidifies their reputation as a discoverer).

The dual law Larson states: "Distribution is always a problem, but it's never the only problem" — and the corollary, "content is always a problem, but it's never the only problem." A piece can underperform because it's boring, redundant, badly edited, or out of sync with the cyclical fashions of thought leadership — not just because nobody saw it.

## Experiment across mediums, then specialize

Larson's advice on writing vs. speaking vs. video vs. books: try each enough to develop basic skill, then move on. A few longer speaking clips and a few pieces in a published venue have meaningful diminishing returns; it's not worth becoming prolific in a medium you don't enjoy unless you're trying to make a living from it. Treat medium experimentation as self-development, not a career strategy.

## Don't assume shared rules

The closing point is about meta-strategy: people creating content online are playing different games — visibility-seeking, financial extraction, distributing others' work for indirect compensation, genuine influence. The rules and values conflict, and frustration usually traces back to assuming everyone is playing yours. Be mindful of your values, and others'.

## Temporal notes

Published 2021. The medium-specific advice (Substack, Twitch, Medium-vs-self-hosted) has shifted — by 2026 Substack has become the default professional writer's platform, Twitter/X has fragmented, and short-form video (TikTok, YouTube Shorts, LinkedIn video) has become a more viable channel than it was when Larson wrote. The structural argument — that a small number of pieces does most of the work, prestige matters more than visibility, and distribution gates everything — appears to be holding.
