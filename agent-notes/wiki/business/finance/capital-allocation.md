# Capital Allocation

Capital allocation is the process by which companies raise, manage, and deploy financial resources. Mauboussin and Callahan argue it is the most important responsibility of senior management, yet one most CEOs are poorly prepared for — the skills that make someone a CEO (general management, domain expertise) are not the same skills needed to oversee how capital is raised and disbursed.

## Why Capital Allocation Matters

Two forces make value creation through capital allocation imperative:

1. **Competition** — a firm that misallocates resources will lose to better-managed competitors.
2. **Opportunity cost** — all capital has an alternative use. Capital that fails to earn its cost of capital over the long term destroys value.

The aggregate ROIC for U.S. public companies (9.2% average, 1970-2024, adjusted for internally-generated intangible assets) exceeds the aggregate NOPAT growth rate (7.9%), meaning public companies collectively generate more cash than they invest. This creates a persistent capital allocation problem: what to do with the excess.

## Sources and Uses of Capital

Capital comes from two sources:

- **Internal** — cash flow from operations and asset sales. This has been the primary source for U.S. corporations since 1970.
- **External** — equity or debt issuance. Required when a firm's NOPAT growth rate exceeds its ROIC (as was the case for Walmart in its first 15 years as a public company, which still created enormous value because its ROIC far exceeded its cost of capital).

Peter Bernstein proposed a thought experiment: what if companies disbursed all earnings to shareholders and then asked markets for investment funds? His logic was that markets are better capital allocators than companies, so the discipline of external fundraising would improve allocation.

U.S. companies deployed $7.5 trillion in capital in 2024, across eight categories:

| Category | % of Sales (2024) | Trend Since 1970 |
|---|---|---|
| Investment SG&A ex-R&D | ~10.1% | Rose from 9.3% to peak of 11.2% (2020) |
| Capital expenditures | ~6.3% | Declined from 9.4% peak (1973) |
| M&A | ~5.8% | Highly cyclical, ~7% average |
| Gross buybacks | ~4.6% | Near-zero before 1982, rose to 1.3x dividends |
| Dividends | ~3.5% | Relatively stable |
| Investment R&D | ~3.3% | Rose from 1.0% (1970s) |
| Net working capital | ~0.6% | Declining significance |

The most striking long-term trend is the **shift from tangible to intangible investment**. In the 1970s, tangible investment was roughly double intangible; that ratio has now flipped. Analysis suggests 26-68% of U.S. firm value is attributable to intangible capital. This shift tracks the changing composition of the S&P 500: information technology, healthcare, and communication services doubled from 26% to 52% of the index (1970-2024), while industrials, energy, and materials declined from 30% to 13%.

## Capital Allocation Alternatives

### Mergers and Acquisitions

M&A is one of the largest and most cyclical forms of capital deployment. Mauboussin and Callahan identify several robust empirical patterns:

- **M&A creates value in aggregate** but the seller captures more than 100% of it — the buyer's stock drops on announcement ~60% of the time (average change: -1.6%).
- **Cash deals outperform** stock or mixed deals. Managers signal confidence in synergies by using cash; stock signals uncertainty.
- **Operational ("bolt-on") deals outperform** transformational ones. Danaher is cited as an exemplar of this approach.
- **Companies with dedicated M&A teams** see higher abnormal returns. About one-third of public companies have them.
- **Lower premiums correlate with higher returns**. The average deal premium from 1980-2024 was 30%.
- **EPS accretion/dilution is a poor predictor** of deal success. In a study of 95 deals, the most common outcome (45 of 95) was positive EPS but negative abnormal return. Yet three-quarters of investor relations professionals say EPS gets "strong emphasis."
- **Buyer success rates have improved** over time: 36% (1995-2002), 40% (2003-2010), 44% (2011-2018).
- **Cost synergies are more reliable than revenue synergies.** A McKinsey survey found 36% of acquirers met cost targets vs. only 17% for revenue synergies.

The key valuation formula: **NPV of deal = PV of synergies - premium**. Mauboussin and Callahan suggest using Sirower's SVAR (shareholder value at risk = premium / buyer market cap) to gauge deal materiality.

### Intangible Investment (SG&A ex-R&D and R&D)

The accounting treatment of intangible investment — expensing rather than capitalizing — makes financial statements less informative over time. The FASB decided in 1974 to expense R&D because "a direct relationship between research and development costs and specific future revenue generally has not been demonstrated." The common adjustment is to assume all R&D and 30% of non-R&D SG&A constitute investment, then capitalize and amortize them.

Companies that invest heavily in intangibles through SG&A earn positive excess returns on average. Returns to R&D spending appear to be declining, however — Anne Marie Knott's "research quotient" shows a long-term downward trend, suggesting diminishing returns. About one-third of companies underspend on R&D while nearly two-thirds overspend.

### Capital Expenditures

Only about 38% of total capex represents growth investment (the portion above depreciation). Through H1 2025, capex was up ~15%, with 60% of the increase driven by just four hyperscaler companies (Amazon, Meta, Alphabet, Microsoft). Depreciation understates true maintenance capex by about 20% due to inflation and technological obsolescence.

### Dividends and Share Buybacks

Mauboussin and Callahan demonstrate that **dividends and buybacks are theoretically equivalent** under strict assumptions (no taxes, identical reinvestment rates, stock at fair value) but differ meaningfully in practice:

- **Dividends are sticky** — once set, 60%+ of CFOs would not cut them even to fund value-creating investments. Growth rate volatility is ~1/6th that of buybacks.
- **Buybacks are discretionary** — used to distribute residual cash. Three "schools" of motivation:
  - *Fair value*: steady buybacks at whatever price, offering shareholders flexibility
  - *Intrinsic value*: buy only when undervalued (the "golden rule"), but CFOs are notoriously bad at valuing their own stock
  - *Accounting-driven*: boost EPS or offset SBC dilution — 76% of CFOs cite EPS as important, but there is no evidence this creates value
- **Buybacks do not create or destroy value for the company** — they transfer wealth between selling and continuing shareholders. Buying undervalued stock benefits holders; buying overvalued stock benefits sellers.

The total shareholder yield (buybacks + dividends / equity market cap) rose from 27% of the cost of equity in 1970 to 38% in 2024.

### Divestitures

Divestitures are chronically underused. Executives resist them because they represent an admission of past error, reduce firm size (and thus compensation), and face internal political resistance. Yet a meta-analysis of ~100 studies shows divestitures are consistently associated with positive performance for the parent firm. It is common for a small percentage of a company's assets to create most of its value — divesting the rest to owners who can extract more value is "addition by subtraction."

## Assessing Capital Allocation Skill

### Behavioral Obstacles

Mauboussin and Callahan identify several cognitive and institutional barriers, drawing on CFO survey research by John Graham:

- **Status quo bias** — less than 1% of firms reallocate capital at the optimal rate across divisions. The vast majority underallocate.
- **Escalation of commitment** — managers prefer to double down on prior decisions rather than change course.
- **Overprecision** — CFO forecasts are directionally correct but far too narrow in their range of outcomes.
- **Sticky decision-making** — capital allocation patterns persist year-to-year with little variation.
- **Conservative hurdle rates** — average hurdle rate is 600 basis points above cost of capital, causing underinvestment.
- **Near-term earnings pressure** — nearly 80% of financial executives would pass on a value-creating investment to hit near-term earnings targets.
- **Institutional imperative** — Buffett's term for the tendency of executives to mindlessly imitate one another in compensation and capital allocation practices.

### The Five Principles

Mauboussin and Callahan synthesize five principles of good capital allocation (building on McTaggart, Kontes, and Mankins's *The Value Imperative* and Thorndike's *The Outsiders*):

1. **Zero-based capital allocation** — deploy capital to its best use without regard for past spending. Companies that proactively reallocate generate higher ROAs.
2. **Fund strategies, not projects** — a good project within a bad strategy is a bad allocation. Start with strategy, then determine which projects support it.
3. **No capital rationing, but earn sufficient returns** — all capital, whether internal or external, has an opportunity cost. Reject the mindset that internally generated cash is "free."
4. **Zero tolerance for bad growth** — exit businesses and strategies with no prospect of creating value. Recognize the value of quitting.
5. **Know the value of assets and act on gaps** — great allocators sense the difference between price and value across their portfolio and are willing to act when gaps are large enough to overcome frictions.

### The ROIC Framework

- **ROIC** = NOPAT / Invested Capital. An ROIC above the cost of capital indicates value creation. From 1970-2024, ROICs exceeded the cost of capital for about half of observations.
- **ROIIC** = change in NOPAT / lagged change in invested capital. Measures marginal returns on new investment. Noisy for lumpy investors (e.g., M&A-heavy firms), so use 3- or 5-year rolling periods.
