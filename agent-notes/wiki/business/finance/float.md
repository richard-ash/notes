# Float

Float is capital a business holds temporarily because of timing differences between receiving payments and disbursing them. Unlike debt, float typically carries no interest cost — making it effectively free leverage that can transform ordinary returns into extraordinary ones.

## How Float Works

The simplest example: a business negotiates to pay its suppliers *after* it collects from its customers. The gap between collection and payment creates a pool of capital the business can use without putting up any of its own equity.

10-K Diver illustrates this with a candle-making business: if Alice arranges to pay suppliers only after the home goods store pays her, she needs $0 of her own capital — yet can still extract $87.6K/year in profit. The same business requiring upfront supplier payment would need $14.6K in equity, yielding a much lower return on equity for identical unit economics.

## Float vs. Debt

Both float and debt are non-equity sources of capital that provide leverage. The critical difference:

- **Float** is typically free (no interest), arises naturally from business operations, and doesn't create contractual obligations that can trigger default.
- **Debt** carries interest expense, has covenants and maturity dates, and can make a business fragile — especially in downturns when cash flows decline but debt service remains fixed.

Float provides leverage without the fragility that debt introduces. This is why Buffett considers float one of the most important features of insurance businesses.

## Buffett and Insurance Float

Warren Buffett has built Berkshire Hathaway's investment engine substantially on insurance float. In his 2009 shareholder letter, he emphasized that float — premiums collected and held before claims are paid — is the foundational capital source that powers Berkshire's investment returns.

The insurance model is the canonical example: policyholders pay premiums upfront, but claims are paid months or years later. The interim pool of capital is enormous, long-duration, and — if underwriting is disciplined — cost-free or even profitable (negative-cost float when combined underwriting profits exceed zero).

## Other Sources of Float

Float isn't limited to insurance. Common sources include:

- **Accounts payable** — negotiating extended payment terms with suppliers
- **Prepaid subscriptions** — customers paying annually for services delivered monthly
- **Gift cards** — revenue collected that may never be redeemed (breakage)
- **Customer deposits** — held before goods are delivered

Any business with a [[capital-allocation|capital-light model]] that collects before it pays is generating float.

## Implications

Float-heavy businesses appear unremarkable on a unit-economics basis but can deliver exceptional returns on equity because the denominator (equity capital required) approaches zero. This makes float a form of hidden leverage that doesn't show up in traditional debt/equity ratios — a balance sheet reader who focuses only on debt misses it entirely.
