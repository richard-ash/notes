# Private Credit and the Direct Lending Cycle

"Private credit" is the broad category of non-bank lending that filled the vacuum left when post-GFC bank regulation crimped traditional corporate lending. "Direct lending" — private loans made to mid-market, private-equity-sponsored companies with sub-investment grade ratings — is the fastest-growing subset and the part most laypeople actually mean when they say "private credit." Howard Marks insists on the distinction; most commentators don't.

This article uses Marks's April 2026 Oaktree memo as its anchor source. Marks frames private credit as the latest in a 50-year sequence of credit-market innovations and walks through how the direct lending boom is now meeting its first real stress test.

## A 50-year chronology of credit innovation

Marks lays out the sequence as a single story:

| Decade | Innovation |
|---|---|
| 1970s | Acceptance of non-investment-grade ("junk") debt — Michael Milken's idea that risk could be priced rather than excluded |
| 1980s | Leveraged buyouts; corporate leverage as a strategy |
| 1990s | Broadly syndicated loans; tranched securitizations (CLOs, MBS) |
| 2000s | "Alternative investments" as a category; subprime RMBS that detonated in 2008-09 |
| 2010s | Direct lending fills the vacuum left by post-GFC bank retreat |
| 2020s | Direct lending vehicles marketed to individual and retirement investors |

Each step depended on the previous one. High yield bonds enabled LBOs; LBOs created the borrower base private equity needed; private equity's appetite for capital created demand for syndicated loans, then CLOs, then direct lending. The U.S. high yield market is now ~$1.5T; broadly syndicated loans are also ~$1.5T; direct lending has gone from ~$150B (the entire private credit sector) two decades ago to roughly $2T of loans made in the last 15 years.

Marks's framing point: "the limiting factor in the credit markets is never borrowers' appetite for capital, but rather lenders' willingness to supply it." When supply expands, the makeup of the credit market shifts toward whatever asset class lenders and sponsors agree is fundable — which in the 2010s and 2020s became software companies.

## The "normal pattern" of investment fads

Marks's eight features of every bubble:

1. **Newness is essential** — untested means flaws haven't surfaced yet.
2. **Grain of truth** — Nifty Fifty *were* good companies, the internet *did* change the world. Bubbles need a real thesis to anchor delusion.
3. **Early entrants get rewarded** — they buy before popularity inflates prices.
4. **Envy pulls in latecomers** — Kindleberger: "There is nothing so disturbing to one's well-being and judgment as to see a friend get rich."
5. **Possibility morphs into certainty** — skepticism evaporates.
6. **The price question stops being asked** — what price is safe to pay drops out of the conversation.
7. **Latecomers lower standards and bid up prices** — "what the wise man does in the beginning, the fool does in the end."
8. **Disillusionment and loss when the new thing fails to deliver.**

The model: investors swing between "flawless" and "hopeless" with very little time spent on Graham and Dodd's "weighing machine" of dispassionate analysis.

## How the pattern fits direct lending

Marks argues the 15-year direct lending boom hits nearly every checkpoint:

- Banks pulled back; PE demand exceeded supply, so early direct lenders extracted high yields and strong covenants.
- The 2010s low-rate environment made direct lending's nominal yields look great, especially when levered.
- Hundreds of new managers entered, *almost all of whom had never been tested through a downturn.*
- More capital chasing the same loans → lower yields, narrower spreads, weaker safety provisions.
- A 17-year benign credit environment (2008-25) masked the slipping standards.
- Marketing pivoted to retail and retirement accounts, inflating AUM further.

A particularly sharp Marks point: advocates claimed direct loans deliver "high risk-adjusted returns" but the supporting evidence was actually *volatility-adjusted* returns. Direct loans don't trade, so prices don't move — but the underlying credit risk is no less than for high yield bonds or broadly syndicated loans. **Risk and volatility are not the same thing.** Sharpe ratios on illiquid assets flatter the asset class.

## The 2025-26 turning point

Two events broke the calm:

1. **Cockroaches in the Coal Mine (Nov 2025)** — Marks's earlier memo on the First Brands and Tricolor bankruptcies, both of which raised fraud concerns and were treated as idiosyncratic at the time.
2. **Software debt and AI (Feb 2026)** — Anthropic's November 2025 coding model and a January 2026 release of "11 plug-ins" automating tasks across fields appear to have been the cognitive tipping point. Investors suddenly noticed that ~20-30% of direct lending exposure is to software companies bought at ~20x EBITDA with high leverage, and that AI is now eating into the equity cushion that protects lenders.

The retail BDC channel is where the stress shows up first: non-traded BDC investors hit redemption gates; publicly-traded BDC shares started trading at wider discounts to NAV; investors began questioning whether the NAVs at which earlier redeemers exited were overstated, which triggers a reflexive desire to put more shares in for redemption next time. Marks's understated observation: "it would be understandable if investors reacted negatively to being told they can't get their money out when they want."

Related wiki concepts: [[ai-and-the-future-of-work]] (the AI displacement story Marks invokes) and [[the-bitter-lesson]] (general scaling-driven AI improvement).

## The structural problem: liquidity mismatch and retail investors

Marks's diagnosis maps cleanly onto his reading of *1929: Inside the Greatest Crash in Wall Street History* (Andrew Ross Sorkin). Three things drove 1929 and tend to drive every retail-led bust:

1. Sale of investments to the public without regard for suitability.
2. Heavy leverage extended to buyers.
3. **Mismatch between the illiquidity of the underlying assets and the short-term nature of the financing.**

Direct lending vehicles marketed to retail rhyme with this: illiquid private loans → semi-liquid wrappers → individual investors who expected liquidity. The redemption-gate machinery was designed precisely to prevent fire sales, and it appears to be working. But "working as designed" still erodes investor trust, and the trust loss is what triggers the next round of redemption requests.

Marks quotes Sorkin's punchline: the antidote to irrational exuberance "is not regulation by itself, nor skepticism, but humility — the humility to know that no system is foolproof, no market fully rational, and no generation exempt."

## The PE-direct-lending feedback loop

Marks devotes the second half of the memo to a thesis many commentators miss: **private equity and direct lending have intertwined fates.** Three quarters of direct loans finance LBOs; if the PE asset class struggles, direct lending struggles by extension.

His *Sea Change* (Dec 2022) argument applies here: a 40-year, ~2,000 bp decline in interest rates (22.25% in 1980 → 2.25% in 2020) gave PE a tailwind that's now reversing. The 2022 rate hike to 5.25-5.5% has, in Bruce Karsh's phrase, "saddled" PE portfolio companies "with capital structures that didn't anticipate a 400-basis point increase in interest rates." Concrete consequences:

- Higher interest costs → less profit, lower coverage ratios, harder refinancing.
- Higher discount rates → lower exit valuations → slower portfolio sales.
- Slower exits → lower distributions to LPs → less LP capital available for new commitments.
- MSCI: U.S. PE index returned 5.8% annualized 2022-Q3 2025 vs. 11.6% for the S&P 500.

This matters for credit because debt taken on at low rates may now exceed the equity value of the underlying companies. PIK features compound the problem: declining profitability pushes companies to PIK rather than service debt in cash, deferring stress to maturity. Whether lenders kick the can down the road or force the issue is the open question for the next several years.

## What "good" looks like in this environment

Marks's prescription, lightly disguised as Oaktree's positioning:

- **Stay disciplined when the music plays.** Citigroup's Chuck Prince in 2007: "as long as the music is playing, you've got to get up and dance." Marks's framing: that's the conscientious manager's worst dilemma — join in and lower standards, or sit out and watch competitors collect AUM and clients close their accounts. Oaktree claims to have done the latter: AUM only doubled in a decade (modest by alt-credit standards), direct lending is ~20% of performing credit, software exposure is small and predominantly first-lien, and 80% of private credit is institutional rather than retail.
- **Do real underwriting.** The reassurance for surviving lenders: high yield bonds and broadly syndicated loans — direct lending's precursors — did fine through multi-year periods that included the GFC, *if* underwriting was disciplined.
- **Maintain a balance between belief and doubt.** "True believers make the most money in manias, and skeptics lose the least when they crash." The success Marks aims for sits in the middle.

## Implications

A few things this memo implicitly argues but doesn't quite state:

- **Direct lending will go through a real credit cycle for the first time.** The asset class formed in 2010-12 and has only operated in benign conditions. The pattern Marks describes — flawed loans, redemption gates, valuation discounts, retail investor rotation — looks like the early phase of a mark-to-reality cycle, not a one-off event.
- **Software-as-collateral was a category error in the AI era.** The thesis was that subscription cash flows + sticky moats made software bankable. AI doesn't necessarily kill software companies, but it compresses the equity cushion below what credit underwriters assumed. Lenders in 2018-23 priced credit assuming 20x EBITDA exits would hold up; if AI drops exit multiples to 12-15x, junior debt tranches lose their margin of safety.
- **The retail distribution channel is the structural weakness, not the loans themselves.** Even if the underlying credits perform, gating retail capital that was promised quasi-liquidity will produce political and reputational consequences. This is the part Marks seems most worried about.
- **Marks is positioning Oaktree's dry powder.** The closing argument — "Investors' newly elevated skepticism is likely to give us investment opportunities in the days ahead that are much better than those we passed up" — is a soft signal that Oaktree expects to be a buyer in the cycle's distress phase.
