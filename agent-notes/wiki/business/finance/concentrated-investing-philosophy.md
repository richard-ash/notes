# Concentrated investing philosophy (XN / Kapadia)

Gaurav Kapadia's investment firm XN runs a deliberately narrow book — 10 to 15 public-market positions at a given time, plus about a third of capital in opportunistic best-in-class private investments. The structural choice is downstream of a single conviction: there are very few genuinely good ideas, so the right response is to concentrate capital on the ones that pass the bar rather than diversify into mediocrity. Kapadia describes the approach as "rhyming with Buffett" — concentrated value investing with long holding periods.

## Why the position count stays small

The math is structural: if you only hold 10–15 positions and the holding period is multi-year, you are *only adding one to three new ideas to the corpus per year*. That forces the bar extremely high on every addition, but it also means the firm can spend a great deal of analytical depth on each one. Kapadia frames this as a feature, not a constraint — at this scale of curation, you can't afford FOMO about the ideas you missed.

This connects to [[circle-of-competence]]: XN explicitly excludes healthcare despite it being a huge percentage of global GDP, on the grounds that "there's just no chance" they can be the *best* healthcare investor given the regulatory and scientific-research demands. The goal in everything, Kapadia says, is "to be the best at what we do" — and being best at anything requires consciously not being in things you can't dominate.

## What makes a sector "transparent"

A sector is investable for XN when business analysis, logic, and valuation tie together in a way that allows differentiated conclusions versus consensus. The phrase Kapadia uses for the resulting investments: "obvious in retrospect" — at the time, they were hard to make. The format is essentially writing the future narrative in advance:

> "In a few years, it'll be very clear that we're going to need a lot more power for data centers" — or "we're massively short housing stock."

Industrials, media, and certain areas of technology, in his view, lend themselves to this kind of high-fidelity forward-plotting. The asymmetry around [[buy-borrow-die|long-duration capital]] is implicit: you can afford to be early if you've genuinely got the forward picture right.

## The Figma case study as template

XN bought Figma after antitrust authorities blocked its Adobe acquisition. The setup Kapadia describes is a textbook asymmetric risk/reward:

- Roughly half the price Adobe was going to pay
- Significant break fee already on the balance sheet (net-cash position)
- "Best-in-class asset" with founder-mode leadership ([[founder-mode-investment-firms|founder mode applied to the leader of the investee, not just the investor]])
- Earnings compounding at a high rate
- "One foot in the future"

The pattern is bounded downside (cash on balance sheet, blocked-deal pricing) and potentially unbounded upside (founder, growth rate, secular position) with high confidence in management's ability to navigate. Kapadia presents General Electric over the years as the same shape on a different scale: articulate an extremely good risk/reward where downside is bounded and upside is forecastable.

## Two types of errors — and which one to lose sleep over

Kapadia distinguishes two kinds of investing mistakes:

1. **Investments you didn't make that worked out anyway.** Usually these worked for a different thesis than the one you considered. You pulled the plug because *your* thesis didn't hold; some other thesis showed up and drove the stock. "I don't lose one second of sleep over those because that was unpredictable. For us, we have to focus on our process, and that process will engender really good outcomes."

2. **Passive non-decisions** — where you were really close, but got distracted, or things got busy, and never engaged. *These* are the ones to lose sleep over. They're the only category XN treats as a process failure worth iterating on.

This is a sharper distinction than the usual "errors of omission vs. commission" frame because it separates predictable from unpredictable misses. The bar-stays-high stance only works if you also build the discipline to *actually engage* with everything that clears the funnel.

## How this contrasts with adjacent strategies

Kapadia explicitly contrasts XN with three other shapes of investing:

- **Citadel-style short cycle, short-oriented** — "very high fidelity on short-term data points." Excellent at what it does, but a different game.
- **Great private-equity firms** — "really good long-term signal and financial leverage on LBOs." [[leverage|Leverage]] does work that XN's public-side approach doesn't.
- **XN's approach** — articulate the asymmetric risk/reward, hold long, concentrate. No leverage, no high-frequency signal.

The framing puts XN in the [[elementary-worldly-wisdom|Munger lineage]] of concentrated public-markets investing while acknowledging that the other styles aren't worse — they're answering different questions.

## Why this is hard to replicate

Two structural barriers, both implicit in the conversation:

1. **You need a way to actually be patient.** Soroban, the firm Kapadia co-founded before XN, was where he managed outside capital and grew "less enthusiastic about the process." He eventually wound it down and ran his own money through a family-office incarnation of XN for a couple of years before re-opening to outside capital. The implication: outside capital structures often don't support a 10-15-position, multi-year hold profile without enormous trust from LPs. Running the family-office version "brought back the spark and purity" — i.e., the philosophy isn't fundable in standard hedge-fund formats.

2. **You need [[founder-mode-investment-firms|founder mode]] in your own organization.** Industry pressures in public-markets investing "encourage you not to innovate," in Kapadia's view. The concentrated approach is itself a form of innovation that drifts back to the mean under normal organizational entropy.
