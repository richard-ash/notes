# The Doorman Fallacy

The **Doorman Fallacy** is Rory Sutherland's name for the mistake of assuming a human role can be replaced by technology (or eliminated outright) without consequence. The canonical example: a hotel reasons that its doorman exists "to open the door," concludes a button or automatic door does that more cheaply, and fires him — overlooking that the doorman also hailed taxis, carried bags, recognized regulars, deterred undesirables, and signaled prestige. The role was **defined down to its most measurable function**, and everything unmeasured was treated as worth zero.

The fallacy is a special case of a measurement bias: when you can quantify the cost of a person or process but not the full bundle of value they deliver, optimization systematically deletes the unmeasured value. On a spreadsheet the decision looks unambiguously smart — fewer staff, lower operating cost — because the spreadsheet only has columns for the things that were easy to count.

## Why it persists

Sutherland's broader argument (he is a behavioral-economics-minded ad man) is that businesses over-trust the legible and under-trust the intangible. Costs are concrete and land on this quarter's P&L; the value destroyed is diffuse, delayed, and shows up as a slow erosion of goodwill that's hard to attribute back to the original cut. The incentive structure rewards the visible saving and never charges the manager for the invisible loss. This is the same legibility bias that makes intangible brand value — see [[cultural-imprinting]] — easy to underfund: what you cannot put a number on, you tend to treat as free to remove.

## A worked illustration: the QR-code brunch

The rozumem (2026) essay applies the fallacy to a restaurant that replaced physical menus and human bill-handling with QR codes and a self-pay app. The cost case is identical to the doorman's: less paper, fewer staff, lower cost. The unmeasured losses, observed across one six-person brunch:

- **Lost parallelism.** One physical menu per person lets six people read at once; a single QR code forces serialization — diners take turns scanning, and even modern phones fail on the first try.
- **Imposed friction.** Diners who wanted to *be present* were forced onto their phones; an unrelated parking-expiry notification then hijacked the table's attention and the conversation never recovered. The technology pulled people out of the social experience it was supposed to streamline.
- **Coordination collapse at payment.** Six people splitting a bill simultaneously through the app produced chaos the human waiters used to absorb. The UI showed only the remaining unpaid total, not which items were settled — manufacturing doubt, risk of double-payment, and the forced disclosure of a quiet gesture (paying for a shared dessert) that a human transaction would have kept discreet. The evening ended with the staff chasing a leftover 24 Dhs that the system couldn't reconcile.

The author's synthesis matches Sutherland's: the diners will still remember the brunch fondly, but "somewhere in our subconscious" the friction registers, and the next large-group outing is marginally less appealing. The cost shows up not as a line item but as slightly suppressed future demand — exactly the kind of effect the original optimization had no way to see.

## Implications

- **The human was the error-handling layer.** Much of what a removed human did was absorb edge cases — the split bill, the confused customer, the discreet exception. Automating the happy path while deleting the human leaves the edge cases to fail loudly.
- **"Easier for one" is not "easier for the group."** The waitress's claim that self-pay is easier holds for one or two diners and inverts at six. Per-user optimization can degrade the multi-user case it was never tested against.
- **It is not an anti-technology argument.** The failure is not the QR code as such but replacing a human *without accounting for the full job they did*. The corrective is to enumerate the unmeasured functions before cutting, not to refuse automation.
