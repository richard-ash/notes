# Category Creation: Selling the Transformation, Not the Product

Stewart Butterfield's July 2013 internal memo to Tiny Speck — sent two weeks before Slack's preview release and later published as "We Don't Sell Saddles Here" — is one of the canonical texts on category creation. It frames a question every greenfield startup faces: when no one is shopping for what you've built, how do you sell it?

Butterfield's answer: don't sell the product. Sell the change in human behavior the product enables. This is now the standard playbook for category creation, but the memo is striking for articulating it from inside a company that hadn't yet shipped.

## The core argument

Slack worked. Teams that adopted it were genuinely better off. But "almost all of them have no idea that they want Slack" — they thought they wanted something different (or nothing at all), and "the current fax machine works fine."

Butterfield's reframe: there are not enough buyers for "a group chat system." There are many buyers for **organizational transformation** — "a reduction in the cost of communication," "zero effort knowledge management," "75% less email." The software is just the part Tiny Speck could build and ship; the thing being sold is a different way of working.

He generalizes through the title metaphor. Acme Saddle Co. could sell saddles, competing on leather quality and price. Or it could sell *horseback riding* — sponsoring kids' programs, building trail maps, growing the whole market. The latter "lets them think big and potentially be big." Same product, dramatically larger TAM.

Lululemon is his preferred modern example: they sold yoga (free classes, ambassadors, scholarships) until they had a $1.4B market for yoga apparel. Harley sells freedom. Luxury brands sell "being better than you are."

> "The best possible way to find product-market fit is to define your own market."

## Five operational principles

The memo embeds a tactical framework, not just a slogan:

**1. Marketing from both ends.** Pre-PMF startups are usually told to iterate the product. Butterfield argues you should iterate the *market's understanding* in parallel — "doing a better job of providing what people want (whether they know it or not)" while simultaneously "communicating the above more and more effectively (so that they know they want it)." The two should form a dialectic: usage patterns suggest new framings; new framings clarify which features matter.

**2. Innovation = behavior change.** "The best — maybe the only? — real, direct measure of 'innovation' is change in human behaviour." Not novelty, not technical sophistication, not features. If users don't spend their time differently after adopting your product, you haven't innovated.

**3. The reward must justify the ask.** Customers are being asked to abandon decades of email habits, ad-hoc workflows, and a private-by-default communication model. To say yes to a request that large, the *reward* (who they get to become) must be commensurate, and the *execution* must be near-perfect.

**4. Tolerance is asymmetric.** "When you want something really bad, you will put up with a lot of flaws. But if you do not yet know you want something, your tolerance will be much lower." This is the central reason category-defining products must be more polished than category-extending ones — the buyer arrives skeptical.

**5. Define the customer's future self.** Butterfield's framing question — "who do we want our customers to become?" — borrowed from a Harvard Business Review ebook he describes as "fairly mediocre" but whose central thesis he found useful. For Slack: relaxed, productive workers; masters of their information; people who communicate purposively. The marketing copy and the product roadmap both serve this future self.

## Why "build a customer base" beats "gain market share"

Butterfield deliberately avoids "market share" language. In an undefined category there is no share to gain — there is only a customer base to build. This isn't pedantry: it shapes how the team operates. "We are an exceptional software development team. But, we now also need be an excellent customer development team."

The implication is that everyone — engineers, designers, support — is doing customer development. Onboarding copy, loading screens, error states, welcome emails are all part of "translating the value into their terms." There is no functional silo where marketing happens.

## Connections

- **Distribution.** [[distribution-strategy]] catalogs how distribution failure (not product failure) kills most startups. Butterfield's argument extends this: when you're creating a category, distribution and positioning are inseparable — you have to teach the market what to ask for before any channel can deliver it.
- **Brand mechanics.** [[cultural-imprinting]] (Simler) explains how brand ads work by altering shared cultural meanings rather than conditioning individual emotions. Selling "horseback riding" rather than saddles is cultural-imprinting strategy applied to category creation: change what the cultural artifact *means*, not just what people associate with the brand.
- **PMF as a phase, not a milestone.** [[early-stage-startup-execution]] and [[startup-growth-metrics]] both treat product-market fit as a measurable state to reach. Butterfield's framing is compatible but adds a wrinkle: when you're defining the market, the fit target is also moving — refinements to how you communicate value reshape what counts as fit.
- **Live in the future.** [[organic-startup-ideation]] (Graham) argues the best startup ideas are noticed by founders living in the future. Butterfield is describing the marketing problem that follows: once you've noticed it, most of the world is still in the present and doesn't know to want what you've built.
- **Lifestyle as product.** [[coca-cola]] is the longest-running case study of selling a lifestyle (refreshment, happiness, modernity) rather than a beverage. Same playbook, applied across 140 years.
- **Execution quality as positioning.** [[linear-design-process]] and [[design-process-ai-era]] both emphasize that craft and speed are now first-class strategic assets. Butterfield's "polish every rough edge until this product is as smooth as lacquered mahogany" is the same insight from 2013 — when the buyer hasn't decided they want it, every petty irritation reads as evidence they shouldn't.

## Caveats

The memo is opinionated and was written for a specific moment (a B2B SaaS preview launch into a contested-but-undefined category). A few claims travel less well than the framing:

- **Survivorship bias.** Slack succeeded; the memo reads as prescience. Many category-creation attempts that followed Butterfield's playbook (selling lifestyles, defining new markets) did not work, often because the underlying behavior change wasn't actually valuable enough to justify the switch. The framework is necessary but not sufficient.
- **"Define your own market" as advice.** This works when you've actually noticed a real shift the rest of the world hasn't yet — Butterfield's bet that "centralized internal communication systems will gradually replace email" turned out correct. When founders apply the framework to imagined shifts, they end up with positioning that no one buys.
- **Execution-quality argument is universal, not category-specific.** Butterfield frames craft as especially important for new categories. In practice, it's important everywhere — the asymmetry he describes (lower tolerance from skeptical buyers) is real but the bar in established categories has also risen since 2013.
