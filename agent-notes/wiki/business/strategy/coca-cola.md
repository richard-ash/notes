# Coca-Cola

A 140-year case study in building the world's most recognized consumer brand through capital-light franchising, lifestyle advertising, and relentless scale economics. Coca-Cola's core business model is remarkably simple: manufacture flavored syrup and spend money on marketing, while outsourcing everything else.

## Origins: Patent Medicine to Soft Drink (1886)

Coca-Cola was invented in the post-Civil War patent medicine boom — the same industry that birthed Luden's, Vicks VapoRub, Vaseline, Listerine, Dr. Pepper, and the modern advertising industry itself. Confederate veteran Dr. John Pemberton, addicted to morphine from war injuries, experimented with coca leaves (cocaine) and kola nuts (caffeine) to create a stimulating beverage.

When Atlanta enacted prohibition in 1885, Pemberton pivoted from his cocaine-fortified wine (itself a copy of the French product Vin Mariani) to a non-alcoholic soft drink. In April 1886, he arrived at the formula: sugar, synthetic caffeine from Merck, caramel coloring, citric and phosphoric acid, vanilla, orange oil, cinnamon oil, neroli, and de-cocainized coca leaf extract. A local druggist mixed the syrup with carbonated water, creating the fizzy drink.

Pemberton's bookkeeper and business partner Frank Robinson named it "Coca-Cola" and designed the Spencerian script logo in 1887 — both essentially unchanged since.

**Key insight:** The original Coca-Cola had roughly 4x today's caffeine content and trace cocaine. It was effectively an energy drink. The cocaine was gradually removed (fully gone by 1903–1905 via a de-cocainization process from Schafer Alkaloid Works of Maywood, New Jersey — still the sole supplier of de-cocainized coca leaves to Coca-Cola under a DEA exemption).

## The First Manufacturer's Coupon

Robinson and Pemberton invented the manufacturer's coupon — redeemable tickets for free glasses of Coke mailed to every address in the Atlanta city directory and distributed by traveling salesmen. The oldest known coupon in America is an 1888 Coca-Cola ticket.

This aligned incentives across the entire value chain:
- **Consumers** got free drinks of an addictive, stimulating beverage
- **Soda fountains** got foot traffic and 80%+ retail margins ($6.40 revenue per $1.30 gallon of syrup)
- **Traveling salesmen** got a free benefit to offer customers

## Asa Candler and Professionalization (1892)

After Pemberton secretly sold off rights before his death, businessman Asa Candler reunited ownership for just $2,300 and incorporated the Coca-Cola Company in 1892. First-year results: $46K revenue, $12K profit, with only three employees — each effectively earning 8x the average household income of $500.

Candler and Robinson pioneered point-of-sale ubiquity: oil cloth signs, building murals, billboards, streetcar ads, calendars, serving trays, clocks — over one million branded promotional items per year by 1898. They fabricated and delivered free signs for drugstores bearing both the store's name and Coca-Cola's, making stores look like Coca-Cola franchises.

By 1895, Coca-Cola was sold in every US state and territory. By 1905, the Federal Trademark Act let them sue and shut down over 7,000 copycat cola brands. A 1920 Supreme Court ruling established that "Coca-Cola means a single thing coming from a single source and well-known to the community" — the trademark had transcended its descriptive roots.

## The Bottling Franchise: Best and Worst Deal in History (1899)

In 1899, two Chattanooga entrepreneurs (Benjamin Thomas and Joseph Whitehead) convinced the skeptical Candler to let them bottle Coca-Cola at their own expense. The contract terms:

- **Price:** $1 per gallon of syrup (never renegotiable)
- **Retail price:** fixed at 5¢ per bottle
- **Territory:** exclusive, assignable rights across nearly all of the US
- **Term:** perpetual (no expiration)
- **Cost to Coca-Cola:** $1 token price (never collected)

Thomas and Whitehead split up, then sub-franchised to hundreds of local entrepreneurs. By 1910: 400 bottlers. By 1925: 1,200. The Coca-Cola company entered the bottled market with zero capital investment and zero operational burden.

**The system today:** 200 bottling partners worldwide with 950 facilities, employing 700,000 people. The Coca-Cola Company itself has 70,000 employees. The company captures 27% of system revenue ($47B of $175B) with just 10% of employees — and at far higher margins and returns on invested capital.

This is the "Coca-Cola system": more analogous to Visa's network-of-networks model than to a traditional vertically integrated manufacturer. The bottlers are a [[competitive-moats|cornered resource]] — locked into exclusive territories, printing money, and not bottling for anyone else.

**The fixed-price trap became a weapon.** The $1/gallon and 5¢/bottle locks couldn't be raised, which squeezed Coke's margins with inflation — but also crushed subscale competitors during the Depression. Coke had latent pricing power it chose not to exercise, forcing competitors to match its price at much worse margins.

## The Contour Bottle (1916)

The design brief to 10 glass companies: "a bottle so distinct that you would recognize it by feel in the dark or lying broken on the ground." Root Glass Company of Terre Haute, Indiana won with the iconic contour ("Mae West") bottle — inadvertently based on a misidentification of the coca plant. Patented 1915; through iterative patent renewals, protection lasted until 1951 when Coca-Cola successfully argued it deserved trademark status. By 1949, less than 1% of Americans could not identify a Coke bottle by shape alone.

## Robert Woodruff and the Invention of Lifestyle Advertising (1923–1985)

Robert Woodruff became president in 1923 at age 33 and controlled the company for the next 62 years. He was the most transformative figure in Coca-Cola's history.

### From Intrinsic to Extrinsic Advertising

With ad man Archie Lee at D'Arcy Agency, Woodruff made a radical pivot. Early Coke advertising was intrinsic ("ideal brain tonic and sovereign remedy for headache"). The new approach was extrinsic — associating Coca-Cola with feelings, not features. See [[cultural-imprinting]] for the theoretical framework.

Lee stripped ad copy to minimal slogans:
- 1923: "Coca-Cola always delightful"
- 1924: "Refresh yourself"
- 1929: "The pause that refreshes" — the most successful campaign of the era, resonating deeply during the Depression

Lee's commandments for Coca-Cola advertising:
- Never split "Coca-Cola" across two lines
- Never refer to Coca-Cola as "it"
- Never use Coca-Cola in the personal sense ("Coca-Cola invites you...")

They commissioned Norman Rockwell, N.C. Wyeth, and Haddon Sundblom to paint idyllic American lifestyle scenes for Coca-Cola campaigns.

### Santa Claus (1931)

Coke didn't invent Santa, but effectively standardized the modern Santa. Before 1931, Santa's appearance varied (sometimes small/elflike, various colors). Sundblom painted Santa as big, jolly, and draped in Coca-Cola red for a Saturday Evening Post ad — at a time when mass-produced color images were new and Santa had no canonical color. Sundblom created annual Coca-Cola Santa paintings for 33 years (through 1964). The result: Coca-Cola essentially owns Christmas in the consumer imagination.

### Gas Stations and Vending Machines

Woodruff's prior career at White Motor Company (trucks) and being recruited by Standard Oil of New Jersey (Exxon) gave him the insight: get Coke into gas stations. They installed 32,000 branded coolers in gas stations in the first year alone. Gas station owners loved it — they made more margin on Coke than on gasoline. First coin-operated vending machines followed in 1937.

## World War II: "The Greatest Sampling Program in History"

Coke secured sugar ration exemptions for military supply, and Eisenhower granted Coca-Cola employees "technical observer" status — they traveled with the advancing military, setting up 64 portable bottling plants across Asia, Europe, and North Africa. An estimated 5–10 billion bottles were distributed to troops.

GI letters became powerful marketing: "If anyone were to ask us what we are fighting for, we think half of us would answer the right to buy Coca-Cola again."

The war accelerated 25 years of international market development into four. By 1950, a third of Coke's profits came from abroad. Time Magazine put Coke on its cover: an anthropomorphized Coca-Cola disc, larger than the Earth, feeding the smiling planet a bottle of Coke. Caption: "World and Friend."

**Fanta's origin:** Cut off from Coca-Cola supply during the war, German bottlers improvised a substitute drink from available ingredients. That drink was Fanta.

## The Cola Wars

### Pepsi's Counter-Positioning

Pepsi tried to sell itself to Coca-Cola three times and was rejected each time. In 1934, backs against the wall, Pepsi started selling 12-ounce recycled beer bottles for the same 5¢ as Coke's 6.5-ounce contour bottle. Coca-Cola couldn't respond — they were locked into the contour bottle's capital investment and IP.

This is textbook [[competitive-moats|counter-positioning]]: twice the cola for the same price, exploiting the fact that incremental liquid costs almost nothing (the expensive inputs are sugar and the bottle, not the water).

A trademark settlement gave Pepsi permanent rights to use "cola" — destroying Coke's ability to claim exclusive ownership of the term.

### Pepsi's Post-War Playbook (Alfred Steele, 1950s)

Former Coke executive Alfred Steele defected to Pepsi and executed three radical moves:
1. **Marketed to Black Americans** — all-Black sales team, campaigns with Black celebrities
2. **Positioned as "lighter"** — diet-adjacent messaging before diet products existed
3. **Embraced television** — discovered James Dean (his first acting job was a Pepsi commercial)

Pepsi's US share jumped from low-20s to 35% by 1955.

### The Hilltop Ad (1971)

Bill Backer at McCann Erickson created "I'd Like to Buy the World a Coke" — considered one of the greatest TV commercials ever, and the inspiration for the *Mad Men* series finale. Shot after three failed attempts (rain in England and Rome), cobbled together from two locations with a replacement cast found wandering Rome. The song became a standalone hit record.

### The Pepsi Challenge (1975)

John Sculley (later CEO of Apple) discovered via a Dallas bottler's local agency that consumers preferred Pepsi in blind taste tests — a secret Coke's own agency McCann had discovered internally in the late 1950s and buried at Woodruff's orders.

Sculley's genius was keeping it grassroots: he bought home video camcorders and card tables, distributed them to local bottlers, and had real people take the challenge on camera for local TV ads. This was arguably the first "reality television commercial" — and Coke's national one-sight-one-sound-one-sell infrastructure couldn't respond to local guerrilla marketing. From 1975 to 1985, Pepsi's share grew and Coke's declined every single year.

### The McDonald's Relationship

Ray Kroc's 1955 handshake deal with Coke's fountain division became one of the deepest corporate partnerships in history — no written contract for 40 years. Coke tastes better at McDonald's because of: stainless steel syrup tanks (vs bags), pre-chilled water and hoses, a higher syrup-to-water ratio (accounting for ice melt), wider straws, and fresher syrup from high volume. Coke sales teams are prohibited from selling syrup to any restaurant for less than McDonald's pays.

## Diet Coke and New Coke (1982–1985)

### Diet Coke

Launched at Radio City Music Hall in July 1982 with the Rockettes performing. Became the #1 diet drink in America within a year, and the #3 soft drink overall by 1984. The campaign — "Just for the taste of it" — played offense rather than apologizing for being diet.

### New Coke

CEO Roberto Goizueta — a Cuban immigrant chemical engineer who was one of the mythical two people who knew the secret formula — replaced the original Coca-Cola formula on April 23, 1985. New Coke beat both Pepsi and original Coke in 200,000-person blind taste tests. They never asked how people would feel if it *replaced* old Coke.

Woodruff reportedly gave his blessing on New Year's Day 1985. He stopped eating the next morning, was hospitalized, and died March 7 — one month before the announcement. The formula was never changed while Woodruff was alive.

Pepsi took out full-page ads: "After 87 years of going at it eyeball to eyeball, the other guy just blinked." They gave all employees the day off.

The backlash was immediate and visceral. Letters poured in: "You have become corrupted by money denying your ideals." A Georgia woman attacked a delivery man with her umbrella. After just 79 days, old Coke returned as "Coca-Cola Classic."

**The paradox:** Within a year, Coca-Cola Classic surged past its pre-disaster heights. New Coke accidentally became the greatest publicity stunt in marketing history. As Don Keough said: "The truth is, we are not that dumb and we are not that smart." The Acquired hosts disagree: they were both.

## Buffett's Investment

Warren Buffett (a former Pepsi man, converted by Cherry Coke via his old neighbor Don Keough) invested ~$1.3B in Coca-Cola stock after the New Coke disaster. Berkshire Hathaway holds ~9.5% today worth ~$28B, plus ~$12B in cumulative dividends — a ~$40B total return. But over 40 years that's only ~10% IRR, underperforming the S&P 500's ~11% annual return over the same period.

At Sun Valley, Bill Gates mentioned Buffett's quip that "Coca-Cola could be run by a ham sandwich." CEO Roberto Goizueta, sitting right there, reportedly never spoke to Gates again.

## The Total Beverage Company (2000s–Present)

The story of the last 25 years is Coca-Cola trying to diversify beyond colas amid an obesity crisis (a 12oz Coke has 39g of sugar; the AHA daily limit is 25–36g). Revenue growth has averaged only 3–4% annually since 1998.

**Missed acquisitions:**
- Frito-Lay (1965) — Atlanta-based, preferred buyer, turned it down. Pepsi bought it; Frito-Lay now generates 2x Pepsi's beverage profits.
- Quaker Oats/Gatorade (2000) — CEO announced the $16B deal without board approval. Board killed it. Pepsi swooped in.
- Monster Energy (2012) — market cap was $11B, Coke passed. Monster is now worth $70B.

**What they did do:**
- Bought Minute Maid (1960), Glaceau/Vitaminwater ($4B), BodyArmor ($5B), Costa Coffee, Topo Chico
- Took a 20% stake in Monster for ~$2B (now worth ~$12B) with a cross-brand swap deal
- Launched Coke Zero (2005) — growing ~10% annually for years, the biggest organic success alongside Diet Coke

## Seven Powers Analysis

The Acquired hosts apply Hamilton Helmer's [[competitive-moats|Seven Powers]] framework:

- **Scale economies** (primary power): Coca-Cola can amortize advertising, manufacturing, and distribution over unmatched volume. When Pepsi outspent Coke in advertising in 1977, it signaled real danger — breaking the scale advantage.
- **Branding**: Unusual case — brand power manifests not through premium pricing but through latent pricing power. Coke keeps prices low and uses ubiquity itself as brand reinforcement (every bottle sold is a billboard). The New Coke backlash is the one-time natural experiment proving brand depth.
- **Cornered resource**: Debated. The secret formula is theatrics — Pepsi has a formula people prefer, and someone who stole Coke's formula tried selling it to Pepsi, who called the FBI. The *bottlers* may be the true cornered resource: locked into exclusive territories, deeply incentivized, and not available to competitors.
- **Counter-positioning**: Coke was the one *getting* counter-positioned (by Pepsi's 12oz bottles, TV advertising, Pepsi Challenge).
- **Switching costs, network economies, process power**: Minimal to none.

## Key Principles

**"Everyone who has anything to do with Coca-Cola should make money."** — Robert Woodruff's motto. The entire system — bottlers, retailers, gas stations, restaurants, billboard owners — is incentivized to promote Coca-Cola. During the Depression, billboard companies put up free Coca-Cola ads rather than show blank walls.

**Trade secret over patent.** Coca-Cola deliberately never patented the formula, keeping it as a trade secret to avoid eventual public disclosure. The lore (two people know it, they can't travel together, it sits in a vault) is more valuable as mystique than the formula is as chemistry.

**Repetition works.** Unlike luxury brands that chase novelty, Coke has repeated the same core message — delicious, refreshing, happiness — for 140 years. The medium changes; the message doesn't.

**It's a system, not a company.** The Coca-Cola Company sells syrup and marketing. The system of 200+ bottling partners, thousands of retailers, and millions of touchpoints is what delivers 2.2 billion daily servings. Understanding Coca-Cola requires looking at the system holistically.
