# Strategy Execution

Why most "strategy execution" failures are not execution failures at all — they are failures of strategy formulation upstream. London Business School professor Freek Vermeulen's argument: when companies announce a "strategy" that is really a goal, a wishlist, or a list of priorities that don't fit together, no amount of town halls, balanced scorecards, or change-management theater will produce results. The fix begins with the definition of strategy itself.

## A strategy is a coherent set of choices

Vermeulen argues a real strategy must satisfy two tests:

1. **It specifies what the firm will do and what it will *not* do.** A goal ("be #1 or #2 in every market we operate in") describes a hoped-for outcome, not a plan to get there. Goals masquerading as strategies are the most common failure mode.
2. **The choices must fit together.** A list of independently reasonable decisions ("increase operational efficiency; target EMEA; divest business X") does not become a strategy by virtue of being on the same slide. Strategy is a *system* of mutually reinforcing choices.

This connects directly to Porter's framing in [[competitive-moats]]: "A strategy delineates a territory in which a company seeks to be unique" — and is defined as much by what you refuse to do as by what you do.

## The Hornby case

Vermeulen's worked example: around the early 2000s, Hornby Railways (UK maker of model trains and Scalextric slot cars) was facing bankruptcy. CEO Frank Martin's strategy was three coherent choices:

1. Make **perfect scale models**, not toys
2. Target **adult collectors**, not children
3. Lean into **nostalgia** — appeal to childhood memories

The choices reinforce each other: collectors will pay for fidelity that children won't notice; nostalgia is an adult emotion; adults have more disposable income for hobby spending. Share price went from £35 to £250 over five years.

Vermeulen notes a coda: Hornby later abandoned the choice set and issued a string of profit warnings — evidence that the discipline of *holding* the choices is as important as making them.

## Communicate the *logic*, not just the choices

Sly Bailey (then CEO of Trinity Mirror) told Vermeulen: focus on the *logic* behind the choices at least as much as the choices themselves. Hornby's engineers and designers could recite the three choices — but they could also explain *why* (iconic brands resonate with adults who remember them; hobby markets have higher switching costs and less competition; etc.). That's why behavior actually changed.

The implication: a strategy you can't explain with a brief causal chain is one your employees can't act on. A 20-item priority list fails this test mechanically — no one remembers 20 items, so 20 items cannot influence behavior.

## Top-down direction + bottom-up experimentation

Vermeulen leans on Stanford's Robert Burgelman: *"Successful firms maintain bottom-up internal experimentation and selection processes while simultaneously maintaining top-driven strategic intent."*

The Intel example: Intel's top-down strategy was three choices — be on the *forefront* of *semiconductor* technology, aimed at the *memory* business. But execution was decentralized: groups had autonomy and budgets to experiment within those boundaries. Many experiments failed; one became the Pentium microprocessor (which incidentally took Intel out of memory and into microprocessors entirely — the bottom-up experiments selected a new strategic position the top-down direction did not anticipate).

This pattern echoes Rabois in [[how-to-operate]]: high-conviction delegation within a clear focus, not micromanagement masquerading as alignment.

## Let selection happen organically

A common failure: top managers can't resist hand-picking which bottom-up initiatives live or die. Vermeulen's prescription is the opposite — design a *system* that does the selection. At Intel, production capacity was assigned by formula, not executive whim; division managers chose what technologies to staff, so projects no one believed in starved automatically.

The skill is *resisting* the urge to pick winners and instead designing the selection mechanism. (This is structurally the same problem as letting [[network-effects|network-effects platforms]] tip without trying to engineer the tipping point manually.)

## Make change the default

Most implementation requires changing habits. Habits are sticky and often invisible to the people performing them. Vermeulen's heuristic: invert the default. Instead of "we'll change this only if the new way is clearly better," try "we'll change this unless the old way is clearly better." Concretely:

- Take on clients or projects that *force* the firm to learn the new strategy
- Reshuffle people across units to disrupt habitual patterns
- Audit "why do we do it this way?" — if the only answer is "we've always done it this way," that's a candidate for change

## Synthesis: where the failure modes stack

Vermeulen's four pathologies of failed execution chain together:

| Failure | Symptom | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Not a strategy | Goals or priority lists masquerading as strategy | Force a small set of mutually reinforcing choices |
| No transmitted logic | Employees know the choices but not the reasoning | Communicate causal chains, not just slogans |
| Pure top-down | Strategy delivered as a finished cascade | Pair clear intent with autonomous experimentation |
| Centralized selection | Execs pick winners among bottom-up initiatives | Design a selection *mechanism*; let it run |
| Sticky habits | "We've always done it this way" | Default to change unless the old way is clearly better |

The article is short on its own "what is a coherent choice set" methodology — it diagnoses the failure mode and shows successes (Hornby, Intel) but does not give a template for *generating* the three-to-five fitting choices in the first place. That gap is what Rumelt's *Good Strategy/Bad Strategy* and Lafley & Martin's *Playing to Win* address; future ingestions on those works should integrate here.
