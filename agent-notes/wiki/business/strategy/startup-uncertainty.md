# Startup Uncertainty

Why do startups exist at all, given that incumbents have more capital, more talent, more distribution, and more time? Jerry Neumann's answer, drawing on Frank Knight's 1921 distinction between *risk* and *uncertainty*, is that high-growth startups occupy a structural niche that is **not** "we are smarter than incumbents" but rather "we are willing to act in conditions where incumbents are forbidden by their own decision processes from acting." Uncertainty is not the headwind founders fight through — it is the moat that exists only at the moment when no other moat does.

This is the third article in Neumann's strategy trilogy, alongside [[entrepreneurial-profit|Schumpeter on Strategy]] (where surplus profit comes from) and [[competitive-moats|A Taxonomy of Moats]] (which decay-slowing mechanisms exist). The trilogy's combined thesis: surplus profit comes from innovation; durable surplus requires a moat; at founding, the only moat is the uncertainty that keeps incumbents out.

## Knight's distinction: risk vs. uncertainty

Mainstream economics often collapses these into one. Neumann, following Frank Knight, insists they are categorically different:

- **Risk** is *quantifiable* unpredictability. The dice will roll seven 1/6 of the time. The expected value of a portfolio of independent risks converges. Insurance can transform risk into a known cost via aggregation. Casinos and insurance companies make a living on risk.
- **Uncertainty** is *unquantifiable* unpredictability. There is no probability distribution to form, because the experiment has never been run, the market does not yet exist, or the system in question is too complex for cause and effect to be traced. No insurance is available, because no actuarial base exists.

Knight's own framing: "A measurable uncertainty, or 'risk' proper, is so far different from an unmeasurable one that it is not in effect an uncertainty at all." Keynes, in 1937, gave canonical examples: roulette and life expectancy are not uncertain in this sense; "the prospect of a European war… or the obsolescence of a new invention… or the position of private wealth-owners in the social system in 1970" are.

The bridge to startup strategy: there are exactly two ways to make reliable predictions about the future, and **both fail for high-growth startups**:

1. **Deduction** — playing out an inevitable cause-and-effect chain. Requires knowing the starting state and all transition mechanisms. Fails for startups because they operate in a complex network of agents (employees, suppliers, competitors, customers, regulators, financiers, society) reacting to each other.
2. **Induction** — assuming the future resembles the past. Fails for startups doing something genuinely new, because there is no historical base rate to draw from.

When neither deduction nor induction works, what remains is uncertainty in Knight's strong sense — and this is, by definition, the territory of any high-growth-potential startup.

## Why incumbents cannot enter uncertain markets

Neumann's load-bearing claim is not that incumbents are stupid or that big companies are bureaucratic. It is that they are, individually and structurally, **rationally** uncertainty-averse. Two distinct mechanisms reinforce each other.

### The cognitive layer: ambiguity aversion

Even individual decision-makers, even well-informed ones, prefer quantified risk to unquantified uncertainty:

- **Ellsberg paradox** (1961). Two urns: the first has 50 black + 50 red balls; the second has 100 balls of unknown red/black mix. People asked to bet on red prefer urn 1. Asked again to bet on black, they *still* prefer urn 1. They are not implicitly assigning a 50/50 prior to urn 2 — if they were, they'd switch. They simply prefer the devil they can quantify to the one they can't.
- **Zeckhauser's asteroid bet.** Offered $2,000 if a numbered ball draws #17 (1% known) vs. $1,000 if a 10,000-ton asteroid passed within 40,000 miles in the previous decade (subjectively estimated at 3%, so 3% expected value of $30 vs. $20), most people pick the smaller-EV quantified option.

These results undermine the L.J. Savage view that "you can find someone to sell you insurance on anything, no matter their lack of actuarial data." People — and the firms they staff — prefer the known-probability gamble even when the unknown one has higher expected value.

### The structural layer: justification regimes

Large organizations amplify ambiguity aversion through the way decisions get approved. Investment memos, financial models, payback periods, DCFs — these are not just discipline tools; they are **justification regimes**. Decisions made within them can be defended ex post regardless of outcome ("the model said 50% chance of 3x"); decisions made outside them cannot. Experiments find that anticipating having to explain a choice makes ambiguity even more aversive than in private decision-making.

This means a manager presenting an uncertain bet faces a structural impossibility:

> "How likely is this to succeed?" "I don't know." "How big will it be if it works?" "I don't know." "Why don't you know?" "Because the customers may be different than who we think; because the customers may want a somewhat different product; because the other companies we need to produce complementary products may decide not to."

Neumann argues this conversation never approves a project. And it is not because the boss is stupid — it is because the boss has no defensible way to approve it. Christensen makes the same point in [[entrepreneurial-profit|disruption-theory]] terms: "Companies whose investment processes demand quantification of market sizes and financial returns before they can enter a market get paralyzed or make serious mistakes when faced with disruptive technologies."

Kenneth Arrow's framing closes the loop: the information needed to make the decision will be produced *by the market that doesn't yet exist*. The optimizer is structurally too late; only an actor unconstrained by justification can create the information by acting. That actor is the founder.

### Why this is *rational* for incumbents

Crucially, Neumann does not claim incumbents are wrong to behave this way. They have moats; they earn surplus profit from durable innovations; their job is to keep that going. Risk-based portfolios across many measurable bets work fine for them. **Uncertainty-aversion is the right policy for an organization with assets to defend**, because the cost of defending against a bad uncertain bet (loss of the asset, or just internal politics if the bet fails) outweighs the expected value of any single uncertain success. This is why the Type-2 Schumpeterian role ([[entrepreneurial-profit#two-fundamentally-different-kinds-of-startups|Type 1 vs Type 2]]) almost has to fall to outsiders.

## Uncertainty as the only first-day moat

Connect this to the [[competitive-moats|four-source moat taxonomy]]: state-granted, special know-how, returns to scale, system rigidity. None of these is reliably available at founding (a patent is fungibly priced; closely-held knowledge is rare and leaks; scale requires scale; rigidity requires embedding). What protects a startup in the *interim* — between founding and the moment a real moat is built — is the uncertainty that keeps incumbents out.

Neumann's load-bearing inversion: **the existence of a startup is evidence that the founders disagree with the market's valuation of the opportunity.** If everyone could see the future, an incumbent would buy whatever input the startup is built around (the patent, the know-how, the team) at fair value, and there would be no surplus left for the startup to capture. A startup *is* a bet that the market is mispricing the future because it cannot price what it cannot quantify.

This reframes the question every early-stage founder and investor implicitly faces: not "is this startup going to win?" but "is the uncertainty thick enough and long-lasting enough to give the startup time to build a real moat before the market wakes up?" Once the uncertainty resolves — once a market is shown to exist, once a business model is shown to work — incumbents and lateral entrants flood in. Apple and Google survived this transition because each had **converted their uncertainty window into one of the four real moats** before it closed: Apple into brand, ecosystem, and learning-curve scale; Google into network effects (ad inventory, search-quality data feedback loops) and infrastructure scale.

The framing implies a strategic clock: the moment your startup's strategy becomes legible to outsiders, your moat starts to evaporate. Wise founders use the uncertainty window to build something that survives its end.

## The Apple and Google case studies

Neumann grounds the abstract argument in two famous case studies, each illustrating a different aspect of the uncertainty moat.

### Apple (1976–1981): market existence uncertainty

When Mike Markkula said Apple would be a Fortune 500 company in two years, Apple had ~$200K in revenue and had sold ~175 computers, mostly to hobbyists. Meanwhile, IBM, DEC, and the "seven dwarfs" of mainframe and minicomputer manufacturers had decades of computing experience, vastly more capital, and far better-resourced engineering organizations. Why didn't they enter the personal computer market in 1976?

Because **no one knew if there was a personal computer market**. Fewer than 50,000 PCs had been sold; software didn't exist; the spreadsheet was years away; word processing was better on dedicated machines; games were better in arcades. The PC "did nothing best." There was no defensible analysis a manager at IBM could write to justify a billion-dollar push into a market with no demonstrated customer segment.

The companies that *did* enter early enough — HP's "Capricorn," Xerox — could not resist tailoring their products to the less-uncertain audiences they already understood (scientific/professional users at price points starting at $16K). They built for the customers their existing processes could conceive of. Apple alone bet on the *yet-unproven* consumer and small-business markets, which turned out to be the actual prize.

IBM only entered in 1981, when Bill Lowe convinced executives by **showing them how big the PC market had already become** — i.e., once Apple, Tandy, and Commodore had themselves resolved enough of the uncertainty to make the market analyzable. Apple's five-year head start built the brand and balance sheet that let them survive IBM's entry.

### Google (1998–early 2000s): business-model uncertainty

Google had a different uncertainty profile. The technology was demonstrably better — PC Magazine ranked it a top-five search engine three months after incorporation. Google had a patent (a real, if narrow, moat). What it didn't have was **a known way to make money**. Page and Brin tried to sell the technology to Yahoo!, Excite, and others for $1M and were rejected — not because the buyers were stupid, but because better search results were *strategically bad* for incumbent search engines under the prevailing portal/banner-ad business model: better results meant users left the site faster, lowering ad revenue. The CEO of Excite reportedly wanted his search engine to be "80 percent as good as the other search engines."

The business-model innovation that eventually unlocked Google's value — pay-per-click advertising, originally from Bill Gross at Goto.com — was not a technological breakthrough. It was an idea that "had been nay-sayed by critics across the board" when introduced, and would have been instantly copied by incumbents if it hadn't appeared too uncertain to bother with. Once Google demonstrated PPC alignment between searcher quality and ad revenue, the strategic obvious-in-retrospect quality emerged. Had it been obvious *in advance*, Yahoo! would have copied it and used its distribution advantage to crush Google.

The lesson: Google's moat at founding was not the patent. It was the fact that **no incumbent could justify, ex ante, an investment in better search at the cost of their existing ad business**. The uncertainty was about the future business model — and that uncertainty was sufficient to keep the field open until Google had network-effect scale on the demand side and enough cash to defend it.

## What this implies for founders

Neumann is dense with implications even where he doesn't spell them out. A few that fall out of the framework:

**1. Seek out uncertainty rather than try to eliminate it pre-launch.** A startup whose plan is fully defensible in a justification regime — known market size, known unit economics, known competitor playbook — is *by construction* a startup with no moat. If the plan is that legible, an incumbent can run the same analysis and either build it themselves or buy it. Surplus profit is paid to whoever takes the bet that no one else can defensibly take.

**2. Never claim certainty you don't have, but never apologize for the uncertainty either.** Pitching to a financier looks superficially like pitching to your boss in the Christensen example. The difference is that VCs are professionally selected for ambiguity tolerance, and their explicit job is to underwrite portfolios of uncertain bets. Trying to manufacture defensible-sounding numbers (TAM, conversion rates, projections) for a genuinely uncertain venture often looks weaker than acknowledging the uncertainty and articulating what you'd need to learn to resolve it. (In conversation with [[four-kinds-of-luck|preparation luck]] and the lean-startup methods Neumann name-checks.)

**3. The uncertainty window is depleting from day one.** Every successful experiment, every public milestone, every press cycle erodes the uncertainty that protects you. Founders should think of the early years not as "validating the idea" but as "racing to convert uncertainty into a [[competitive-moats|durable moat]] before the window closes." Anything that increases legibility without correspondingly building moat is, strategically, an own goal.

**4. Lean, customer development, design thinking are tools for navigating uncertainty, not eliminating it.** Neumann credits these methodologies as "created out of necessity by hands-on practitioners" to manage uncertainty. The point is not to make the future predictable — it is to make incremental investments in resolving *which* uncertainties matter most for the next decision, while preserving the option value of the others.

**5. Do not measure yourself against the management practices of large companies.** Most published management science is, implicitly, about decision-making under risk inside an organization with moats. A founder importing those practices wholesale will run their startup through justification regimes designed for incumbents — and find themselves unable to approve the very bets that are the reason the startup exists. The classic mode-confusion that kills founders who came up at McKinsey or Google.

## Connection to the broader strategy literature

Neumann positions his trilogy as a unification: Schumpeter says innovation creates surplus; Knight says the surplus only sticks where uncertainty kept competitors out; the moat literature says how to keep them out *after* the uncertainty resolves. Read this way, much of mainstream startup advice maps to specific moves on the underlying uncertainty curve:

- **Lean / customer development** ([[minimum-viable-product|MVP methodology]]) — incremental uncertainty reduction biased toward the customer-demand axis, preserving capital while you do it.
- **Schlep blindness** ([[schlep-blindness|Graham]]) — describes a *specific class* of uncertainty (operational unpleasantness) that incumbents systematically misprice, creating a niche of available bets.
- **Live in the future** ([[organic-startup-ideation|Graham]]) — a heuristic for identifying which uncertainties are about to resolve favorably, before incumbents can quantify them.
- **Capital discipline** ([[capital-discipline|Stitch Fix playbook]]) — extending the runway you have inside the uncertainty window. Burning capital fast on illegible bets is the failure mode this guards against.
- **Type-2 Schumpeterian framing** ([[entrepreneurial-profit#two-fundamentally-different-kinds-of-startups|Neumann]]) — Type-1 startups operate in *risk*, not uncertainty; Type-2 startups operate in uncertainty. The two require fundamentally different management.
- **Disruption theory** (Christensen) — a specific structural form of uncertainty that protects entrants from incumbents whose existing assets actively impede them from responding.

## Temporal notes

The article is from 2019, before the AI foundation-model wave. Two updates worth flagging:

- **AI foundation models present a current open question** about what kind of uncertainty window protects them. The first generation of GenAI startups (2020–2023) operated in genuine Knightian uncertainty about both technical capability and business model. By 2025, both axes are largely resolved — capabilities are well-characterized, business models (API rev share, vertical SaaS markup, agentic workflows) are mainstream. Per [[ai-platform-moats|Evans]], the substrate may not have the structural-rigidity properties needed for a durable post-uncertainty moat, which would predict short Schumpeterian decay curves despite high spike heights.
- **The "uncertainty window" framing implies a falsifiable prediction about which startup waves create durable winners.** Waves where the uncertainty resolves quickly but no underlying [[competitive-moats|moat]] is available (e.g. "AI wrappers" of generic foundation-model capabilities, most NFT-era projects) should produce no large durable companies even if individual bets win. Waves where uncertainty resolves slowly enough to permit moat construction (social networking 2004–2010, marketplaces 2008–2015, modern fintech 2013–2020) should produce a small set of dominant survivors. The 2010s and 2020s vindicate the framework reasonably well.
- **Markkula's two-year prediction was off by 4x** — Apple took six years to reach the Fortune 500. The point Neumann emphasizes is that the *direction* was right under conditions where any directional prediction was unjustifiable. Modern equivalents (founders confidently sizing markets and timing for uncertain ventures) should be read as expressions of conviction, not as analyses, and judged accordingly.
