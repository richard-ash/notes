# Competitive Moats

A taxonomy of sustainable competitive advantages — what Warren Buffett calls "moats" — drawn from the collected wisdom of investors, operators, and strategists. The central question: what makes a business durably defensible rather than temporarily profitable?

## The moat hierarchy

Not all competitive advantages are equal. Griffin (via Fred Wilson and Michael Porter) suggests a rough ordering by durability:

1. **Network effects** — the strongest moat. Businesses that can't be commoditized because each additional user increases value for all others. Once established, network effects create winner-take-most dynamics that are nearly impossible to dislodge.
2. **Differentiation through strategic focus** — choosing a territory and refusing to compete on your competitor's terms. Porter's core insight: "A strategy delineates a territory in which a company seeks to be unique." Strategy is defined as much by what you choose *not* to do as by what you do.
3. **Operational focus / specialization** — doing a few things extraordinarily well over long time horizons. Scale advantages and cost reductions compound when scope stays narrow (Costco, hawker stands, Dick's Drive-In).
4. **Scalable back-end systems** — physical businesses can build moats, but usually only when powered by great software infrastructure (Amazon, Walmart, McDonald's).

## Optionality and asymmetric bets

Complementary to moat-building is bet selection. Sam Zell's heuristic: low downside + big upside → go; big downside + small upside → run; big downside + big upside → the only case requiring real work.

Taleb formalizes this as **convexity** — propositions that benefit from randomness (unlimited upside, capped downside) should be embraced. Concave propositions (capped upside, unlimited downside) should be avoided. This maps directly to how [[elementary-worldly-wisdom|Munger]] thinks about bet sizing: concentrate heavily when odds are substantially in your favor.

## Supply, scarcity, and pricing

Bill Gates: "Supply is the killer of value." Abundant supply creates platforms, but profit accrues only to whoever attaches something *scarce* on top. Griffin calls the belief that supply itself creates value "George Gilder's folly" — a contributor to the 1999 Internet bubble. Metcalfe himself noted that no one made a nickel on Ethernet itself; profit came from selling things atop the standard.

The corollary (Bezos): "Your margin is my opportunity." High margins attract competition. Companies that deliberately keep margins thin and pass value to customers create a self-reinforcing flywheel that is itself a form of moat — because competitors find no margin pool to fund entry. This connects to [[aggregation-theory|Aggregation Theory]]'s observation that zero-distribution-cost platforms commoditize supply and own demand.

## Cash as survival

Harold Geneen: "The only unforgivable sin in business is to run out of cash." Accounting bankruptcy is survivable; actual illiquidity is fatal. Griffin endorses Munger's view that EBITDA should be mentally replaced with the word "bullshit" — interest, taxes, and depreciation are real expenses of the worst kind.

## Customer acquisition

Bill Gurley: a customer who *chooses* your product is worth far more than one acquired through spend. The essence of durable business is cost-effective customer acquisition. Marketing spend must be tracked per-customer, not as a lump sum — companies spending "$100 million on marketing" without unit economics are headed for a fall. This reinforces why moats matter: they reduce customer acquisition cost to near zero by making the product the obvious choice.

## Hiring

Peter Schutz (former Porsche CEO): "Hire character. Train skill." Character is the scarce input; skill is the trainable one. This mirrors the moat logic applied to human capital — invest in what's durable and hard to replicate.

## Temporal notes

This source is from 2013. The moat taxonomy remains sound, but the relative importance has shifted:
- Network effects have proven even more dominant than anticipated (winner-take-all in social, search, marketplaces).
- AI foundation models have introduced a new question about whether [[ai-platform-moats|model capabilities constitute a moat]] or are rapidly commoditized.
- The "supply kills value" principle has played out dramatically in content (journalism, music, video) where infinite digital supply destroyed per-unit pricing.
