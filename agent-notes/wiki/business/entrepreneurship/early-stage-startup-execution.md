# Early-Stage Startup Execution

A practitioner's account of what the first three years of building a startup actually look like, drawn from a YC S23 founder (u/horrible_normalcy) who built an EMR / practice management platform for clinics. The company went from batch to 150+ clinics and a team of 15 --- a trajectory that felt impossible during the sleep-deprived early months. The post is a single founder's experience in healthcare SaaS, not a general framework, but the lessons map well beyond that vertical.

## The three-year arc

horrible_normalcy describes three distinct phases that may generalize beyond their specific company:

**Year 1: Survival.** The product pitched at YC demo day is not the product they sell today. They pivoted hard, but what stayed constant was the *customer* and the *problem space*. The key claim: if you're deeply embedded with users, the right product reveals itself --- but you have to let go of the version you fell in love with. This echoes the [[startup-ideation-framework]] principle of validating pain over solution: the pain was real, even when the first product built for it was wrong.

**Year 2: Chaos that feels like progress.** Closing customers creates demand faster than a small team can service it. The nature of stress changes shape but doesn't shrink --- early on it's "nothing works," later it's "everything works but it's held together with duct tape and people are depending on us." Neither is easier.

**Year 3: The first time it feels real.** Customers renewing without begging. Features shipping because users asked for them. A team small enough that everyone has full context. They claim to have consistently out-shipped companies with 50x their headcount, attributing this to staying small deliberately.

## Cold calling as a learning engine

horrible_normalcy's most actionable advice centers on founder-led cold calling as the primary early-stage activity --- not primarily for sales, but for learning:

- **Listen for emotional words.** "Nightmare," "hate," "frustrating" signals real pain. Polite "yeah it's kind of annoying" usually isn't enough to drive a purchase.
- **Distinguish interest from politeness.** "Send me a link, looks great" is a polite exit. "When can we start?" is real interest. Confusing the two wastes weeks.
- **Lean into rejection.** Someone telling you exactly why they don't care is more useful than someone nodding along. The calls that feel like failures often produce the sharpest insights.
- **Notice when they start asking you questions.** That's the moment the problem resonated and they're reaching for a solution --- a strong ICP signal.
- **Reframe from selling to learning.** This takes pressure off and, paradoxically, leads to more sales once the product sharpens from what you've learned.

The language prospects use to describe their pain becomes your landing page copy. This is a specific, under-discussed application of customer development: the vocabulary of the customer is itself a product artifact.

## The partner model for 0-to-1

When you have no testimonials or logos, horrible_normalcy argues you shouldn't try to *sign customers* --- you should *recruit partners*:

> "You won't get polish, you'll get us and we'll work harder on your problem than anyone you've ever hired."

The people who say yes to that pitch are your right first customers. The ones who need the safety of a known brand aren't your ICP yet. Those first few partners co-build the product with you. The startup's only real edge as an unknown entity is the willingness to bend the product around the partner's workflow in ways no established vendor will.

This converges with the [[startup-ideation-framework]]'s advice on finding first customers ("go in person," "lean into being early-stage"), but adds a specific framing: the transition from partners who need custom work to customers who don't is itself the signal that you've found ICP.

## Small teams as competitive advantage

horrible_normalcy is emphatic that staying small is a deliberate strategic choice, not a constraint to grow out of:

> "Every unnecessary hire, every premature process, every 'alignment meeting' chips away at the thing that makes you dangerous."

At 15 people, they claim to want to stay small as long as possible. The implicit argument: the speed advantage of a small team compounds over time, and most growth-stage companies trade it away too early for headcount that creates coordination overhead without proportional output.

## Marketing in the AI era

On marketing, horrible_normalcy argues that brand and creative marketing --- not AI-generated content --- will be the primary differentiator for growth. The reasoning: AI has made it trivially easy to build a good-looking website and product, so the scarce resource shifts to creative campaigns that actually reach and resonate with customers. "AI slop is not relatable." They predict CMOs and marketing teams will become the highest-paid people at startups in the coming years.

## The meta-lesson: persistence over any single bet

horrible_normalcy's strongest conviction: **the leading way startups fail is that founders give up.** Don't treat any single moment --- including a YC application --- as make-or-break. The corollary from the [[startup-ideation-framework]]: "never give up" applies to the dream of entrepreneurship, not to any specific idea. But horrible_normalcy adds the emotional dimension: persistence is "a loooooot easier said than done" when factoring in financial pressure, waning passion, and co-founder relationship strain.
