# Minimum Viable Product

The minimum viable product (MVP) is the smallest deliverable a startup can ship to convert an unproven hypothesis about customer value into validated learning. Tren Griffin's synthesis of operator and investor commentary — Eric Ries, Steve Blank, Marc Andreessen, Sam Altman, Bill Gurley, Jessica Livingston — frames the MVP not as a stripped-down product but as an *experiment* whose only purpose is to falsify or confirm the value hypothesis before cash runs out.

## Why an MVP at all: the cash-vs-uncertainty squeeze

The MVP exists because two facts collide. First, most startups die: Mattermark data suggests an ~84% fatality rate at seed stage, and Shikhar Ghosh's research finds 30-40% of high-potential VC-backed startups liquidate entirely. Second, every dollar of seed capital is precious — running out of cash is, in Griffin's framing, "the only unforgivable sin in business" (a principle echoed in [[capital-discipline]]).

The MVP is the response to this squeeze: a tactic for converting limited capital into maximum learning per dollar. Bill Gurley's observation that "it's only cheap to build 2-3 person companies with sweat equity — the minute you start paying engineers you will realize it is quite expensive" sets the cost ceiling. The MVP is what fits inside it.

## Value hypothesis before growth hypothesis

Marc Andreessen: "A full executive team with a salesforce and all that stuff before you have a killer product is a complete waste of time." Andy Rachleff distinguishes the two hypotheses every startup must test:

- **Value hypothesis** — what features, audience, and business model make customers pay
- **Growth hypothesis** — how acquisition, retention, and monetization scale

Spending on growth before validating value is "a bonfire of cash generating zero value." Steve Blank's reframe: early-stage work is *search*, not *execution*. The MVP is the search instrument. This sequencing — validate value, then unlock growth — is the same prescription the [[startup-ideation-framework]] formalizes as Step 5 (test the market) and what [[capital-discipline]] argues over-funded startups skip to their detriment. Once growth begins, [[startup-growth-metrics]] measures it.

## What an MVP actually is

Eric Ries's working definition: "The minimum viable product is that product which has just those features (and no more) that allows you to ship a product that resonates with early adopters; some of whom will pay you money or give you feedback." The decision rule that follows: **"Remove any feature, process, or effort that does not contribute directly to the learning you seek."**

Two extremes Ries warns against:

1. **Maximize chance of success** — five years of stealth R&D, ship one perfect product. Rational only if you have one shot, but the failure mode is finding out at year five that customers don't want it.
2. **Release early, release often** — ship anything, listen to whoever talks. The failure mode: three customers give thirty contradictory opinions and there's no signal.

The MVP is the synthesis: ship something *complete* in what it does (Sam Altman emphasizes the "viable" half of the term — flat, broken first impressions kill the experiment), but radically narrow in what it *attempts*. The classic visual is a slice of cake, not half a cake — every layer present, just smaller — or a skateboard before a car. Yevgeniy Brikman: an MVP "should have the smallest possible feature set that creates gains for customers and reduces pain — but it can't be so small that customers have nothing to evaluate."

## The MVP as a loop, not a deliverable

Brikman's process framing: "Identify your riskiest assumption, find the smallest possible experiment to test that assumption, and use the results of the experiment to course correct." The MVP is depicted as a flywheel — build, measure, learn — because most first attempts fail to validate. The point isn't the artifact; it's the iteration cycle.

Ev Williams's analogy fits: "A plane flying from California to Hawaii is off course 99% of the time but constantly correcting. The same is true of successful startups — except they may start out heading toward Alaska." The MVP is the navigation instrument that makes those corrections cheap.

## MVP need not be code

Steve Blank: "No plan survives first contact with customers." Several canonical MVPs were not software at all:

- **Zappos** — Nick Swinmurn photographed shoes at local stores and listed them online; when an order came in he bought retail and shipped it. No inventory, no logistics, no warehouse. The hypothesis tested was simply: will people buy shoes online?
- **AngelList** — initially an email list making manual investor introductions
- **Virgin Airlines** — a single plane flying one route
- **Stitch Fix** (cross-reference [[capital-discipline]]) — Katrina Lake bought clothes at Boston boutiques and brought them to people's houses while running surveys

Griffin's takeaway: "If you can validate your thesis without paying to create lots of code, that approach is like found gold." Every dollar saved on the MVP is a dollar available for the pivot or scale-up that follows.

## The MVP is sold to visionaries, not everyone

Steve Blank emphasizes that the MVP's audience is not the median customer but the early adopter who buys the *vision* and tolerates rough edges. The minimum feature set is "(1) a tactic to reduce wasted engineering hours and (2) to get the product in the hands of early visionary customers as soon as possible." Ries adds that early adopters "fill in their minds the features that aren't quite there if we give them the core, tent-pole features that point the direction of where we're trying to go."

The corollary: rejection by mainstream users is not necessarily a falsified hypothesis. It only counts as falsification if even visionary early adopters won't engage.

## The Thiel objection

Peter Thiel disagrees with the entire methodology, arguing in *Zero to One* that "iteration without a bold plan won't take you from 0 to 1" — small adjustments find local maxima, not global ones, and "intelligent design works best" in startups. Griffin's rebuttal is structural rather than philosophical: Thiel and entrepreneurs like Elon Musk are *portfolio-hedged* (Thiel makes many bets) or capitalized at a scale where multi-year stealth R&D is affordable. Founders and early employees of a typical seed-stage startup are not — they have all their eggs in one basket. The MVP is right for the under-capitalized; the bold-plan approach is right for those who can afford to be wrong for years.

This generalizes: methodology should match capital structure. The lean approach is a constraint-driven optimum, not a universal truth.

## What makes MVPs hard

Ries's honest assessment: "MVP is quite annoying, because it imposes extra overhead." Two specific costs the formula obscures:

1. **You must actually learn something.** Shipping the MVP is the easy part. Talking to customers, instrumenting metrics, and synthesizing analysis is the work most founders skip — partly because it's less glamorous than design, fundraising, or speaking at conferences.
2. **The definition is non-formulaic.** "Maximum" and "minimum" both require judgment for the specific context. There is no template. Each MVP is an act of taste under uncertainty.

Ries's bleakest observation: **"The worst fate of any shipping is that nobody cares. You don't get any feedback at all."** Indifference is worse than rejection because rejection produces signal. Chamath Palihapitiya, more bluntly: "Core product value is really illusive and most products don't have any." Founders who ship to silence and then start pumping money into growth are, in Griffin's image, "flying the business at high speed into the side of a mountain."

## Temporal notes

Tren Griffin wrote this in 2017 — Eric Ries's *The Lean Startup* (2011) and Steve Blank's *Four Steps to the Epiphany* (2005) had already canonized the MVP as orthodoxy. By the mid-2020s several caveats apply:

- The 2021-2023 ZIRP-era exuberance produced many over-funded startups that skipped the value-hypothesis stage entirely; the subsequent capital tightening has made the MVP discipline more relevant, not less.
- The Thiel critique has gained ground in deep-tech, hard-science, and AI-foundation-model contexts where iterative MVPs are genuinely impractical — you cannot ship a half-built rocket or a partially-trained foundation model. For these companies, [[ai-native-company-building]] suggests the unit of iteration shifts to the *organization*'s software factories rather than the customer-facing product.
- "MVP" has been over-extended to mean "anything cheap" — Brikman and Blank are explicit that an MVP is not just a stripped feature set; it must still deliver enough value for customers to evaluate. The Zappos and Stitch Fix examples remain the canonical illustrations of *what counts*.

The 84% seed-to-Series-A fatality rate is a 2016 Mattermark figure; current rates vary with funding climate but the order of magnitude — most startups die before Series A — has been durable.
