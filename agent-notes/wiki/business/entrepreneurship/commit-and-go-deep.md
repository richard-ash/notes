# Commit and Go Deep

YC General Partner Jon Xu's rubric for founders stuck between ideas: stop hunting for the perfect idea, commit to one, and go deep enough to learn whether it works. The central claim is that depth, not breadth, is what generates information — and that going deep on one idea is primarily a *search process* for the better idea underneath, not a validation process for the idea you started with.

Where [[startup-ideation-framework]] is about how to *select and validate* an opportunity and [[organic-startup-ideation]] is about how to *notice* ideas in the first place, this note covers what happens after: the discipline of commitment and the depth standard that makes commitment pay off.

## The two overthinking traps

Xu identifies two failure modes that keep founders from committing:

1. **"I need the perfect idea."** You cannot find the perfect idea in the abstract — only by making contact with reality and getting customer feedback. The perfect idea is discovered through execution, not before it.
2. **"Am I the perfect founder for this?"** Founder-market fit is real (a non-technical founder is unlikely to nail a DevTools idea), but second-time founders especially *weaponize* it against themselves, convincing themselves they need a decade of domain experience. Xu's counter: pick something you're curious about, go extremely deep, and talk to customers — extraordinary domain knowledge is often buildable in a short time. (Example: Blake Scholl, adtech background, now running Boom Supersonic.)

## Why juggling ideas fails: bad data

Working on multiple ideas at once feels like a way to discover which one works. It isn't — it **produces bad data**. Without depth you get no clean signal, so you either talk yourself out of a good idea prematurely or keep grinding on a bad one. The fix is to **go in depth first**: if several ideas look equally attractive, pick one and go deep on *it*.

## What "going deep" means

- **Burn the other boats.** Explicitly foreclose the alternatives — stop working on them, tell those customers you've pivoted, focus single-mindedly on the chosen idea.
- **Wear a new skin.** Going deep should make you an almost unrecognizable version of yourself: new company name, emails, website, even a new internal narrative for why you're building. Xu's example, GovDash (government-contract-winning), pivoted ~5 times, changing name and mission each time, before becoming domain experts in government procurement and hitting demand they couldn't keep up with (later a Series B).

## The depth bar: could you run your customer's business?

The high-watermark test for whether you're actually going deep:

> If I dropped you into your customer's business tomorrow, would you know how to run it?

For a hypothetical (voice customer-service agents for cleaning services), that means knowing their daily crises, whether answering the phone is even a top-five problem, how much business a missed call costs, and what they'd pay to never miss one — answerable with **high confidence**. An equivalent framing: *could you teach a class on the problem?* Are you one of the most informed people in the world on it?

Getting there takes many customer conversations and sometimes doing the job yourself — but don't gate code behind talking to hundreds of customers first. Run both in a **tight loop**: deep understanding → product delivery → deeper understanding → better delivery. Real customers using the product produce concrete data that complements abstract domain knowledge. This is the same build-measure-learn loop that animates the [[minimum-viable-product]].

## Three qualities of a good idea in the AI era

Beyond raw customer pull, Xu offers three markers worth watching for as you go deep — all distinctly AI-era:

1. **Sits at the edge of what models can do today.** The product may *barely* work on frontier models now but clearly improves as they get better. Understand the performance bottlenecks intimately — if one doesn't clear the way you hoped, *solving it might become the company*. A version of Paul Graham's "live in the future, then build what's missing" (see [[organic-startup-ideation]]).
2. **Verticalizes — sell an outcome, not software.** As the cost of producing software goes to zero, the durable value is customer trust, licenses, regulatory permission, and outcome ownership. So don't sell software *for* insurers — be the insurer; don't sell back-office software *for* banks — be the bank. Example: Corgi Insurance (YC S24) refused to be a broker or MGA and acquired an insurance carrier mid-batch to own the full stack from underwriting to service, letting it underwrite any line with a fraction of incumbent headcount. This is the founder-side complement to [[outcome-based-pricing]].
3. **The most ambitious version of itself.** Counterintuitively, the ambitious version and the modest version cost roughly the same — both are extremely hard and both consume all your time. So aim at the version that, if it works, rewrites a sector, because that version is also what gives you a moat, repels competitors, and attracts the best talent. Concretely: the most regulated industries (legal, healthcare, financial services), the largest incumbents, or hard tech (robotics for space assembly). This connects to Graham's [[schlep-blindness]] — the ambitious, operationally daunting version is systematically undervalued precisely because it's intimidating.

## Going deep as a search process, not a validation process

The most important reframe: **going deep isn't primarily about validating the idea you started with — it's how you find the better idea underneath.** Founders begin by solving surface-level pain points; the real opportunities are almost always deeper structural problems that only become visible once you're operating at the frontier and noticing the bottlenecks, gaps, and unbuilt dev tools.

So even when the original idea fails, you come out dramatically ahead: unambiguous customer data on whether the "hair on fire" problem was real, genuine conviction to base a pivot on, and often a new idea that actually works.

## The thesis: walk fast in one direction

The closing metaphor: in the early idea fog you can see only ~10 feet ahead. The instinct is to take a few cautious steps in every direction — sample a little here and there, stay close to home — but that yields almost no information. **Committing to one direction and walking fast generates far more information per unit of time**, and may carry you to a better destination you couldn't have seen from the start. The worst failure mode isn't being wrong; it's never deciding — dabbling between ideas and never going deep enough on any one to learn anything.

This is the depth-first answer to the "deciding to not decide" trap noted in [[startup-ideation-framework]], and the commitment step there ("commit to one thing — your limited resource is always time") is the same move framed from the selection side.
