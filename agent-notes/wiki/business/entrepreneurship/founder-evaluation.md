# Founder Evaluation

How seed-stage VCs decide whether a founder has a non-zero chance of changing a vertical or the world. Synthesized from a January 2026 Uncapped conversation between Vinod Khosla and Keith Rabois (both managing directors at Khosla Ventures), where they articulate their separate-but-converging frameworks for the read on a founder. They report that across roughly eight years of co-investing they can name only "two or three" cases of significant divergence on a founder assessment — the disagreements they have are about markets, not people.

This is explicitly a framework for **first-institutional-capital** investing. The traits that matter for backing someone before they've proven anything are different from those that matter for later-stage diligence, where revenue trajectory and team quality are observable.

## Rabois: A+ incomplete on one dimension, or an unusual Venn overlap

Rabois argues a great founder satisfies one of two criteria:

1. **Top-one-basis-point on some dimension you've ever met.** It can be raw intelligence, tenacity, ability to assess people, strategic depth — the dimension varies, but the level is "the best I've ever met in my life." When this is present, it shows up in roughly three minutes; you feel the energy.

2. **An unusual Venn overlap of strong traits.** Examples Rabois cites: Max Levchin (first-rate technologist *and* first-rate business mind — fewer than five people in the Valley combine those, by Reid Hoffman's count); Jack Dorsey (good design mind + good technologist + good business strategist — three traits, which is why he's been successful across multiple companies).

The implicit reformulation is **"A+ incomplete beats B+ at everything."** A founder who is exceptional on one dimension and obviously incomplete on others is more interesting than a founder who is uniformly strong but lacks any peak. The interviewer crystallized this with the phrase "incomplete A-plus."

Khosla agrees on the primacy of exceptionality but adds that the gaps are fine because they're what venture assistance is for: a professor-founder who doesn't know go-to-market is perfectly backable if you can fill the gap around them. This is the explicit business model of Khosla Ventures — Khosla refuses the label "venture capitalist" and insists on "venture assistant," meaning the firm exists to make incomplete founders complete.

## Khosla: learning rate as the durable trait

For Khosla, the trait that compounds is **the learning rate of the founder** — how open-minded they are to new ideas and, crucially, how good they are at *rejecting* bad ones.

The diagnostic is bidirectional:

- **Founders who agree with Khosla on everything are disqualifying.** "If a person listens to me all the time, I'll almost never invest with them. I know they're not critically examining." Khosla deliberately advances positions he doesn't believe in just to test whether the founder pushes back.
- **Founders who take every idea and try to execute it** are also disqualifying — they're sponging input rather than processing it.

The reason this works as a single-meeting test is that learning rate over time is hard to assess from one data point ("infinite lines through one dot"). The fastest proxy Khosla uses for YC founders he's tracking: ask the partner working with the company **"how much have they learned in the last three months?"** Three months is enough to tell.

This connects directly to [[hiring-for-curiosity]] — Shapiro's argument that curiosity is the most predictive hiring trait — and to the core observation in [[its-not-magic]] that the best leaders are made by behavior rather than personality.

### Stress-test questions Khosla uses

- "Imagine you were in this business that you're not familiar with. How would you go about learning X?" — pulls the candidate out of pre-rehearsed answers and surfaces how they actually attack unfamiliar problems.
- "If I gave you seed funding to investigate three ideas, which three would you pick? How would you go about evaluating them over six months?" — works for founder candidates and operating-role candidates equally.

Khosla notes he has a public document on how to interview, and a *private* document — given only to portfolio founders — on how to interpret the answers. He won't publish the latter because once the interpretive grid is public, candidates calibrate to it. The asymmetry is the entire point.

## Grit: not assessable in a meeting, but knowable through stories

Both VCs agree that less obvious traits — grit, in particular — are hard to surface from an interview. They show up in anecdotes that *the founder doesn't know are revealing*. Rabois's two examples:

- A founder who, on his second day on a new Uber team in a foreign country, learned the team was running a triathlon on Saturday. He hadn't trained, didn't own a bike, and rented a city bike to run the triathlon Saturday morning to fit in.
- A founder's co-founder finished second in a national spelling bee in high school and passed out from stress. Rather than quit, he came back senior year and tried to win it.

The point isn't the specific stories — it's that grit reveals itself through "I cannot help myself from doing this absurd thing" patterns that a founder mentions casually. Rabois explicitly does **not** use the Daniel Gross "tell me about your siblings and your childhood" interrogation style; he stays free-form and lets the spark emerge naturally from a conversation about why the founder is doing the company.

## Recruiting: the first 10 are the company

Both treat recruiting ability as a first-class founder trait, citing Patrick Collison: **the first 10 hires multiply by 10.** Whatever those people are, that's what your hundred will be.

The trait isn't just "can charm humans" — it's **"can parlay your unique assets into convincing the right people to work with you."** Even an uneven founder can recruit well if their special-sauce dimension comes through with enough force that candidates feel "wow, that was interesting" leaving the meeting. This is the same machinery as fundraising, customer acquisition, and beta-user recruitment — Rabois argues "good at selling anyone" really is good at selling everyone, because the underlying skill is making people commit before there's proof.

For founders who are clearly exceptional but obviously can't recruit yet, this is again where venture assistance comes in: a board partner can help articulate why the ambition is worth chasing.

## Ethics: the only non-negotiable

The single dimension where a founder cannot be deficient is ethics. Both Rabois and Khosla agree on this without hedge. The diagnostic is references — pattern-matching across people who've worked with the founder over time, since ethics is hard to surface in a meeting.

A counterintuitive observation: highly disagreeable, intense founders often have *unusually strong* moral compasses. Rabois argues the abrasiveness and the ethics come from the same source — strong principles that the founder won't compromise. The corollary is that polished, agreeable founders sometimes lack a moral spine; the disagreeable ones have one and are willing to wear it externally.

## Why VC consensus is bad signal at the seed stage

Rabois and Khosla are emphatic that **consensus hot seed deals do not outperform non-consensus ones.** The argument:

- Many of the great investments of the last 15 years (Airbnb, OpenAI, Rocket Lab, Commonwealth Fusion) were widely passed on at the seed stage.
- Khosla Ventures bought a third of Rocket Lab for $5M because nobody wanted to invest in space. The company is now ~$40B.
- KV was the only venture investor in OpenAI's 2018 round (a nonprofit with no product or revenue plan). The investment hypothesis was a single sentence: this is the only critical density of research-grade AI talent outside Google and DeepMind. KV sent an apology letter to LPs at the time because the check was twice the largest initial investment they'd ever made; the letter is now part of their fundraising deck.

Rabois's nuance: when the seed-stage consensus *is* hot, it's almost always around **people**, not ideas. "Two people leaving Cursor" raises easily. But for an undiscovered founder, the consensus signal is roughly noise — if anything, anti-correlated, because the people best at seeing the truly weird thing have already learned that most VCs can't.

This is a sharper version of the [[four-kinds-of-luck|Andreessen/Austin "preparation luck"]] thesis: the alpha at seed comes from what *you* see that others can't, not from what's bid up.

## Pattern-matching pitfalls VCs themselves create

Both note that VC firms make a systematic mistake when issuing hiring specs to portfolio companies: **they look for someone with the same job title at a similar-stage company.** Khosla's marketing example is the cleanest: most startups need to *create* a brand. If you've been a marketer at Cisco, you know how to *sustain* a brand — a completely different skill. Cookie-cutter "ex-Cisco VP Marketing" specs miss the dimension that actually matters.

Rabois's stronger version: "If you're at Cisco more than 10 years, you're not qualified for a real job in the entrepreneurial world." The deeper point is the [[manager-anti-patterns|expert trap]] — Khosla's formulation is "most experts are experts in a previous version of the world, not the one you're trying to create." In rapidly evolving contexts (AI especially), fast learners with no domain experience routinely beat slow learners with deep credentials. This is why founder learning rate compounds and résumé-fit doesn't.

## The founder-VC-feedback loop

A second-order trait both VCs use: **how does the founder process feedback?** Strong founders select for the best feedback they can get *and* know how to say "thank you, I disagree" without flinching. Weak founders select for hypocritical politeness and react to bluntness as a personal attack.

Rabois calls hypocritical politeness "pervasive in our business" and argues it actively hurts founders — analogizing it to permissive parenting. Khosla's frame: "we're trying to help build successful companies, not get more referrals." The founder's tolerance for direct feedback is itself a screening trait — the ones who can absorb and counter-argue without ego are the ones whose learning rate will compound. This is roughly the same mechanism [[truth-telling-leadership|Horowitz]] identifies in leadership: trust and communication are inversely related, but only if the communication is honest.

## Implications for the founder side of the table

If this framework is right, the founder takeaways are:

- **Find your A+ dimension and lean into it publicly.** The trait that gets you funded is the one a sharp investor can identify in three minutes; vague competence on many axes is worse than visible peak on one.
- **Build a portfolio of unfamiliar problems and document how you attacked them.** That history is what makes the "imagine yourself in this unfamiliar business" question a layup rather than a stumble.
- **Disagree with your investors on something specific, with reasoning.** Khosla is *deliberately testing* this; agreeing with him is the failure mode.
- **References are doing more work than the meeting.** Especially for ethics. Curate them, but more importantly, behave consistently across years so that the references don't have to be curated.
