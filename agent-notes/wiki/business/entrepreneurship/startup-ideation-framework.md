# Startup Ideation Framework

A practitioner's framework for moving from "I want to start a business" to a validated, committed opportunity. This synthesis draws on Troy (u/ssbmomelette), a serial founder across 9 startups (cumulative valuation >$1B, current venture at $5M ARR), who lays out a seven-step process. The framework is opinionated and experiential rather than academic --- but an Antler founder in the comments noted it covers 70-80% of what their program teaches.

This process-oriented framework contrasts with Graham's [[organic-startup-ideation|organic approach]], which argues the best ideas are "noticed" from lived experience rather than systematically generated. The two are complementary: organic ideation describes how to *become* the sort of person who has good ideas, while this framework describes what to do once you're looking.

## The pain vs. enjoyment distinction

Troy argues every business either **solves a pain point** or **provides enjoyment**, and that pain businesses are almost always the better bet for a new founder:

1. **Pain drives willingness to pay.** Recurring pain creates retention. Intensity of pain sets price ceiling.
2. **Enjoyment businesses compete with *all* entertainment.** A new game competes not just with other games but with Netflix, TikTok, hiking. The bar for standing out is enormous.
3. **Enjoyment requires deeper passion and domain expertise** to overcome the lack of urgency on the buyer's side.

A commenter (Any_Barber1453) adds that VCs almost always back pain-solving companies because the sales cycle is shorter and retention is more predictable. This framing has limits. Troy acknowledges enjoyment businesses can be great; the point is that choosing one should be a deliberate decision to take a harder path.

## Step 1: Evaluate your skillset

Before looking for ideas, do a brutally honest self-assessment of founder-relevant skills (sales, marketing, programming, design, finance, ops, leadership, etc.) tiered into Good / Acceptable / Bad. The "business idea Venn diagram" then maps: your likelihood of success is proportional to how many of a business's key skills you already possess.

This is essentially **founder-market fit from the inside out** --- starting with your capabilities rather than a market opportunity. A commenter notes the investor-side version matters too: VCs look for whether you have an "unfair insight" from living inside the problem.

## Step 2: Identify opportunities by chasing pain

Maintain a living document of pain points you or people close to you experience. For each, rate:

- **Pain intensity** (1-5): how much does it suck?
- **Frequency** (1-5): how often does it occur?
- **Niche-ness** (1-5): how specific is the affected group? (higher = more niche)

Troy emphasizes that the ratings themselves matter less than the *thinking they force*. Key insight from the comments (Any_Barber1453): **frequency may matter more than intensity**. Daily moderate pain (DoorDash, HubSpot) beats annual severe pain for retention and conversion.

### Why niche is good

Counterintuitively, more niche is generally better:

1. Less competition with true product-market fit
2. Easier to charge premium prices
3. Tighter feedback loops
4. Natural growth loops within communities
5. Expanding *out* of a niche is easier than trying to serve multiple niches from day one

## Step 3: Research the opportunity

Time-box research to **10 hours per opportunity** --- Troy warns that the "permanent research and planning phase" is where the majority of would-be founders get permanently stuck. A commenter (kreato123344) calls this "deciding to not decide": research never ends because shipping means facing a verdict.

### Competition is validation

**You want to find competitors.** No competition usually means someone already tried and failed, or there's no willingness to pay. When analyzing competitors, answer:

1. What's their pricing strategy? (subscription vs. one-time, price points, price transparency)
2. What's their current size and growth story? (employees, bootstrapped vs. funded)
3. How do they solve the pain point?
4. How do they frame/position their solution?

### Where are the customers?

Identify who they are and where they spend time (online and offline). A useful litmus: *if someone asked you to find one potential customer online, how long would it take?*

### Marketing company or sales company?

Nearly every early startup is primarily one or the other:

| Marketing company | Sales company |
|---|---|
| Low price / deal size | High price / deal size |
| Easy to target audience | Complex setup process |
| Self-serve onboarding | Contracts or volume commitments |
| Selling to individuals | Selling to businesses |

**Critical filter:** if the opportunity needs a sales motion but the price is too low to fund commission, it's likely a bad business.

### Market sizing (quick TAM/SAM/SOM)

- **TAM** = total people in space x charge amount x frequency
- **SAM** = people who actually need the solution x charge x frequency
- **SOM** = SAM x 10%

Anchor assumptions low. The point is order-of-magnitude sizing, not precision. Troy's worked example (international restaurant booking) landed within range of a competitor's public revenue figures, suggesting the back-of-envelope approach works well enough at this stage.

## Step 4: Categorize and compare

Score each surviving opportunity on three axes:

| Dimension | Values |
|---|---|
| **Timeline to ship** | < 1 month, 1-3 months, 3+ months, ongoing |
| **Opportunity size** | Low (<$500K), Mid (<$1M), Large (<$10M), Venture (>$10M) |
| **Confidence** | Low (<10%), Fair (10-50%), Likely (50-80%), High (>80%) |

Key heuristics:

- **Short ship time reduces the need for high confidence.** If you can test in weeks, lower-confidence bets are fine.
- **Bigger market does not equal better opportunity.** More competition, more resources required. Only chase venture-scale with very high confidence.
- If most of your ideas need 3+ months to ship, you're not thinking lean enough.

## Step 5: Test the market

Build the **leanest possible solution** --- not an MVP, possibly not even software. Troy knows founders who validated with a Google Form and manual labor. The goal is 10 paying customers. Key things to learn:

1. Were your research assumptions correct?
2. Can you actually acquire customers?
3. What do customers actually want?

### Finding first customers

- **Go in person.** Much higher conversion and richer feedback than cold emails.
- **Lean into being early-stage.** People root for scrappy founders; don't waste time on polish, landing pages, or swag.
- **Advisor shares** (0.1-0.5% equity) can hook prestigious early users and trigger the Ikea effect --- people value what they helped build.
- **Always charge something.** Free users give worse feedback. Options: sell at cost, "godfather offer" (aggressive long-term price lock), or free trial with payment info collected.

### When to fold

Troy is emphatic: **give up when it's not working.** "Never give up" applies to the dream of entrepreneurship, not to any specific idea. If 100 conversations in your target market yield zero interest, the opportunity or approach is wrong.

Separating passion from delusion, per Troy: if you can find 10 paying customers without excessive friction, the underlying pain is real even if your current solution is wrong. If you can't, you have your answer.

## Step 6: Commit

Once you have validation, commit to one thing. Troy's meta-observation: "Your limited resource is always time." The pull toward other ideas is constant, but splitting focus is how validated opportunities die.

## Gaps and commentary additions

Several commenters flagged dimensions Troy's framework doesn't deeply cover:

- **Timing / market readiness** (leolock567, Dramatic_Turnover936): Is the market ready *now*? Troy's response: timing is largely out of your control; if the market isn't ready, wait and try something else rather than hoping it catches up. "Time in the market beats timing the market."
- **Post-launch decision frameworks** (kreato123344): The categorization structure (timeline / size / confidence) is useful beyond ideation --- hiring, pricing, and pivot decisions need the same structured evaluation, but most founders abandon the habit after the idea phase.
- **Hardware / physical product validation** (gogosomewhere): When the form factor *is* the value proposition, Troy suggests pre-orders / Kickstarter, or lean physical prototypes shown at events.
- **Trust-heavy domains** (valkla123): For products where users must trust the output (e.g., AI investment tools), 1% conversion from warm traffic suggests switching from self-serve to a sales/demo flow to build trust directly.
