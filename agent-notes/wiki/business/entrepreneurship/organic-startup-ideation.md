# Organic Startup Ideation

Paul Graham argues that the way to get startup ideas is *not* to try to think of startup ideas. The best ideas emerge organically from founders' lived experience — "noticed" rather than "thought up." This essay, one of Graham's most influential, lays out a philosophy of ideation that is more psychological than procedural, complementing the step-by-step approach of a [[startup-ideation-framework|practitioner's ideation framework]].

## The core thesis: notice, don't invent

The best startup ideas share three properties: the founders themselves want the product, can build it, and few others realize it's worth doing. Microsoft, Apple, Yahoo, Google, and Facebook all began this way. Graham calls ideas that grow naturally from founders' own experiences "organic" ideas, in contrast to "made-up" or "sitcom" ideas — the kind a TV writer would invent for a character starting a startup.

The danger of made-up ideas is that they sound plausible. A social network for pet owners doesn't sound obviously wrong. But when you test it, nobody says "I would *never* use this" — they say "yeah, maybe." Sum that lukewarm reaction across the population and you have zero users.

## The well metaphor

Graham proposes visualizing demand as a graph: the x-axis is how many people want what you're making, the y-axis is how much they want it. Startups can dig a hole that's broad and shallow, or narrow and deep — a well. Nearly all good startup ideas are wells.

**The litmus test:** Who wants this *right now*? Who wants it so much they'll use a crappy version one made by a two-person startup they've never heard of? If you can't answer that, the idea is probably bad.

But a well alone isn't sufficient. There must also be a **path out** — a way the narrow initial market expands to something large. Facebook started at Harvard (narrow), but colleges are similar enough that what works at one works at all of them, and from all college students you eventually reach everyone. Microsoft followed the same pattern: Basic for the Altair → Basic for other machines → other languages → operating systems → applications.

Graham acknowledges that paths out are often invisible at the start. Airbnb's founders initially thought they were renting floor space during conventions. The key meta-insight: these paths are hard to see, and that's OK. If you're the right sort of person at the leading edge of a changing field, you'll have the right sort of hunches.

## "Live in the future, then build what's missing"

Graham synthesizes Paul Buchheit's observation that leading-edge practitioners "live in the future" with Pirsig's advice from *Zen and the Art of Motorcycle Maintenance*: "Make yourself perfect and then just paint naturally." The result:

> Live in the future, then build what's missing.

This is how most of the biggest startups got started. They weren't supposed to be companies — they grew out of things founders built because there seemed a gap in the world. The process is one of an **external stimulus hitting a prepared mind**: Bill Gates hears about the Altair; Drew Houston forgets his USB stick. Lots of people had those same stimuli. The difference is that these founders' experiences had prepared them to notice the opportunity.

This connects directly to [[four-kinds-of-luck|Austin's Chance III]] — luck from preparation. The "prepared mind" that recognizes faint signals of opportunity is the same mechanism Graham describes: years of domain immersion make certain gaps obvious to you that remain invisible to everyone else.

## Filters that block good ideas

Graham identifies several unconscious filters that prevent founders from seeing good ideas:

**The status quo filter.** The most powerful: taking the current state of the world for granted. To find ideas, sacrifice some of that efficiency and start questioning — why is your inbox overflowing? Is an inbox the optimal tool? Things that chafe you may be signals that you're "living in the future."

**The [[schlep-blindness|schlep filter]].** Unconscious avoidance of ideas involving tedious or intimidating operational work. Graham's canonical example is Stripe: thousands of developers knew payments were painful, but nobody built the solution because dealing with banks and fraud seemed intolerable. The fear of schleps kept competitors away, giving Stripe smooth sailing in user acquisition.

**The unsexy filter.** Avoidance of problems you despise rather than fear. Graham overcame this to build Viaweb — ecommerce wasn't interesting to him, but the problem clearly needed solving. The unsexy filter is less dangerous than the schlep filter because ideas you find sexy are somewhat correlated with what's valuable, especially as you gain experience.

**The "toy" filter.** Working on things that could be dismissed as toys often produces great ideas. Microcomputers seemed like toys. BackRub (Google) seemed like an inconsequential science project. If know-it-alls on forums would dismiss it, that's positive evidence at YC.

## Recipes for deliberate ideation (Plan B)

When you can't wait for organic ideas, Graham offers a disciplined alternative — but warns that it requires more self-discipline because you'll see many more ideas, most of them bad:

- **Stay in your domain of expertise.** A database expert shouldn't build a teen chat app. Expertise raises your standards in your own domain, which feels like a disadvantage but is actually a filter against bad ideas. In unfamiliar domains you give yourself a Dunning-Kruger pass.
- **Start with your own needs.** Ask: "Why doesn't someone make X? If someone made X we'd buy it in a second."
- **Look for dying industries.** Startups often garbage-collect broken companies. But look for what people will later say *replaced* the incumbent, not for a direct substitute — disruption comes from the side.
- **Find ignored markets with disdainful incumbents.** HP turned down the Apple I because it used a TV for a monitor. When big players' disdain blinds them to a market, that's a startup's opening.
- **Ride technology waves.** Gene sequencing and 3D printing costs declining on Moore's Law curves. What are we unconsciously ruling out as impossible that will soon be possible?
- **Cross-domain collisions.** A programmer learning genetics will see problems software could solve that biologists don't — both because the domain's inhabitants haven't thought to use software, and because the newcomer doesn't take the status quo for granted.

## Competition as signal

Graham argues that worrying you're late is actually a sign of a good idea — good ideas seem obvious, so of course someone else might be working on one. But startups are almost never killed by competitors. A crowded market means demand exists and no solution is good enough yet.

The key is having a thesis about what incumbents are overlooking, phrased more precisely than "we'll make an X that doesn't suck." Best case: incumbents lacked the courage of their own convictions. Google was this — prior search engines shied away from the implication that the better they worked, the faster users would leave.

## Temporal notes

This essay is from November 2012. Its core ideas — organic ideation, the well metaphor, schlep blindness — have become foundational vocabulary in startup culture. The specific examples (Facebook's college-only launch, Stripe's payment schlep, Dropbox's USB-stick moment) have only grown more canonical with time. The essay's advice to "live in the future and build what's missing" may apply even more strongly in the 2020s, where AI is the rapidly changing field most likely to produce organic ideas for technical founders.
