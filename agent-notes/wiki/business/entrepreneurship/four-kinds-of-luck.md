# The Four Kinds of Luck

Dr. James Austin, a neurologist and philosopher, proposed a taxonomy of four distinct types of luck (or "chance") in his 1978 book *Chase, Chance, and Creativity*. Marc Andreessen popularized the framework in the context of entrepreneurship, arguing that while luck is unavoidable in startup outcomes, founders can systematically cultivate three of the four types.

## The taxonomy

**Chance I — Blind luck.** Completely accidental, impersonal, and uninfluenceable. The cosmic dice roll.

**Chance II — Luck from motion.** Generated by energetic, undirected activity. Constant experimentation stirs up random collisions between ideas and opportunities. As Charles Kettering put it: "I have never heard of anyone stumbling on something sitting down." Andreessen connects this to the "maximize swings of the bat" principle — in uncertain environments, a [[startup-ideation-framework|bias to action]] dominates over extended deliberation.

**Chance III — Luck from preparation.** Pasteur's "chance favors the prepared mind." An opportunity presents only a faint signal, recognizable solely by someone with the right background knowledge and perceptual readiness. Austin's canonical example is Alexander Fleming's discovery of penicillin in 1928 — Fleming recognized the significance of mold killing bacteria in a culture dish because nine years earlier he had observed a similar phenomenon with his own nasal secretions (leading to the discovery of lysozyme). The prepared mind connects dots that others cannot even see.

**Chance IV — Luck from distinctive individuality.** The most personal and elusive form. It arises from a person's unique combination of hobbies, quirks, and behavioral patterns — a "quixotic rider cantering in on his own homemade hobby horse to intercept the problem at an odd angle." Disraeli captured the spirit: "We make our fortunes and we call them fate." By the time Chance IV operates, the easy problems have already been solved by conventional means; what remains requires an approach so idiosyncratic that only one particular person could have stumbled into it.

## The neurological distinction

Austin frames the four types along two axes of human agency:

| Type | Motor activity | Sensory receptivity |
|------|---------------|---------------------|
| I | None | None |
| II | Generalized, energetic | Passive |
| III | Passive | Highly specialized |
| IV | Highly specific, idiosyncratic | Passive |

Chance III is primarily about *sensory receptivity* — the ability to notice and synthesize. Chance IV is about *motor behavior* — the distinctive actions that only you would take, which place you in positions no one else would occupy.

## Implications for entrepreneurs

Andreessen draws four practical questions from the framework:

1. **Energy** — How biased toward action are you? Motion creates Chance II.
2. **Curiosity** — How broadly are you learning? Andreessen argues curiosity matters more than raw intelligence because curious people accumulate the building blocks that enable Chance III.
3. **Synthesis** — How aggressively do you link disparate experiences? This is the operational skill behind Chance III — harder to train, but critical to cultivate.
4. **Individuality** — How distinctively are you developing your own point of view, hobbies, and unconventional behaviors? This is the Chance IV driver, and it is why Andreessen argues that diverse life experience often beats formal domain-specific education for creative work.

The framework suggests that "getting lucky" is partly a skill that compounds: more motion generates more raw material, deeper preparation makes you better at recognizing it, and a more distinctive personal profile creates opportunities that literally cannot arise for anyone else.
