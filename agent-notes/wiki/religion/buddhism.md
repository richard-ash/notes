# Buddhism

Notes drawn from a conversation between Tyler Cowen and Donald S. Lopez Jr. (University of Michigan), one of the West's leading scholars of Buddhism. Lopez's running project is to distinguish historical and textual Buddhism from the romanticized Buddhism that 19th- and 20th-century European and American writers assembled out of their own needs. Most of what follows is Lopez's scholarly position — attributed rather than asserted.

## Western "Buddhism" is partly a 19th-century invention

Lopez argues that much of what Westerners take to be core Buddhism is a product of 19th-century European scholarship rather than of Asian Buddhist practice. Key moves:

- **Pre-19th-century taxonomy.** Europeans classified world religions into four categories — Christians, Jews, Muslims, and *idolaters*. Buddhists sat in the idolater bucket until Sanskrit scholarship carved them out.
- **The Sanskrit craze.** Once Sanskrit's cognate relationship to Latin and Greek was established, Buddhist texts arriving via the British in India became exciting to French, British, and German intellectuals. Many of those intellectuals were atheists and anti-Catholics looking for a "religion of reason"; they portrayed Buddhism accordingly.
- **The classical-civilization illusion.** When Vasco da Gama reached India in 1498, Buddhism was already effectively dead on the subcontinent — "temples and ruins, caves overgrown, broken statues." Europeans treated this as a lost classical civilization they could *own*, and assumed living Buddhisms in China, Tibet, and Southeast Asia were degraded reflections polluted by local belief.
- **The peaceful-religion myth.** Lopez is emphatic that Buddhism-as-pacifism is a 19th-century construction. History records Buddhist armies, Buddhist wars, Buddhists killing each other, and Buddhist monks serving as battlefield chaplains as recently as WWII. The Buddha did preach against violence; the institutions did not consistently follow.

This framing connects to a broader pattern in which Western observers project desired features onto Asian traditions — see [[tibetan-book-of-the-dead]] below for an extreme case.

## The theological puzzle: supernatural without a god

Buddhism is commonly described as atheistic, and Lopez confirms there is no creator deity. Yet the tradition is full of supernatural machinery: omniscient Buddhas, wrathful deities, hungry ghosts, eight hot hells and eight cold hells, rebirth across six realms, and beings who can relinquish their life force and predict earthquakes.

The reconciliation runs through **karma as natural law**. "Karma" in Sanskrit just means *action*. The universe is *naturally ethical*: virtuous deeds produce happiness in some future lifetime; non-virtuous deeds produce suffering. This is how Buddhism, without a judging god, explains why virtuous people suffer and evil flourishes. No one needs to issue verdicts — the mechanism is built into reality the way physics is.

Supernatural beings (gods, demigods, wrathful deities) are not ontologically separate from humans; they are just other birth-categories in the same rebirth cycle. A wrathful deity was a human in some past life and will be something else next.

The Buddha's special powers, likewise, aren't divinely delegated — they're the accumulated result of practicing the six perfections (giving, ethics, patience, effort, concentration, wisdom) over billions of years of past lives. He is special because he discovered the path to enlightenment on his own at a time when no Buddha and no Buddhism existed in the world; that self-discovery is what distinguishes *a* Buddha from the many who later achieve nirvana by hearing his teaching.

**Nirvana** is not a place. Lopez uses the flame metaphor: when the Buddha died, he was not reborn. Where did the fire go? Nowhere — the wood that produced it is exhausted. Nirvana is cessation, extinction.

## The historical Buddha: what we actually know

Very little, by scholarly standards.

- **Death date.** Theravada tradition says 544 BCE; Chinese Buddhism gives different numbers; most Western scholars write "400 BCE ± 50." Time in Buddhism is measured *After Nirvana* rather than after his birth.
- **Body.** Canonical descriptions give the Buddha 32 major and 80 minor bodily marks — a tongue that can cover his face, a retractable penis, more teeth than a normal person, webbed fingers and toes, arms that reach below the knees while standing. Lopez relays an art-historical speculation that the webbed-hands tradition may derive from Greek and Roman statues where marble was deliberately left between the fingers to prevent the fingers from snapping off.
- **Death.** Tradition records "red flows" — dysentery-like symptoms — after a final meal served by a low-caste blacksmith (vegetarian or pork, depending on the source). The theological framing is that the two most important meals a Buddha receives are the one before enlightenment and the one before nirvana; both are infused with special power, and the dysentery is explained by the meal being *too* powerful for ordinary monks.
- **Family.** One son, Rahula, born or conceived on the night the Buddha left the palace. When the Buddha returned post-enlightenment, his wife asked that Rahula receive his birthright (the throne); the Buddha instead ordained him as a child monk. Rahula achieved enlightenment. No surviving line.
- **Caste.** The Buddha belonged to the Kshatriya (warrior) caste. Lopez debunks the 19th-century European claim that he rejected caste: in the Pali Canon, monks and nuns whose caste is specified are overwhelmingly Brahmin, secondly Kshatriya, and rarely from the lowest class. Caste did not preclude ordination or enlightenment, but it also wasn't abolished.

## Oral transmission

Buddhism, like Hinduism, began as an oral tradition. After the Buddha died, a council of monks in a cave had two monks recite everything — one the monastic code, one the sutras — and the assembled monks memorized it. Centuries passed before anything was written down. There are no carbon-dated Buddhist texts from before the 1st century BCE. Specialized monks held roles like "reciter of the middle-length sutras." Lopez considers memorization in India reliable enough that oral transmission probably preserved the core accurately — the Vedas are the comparison case, with Brahmins reciting every odd and even syllable backward and forward.

## The two main branches

- **Theravada** ("tradition of the elders") — Sri Lanka, Thailand, and Southeast Asia. Follows the Pali Canon. Europeans also called it "Southern Buddhism."
- **Mahayana** ("great vehicle") — China, Japan, Korea, Tibet, Nepal, Vietnam. Accepts the Mahayana sutras (Lotus Sutra, Diamond Sutra, Heart Sutra) as the word of the Buddha. These texts appear around the beginning of the common era — clearly composed centuries after the Buddha died.

From the Theravada view, the Mahayana sutras are heretical. From the Mahayana view, Theravada is a lower vehicle that rejected higher teachings. Lopez's scholarly judgment is blunt: the "great vehicle" self-description is *an advertisement*. The Mahayana sutras were not taught by the historical Buddha. Despite that, they are the sutras best known in English — Heart Sutra, Diamond Sutra, Lotus Sutra.

Lopez's own instinct on "which Buddhism is closest to the original": the monks of Sri Lanka — in their robes, their comportment, and the continuity of the lineage — probably resemble monks in the Buddha's time more than any other living tradition.

### Zen's awkward origin story

Zen is the one major tradition Lopez says cannot be traced to India at all. The origin story — the Buddha silently holds up a flower, one monk understands, and mind-to-mind transmission passes down the generations to Bodhidharma, who brings it to China — rests on Bodhidharma, who is probably not a historical figure.

Etymologically: Sanskrit *jhāna* ("meditation") → Chinese transliteration *chán nà* → shortened to *chán* → Japanese pronunciation *Zen*. So the name is the Japanese pronunciation of the Chinese mispronunciation of a Sanskrit word. Zen styles itself "a special transmission outside the scriptures" — it deprioritizes sutras in favor of direct teacher-to-student transmission of enlightenment experience.

## Buddhism's global decline

Lopez agrees Buddhism is contracting: gone from Java (now Islamic) and India (gone for centuries), plummeting in Japan, declining in Korea, uncertain in China, suppressed in Tibet since 1950. The Pew study from 2025 confirms the trend.

His explanation is structural and political rather than doctrinal: **Buddhism has always depended on royal patronage**. The Sanskrit word for monk, *bhikshu*, literally means *beggar*. Monks and nuns may not touch money, may not till the soil, and must beg for food. The institution needs patrons, and historically the patrons were kings. The implicit bargain was that monks, by keeping their vows, protected the kingdom from famine, disease, and foreign invasion.

Kings are mostly gone. The bargain no longer works. The one exception Lopez highlights — Thailand — is the one country that retained its monarchy, never became a European colony, and still has kings who serve time as monks and support the Sangha directly. Thailand is also by far the wealthiest Buddhist country.

Cowen pushes back: Ethiopia and Tibet weren't colonized either and are extremely poor. Lopez's counter: Thailand's wealth came from *trading* with Europeans without turning the government over to them. Tibetan trade ran through China and was mostly just tea; Tibet's economy was effectively run by the monasteries, which is why 1959 (when the Chinese closed the monasteries) was an economic catastrophe as well as a religious one.

The Buddhist tradition itself predicts its own decline — a future time "when the yellow robe of the monks will turn white" (white being the robe color of laypeople). Lopez notes this is happening faster than the early texts predicted.

Who speaks for Buddhism today? "There's no pope." Monastic abbots once mediated doctrine; with monasteries emptying, the question is genuinely open. YouTube and TikTok now host every Buddhist tradition, every language, every level of teacher. AI translation is likely to unlock the majority of the canon that has never been translated within the next few years — a conference at Berkeley on *AI and the Future of Buddhist Studies* put the timeline at about five years for most canonical languages.

## The Dalai Lama and Tibetan incarnate succession

Tibetan Buddhism has a unique institution: the incarnate lama. A great teacher dies, followers find the little boy reborn as him, educate him, and he becomes the next lama. This pattern begins in the 11th century. The Dalai Lama title was bestowed by a Mongol Khan (*dalai* = "ocean" in Mongolian). The fifth Dalai Lama was installed as temporal-and-religious ruler of Tibet in 1642 after a Mongol-assisted civil war victory. There have been 14 Dalai Lamas; the current one (90 years old, in exile since 1959) says he hopes to live to 130.

Lopez considers the succession process dramatically inefficient. A ~25-year interregnum between Dalai Lamas has the country ruled by a regent; there is good evidence that several Dalai Lamas were poisoned by their regents who wanted to stay in power. The process — waiting two or three years for a child to be born, sending search parties, using oracles and possession-recognition tests, bringing the child to the Potala Palace — is then followed by ~20 years of education before the new Dalai Lama reaches head-of-state age.

And yet, Cowen points out, the current Dalai Lama is so effective that five years of Hollywood central casting couldn't do better. Lopez agrees he is unusual — he expects history to remember the fifth and the 14th as the two greatest. The sixth is the famous failure: he refused the vow of celibacy, threatened to kill himself if forced, stepped down, and died mysteriously after writing beautiful love poetry. None have been tyrants.

Respect for the Dalai Lama across Buddhist traditions is real but variable — he is central to Tibetans and Mongolians (regarded as an incarnation of the bodhisattva of compassion); for other Buddhists he is respected as a promoter of Buddhism during his exile.

## The Tibetan Book of the Dead: a constructed Western classic

A case study in Lopez's broader thesis that the Western canon of Buddhism is partly an invention.

The Tibetan tradition holds that for a text to be authentic it must come from India. To satisfy this requirement without traveling to India, Tibetans developed the **treasure text** genre: Padmasambhava, an Indian teacher who came to Tibet, is said to have buried texts across the country, later to be dug up by discoverers who alone could read the secret script.

The "Tibetan Book of the Dead" is one of these treasure texts. It was brought to Western attention by **Walter Evans-Wentz**, an American theosophist born Walt Wentz in Trenton, New Jersey. He studied in the UK, adopted his mother's maiden name Evans and hyphenated it to sound more British. Visiting Sikkim in the early 20th century, he had the text translated by the local Tibetan schoolteacher. Because he loved the *Egyptian Book of the Dead* — central to Madame Blavatsky's theosophy — he titled his version the *Tibetan Book of the Dead* and wrapped it in theosophical footnotes. It became one of the most influential works in Western transmission of Buddhism, despite being something of an accidental translation repackaged through a theosophist's lens. Better translations now exist.

## Theosophy and the romance of Tibet

Theosophy, per Lopez, systematically distorted Western understanding of Buddhism — especially the British and American versions. Blavatsky claimed enlightened masters originally lived on Atlantis, moved to Tibet when Atlantis sank (because Tibet had less magnetism), and still lived hidden in Tibetan mountains. She called them the *Mahatmas* — a term Mahatma Gandhi's honorific derives from. She claimed to have visited Tibet, met the Panchen Lama, and discovered texts in a language called *Senzar*. No evidence supports any of it; there is no Senzar language.

Lopez's broader point: as 19th- and 20th-century European powers took control of Asia, Tibet remained the one place they couldn't reach. So every myth about Asia that had nowhere else to go migrated into Tibet and stayed there until 1959, when the Dalai Lama fled into exile and reality began to intrude. Shangri-La and *Lost Horizon* are products of this process.

## A three-week tour of Buddhism

Lopez's recommendation for a serious traveler: go to India. The four sacred sites (birth, enlightenment, first teaching, passage into nirvana) plus the cave temples at Ajanta justify a four-week itinerary on their own, and Buddhist pilgrims from around the world converge there. If you add stops: Bangkok in Thailand (great monasteries, visible king-Sangha relationship), Borobudur in Java (the greatest Buddhist monument), and Tibetan monasteries if accessible.
