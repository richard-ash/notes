# Cultural drift into maladaptation

Robin Hanson argues that humanity's defining superpower is *cultural evolution by natural selection* — the application of variation-and-selection dynamics to our unique ability to reliably copy each other's behaviors — and that the parameters governing this process have, over the past three centuries, drifted into a region where culture should drift into maladaptation. He calls this "our big oops": humanity has accidentally broken its own engine of progress, few are aware, and the people who are aware can't yet see attractive ways to fix it.

## The driving-parameters analogy

Hanson frames cultural evolution by analogy to driving a car: keeping the car on the road requires a set of parameters — speed, road curvature, sensor noise, control delay, tire traction — to all stay within acceptable ranges. If overall conditions degrade enough, the car drifts off the road and crashes.

Cultural evolution has analogous parameters. To stay on the "road of progress" Hanson identifies four:

1. **Variety** — how many distinct cultural variants exist to be selected among.
2. **Selection pressure** — how strongly maladaptive cultures get culled.
3. **Rate of environmental change** — how fast adaptive targets move.
4. **Rate of internal cultural drift** — how much culture changes for reasons unrelated to selection.

The first two need to be high relative to the second two. When the comparison fails, drift outpaces selection and culture migrates into maladaptation regardless of which direction it drifts.

## The 300-year decay of the parameters

Hanson's empirical claim is that all four parameters have moved against us since ~1700:

- **Variety has collapsed.** Hundreds of thousands of largely-independent reluctant-to-change peasant cultures were first crushed into a few hundred national cultures, then into a single dominant elite world monoculture. The space of cultural variants under simultaneous trial has shrunk by orders of magnitude.
- **Selection pressure has collapsed.** Health, wealth, and peace mean cultures hardly ever die. War, famine, and pandemics — the historical culling agents — are vastly attenuated. The main remaining cultural-selection mechanism is *differential fertility*, which takes centuries to impose its discipline.
- **Environmental change has accelerated.** The world economy has been doubling faster than every twenty years for over a century, vs. doubling roughly every thousand years three centuries ago.
- **Internal cultural drift has accelerated.** Modernity's hero archetype is the *cultural activist* who pushes for big culture changes mostly unrelated to adaptive pressures. Conformity pressures imposed by a shared elite monoculture mean those changes propagate fast and uniformly.

Hanson distinguishes within "behavior" between (a) easy-to-vary individual behaviors, where innovation in tech and business practices is at record rates, and (b) the harder-to-change cultural norms and game-theory equilibria that are the true "species-defining features." The first category looks fine. The second is the one drifting.

The biological analogy he leans on: in biology, DNA evolves faster when there are more, smaller species, because that better promotes the species-defining features that don't vary easily within a species. The same logic predicts cultural species-defining features should evolve faster with more, smaller cultures — exactly the variety we've lost.

## Two mechanisms of drift

Hanson distinguishes:

- **Random-walk drift** — unpredictable wandering away from previously adaptive behaviors. No direction; just that without selection pulling behaviors back to the adaptive peak, distance from it increases over time.
- **Predictable reversion drift** — culture had moved us *away* from human nature (laziness, selfishness, decadence, present-orientation); when selection pressure relaxes, we revert toward defaults. Specifically, Hanson speculates we are reverting to **forager** styles, after the relaxation of the selection pressures that turned foragers into farmers. He associates the forager reversion with: more travel, leisure, democracy, promiscuity; less war, dominance, self-control, work, religion, and fertility.

The "predictable" channel is the more falsifiable claim and the one Hanson seems most committed to. It connects to his broader work on dreamtime / forager-vs-farmer.

## Examples of the drift

Hanson asserts the following are most plausibly explained by cultural drift into maladaptation:

- **Fertility decline** — the strongest case, because differential fertility is the one remaining selection mechanism and current TFRs in the elite monoculture are sub-replacement.
- Increasing mental illness and drug addiction.
- Legal over-complexity.
- Over-regulation of commerce.
- Over-spending on education and medicine.
- Many other "problematic social trends."

He concedes behavior is still selected to copy world elites — but argues the *status markers* driving that copying are themselves drifting into maladaptation, so the copying mechanism imports the drift rather than correcting it.

## The projection: peak, decline, replacement

A straightforward extrapolation:

1. World population and economy peak, then decline.
2. Innovation grinds to a halt.
3. Today's small insular fertile religious subcultures (Hanson names the Amish and Haredi Jews, who have been doubling every twenty years) come to dominate the world demographically over a few centuries.
4. He draws an explicit parallel to Christians taking over the Roman Empire by doubling at this rate for three centuries.
5. The successor monoculture eventually replicates the same dynamics — rich, healthy, peaceful, full of cultural activists — and again drifts into maladaptation.

So the pattern is cyclic: insular fertile subcultures provide the next monoculture, which then succumbs to the same drift, which then is again replaced by whichever insular subcultures are then most fertile.

## The "but I don't care if I'm maladaptive" rejoinder

Hanson preempts the obvious objection — *I'd rather pursue my values than maximize fitness* — with: maladaptive lineages go away. If you want to achieve anything via your long-term lineage, that lineage has to be adaptive enough to *exist* in order to do other things. Adaptation is a precondition for any other goal that propagates over time, not a goal that competes with them.

This is a Hansonian-style debunking move: it doesn't argue you should value fitness terminally; it argues that any non-fitness terminal value you actually want propagated has to ride atop fitness-adaptive behavior or it doesn't propagate.

## Two paths out (both bad)

Hanson identifies only two structural responses:

### Parameter changes: restore natural cultural selection

- **Fragment the world** into many small insular groups with deep cultural differences. Increases variety. Hard to enforce; comes at great cost to innovation (since modern innovation requires large connected markets and idea-sharing).
- **Revive poverty, war, and disease.** Increases selection pressure. Obvious severe costs.
- **High human fertility.** Restores Malthusian competition.
- **Human-level AI (or ems).** Would naturally have Malthusian wages — but only adds to *cultural* selection pressures if the AI/em populations are free to evolve their own cultures independently of human cultures.

He flags that all of these run real risks of not actually improving the parameters enough to return to good adaptive driving — we may already be too far into the bad-parameter region.

### End of culture: replace natural selection with something else

The alternative — replacing the whole natural-selection system humanity has used for ~1M years with some other process driving cultural change — would be even more radical. He defers this discussion to a later post.

## Connections and tensions

This argument sits in dialogue with several other concept clusters:

- It is the *cultural-evolution analogue* of [[../philosophy/technological-evolution|technological-evolution]]. Both are evolution-by-natural-selection systems with parameters that need to stay good (variety, selection, decomposability). Hanson's claim is structurally parallel but inverted in conclusion: technological evolution looks healthy (record innovation), cultural evolution does not. The Arthur–Polak finding that complex circuits emerge only when stepping-stone goals are present is in the same spirit as Hanson's "we need many smaller cultures to evolve culture-defining features faster."
- It tensions against [[../philosophy/tech-tree-divergence|tech-tree-divergence]]. Nielsen's claim is that civilizations exploring the science/tech tree end up in radically different places due to path-dependence and sensory bias — i.e., divergence is the default. Hanson's lament is that cultural divergence has been *crushed* into a monoculture. The two are not contradictory (Nielsen is about the design-space the civilization explores; Hanson is about how many simultaneous explorers exist), but the implicit empirical pictures diverge: Nielsen sees richness in latent tech-space, Hanson sees collapse in realized cultural-space.
- The forager-reversion mechanism is in the same family as evolutionary-mismatch arguments in psychology and public health: relaxation of a selection pressure that previously bent humans away from a default makes the default reassert itself.

## Confidence and framing notes

This is a single-source opinionated essay by Hanson, who is not a sociologist or demographer by training and whose habit is to provoke with strong structural claims. Claims to weigh skeptically:

- The "monoculture" claim is rhetorically strong but empirically slippery — there is still substantial sub-cultural variation within the global elite layer (national, religious, professional), and the relevant question is *which features* are monocultural, not whether a monoculture exists.
- The list of supposedly maladaptive social trends (mental illness, drug addiction, legal over-complexity, over-regulation, over-spending on education and medicine) is asserted, not argued. Each item has competing explanations that don't require cultural drift.
- The differential-fertility-selection conclusion (Amish/Haredim inherit the earth) is a recurring claim in pronatalist intellectual circles. The empirical evidence on whether religious-subculture fertility advantages survive integration with the monoculture is contested.
- The "we are reverting to foragers" framing is Hanson's recurring trope and depends on his particular reading of forager-vs-farmer differences. Treat as a Hanson-specific lens, not consensus anthropology.

What's strongest in the essay is the *structural* argument: a four-parameter model of cultural evolution that yields concrete falsifiable predictions about when cultural drift will dominate selection. Whether the empirical parameters today are actually as bad as Hanson claims is the soft point; the framework itself is a useful object.
