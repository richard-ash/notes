# Humans Are Not Automatically Strategic

Anna Salamon's 2010 LessWrong essay answers a puzzle posed by "Lionhearted": why do otherwise smart, motivated people pour effort into semi-productive routes (the aspiring comedian who studies *Garfield* re-runs) while massively more effective routes sit untapped?

## The core claim

Being *strategic* — actually optimizing your actions toward a goal — is **not a default human behavior**. It is a narrow, learned skill, not a property that switches on the moment you sincerely want something.

Salamon's explanatory move is a **fitness-landscape / default-to-failure argument**: a randomly chosen eight-year-old fails a calculus test not because of some hidden "fear of success" but because *most* possible answers are wrong and nothing guides him to the right one. "People, and rocks, fail calculus tests by default." Effective goal-pursuit is likewise a tiny target in a huge space of possible actions, and there has been **no strong evolutionary or cultural force** to concentrate our behavior on that target. So ineffective pursuit is the baseline, and needs no special psychological pathology to explain it — the same "most configurations are failures, so failure is the null result" logic Yudkowsky uses when he insists [[shut-up-and-do-the-impossible|"impossible" usually just means "I see no path"]].

## Weak goals vs. real goals

Salamon concedes we have goals *in limited senses*. We (1) tell ourselves and others stories about aiming at goals; (2) seek out activities *consistent with the role* ("learning math," "becoming a comedian"); and (3) feel glad or disappointed at success/failure. But this is goal-*flavored* behavior, not goal-*optimizing* behavior. The gap is a list of heuristics we do **not** automatically run:

- **(a) Name the target** — ask what you're actually trying to achieve.
- **(b) Define the success signal** — how would you *know* you achieved it, and how do you track progress?
- **(c) Be curious** about information that would help — intrinsically, not as a chore.
- **(d) Gather that information** — ask how others achieved the same goal; tally which of your own past strategies worked.
- **(e) Experiment systematically** — test many conjectures, including non-habitual methods, tracking what works.
- **(f) Exploit** — pour the non-exploration energy into the best-performing methods. (Elements (e) and (f) are an explicit **explore/exploit** framing of goal-pursuit.)
- **(g) Verify the goal is really yours** — coherently wanted, not blocked by fear or unresolved "is it worth it?" questions that silently drain energy.
- **(h) Engineer motivation** — use environmental cues and social context to sustain effort against frustration and hyperbolic discounting.

Absent these, "we mostly just do things": act from habit, act from convenience when primed by whatever is in front of us, and pick actions that merely *feel associated* with the goal.

## The mechanism: two systems, unequal training

Salamon's causal story is a **dual-process** one. Humans are "only just on the cusp of general intelligence." Perhaps ~5% of people can even *verbally understand* that these heuristics are useful once they're pointed out — and verbal understanding is a different thing from *automatic implementation*. Our verbal/conversational reasoning systems are far more capable than the **motivational systems** that actually pull behavior. Knowing the glass floor is safe, that ice cream is unhealthy, that exercise serves your goals — none of it auto-updates the reward gradients that move you.

The motivational systems *can* be retrained (visualize ice cream as artery-clogging; walk the glass floor until the fear extinguishes) — but **that retraining is itself not automatic**, which is why most people never do it. This is the load-bearing insight: strategicness fails not because people lack the *idea* but because there's no default pipeline from abstract-verbal-knowing to motivational-doing. The essay is an invitation to deliberately train that pipeline.

## The everyday-evidence footnote

Salamon's most persuasive move is a list of ordinary, low-cost, high-return omissions that reveal how rarely people optimize even when the payoff is obvious:

- Entering long training programs "to make money" without spending a few hours comparing salaries first.
- Typing for hours daily for years while staying a two-finger typist, never running a typing tutor.
- Spending Saturdays "enjoying themselves" without ever tracking which leisure activities are *actually* enjoyable.
- Numerate people fearing illness and accidents and taking safety measures without looking up the relative base rates.
- Settling into one stereotyped mode of studying/writing/socializing without A/B-testing alternatives — even when past experiments gave big boosts.

These are cheap experiments with large expected value that people simply never run. The pattern is the point.

## Connections

- **[[high-agency]]** — Cate Hall's "manifest determination to make things happen" is the temperament that would *drive* someone to run Salamon's heuristics; her "assume every trait is learnable" hack is the practical corollary of Salamon's "you can retrain the motivational system." Salamon supplies the *why it doesn't happen by default*; Hall supplies the *how to force it*.
- **[[shut-up-and-do-the-impossible]]** — shares the default-to-failure logic and the concern with closing the gap between verbal intent and effective action, but Yudkowsky's essay is about *effort at the frontier of the possible* whereas Salamon's is about *the ordinary failure to optimize at all*. Salamon's element (b) (define the success signal) and (e) (systematic experimentation) are the mundane groundwork Yudkowsky assumes.
- **[[personal-productivity-systems]]** — Salamon's (h) (engineer motivation via environment) and the observation that unresolved decisions "sap energy" (g) are the psychological substrate under why productivity systems are needed and why they decay.
- **[[speed-compounds]]** — Kuhn's "impatience → tighter feedback loops" is one concrete instantiation of Salamon's (e)/(f) explore/exploit loop: faster iteration means faster discovery of which methods work.

## Temporal notes

The essay predates (and helped seed) the applied-rationality movement — Salamon co-founded CFAR (the Center for Applied Rationality) in 2012, which turned exactly this "strategicness is a trainable skill, not a default" thesis into a workshop curriculum. The post's closing questions ("has anyone trained themselves to be more strategic? how?") were literally the research program CFAR then pursued. Read today, the piece is best understood as the problem statement whose answer became an institution.
