# High Agency

Cate Hall — a Supreme Court advocate turned world #1 female poker player turned co-founder of Alvea (which set the record for fastest startup to a drug in clinical trials) — defines **agency** as "manifest determination to make things happen." Her central claim, expanded into her 2026 book *You Can Just Do Things*, is that agency is **learned, not innate**: she describes herself as having grown "dumber relative to my peers" with age and compensated by dialing up agency, and says she learned it late (her teens and 20s were mostly low-agency, including a decade spent in a career she disliked "for no reason other than its obviousness").

## The core frame: agency is finding real edges

Radical agency is not "grinding harder than everyone." It's **finding real edges — things you are willing to do that others aren't, usually because they're annoying or unpleasant.** The aversion is the moat: these tradeoffs often never surface into conscious choice, living in "a cloud of aversion that strategically obscures the tradeoff," so most people never notice the edge is available.

Her origin example is poker physical reads. Modern pros grind solver models for tiny mathematical edges while a massive edge — reads — sat almost entirely ignored. Hall and two friends studied reads obsessively and got out-of-distribution results; other pros' response was "nuh-uh, that's not a thing," refusing to consider it *maybe because admitting it would obligate them to study it*. All her agency hacks share this shape: **big, glaring edges that people would rather ignore.** This is the inverse of [[probability-neglect]]'s lesson — instead of exploiting a bias, you exploit other people's *unwillingness to look*.

## The six hacks

1. **Court rejection.** Ask for unreasonable things to calibrate what's actually reasonable; if you only ask for things you get, you're aiming too low. Early-career, you should be rejected from *most* things you apply for. Deliberately apply to jobs you won't get to decouple "no" from dejection. (Her worked example: emailing an org founder "I'm planning to start something similar — would you let me run yours instead?" Crickets from that person, but the same audacity delivered elsewhere launched a co-founded org.)

2. **Seek real feedback.** "If you aren't trying to get real feedback from people who know you, you're cooking without tasting" — the highest-ROI, least-picked self-improvement fruit. Mechanism: lower friction by enabling **anonymous** feedback (she keeps a feedback-form link in her Twitter bio). Anonymity rarely produces cruelty in practice (≈two hurtful messages in a year); occasional gut-punches flag real issues worth fixing.

3. **Increase your surface area for luck.** Meet many people doing adjacent work even with no obvious benefit. Key insight: **relevance is predictable but is a poor proxy for usefulness**, which depends on the other person's enthusiasm and breadth. Counterintuitively, the more confident she is a meeting is relevant, the *less* likely she discovers something exciting. Most of her best collaborations came from near-random meetings.

4. **Assume everything is learnable.** Most subject matter is learnable, and so are supposedly fixed *traits* — confidence, charisma, warmth, tranquility, optimism — if you (1) believe they're malleable and (2) put in real work. Charisma, e.g., is learnable by reading a few books, watching many hours of charismatic people, and adopting some habits; the only blocker is the belief that it's innate. Agency itself is the headline example.

5. **Learn to love the moat of low status** (a concept from her husband Sasha Chapin). Acquiring new skills requires crossing a period where you're visibly bad and ignorant of obvious things. It's a *moat* because you can't leap it and because crossing it confers a real advantage. You can cross quietly (don't ask questions, don't collaborate) but that "nerfs learning" — "learn by doing" requires splashing around in the moat. Thriving there is liberating: she points to deliberately making a famously bad poker call on camera, accepting certain ridicule, as the moment she realized she'd crossed a threshold of unflappability — "the right wrong reason."

6. **Don't work too hard** — which she calls possibly the most important item. Grinding may briefly raise output but **kills creativity and big-picture thinking**, and burnout is the ultimate agency-killer. She uses *reduced agency as an early-warning signal of burnout* — a switch flips and she starts ruling ideas out ("won't work," "not necessary") rather than chasing better versions. Her countermeasures: offline by 6pm, a strict effort-free Sunday Sabbath. Her rule: **never take instructions on how hard to work from someone who hasn't burned out before.**

## Relation to other concepts here

Hack #6 is in productive tension with the determination-maximalist framing of [[shut-up-and-do-the-impossible]] — both prize extraordinary will, but Hall insists the constraint on output is creative capacity, not hours, so the agentic move is often to *stop*. It complements rather than contradicts: Yudkowsky's "refuse every exit from the tension" is about not giving up on the *problem*; Hall's "don't work too hard" is about protecting the faculty that solves it.

The whole essay is downstream of a **growth-mindset** premise applied to character traits, and the "edges others won't take" framing rhymes with the founder-evaluation virtue of being "relentlessly resourceful" — agency is roughly resourcefulness pointed at one's own life rather than a startup.

## Caveats

Single-source, self-reported, and survivorship-flavored: the evidence is one exceptional person's introspection about why *she* succeeded, with the failures (the "crickets" email, the bad poker call) reframed as wins. The advice is directionally plausible and self-consistent, but "I did agentic things and succeeded" can't distinguish the agency from the underlying talent it was compensating for. Treat as a useful operating heuristic, not an established finding.
