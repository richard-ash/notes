# Probability Neglect

Ted Lamade (Collab Fund) argues that the average person's defining cognitive weakness — the "Achilles heel" — is an inability to reason about probabilities, and that this blind spot is both a chronic source of stress and, paradoxically, an engine of human achievement.

## The blind spot

The core failure is **base-rate neglect**: people evaluate outcomes against their hopes rather than against the underlying supply of slots. Lamade's worked example is elite college admissions:

- There are ~25,000 U.S. high schools (most people guess far lower — his interlocutor said 12,000), hence ~25,000 valedictorians per year.
- The Ivy League enrolls ~15,000 freshmen annually. So even if every Ivy seat went to a valedictorian, ~40% of valedictorians could not get one — and far fewer once international students are counted.

The math makes rejection the *statistically normal* outcome, yet families experience it as personal failure. The error isn't in the admissions system; it's in carrying an unconscious prior that the base rate is far more favorable than it is.

## Why it persists: it's exploitable

Lamade's second claim is that the blind spot survives because incentives reward exploiting it, not correcting it:

- **Youth travel sports** charge thousands promising scholarship and admissions pathways that the odds don't support.
- **Sports franchises** monetize fan devotion despite tiny championship probabilities.
- **Active investment managers** sell market-beating strategies even though most underperform the S&P 500 over time.

The mechanism is captured in a Charlie Munger anecdote: a manager promising clients 20% annual returns concedes the number is impossible — "if I told them a lower number, they wouldn't give me any money to invest." The optimistic-but-false probability is a *selling tool*; honest base rates don't raise capital. This connects probability neglect to the persuasion machinery catalogued in [[psychology-of-human-misjudgment]] and [[cialdini-influence]] — the bias is a surface that compliance professionals price into their pitch.

## The inversion: the weakness is also a strength

The essay's distinctive move is to refuse the obvious prescription (teach more probability). Lamade notes that viewed purely through expected-value math, **most of humanity's defining achievements look irrational** — founders starting companies in garages, explorers, the American Founders declaring independence. If everyone correctly internalized the odds, few would attempt long-shot, high-value bets. The thing that produces personal stress (overweighting unlikely good outcomes) is the same thing that produces civilizational upside. The missing variable in the EV calculation is **passion** — a motivation that doesn't require favorable odds to justify the attempt.

This is a survivorship-aware framing of optimism bias: it doesn't deny the bias, it argues the bias is load-bearing for a class of outcomes that rational actors would never pursue.

## The resolution: when the pursuit matters as much as the outcome

Lamade's reconciliation is a decision heuristic for *when to honor the odds and when to override them*:

> Challenge the odds when **the pursuit matters as much as the outcome.**

The diagnostic is the *reason* for pursuing something:

- **Pursuing for love of the activity** → odds matter less, because the process itself pays out regardless of the result. Studying hard, playing a sport, backing a conviction — the education, the teammate lessons, the investing lesson that "even the best are wrong much of the time" survive even when the trophy doesn't.
- **Pursuing for status, recognition, or a promised external outcome** → disappointment is "almost inevitable," because the only payout is the low-probability one.

So the practical takeaway is two-step, not anti-probability: *first* understand the base rates accurately (to stay sane in a world of curated success stories and unrealistic expectations), *then* decide whether the dream is worth pursuing anyway on grounds other than expected value. The probability literacy is what lets the passion be a choice rather than a delusion.

## Implications and caveats

- The heuristic is a useful **regret-minimization** filter, but it leans on a clean separation between intrinsic and extrinsic motivation that is fuzzier in practice — most real pursuits (a startup, a degree) mix process-love with status-seeking, and the essay offers no method for the mixed case.
- "The pursuit was worth it regardless" is itself vulnerable to *post-hoc* rationalization; it works as an *ex ante* commitment ("I will value the process") far better than as an *ex post* consolation.
- The essay is a popular reflection, not behavioral-science writing — claims like "our greatest weakness is probability" are asserted, not measured. Treat it as a framing device rather than evidence about which bias dominates.
