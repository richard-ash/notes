# Cialdini's Six Principles of Influence

Robert Cialdini's *Influence* (1984) catalogs six psychological levers that "compliance professionals" — salespeople, advertisers, fundraisers, cult recruiters, con artists — exploit to extract a yes from people who would otherwise say no. The book's enduring power comes not from claiming people are stupid, but from showing that each principle is a *useful* heuristic that misfires under deliberate manipulation. Charlie Munger reportedly thought enough of the book to send Cialdini a share of Berkshire A stock (then worth ~$75,000) as a thank-you, and *Influence* is one of the foundational sources for [[psychology-of-human-misjudgment|Munger's 25 tendencies]].

Cialdini's framing principle: **a tendency is not destiny**. Awareness alone doesn't immunize you against these levers — but it raises the cost of being captured by them, and lets you build [[mental-models#general-thinking-tools|systems and rules]] that catch you before you commit.

## The six principles

| # | Principle | Core mechanism | What "compliance professionals" do | Defensive posture |
|---|-----------|----------------|------------------------------------|-------------------|
| 1 | **Reciprocity** | We feel obligated to repay what we receive, often disproportionately. | Free salmon dinners from stockbrokers; flowers from Hare Krishna; lavish hospitality before negotiations; "I'll give you a reason"-style price justifications. | Don't accept the gift in the first place. If unavoidable, reciprocate immediately and at equivalent value. Sam Walton's purchasing-agent rule: "not even a hot dog." |
| 2 | **Commitment & Consistency** | Once we commit publicly, we distort facts to stay consistent — even when new evidence should change our minds. | Get the small yes first (foot-in-the-door); have you write it down; have you say it in front of others. | "Strong opinions, weakly held." Avoid public statements about positions you may need to abandon. Watch for sunk-cost escalation. |
| 3 | **Social Proof** | When uncertain, we copy what similar others are doing — especially others like us. | Restaurant front-window seating; "if operators are busy, please call again"; manufactured testimonials. Bernie Madoff weaponized this with celebrity client lists. | Recognize that the crowd's existence is not evidence of its correctness — see Ben Graham: "You are right because your data and reasoning are right." |
| 4 | **Liking** | We say yes to people we know and like. Triggered by physical attractiveness, similarity, compliments, cooperation, association. | Salespeople who "happen" to share your hobby; celebrity endorsements of products the celebrity doesn't use; rapport-building before the ask. | Mentally separate the person from the proposition. Ask: would I take this deal from a stranger? |
| 5 | **Authority** | We defer to titles, uniforms, and expertise — often without checking whether the authority applies to the question. | Actors in lab coats; fake credentials; "Nigerian prince" cons; IRS impersonation. Cialdini's distinction: someone *in* authority (title) vs. someone *an* authority (real expertise). | Decouple expertise from status. Check whether the authority is *in the relevant domain*, and whether they have skin in the game. |
| 6 | **Scarcity** | Things become more desirable when their availability is restricted — or appears restricted. | "Limited time offer"; "only 3 left"; Madoff's pose of refusing investors; limited-edition luxury goods. | Buffett's frame: "Investing is a no-called-strike game" — you don't have to swing. If forced urgency is the only reason to act, don't act. |

## What's actually being exploited

Each principle is a useful shortcut. Reciprocity makes cooperation possible. Consistency makes us legible to others and to ourselves. Social proof is how culture works. Liking is how relationships form. Authority is how expertise scales. Scarcity is how prices encode information. The "compliance professional" doesn't invent the lever — they trigger it artificially, in contexts where the heuristic no longer tracks reality.

This is the same architecture as [[psychology-of-human-misjudgment#the-grand-general-principle|Munger's "ant principle"]]: the bug isn't the heuristic, it's the heuristic firing in an edge case the brain wasn't built for.

## Lollapalooza: the principles in combination

Cialdini stops at six principles operating individually. Munger's central addition is that real-world manipulation usually stacks them — what Munger calls the **lollapalooza effect**. Multi-level marketing, cult recruitment, and Ponzi schemes don't pick one lever; they fire all six simultaneously:

- **Madoff** combined Authority (sophisticated returns), Social Proof (celebrity client list), Scarcity (the pose of refusing money), and Liking (warm Palm Beach social ties).
- **Cult conversion** combines Commitment (small public confessions escalating to large ones), Liking (love-bombing), Authority (the leader), Social Proof (everyone else has converted), Reciprocity (gifts of food/shelter), and Scarcity (you're "chosen").
- **Stanford Prison Experiment**: Munger argues Zimbardo is misread — it's not just role theory and reciprocation, it's commitment-and-consistency working on each guard *as he acts the role*. The action itself rewrites the belief.

The lollapalooza framing is the bridge from Cialdini's clean six to Munger's [[psychology-of-human-misjudgment|25-tendency catalog]]: the principles are real but their nonlinear combination is what produces extreme outcomes.

## The constructive uses

The post emphasizes that each principle can be deployed for good. The compiled examples:

- **Reciprocity** — career mentorship works by doing favors *first*. Cialdini's "prework" advice: when someone thanks you, label the act as partnership rather than dismissing it ("Of course, it's what partners do for each other") — this preserves the persuasive power of the favor for when you later need help.
- **Commitment** — the freshman study-habits experiment: students who wrote down their commitment *and showed it to the room* improved by a full letter grade; private commitment had no effect.
- **Social Proof** — Cialdini's hotel-towel study: "most guests in this room reused their towels" beat "do it for the environment" by 33%. The Petrified Forest sign that said "many people steal wood" *tripled* theft, because it normalized the behavior; the right framing is *the 98% who don't steal*.
- **Liking** — Cialdini's salesperson advice inverts the obvious play: don't try to make clients like you, find a way to genuinely like *them* and show it. "People buy from you because they perceive that you like them," not the reverse.
- **Authority** — emphasize real expertise on questions of fact. The internet has weakened pure authority appeals; people now weight peers more heavily.

## Defensive heuristics

Distilled across the principles:

1. **Refuse the gift.** Most levers are loaded by an upfront concession, gift, or rapport investment. The cheapest counter is to never accept the inbound.
2. **Watch for "the reason."** Cialdini cites the Langer copy-machine experiment: "May I use the Xerox because I have to make some copies" worked nearly as well as a substantive reason. Munger calls this "claptrap reasons" — be alert to *any* explanation, especially when it sounds reasonable but doesn't actually justify the request.
3. **Hold opinions weakly when stated publicly.** Guy Spier's discipline of refusing to discuss current holdings in public is the operational form of this — the public statement creates a [[psychology-of-human-misjudgment#cognitive-conservatism|consistency trap]] that makes the underlying analysis worse.
4. **Distrust manufactured urgency.** If the only reason to act now is the salesperson's clock, don't act now.
5. **Decouple authority from domain.** A title from another field, or a media-confected "expert," is not authority on your question.
6. **Look for the lollapalooza.** When multiple levers are firing simultaneously and aligned in the same direction, that's the strongest signal manipulation is deliberate rather than incidental.

## Connections

The six principles map cleanly onto specific entries in [[psychology-of-human-misjudgment|Munger's 25 tendencies]]: Reciprocity → Tendency #9 (Reciprocation); Commitment & Consistency → Tendency #5 (Inconsistency-Avoidance); Social Proof → Tendency #15 (Social-Proof); Liking → Tendency #2 (Liking/Loving); Authority → Tendency #22 (Authority-Misinfluence); Scarcity → Tendency #14 (Deprival-Superreaction). Munger's catalog is broader (it adds reward/punishment superresponse, envy, denial, availability, contrast, etc.), but Cialdini is one of the named primary sources Munger draws from.

The framework is also adjacent to [[mental-models|the latticework concept]] — Cialdini's six are themselves a small mental-models catalog for one specific domain (compliance), and Munger's argument for cross-disciplinary models is partly that no single framework, including Cialdini's, is enough on its own.

## Temporal notes

The post is from 2016 and predates Cialdini's expansion of the framework. In *Pre-Suasion* (2016) and the 2021 expanded edition of *Influence*, Cialdini added a **seventh principle, Unity** — the persuasive force of shared identity ("we" categories like family, ethnicity, sports team, profession). Unity differs from Liking in that it's not about positive affect toward an individual but about category co-membership; it's the lever behind tribal political appeals and "as a fellow [X]" framings. Readers using the six-principle framework today should treat it as a slightly outdated subset.

Cialdini also describes *pre-suasion* itself as a meta-principle: the practice of priming the audience to be receptive *before* the message is delivered, by directing attention to concepts that make the message land. The six principles describe what to ask for; pre-suasion describes when and how to set up the ask.
