# The Psychology of Human Misjudgment

Charlie Munger's "The Psychology of Human Misjudgment" is a comprehensive taxonomy of cognitive error, revised in 2005 (at age 81) for *Poor Charlie's Almanack*. It catalogs twenty-five psychological tendencies that, while generally useful for survival, systematically mislead modern humans. The framework is distinctive for three reasons: it prioritizes *practical antidotes* over academic description, it insists on analyzing tendencies *in combination* rather than isolation, and it draws heavily from business case studies rather than laboratory experiments.

The talk is a foundational source for the [[mental-models]] latticework concept — Munger's argument that effective thinking requires a checklist of models drawn across disciplines, not mastery of any single framework.

## Munger's meta-method

Munger describes three methodological principles underlying his system:

1. **Inversion.** Following the algebraist Jacobi ("Invert, always invert"), Munger sought good judgment primarily by collecting instances of *bad* judgment, then pondering how to avoid such outcomes. This is the same anti-confirmation-bias discipline he admires in Darwin.

2. **Cross-disciplinary hunting.** Munger ignored professional boundaries: "Why should I search for some tiny, unimportant, hard-to-find new stupidity in my own field when some large, important, easy-to-find stupidity was just over the fence in the other fellow's professional territory?" Real-world problems jump across territorial boundaries.

3. **Checklist usage.** The 25 tendencies are designed as a *checklist* — not a theory to be admired but a tool to be run through when diagnosing a situation. Munger demonstrates this in his McDonnell Douglas case study, walking through the list to identify six or more tendencies acting in confluence.

## The grand general principle

Munger frames the entire system with an observation from social insects: ants operate on simple behavioral algorithms that generally work but catastrophically fail in edge cases (dead-ant pheromone on a live ant; circular marching to death). Human cognition has the same structure — useful heuristics that become systematic errors when context shifts. The grand principle: **cognition is situation-dependent**, so different situations cause different conclusions even in the same person thinking about the same subject.

## The twenty-five tendencies

### Drives and emotions (Tendencies 1–3, 6–8)

| # | Tendency | Core mechanism | Key antidote |
|---|----------|---------------|--------------|
| 1 | **Reward and Punishment Superresponse** | Incentives are superpowers — people consistently underestimate their force. Creates "incentive-caused bias" where decent people rationalize terrible behavior to get rewards. | Design systems (cash registers, sound accounting) that make bad behavior hard. "Never think about something else when you should be thinking about the power of incentives." |
| 2 | **Liking/Loving** | We ignore faults of, comply with wishes of, and distort facts to favor what we love. Works in a feedback loop with admiration. | "See it like it is and love anyway." |
| 3 | **Disliking/Hating** | Mirror of #2: we ignore virtues of, and distort facts against, what we hate. | Recognize that "Politics is the art of marshalling hatreds" and build institutions that channel hatred into nonlethal patterns. |
| 6 | **Curiosity** | Innate drive strongest in humans. Culture amplifies its effectiveness (Athens vs. Rome). | Cultivate it — curiosity is a natural antidote to many other tendencies. |
| 7 | **Kantian Fairness** | Deep expectation of reciprocal courtesy (the golden rule). Causes intense hostility when violated. | Recognize this drive when designing systems. |
| 8 | **Envy/Jealousy** | Evolutionary origin in competition for scarce resources. Munger notes it is virtually absent from psychology textbooks despite being among the most powerful forces. Buffett: "It is not greed that drives the world, but envy." | A general taboo prevents naming it, which makes it harder to counteract. |

### Cognitive conservatism (Tendencies 4–5)

| # | Tendency | Core mechanism | Key antidote |
|---|----------|---------------|--------------|
| 4 | **Doubt-Avoidance** | The brain rushes to eliminate doubt by reaching *some* decision. Triggered by puzzlement combined with stress. | Forced delay (as in judicial proceedings). Wear a "mask" of objectivity — the mask helps create real objectivity. |
| 5 | **Inconsistency-Avoidance** | The brain conserves programming space by being reluctant to change. First conclusions, habits, identities, and commitments all resist revision. Munger compares the mind to a human egg: "When one sperm gets in, there's an automatic shut-off device that bars any other." | Darwin's practice: intensively consider disconfirming evidence, especially for hypotheses you like. Force yourself to hear the strongest case for the opposing view before deciding. "An ounce of prevention is worth a pound of cure" — it's easier to prevent a bad habit than to cure one. |

Munger notes these two tendencies are especially dangerous *in combination*: a quickly reached conclusion (Doubt-Avoidance) that then resists all revision (Inconsistency-Avoidance) is a recipe for permanent error. This is why courts require hearing both sides before deciding, and why the U.S. Constitutional Convention used secret proceedings with reversible votes.

### Social influence (Tendencies 9, 15, 22, 24)

| # | Tendency | Core mechanism | Key antidote |
|---|----------|---------------|--------------|
| 9 | **Reciprocation** | Extreme automatic tendency to reciprocate both favors and disfavors. Operates substantially at the subconscious level. Cialdini's "door-in-the-face" experiment: an absurd large request followed by a smaller "concession" tripled compliance. | Sam Walton's rule: purchasing agents may not accept even a hot dog from a vendor. "You can always tell the man off tomorrow." |
| 15 | **Social-Proof** | People automatically think and do what they see others thinking and doing. Triggered most easily by puzzlement and stress combined. Inaction by others is equally powerful social proof. | "Learn how to ignore the examples from others when they are wrong, because few skills are more worth having." |
| 22 | **Authority-Misinfluence** | Humans are born to follow leaders in dominance hierarchies. Can turn brains to "mush" — Cialdini's "r. ear" anecdote, the tarpon-fishing "give him the rod" anecdote, Milgram's obedience experiment. | Simulator training for copilots to override foolish orders. Be careful whom you appoint to power — a dominant authority figure will be hard to remove. |
| 24 | **Reason-Respecting** | Humans comply more readily when given reasons, even meaningless ones ("I need to make some copies"). Carl Braun's rule: always tell Who, What, Where, When, and *Why*. | Give correct reasons when teaching or ordering. But beware: the tendency is so strong it makes people comply even with claptrap reasons. |

### Perception and judgment errors (Tendencies 10–14, 16, 18)

| # | Tendency | Core mechanism | Key antidote |
|---|----------|---------------|--------------|
| 10 | **Influence-from-Mere-Association** | Conditioning via association (not just direct reward). High price → assumed high quality. Past success → assumed future success. Includes "Persian Messenger Syndrome" — punishing bearers of bad news. | Examine past success for accidental, non-causative factors. At Berkshire: "Always tell us the bad news promptly. It is only the good news that can wait." |
| 11 | **Pain-Avoiding Psychological Denial** | Reality too painful to bear → distort facts until bearable. Most extreme with love, death, and chemical dependency. | AA's approach: use multiple psychological tendencies in concert to counter denial. Stay far from any conduct likely to drift into chemical dependency. |
| 12 | **Excessive Self-Regard** | 90% of Swedish drivers judge themselves above average. Includes the "endowment effect" (overvaluing what you own) and in-group preference. The "Tolstoy effect": worst criminals don't see themselves as bad. | Force objectivity on yourself. Underweigh face-to-face impressions, overweigh track records. "Fixable but unfixed bad performance is bad character." |
| 13 | **Overoptimism** | "What a man wishes, that also will he believe" (Demosthenes). | Trained habitual use of probability math (Fermat and Pascal). Evolution's risk heuristics are inadequate. |
| 14 | **Deprival-Superreaction** | Loss aversion: a $10 loss hurts more than a $10 gain helps. Extends to *near-misses* — almost getting something triggers the same reaction as losing it. Causes bureaucratic turf wars, gambling addiction, sunk-cost escalation, and irrational resistance to any takeaway. | The Buffett rule for open-outcry auctions: don't go. Good poker skill learned young. |
| 16 | **Contrast-Misreaction** | The nervous system measures by contrast, not absolute units. A $1,000 leather dashboard seems cheap next to a $65,000 car. Small incremental steps toward disaster go unnoticed. "A small leak will sink a great ship." | Be alert to anchoring and boiling-frog dynamics. Real estate brokers' trick: show three terrible houses first. |
| 18 | **Availability-Misweighing** | The mind overweighs what is easily available — vivid, recent, or salient. "An idea or a fact is not worth more merely because it is easily available to you." | Checklists. Emphasize factors that *don't* produce reams of easily available numbers. Hire skeptical advocates for opposing views. |

### Biological and environmental factors (Tendencies 17, 19–21, 23)

| # | Tendency | Core mechanism |
|---|----------|---------------|
| 17 | **Stress-Influence** | Light stress improves performance; heavy stress causes dysfunction or breakdown. Pavlov's late work: extreme stress can reverse conditioned behaviors entirely. Cults exploit this. |
| 19 | **Use-It-or-Lose-It** | All skills attenuate with disuse. Raise skills to fluency (not just enough to pass a test) — fluent skills are lost more slowly and return faster. Practice via "aircraft simulator" equivalents. |
| 20 | **Drug-Misinfluence** | Destructive power widely known; intensely habit-forming. |
| 21 | **Senescence-Misinfluence** | Cognitive decay with age; intensely practiced skills are more resistant. Continuous learning with joy helps delay the inevitable. |
| 23 | **Twaddle** | Humans are born to prattle. The honeybee who cannot communicate "nectar is straight up" does an incoherent dance instead of staying silent. "The principal job of academic administration is to keep the people who don't matter from interfering with the work of the people that do." |

### The meta-tendency (Tendency 25)

**Lollapalooza Tendency** — the tendency to get extreme consequences from *confluences* of psychological tendencies acting in the same direction. Munger argues this is the most important and most neglected principle in psychology. Academic psychology historically studied tendencies in isolation (one variable, one experiment), but real-world catastrophes and cult conversions involve six or more tendencies acting simultaneously.

Munger's canonical example: the **McDonnell Douglas evacuation test disaster**. An airliner evacuation test using elderly passengers in a darkened hangar, 20 feet above a concrete floor, caused ~40 serious injuries across two attempts (the second attempt being repeated *after* the first failed). Munger identifies Reward-Superresponse, Doubt-Avoidance, Authority-Misinfluence, Inconsistency-Avoidance, Social-Proof, and Deprival-Superreaction all acting in confluence.

Similarly, Munger uses the lollapalooza concept to explain Milgram's obedience experiment (which he argues involves at least six tendencies, not just authority) and cult brainwashing techniques.

## Munger's practical implications

Munger closes with ten examples of psychology-informed institutional design that actually work:

1. Carl Braun's communication rule (always explain *why*)
2. Pilot simulator training (practice rarely-used skills)
3. Alcoholics Anonymous (multiple tendencies used to counter addiction)
4. Clinical medical training ("see one, do one, teach one")
5. U.S. Constitutional Convention rules (secret proceedings, reversible votes, single final vote)
6. Granny's Rule for self-management (unpleasant tasks before pleasant ones)
7. Harvard Business School's decision trees (countering intuitive errors with explicit math)
8. Johnson & Johnson's acquisition autopsies (comparing predictions with outcomes)
9. Darwin's anti-confirmation-bias discipline (→ modern double-blind studies)
10. Buffett's rule for auctions (don't attend)

## The man-with-a-hammer warning

A recurring theme throughout the talk: **man-with-a-hammer tendency** — "To a man with only a hammer, every problem looks pretty much like a nail." Munger uses B.F. Skinner as his cautionary example: brilliant early work on incentives, reputation ruined by trying to explain *everything* through incentives alone. The antidote is the [[mental-models]] latticework — maintain a diverse toolkit of psychological (and other) models so you're never locked into one lens.

## Connections

Munger's framework is a *primary source* for the broader [[mental-models]] catalog, which draws directly from his "latticework of mental models" concept. The 25 tendencies overlap significantly with Kahneman and Tversky's cognitive bias research, Cialdini's six principles of influence (which Munger credits extensively), and the systems dynamics perspective in [[leverage-points]] — particularly Meadows's observation that the most powerful leverage points involve changing mindsets and paradigms, not just parameters.

The emphasis on institutional design as antidote (cash registers, accounting systems, judicial procedures) connects to the [[map-and-territory]] insight that acknowledging the gap between perception and reality is only useful if you then build *systems* to correct for it.
