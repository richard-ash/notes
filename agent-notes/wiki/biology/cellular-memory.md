# Cellular Memory

Research increasingly suggests that memory — the ability to encode, store, and respond differently to past experience — does not require a nervous system. Individual cells, including non-neuronal human cells, demonstrate memory-like properties that may represent an evolutionarily ancient adaptation predating nervous systems by hundreds of millions of years.

## The spacing effect in kidney cells

Nikolay Kukushkin and Thomas J. Carew at NYU published research in *Nature Communications* (2025) showing that human kidney cells can distinguish between continuous chemical signals and spaced pulses. They engineered kidney and immature nerve cells to fluoresce when cAMP response elements activated, then exposed them to chemical bursts mimicking neurotransmitter patterns. Spaced stimuli produced longer-lasting responses than continuous ones — replicating the **spacing effect**, the well-established finding across animal memory systems that distributed practice produces stronger retention than massed practice.

The implication is striking: the spacing effect, previously studied only in organisms with nervous systems, appears to operate at the level of individual cells using the same molecular signaling pathways (cAMP/CREB) that underpin synaptic memory in neurons.

## A century of dismissed evidence

The idea that single-celled organisms can learn has a long, contentious history:

- **Herbert Spencer Jennings (1906)** documented that the ciliate *Stentor roeselii* modified its avoidance responses after repeated exposure to irritating dye — a graded behavioral repertoire suggesting learning. His findings were dismissed because they contradicted the dominant "tropism" theory (that single-celled organisms respond mechanically to stimuli).
- **Beatrice Gelber (1960s)** claimed to train paramecia to associate a metal wire with food through associative learning. Her work was rejected under behaviorist orthodoxy, which reserved "learning" for animals with nervous systems.
- **Jeremy Gunawardena (2019)** successfully reproduced Jennings' original experiments, validating the 100-year-old findings.

The article highlights how **definitional bias** — equating memory exclusively with observable behavior in organisms with brains — artificially constrained the field for a century. Researchers whose findings contradicted prevailing dogma were dismissed not because their experiments failed, but because the conclusions were ideologically unacceptable.

## Redefining memory

Kukushkin proposes that cellular memory is "an embodied response to change" rather than something requiring conscious recall. At the cellular level, spaced signals may indicate a stable, recurring feature of the environment worth encoding, while massed (continuous) signals suggest transient chaos — making the spacing effect adaptive across all biological scales.

Sam Gershman (Harvard) argues for an even broader redefinition: memory is any physical mark that carries information about the past — vaccinations, scars, even footprints. Under this framing, ancient cellular memory mechanisms **complement** synaptic plasticity rather than compete with it; neurons inherited and specialized an already-existing cellular capacity.

## Evolutionary implications

If individual eukaryotic cells possess fundamental cognitive properties, these likely trace to free-living unicellular ancestors. Memory, on this account, is not a product of neural complexity but a basic property of life that nervous systems later elaborated. This reframes the evolution of cognition: brains didn't invent memory — they scaled it.
