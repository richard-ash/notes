# Sperm Whale Coda Phonology

Sperm whales (*Physeter macrocephalus*) communicate using short series of clicks called **codas**. What makes their communication system remarkable — and possibly unique among non-human animals — is the degree to which it parallels the phonological structure of human language. Codas were traditionally classified by timing features alone (click count and inter-click intervals), but recent work has revealed a spectral dimension: each click carries one of two discrete formant structures, termed **coda vowels**, that behave like the vowels *a* and *i* in human speech.

## Coda structure and classification

A coda is a short burst of clicks, typically 3–9, produced by vibrating phonic lips in the whale's nasal complex. The resulting sound passes through the distal air sac, which acts as an acoustic filter — analogous to the human vocal tract shaping glottal pulses into vowels. Traditional coda types (e.g., 1+1+3, 5R₁, 5R₂, 7D, 8D) are defined by four semi-independent parameters identified by Sharma et al.:

1. **Rhythm** — the relative timing between clicks
2. **Tempo** — the overall duration of the coda
3. **Ornamentation** — the addition of an extra click
4. **Rubato** — tempo modulation across consecutive codas in an exchange

Orthogonal to these timing features, Beguš et al. (2025) identified a spectral dimension: clicks within a coda consistently have either one formant peak (*a*-quality) or two formant peaks (*i*-quality), with minimal mixing within a single coda. Some *i*-codas additionally show dynamic formant trajectories — rising or falling over the coda's duration — analogized to diphthongs in human speech.

## Five phonological parallels with human language

Beguš et al. (2026) demonstrate five properties of coda vowels that closely mirror phonetic and phonological phenomena in human languages, suggesting convergent evolution of communication system complexity:

### 1. Vowel quality–coda type correlation

Coda vowel quality is not uniformly distributed across coda types. In the 1+1+3 type, *a*- and *i*-vowels occur roughly equally, but in 5R₁ and 5R₂ types, *a*-vowels predominate. This parallels tone–vowel interactions in human languages: in Slovenian, for instance, the vowel *i* preferentially surfaces with high tone, while *ε* prefers low tone.

### 2. Intrinsic vowel duration

*a*-codas are significantly longer than *i*-codas of the same type (β̂ = −0.13, p = 0.017 for 1+1+3 codas). This mirrors a cross-linguistic universal in human speech: low vowels like /a/ are intrinsically longer than high vowels like /i/ or /u/, due to the wider jaw opening required.

### 3. Contrastive vowel length

*i*-codas show a bimodal duration distribution — short *i* vs. long *ī* — while *a*-codas are unimodally distributed (confirmed by Hartigan's dip test in at least 3 of 4 whales studied). This parallels phonological vowel length contrasts in human languages like Hungarian (*bor* 'wine' vs. *bór* 'boron'), Arabic, Finnish, and Latin.

### 4. Baseline duration differences across individuals

Individual whales have different baseline coda durations — Pinchy's *a*-codas average 170 ms longer than Atwood's. This parallels habitual speaking rate differences among human speakers, where individuals consistently produce vowels at faster or slower rates.

### 5. Coarticulation

When a whale transitions between coda vowel qualities (e.g., from an *a*-coda to an *i*-coda), the first click of the new coda is significantly more likely to mismatch — carrying spectral properties of the preceding coda's vowel quality (β̂ = −1.31, p = 0.002). This is structurally identical to coarticulation in human speech, where the pronunciation of a sound is influenced by adjacent sounds (e.g., the vowel in *cli* is pronounced higher than in *cla* in anticipation of the following segment).

Of 165 codas with a single mismatched click, 47.9% of mismatches occur on the first click, suggesting the first click holds a special phonological status — analogous to how word-initial segments in human language are particularly susceptible to coarticulatory influence from preceding words.

## Evidence for active control

Several lines of evidence argue that coda vowel quality is actively controlled by whales rather than being an acoustic artifact:

- Vowel quality has a clear **bimodal distribution** with minimal intermediate values
- Click quality is **consistent within codas** — almost all clicks in a coda share the same vowel quality
- Formant trajectories in diphthong-like *i*-codas are **consistent across tokens** and individuals
- The patterns are **independent of whale movement** and appear across different individuals
- The **uneven distribution** of vowel qualities across coda types, despite no known phonetic coupling between timing and spectral features, implies deliberate selection

## Implications

The depth of these parallels is striking. The sperm whale system exhibits analogs to tone (coda type as pitch contour), vowel quality (formant structure), vowel length (short vs. long *i*), speaking rate (individual baseline duration), and coarticulation — all achieved through an entirely different anatomical apparatus (phonic lips and distal air sac vs. vocal folds and oral cavity). This represents convergent evolution of phonological complexity across ~90 million years of independent evolution.

Whether these structural features carry semantic content remains unknown. The authors are careful to note they have not demonstrated that coda contrasts are meaningful — but the systematicity of the patterns is "highly suggestive that they are." If confirmed, the sperm whale communication system would represent the most phonologically complex non-human communication system documented.

The data come from eastern Caribbean sperm whales (EC1 clan) recorded off Dominica from 2014–2018 by The Dominica Sperm Whale Project. The study analyzed 1,106 focal codas from 3,984 total, with detailed analysis of 628 codas from four well-recorded whales (Atwood, Fork, Pinchy, and TBB).
