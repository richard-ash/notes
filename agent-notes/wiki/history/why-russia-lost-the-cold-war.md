# Why Russia Lost the Cold War

A lecture by Prof. Sarah C. M. Paine (U.S. Naval War College, Sept. 2020) that structures the end of the Cold War as irreducibly multi-causal, organized around argument and counterargument. Paine draws heavily on interviews conducted jointly by the **Gorbachev Foundation and the Hoover Institution** with principals from both the Reagan and Gorbachev administrations, giving the analysis a distinctive "view from the losing side" character.

## External causes ("murder")

### Reagan and the arms race

The simplest narrative: Reagan's military buildup forced a symmetric response the Soviets could not afford. The Soviet economy was roughly one-seventh of the combined Western and Japanese economies, yet military spending consumed an estimated 40–75% of the Soviet budget (vs. <8% for the US). Gorbachev told the Politburo in 1986 that the Americans were "betting on exhausting the USSR through the missile defense race." Paine's lesson: beware symmetric strategies when your economy is drastically smaller.

### Helsinki Accords and human rights

The 1975 Helsinki Accord — which the Soviets wanted to confirm post-WWII borders — included human rights provisions the West inserted almost as an afterthought. These unexpectedly energized dissident movements across the Eastern Bloc. Robert Gates called the results "benefits beyond our wildest imagination." Carter's subsequent human rights campaign resonated deeply. Foreign Minister Shevardnadze asked his own ministry in 1990: "Is it a country which is feared or a country which is respected?" Multiple Russian officials confirmed that ideological delegitimization was devastating.

### The China card

After the 1969 Sino-Soviet border war (during which the Soviets asked the US whether it would accept a nuclear strike on China — the US said no), Nixon and Mao recognized the USSR as their primary adversary. The result: the USSR had to militarize its enormous Chinese border with fully mechanized, nuclear-armed forces — a crippling case of strategic overextension.

### The submarine threat

Under Carter (an Annapolis graduate and submariner), the US adopted aggressive submarine patrolling outside Soviet sub-bastions, undermining Soviet confidence in a second-strike capability. Marshal Akhromeev told American hosts in 1987: "We know you know where our submarines are. We don't know where yours are. You — the United States Navy — are the problem."

### Cumulative presidential effects

Paine synthesizes: each president opened opportunities for successors. Nixon played the China card; Ford achieved Helsinki; Carter launched both the human rights campaign and the military buildup Reagan finished. US foreign policy was "relentlessly consistent" across both parties around free trade, democracy, and anti-communism.

## Internal causes ("suicide")

Paine invokes Arnold Toynbee: "Civilizations die from suicide, not by murder."

### Empire collapse — the domino effect in reverse

The domino theory actually applied to the communists. Once democratic demonstrations began in one Warsaw Pact country, they spread uncontrollably:

- **Poland (1980–89):** Solidarity — the only independent bottom-up opposition in Eastern Europe — leveraged worker strikes, the Catholic Church, and a Polish pope. In the 1989 elections, Solidarity won every contestable seat and organized "none of the above" votes for party-designated seats, destroying communist legitimacy.
- **East Germany (1989):** 70,000 demonstrators in Leipzig grew to 1.3 million across 210 locations. The accidental opening of the Berlin Wall (Günter Schabowski "winged it" at a press conference about travel regulations) was pivotal and irreversible. Within a week, 9 million East Germans visited the West; within a month, over 1% had emigrated.

### Economic collapse

Soviet growth rates lagged the US by 1–2% per year for the decade before Gorbachev — with devastating compounding effects. Worse, pervasive lying throughout the command economy meant no one had accurate data. Everyone was "grossly misallocating labor and capital, unaware they were doing so until it had metastasized into a crisis." Gorbachev told the Central Committee: "We are encircled not by invincible armies but by superior economies."

### Third World overextension and nationality unrest

By the 1980s the USSR had accumulated expensive, non-performing allies worldwide (Angola, South Yemen, Nicaragua, etc.) just as a global recession tanked commodity prices including oil. Simultaneously, ethnic unrest erupted in 70+ locations from the mid-1980s onward.

### Gorbachev's miscalculations

Paine invokes Tocqueville: "The most dangerous moment for a bad government is when it begins to reform." Gorbachev made four critical false assumptions:

1. History only moves forward toward communism, never backward — but Eastern Europeans withdrew from socialism entirely.
2. Liberating Eastern Europe would produce "socialism with a human face" — instead they "brutally turned their backs."
3. If the Warsaw Pact dissolved, NATO would too; if COMECON ended, the EC would too — he failed to grasp the difference between organizations by compulsion and those people want to join.
4. The US, like a continental power, would prefer a divided Germany — instead Bush and Kohl fast-tracked reunification.

### Yeltsin's actions ("suicide on purpose")

Yeltsin amended the constitution in 1990 to remove the Communist Party's "leading role," then in 1991 signed the Belovezha Accords dissolving the Soviet Union.

## The Bush-Kohl partnership

Paine devotes significant time to the diplomatic skill that managed German reunification — the climactic act of the Cold War:

- **Kohl bought reunification:** half a billion DM to East Germany for travel relaxation, half a billion DM to Hungary for opening its Austrian border, ultimately 15 billion DM plus housing for repatriated Soviet soldiers ("put them in fancy apartments and they'll be busy buying furniture").
- **Bush managed Western allies:** deliberately delaying summits so events on the ground advanced beyond reversal, neutralizing French and British opposition (Thatcher never came around: "Germany will be the Japan of Europe, but worse").
- **Tag-team diplomacy** moved so fast Gorbachev could not keep up; financial unification went "right under the Soviet radar."
- **Gulf War calculus:** The First Gulf War (Aug 1990) threatened to derail Cold War termination. Paine argues this explains why the US stopped at the 100-hour mark — going further would have alienated Russia and jeopardized the larger prize.
- **Neither leader humiliated Gorbachev,** buying Eastern Europe two decades free of Russian interference for "the glue to set" as they integrated with the West. Bush paid for this restraint with his 1992 election defeat.

## Umbrella explanations

Paine presents two bookend interpretations:

- **Inevitability:** The Soviet system was so fundamentally rotten collapse was a matter of time. Rocket scientist Ryzhov: "It's the rottenness of the system."
- **Contingency:** It took the confluence of every single factor. Deputy FM Kovalev: "All factors merged — internal, ideological, economic, military — to end the Cold War."

## Implications for today

Paine draws several forward-looking conclusions:

- **Russia and China both studied the Soviet collapse.** Putin tries to reassemble the empire but "doesn't understand economics." China concluded: stick to economic reforms, skip political liberalization, and homogenize nationalities (which Paine connects to the Uyghur repression).
- **A durable Sino-Russian alliance is unlikely** given their long adversarial history and mutual distrust.
- **Military power is a function of economic power.** The parallel to US national debt is real.
- **On China:** containment through strengthening alliances (Japan, India, Australia, Vietnam) and building Asian institutions. But above all: "Fix the United States — that is the absolutely number one thing."
