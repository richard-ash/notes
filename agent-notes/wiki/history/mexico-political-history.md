# Mexico: political and economic history

Historian Paul Gillingham — a Cork-raised, Oxford-trained specialist on the Mexican Revolution, and author of *Mexico: A 500-Year History* (2026) — uses a conversation with Tyler Cowen to sketch a unifying reading of five centuries of Mexican politics. The through-line: **Mexico coheres not because the center is strong but because the center has learned to be hands-off**, and the structural features that look like dysfunction (federalism, ejidos, the cargo system, rigged-but-local elections) are the same ones that kept the country together when every peer state failed.

## The "miracle" of territorial cohesion

After 1821 the other post-colonial super-states — Gran Colombia, the United Provinces of the Río de la Plata — fragmented within a decade. India split within two years of independence. Mexico did not. Álvaro Enrigue calls it "the miracle that Mexico exists."

Gillingham's explanation is **geography-enforced federalism**: Mexico's mountains make central rule impossible, so "savvy governments, or governments with no choice — quite often the same thing — are very hands-off." Hands-off rule became the habit, and the habit became the state's survival strategy.

- **Yucatán** came close to leaving (in the 1840s Texas chartered warships to defend a Yucatán secession) but was held back by its own racial divide: a small white plantocracy in Mérida had no appetite to lead a joined-up independence struggle, and the Maya state wasn't cohesive enough to carry one alone.
- **Quintana Roo** was carved out as an administrative containment zone for the eastern Maya rebels of the Caste Wars — a line on a map drawn in profound optimism. What actually kept it distinct was terrain and depopulation; it only became materially significant when the state invented mass tourism there (Cancún was a village until the late 1960s).

## Independence was a long process, not a date

The textbook date of 1821 is misleading. The Spanish kept a garrison at Veracruz until 1829. A failed French invasion followed within two years, then the US invasions, then civil war. Gillingham dates real independence to **1867**, when Mexican forces shot a European-imposed emperor (Maximilian) — "you don't shoot emperors in global history; the Mexicans do" — which ended other empires' pretensions and opened the door to the stabilization and infrastructure build-out of the Porfiriato.

Porfirio Díaz gets a rare full-throated compliment from Gillingham as "brilliantly gifted." His luck was to arrive when the industrial world craved Mexican resources (at one point absorbing ~half of British global investment), which finally supplied the money for infrastructure.

## Why Mexico avoided postwar military rule and civil war

Mexico's central 20th-century paradox: one of the great egalitarian revolutions produced one of the Americas' most unequal economies, yet delivered clockwork six-year peaceful transfers of power from 1929 on, no presidential assassinations after 1929, and no JFK/RFK/MLK-style alphabet of political murders. No army coups. Gillingham's explanation has three threads:

1. **Qualitative redistribution beyond the Gini.** Rapidly growing urban populations got dirt-cheap subsidized cinema, low housing rates, and a healthcare system that punches far above Mexico's income band.
2. **Rigged-but-permeable elections.** National elections were rigged until roughly the 2010s, but local ones were contestable-through-pressure: any group willing to riot about it — Oaxaqueños being the archetype — could force their candidates in. He argues this unconventional route to representation at times out-punched the British system, where parties simply impose candidates on constituencies.
3. **War weariness as political pedagogy.** The Revolution killed one Mexican in ten. Every leader into the 1950s was trained in the pragmatic imperative of *keep the lid on* — conciliation usually beats repression — which kept the army out of politics.

The Cárdenas land reform fits inside this. Much of it "failed" on its surface terms: peasants got land but were often tied into factory-farming the same commodities. Yet every *ejido* got a medical office (the backbone of rural healthcare), and the symbolic payoff of "having land" kept the countryside quiescent for the 60 years during which it was systematically stripped of resources to subsidize urban industrialization through capped maize prices.

## The ejido and the human-capital debate

Cowen pushes the standard critique: didn't the *ejido* system hold Mexico back by preventing land sales and migration, leaving Mexico under-urbanized versus China? Gillingham largely declines the premise:

- Mexico *did* urbanize massively. The population tipped urban in 1960. Peasants were pushed out by artificially capped food prices that financed cheap industrial labor.
- Ejidos were themselves used as commercial farming vehicles, sometimes fronting for foreign firms like Anderson Clayton, who instructed ejidatarios what to plant. Functionally, the outcome converged on something China-shaped regardless.
- Peasants wanted **subsistence autonomy** — the guarantee they could feed their family through the next harvest — not commodification. In a low-income economy this is the more stable structure, not the less rational one.

On human capital, Gillingham pushes back against the "Mexico under-invested in people" frame. What actually matters for a demographic transition, he argues, is **primary-school retention, especially for women**, and Mexico's record on the first three years of schooling outpaced India, Kenya, and Egypt at the equivalent stage. By the late 1960s, 73% of women were getting those first three years — a rate unique in the middle-income world of the time. Cowen's counter — that Latin America has above-average *years* of schooling but poor test scores and stunningly low English fluency (~7%) for a US neighbor — is acknowledged, but Gillingham suspects the aggregate hides a huge urban-rural chasm.

## The fertility collapse

Mexico's fertility rate has fallen below the United States'. Gillingham credits two things:

- **Church-state separation.** Uniquely in Latin America, the Catholic Church was kept out of political life enough that the government could aggressively promote contraception in the 1970s–80s without priestly backlash. Mexico became the global model for non-coercive population control (unlike India's coercive campaigns) and hosted the UN conferences on the topic.
- **Educated women choosing contraception.** In micro-studies (e.g. San José de Gracia in highland Jalisco), as soon as the pill became available in the 1960s, uptake spiked — even in socially conservative villages. This correlates globally with women's primary education, which Mexico had quietly built up.

This is a sharp corrective to the "Catholic Latin America = high fertility" stereotype: the Mexican Revolution's 19th-century anti-clerical inheritance turns out to have done demographic work a century later.

## Regional patterns: why certain states produce leaders and violence

Gillingham finds regional concentrations of political leadership non-coincidental:

- **Oaxaca** (Juárez, Díaz, two-thirds of Porfirian congressmen at one point): mountainous, decentralized, with an "almost Swiss canton" culture where every village declared itself a county at independence. Centuries of practicing competitive local politics train Oaxaqueños to be good at politics. This also explains why Oaxaca has preserved so much indigenous culture, has a famously distinctive cuisine (he ranks Oaxacan and Yucatecan cuisines head and shoulders above the rest), and now attracts expatriate colonies.
- **Coahuila** (Madero, Carranza): a diversified border state — copper mines, irrigated agriculture, cross-border wealth — producing cosmopolitan landowners (Madero was educated in Paris and Berkeley) who could imagine replacing the sclerotic Porfirian regime.
- **Guerrero**: persistently violent for two centuries because its combination of mountains, a large coastal Afro-Mexican population discriminated against and good at violence, proximity to Mexico City (which makes its rebellions feel existential to the center), and historic oppressive conditions generate a reinforcing cycle of repression and revolt.

## The geography of contemporary violence

Gillingham's working model for where violence concentrates: **produce illicit goods + transship illicit goods + terrain that can kill soldiers** = a violent state. Add multi-cartel frontlines (no single-organization dominance) for amplification.

- **Michoacán**: produces meth, heroin poppy, marijuana; transships via the white-elephant port of Lázaro Cárdenas; mountains hide labs and bury soldiers; avocado and lime farmers make excellent extortion targets; and no single cartel has ever locked it down.
- **Yucatán**: very low violence, even Finland-low in some years. Foreigners have a de facto colonial-era *fuero* — killing tourists is bad business — and the Quintana Roo drug transshipment corridor Gillingham remembers from the 1990s (twice-daily small planes over Tulum) has been superseded by other routes.
- **Sinaloa and Colima**: counter-cases showing that single-cartel dominance produces temporary peace, and that this can invert fast. Sinaloa was safe until ~2024 under unchallenged cartel control; Colima was tranquil until recently and is now Mexico's most violent state.
- **Monterrey / Nuevo León**: Gillingham already feels safe there and predicts it stays that way — nothing to grow, little to tranship, and plenty of small-business extortion opportunities make drug-cartel war bad business.

## The judicial-election reform as regression

The AMLO-era reform electing Mexican judges is, in Gillingham's view, "one of the most unfortunate things I've seen come out of Mexican politics in the last decade." His argument:

- Drug-trading organizations have strong interest in sympathetic judges. **Electing judges is lower-cost than threatening them**, and killing judges is a high-risk strategy that usually provokes the state. Move them into your pocket legally and the problem goes away.
- Mexican electoral turnout is typically high, but judicial-election turnout was around 13% — in itself a condemnation of the project.
- Motives: Morena's drive to cement regional power, plus AMLO-style philosophical populism.

This sits against the broader system's longstanding strengths and is clearly marked by Gillingham as a step backwards, not a mere reorganization.

## Smaller observations worth keeping

- **The "20 minutes outside of town" rule for Mexican food.** Both Cowen and Gillingham independently report that asking a local taxi driver for the best meal in a nearby pueblo consistently beats in-town restaurants. Gillingham's best Yucatán meal was 30 minutes outside Mérida in a warehouse full of locals; Cowen's barbacoa in Tlacolula was the same pattern.
- **Monte Albán is overrated.** Both agree it is the most boring major archaeological site in Mexico, and small tombs like Zaachila or once-empty sites like Cobá and Xochicalco are better experiences.
- **A two-week educational trip.** Gillingham's itinerary deliberately excludes Mexico City and the famous ruins: Tijuana → Zacatecas (Churrigueresque Baroque from the colonial silver boom) → Aguascalientes during the *feria* (bullfights, temporary legal gambling, symphony) → Xalapa, Veracruz (untouristed, temperate) → San Cristóbal de las Casas (colonial Chiapas).
- **Best Mexican restaurant in NYC (Gillingham's pick):** Santo Taco, downtown — order the mushroom taco and the carnitas; a speakeasy-style restaurant sits beneath the taqueria.
- **Gillingham's next book.** A prehistory of money laundering, anchored on a document he found in the British Foreign Office: a 1920s query from a director of bullion dealers Johnson Matthey about a steamship in the Channel Islands carrying £5 million in illicit Mexican government silver. He thinks he can trace how it left Mexico via New York Harbor and what happened to it in Europe, reconstructing what he calls the first great decade of money laundering.
