# Continental vs. Maritime Powers

Prof. Sarah Paine's foundational framework — the first lecture in her 2025 series, the one all her other lectures build on. The argument: two thousand years of grand strategy decompose along a single geographic axis. Continental empires (Russia, China) pursue **security through territorial conquest**; maritime powers (Britain, the US) pursue **prosperity through trade**. The two security paradigms generate mutually exclusive world orders, and the current global system — rules-based, freedom-of-navigation, alliance-backstopped — is the maritime order. Putin and Xi want to break it because their geography won't let them win inside it.

Paine works at the U.S. Naval War College (24 years as of this lecture) and is steeped in [[why-russia-lost-the-cold-war|the Cold War history]] she has lectured on elsewhere; this lecture supplies the theoretical scaffolding for that history.

## The three discriminating factors

Paine collapses the continental/maritime divide to three factors of geography. Each is binary, and each predisposes a specific posture toward the world.

| Factor | Continental | Maritime |
|---|---|---|
| Can you defend at sea? | No — need a competent army | Yes — need a competent navy |
| Threat geometry | Contiguous, multi-front | Insulated by moat |
| Lines of communication | Interior (rail, roads) | Exterior (sea lanes) |

From these flow opposed *preferences*:

- **Territory:** something to take vs. a market to trade with
- **Self-sufficiency vs. access:** insulate vs. trade for it
- **Spheres of influence vs. commons:** exclusive zones (seas, space, cyber, air) vs. shared rules

## The intellectual lineage

Paine spends the first ~12 minutes giving a whirlwind tour of the canonical geopoliticians whose ideas she's synthesizing:

- **Sun Tzu** — *The Art of War* makes essentially no reference to maritime warfare. Paine reads this as evidence that continental empires came first; the maritime paradigm is the late-arriving exception.
- **Alfred Thayer Mahan** (late 19th c., U.S. Naval War College) — articulated the prerequisites for a maritime security paradigm: (1) a moat, (2) dense internal transport grid, (3) reliable sea egress in wartime, (4) dense coastal population, (5) stable government institutions that fund navies and pass commerce-enabling laws. Paine's diagnostic: **neither China nor Russia has the full list**. Both have surfeit-of-neighbor problems; Russia's coastal egress is north into uninhabited Arctic; neither has transparent regular elections.
- **Halford Mackinder** (1904) — counter-argued that Eurasia, not Britain, holds the best geopolitical position: the "pivot area" / "great heartland" is insulated *from* the sea by mountains, deserts, frozen seas, and rivers that drain into inaccessible places. Sea power cannot reach it. Hence Britain (and later the US) must work the periphery.
- **Nicholas Spykman** (1943, completed the year he died) — refined Mackinder: the *rimland*, not the heartland, is what matters. US security is a function of sea power; sanctuary at home requires a maritime shield; the US needs continental allies to project land power. Spykman's worry: in 1943 the Nazis had nearly dominated Eurasia, and US statesmen's expectations had been "consistently wrong."

The naval-war-college shorthand: continental powers are **elephants**, maritime powers are **whales**.

## The continental world — Russia and China as archetypes

In the continental world, **power is a function of land**, third-party intervention is the norm, and the army's purpose is — in order — (1) protect the ruling regime, (2) garrison the empire and prevent defections, (3) border defense. The PLA's primary mission is keeping the CCP in power, not expeditionary warfare.

### The rules of the continental game

Paine extracts a playbook for surviving as a continental power:

1. **No two-front wars.** With many neighbors, getting ganged-up-on ends you.
2. **No great-power neighbors.** Today's friend is tomorrow's foe.
3. **Take neighbors sequentially.** Destabilize the rising, ingest the failing, set up buffer zones in between, wait for the moment to pounce.
4. **Get the neighbors to do the work for you.** Sow resentments, deluge with disinformation, play the **jackal state** — move in after others have weakened each other and steal the kill.

Paine names this Putin's game explicitly.

### The pathologies of the continental security paradigm

- **Surrounded by failing states.** Russia and China sit ringed by some of the most dysfunctional places on the planet — "unlucky or complicit?"
- **No enduring alliances.** Neighbors figure out that the hegemonic power is a long-term problem.
- **No stopping rule.** No internal counsel on when to quit before choking on territory. Both Chinese and Russian histories show repeated overextension and implosion.
- **Catastrophic civilian cost.** WWII deaths: maritime powers (UK 326k, US 295k) measured in hundreds of thousands; continental powers (USSR 25.5M, China 11M, Germany 7M, Poland 7M) measured in tens of millions. The fighting happens at home because the oceans don't insulate you. **A continental power's "agony" is that when invaded, the timing is the invader's; only the capitulate/fight choice is yours.**

### The Russian self-conception

Paine pulls three quotes to show how *internal* to Russian elite thinking the territorial-expansion paradigm is:

- **Klyuchevsky** (late Tsarist historian): "The history of Russia is a history of a country in process of colonizing itself" — with no mention of the people who already live on the land being colonized.
- **Dostoyevsky** (diary): "In Europe we are hangers-on and slaves; whereas to Asia we shall go as masters."
- **Sergei Witte** (Finance Minister, 1903): Russia "has the undisputed right to the lion's share of the expected prey" from the "outlived Oriental states" — meaning the Ottoman Empire and especially China. Witte's prediction: "The absorption by Russia of a considerable portion of the Chinese empire is only a question of time."

Putin reads the world the same way. Paine's punchline: **if a continental power botches strategy, its known world can vanish forever** — what happened to Imperial China, Imperial Russia, and "many venerable civilizations." This is "a world without insurance policies."

## The maritime world — rules, commerce, and the Grotian tradition

The maritime tradition starts with the Athenian Empire hugging the Aegean/Ionian, runs through Rome (the very word *Mediterranean* — sea-in-the-middle-of-lands — privileges sea over land, unlike China's *Zhongguo*, kingdom-among-kingdoms), then Byzantium, then the Dutch.

**Hugo Grotius** is the load-bearing figure. As a Dutch Republic jurist, he wrote *Mare Liberum* (1609): "every nation is free to travel to every other nation and trade with it." His *De Jure Belli ac Pacis* cites Roman and Byzantine law: "To all men belong the use of the sea ... by natural law the following are common to everyone: the air, flowing water, the sea." Paine flags that this is a **product of Western civilization, not universally shared**. UNCLOS (which China ratified in 1996) sets freedom of navigation at 12 nautical miles from coast; China asserts 200. In the East and South China Seas the difference matters.

### Britain's grand strategy — "elephant hunting"

Britain coined the term *grand strategy* because they needed it: how do you, a maritime power, deal with a Napoleon? Paine's six-rule playbook:

1. **Keep the home economy growing.** This funds everything else.
2. **Don't let the elephant forage.** Blockade and commerce raiding throw the enemy back on its own dwindling resources.
3. **Rent an elephant.** Find the continental power most directly threatened and arm/fund/sustain it. That's the main front; you don't fight there yourself.
4. **Fight on a peripheral theater** where sea access beats land access. Attrition adds up, the enemy must fight with divided attention, and ideally pressure on the main front is relieved.
5. **Do not take on the enemy main force directly.** Britain broke this rule at vast cost in WWI. The enemy's strength is its army; yours is navy + wealth-generation + capacity for protracted war. Don't play the continental game.
6. **If you must fight on the main front, only after you've bled the elephant, and only with lots of friends.**

This is a strategy of protracted war won by **opposing trend lines**: you compound wealth while the elephant destroys wealth, until the enemy can no longer sustain the fight. The military instrument is secondary; the focus is economics, coalitions, institutions. Prerequisites: sanctuary at home, access to peripheral theaters, access to allies and overseas markets, institutional continuity to sustain a coherent strategy across decades.

A continental power **cannot** play this game: insufficient maritime access (their fleets get bottled up in narrow seas), no usable merchant marine in wartime, and the ever-present possibility that one of the many neighbors will invade them while they're trying.

## The industrial revolution as regime change

Paine's pivot: the industrial revolution **upends both paradigms** and inaugurates the possibility of a maritime *global* order.

The mechanism: **the currency of power shifts from land to capital/commerce/industry.** Land used to be both the source of saleable commodities and the recruiting pool for peasant-conscript armies. Once wealth comes from commerce, industry, and trade, land is no longer the metric of power. Who's rich and powerful today maps to who's industrialized.

The Suez Canal kills the camel trade and the strategic value of the Silk Road's western terminus. The 1967 Arab-Israeli war's Suez closure (1967–1972) drove a **permanent shift to ultra-large ships** — too big for the canal — and a route around Africa that ended up about *one-third* the per-ton cost of going through Suez in small ships. The strategic lesson Paine drives: **overhead cost dominates distance.**

Then **Malcolm McLean** containerizes shipping: loading costs collapse from ~$6/ton to <$0.20/ton; port turnaround plummets. The International Organization for Standardization standardizes container dimensions for trucks, rail, ships. Paine's aside to international-institutions skeptics: this is an "international organization you've never heard of" that has remade your material life.

Tanker sizes have grown more than 10× since 1960; the largest container ships carry 21,000+ containers worth >$1B per voyage. Land transport cannot compete.

### Why the Belt and Road can't work

Paine treats Xi's Belt and Road as a category error:

- **Continuity and gauge.** The land legs are not continuous and not the same gauge. Endless load/unload.
- **End-to-end control of unstable territory.** You'd have to police the whole length, through some of the most unstable real estate on earth.
- **Sea routes route around problems.** If Suez closes, you go around Africa. Land routes have no equivalent.
- **China's maritime egress is open only in peacetime.** The first island chain becomes a kill zone in wartime; merchant traffic cannot exit unless China is friendly with everyone — currently true, not structurally guaranteed.

## Three more conceptual moves

### Positive vs. negative objectives — the invisibility of the maritime world

This is Paine's "took me 15 years at the War College to notice the obvious" point. The continental world is **visible** because it pursues **positive objectives**: take this territory, build this wall. Either you did or you didn't. The maritime world is **invisible** because it pursues **negative objectives**: prevent the trading system from being destroyed, prevent limits on freedom of navigation, deter expansion. You can never prove you prevented anything — the bad thing didn't happen.

Navy missions are *mostly* peacetime and mostly negative-objective: deterrence, blockade, commerce protection. Antidotes to rogue behavior are alliances, diplomacy, sanctions, embargos, containment.

### Sanctions as economic chemotherapy

"You're preventing one or two percent growth per annum, even with leaky sanctions." Sounds trivial. Compounded over generations, this is **the difference between North and South Korea**. The maritime-world theory of victory is not the operational win of eliminating the rogue state — that's nuclear war if the rogue has nukes. It's **containment at acceptable cost**. The North Koreans get to live miserable lives at home; the rest of the world doesn't.

### Negative-sum vs. positive-sum world orders

The continental MO of beating up neighbors is **negative-sum** — you're destroying wealth at the operational level (smashed buildings, dead workers) even when you "win." The maritime order is engineered for **positive-sum compounded growth**: freedom of navigation, free trade, international laws and institutions that minimize transaction costs. The alliance system is the **insurance policy** that protects the rules.

Paine's clinching observation: most countries are not strictly maritime or continental by geography. But **alliances confer a collective maritime position**. Non-maritime states, together, become functionally maritime — and that's the prescription for everyone who wants the rules to hold.

## The greatest-generation insurance policy

Paine frames the postwar international order — UN, IMF, NATO, GATT/WTO, EU predecessors — as designed by survivors of *both* world wars and the Great Depression. Their conclusion: **the alternative to soldiers is diplomats and lawyers**, and whatever gains soldiers buy are inferior to what diplomats would have produced. Even the Cold War's hot wars happened where they did — on Mackinder's "intermarginal crescent" between the maritime and continental worlds — because both sides were trying to put a finger on the scale of continental-rimland civil wars. Wealth-destroying, even at a distance from either core.

## Implications for today

Paine's closing diagnosis:

- **Putin and Kim** want to hollow out international institutions, kill off the alliance system, and return to warring spheres of influence — because their countries can't win inside a maritime rules-based order.
- **China benefited more than any country** from Deng Xiaoping's reintegration into the family of nations. But the **burden of history** — the continentalist outlook, the "we need Taiwan" reflex — weighs heavily.
- **The only win-win solution is diplomats and lawyers in international forums.** The alternative is a third world war with nuclear follow-on effects.

The framework explains why Paine — in [[why-russia-lost-the-cold-war|her Cold War lecture]] — repeatedly returns to the point that Putin "doesn't understand economics": the continentalist instinct measures power in land, but since the industrial revolution, power has measured in compounded wealth. The Soviet collapse and Russia's current stagnation are the same phenomenon viewed through a 50-year telescope.
