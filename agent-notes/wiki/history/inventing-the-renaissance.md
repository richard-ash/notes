# Inventing the Renaissance

Renaissance historian Ada Palmer (University of Chicago), in conversation with Dwarkesh Patel about her book *Inventing the Renaissance* (2025), argues that the period we retroactively canonized as a golden age was experienced by many of its inhabitants as a continuation of the dark ages — European life expectancy actually fell from ~35 in the medieval period to ~18 during the Renaissance because of bigger wars, urbanization, and endemic plague. The durable through-lines of her account: **the Renaissance was a 200-year project to resuscitate Roman virtue that failed on its own terms and succeeded at something its architects never intended**; technology revolutions (print especially) move in decades-long cascades of successive applications, not single events; and censors, including our own, are always wrong about what to worry about.

## Petrarch's project and its unintended payoff

Palmer's central narrative device is a multi-generation chain of cause and effect where each generation misunderstands the one before it:

1. **Petrarch (1340s)** survives the Black Death in a bandit-ridden Italy. His scholar friends are literally killed on the roads. He reads about the Roman Brutus — who executed his own sons for treason against the state — and says: "Our leaders are selfish Lord Montagues; they care about family honor and not the city. Can we raise them on what young Cicero read?" The theory of education is **osmosis**: exposure to Plato and Cicero will produce Plato-like and Cicero-like rulers.
2. **His successors** pour money into manuscript hunts across the Alps and to Constantinople, raise tutors like Marsilio Ficino around young princes, and build libraries. The project is enthusiastically adopted by upstart rulers with no legitimacy — they can cosplay Roman emperors and make themselves look like the Caesars. Florence's Medici, "extra scummy merchant scum," use Latin, Greek, and ancient statuary as a propaganda weapon that inverts the power dynamic with noble French ambassadors.
3. **The first princes raised this way** are Cesare and Lucrezia Borgia. Cesare, educated on Cicero and Plato, sets fire to half the world with bigger cannons and nastier wars than Petrarch's generation ever saw.
4. **Machiavelli** watches the Petrarchan project fail around him. But he still feels the power of the classics, so he proposes they be used **differently**: not as an osmotic moral vitamin but as a casebook of historical examples — "here are five battles fought near rivers, compare the commanders' decisions." This is political science.
5. **Two centuries after Petrarch**, Francis Bacon generalizes Machiavelli's method to nature itself: nature-as-casebook. The scientific method emerges not as a break from the classical revival but as its fifth iteration.
6. **Lucretius's *De rerum natura***, recovered by Poggio Bracciolini with only ~24 readers in Europe, has 30,000 readers in 30 print editions by 1600. Med students read it, take its atomism seriously, and the 1560s–1580s produce the discovery that the heart is a pump and the first germ theories of disease.

The through-line from Petrarch to germ theory to penicillin is ~600 years. Palmer's wise observation: **Petrarch wanted a world that would share his values; he got a world that does not share them at all but can cure the disease that killed his friends.** He'd be horrified by democracy, but would weep seeing that the Black Death can be cured. "Did it go my way? No. Did it go well? Yes." (She draws the same distinction for the inventor of the train, who did not intend to enable women's suffrage.)

The lesson she and Dwarkesh draw for **AI governance**: nobody in history has a good track record of saying "I will do this specific thing and the huge unanticipated change will go my way." Aim at "go well," not "go my way."

## Florence's weird republic

Every other Italian republic (Venice, Genoa, Bologna, Siena, the Swiss) follows the Roman patrician model: hereditary senatorial families rule commoners. Florence was this way until a near-miss takeover prompted them to **massacre their nobility**, salt the earth, and rebuild as a commoner republic governed by merchant-guild members — specifically the owners of warehouses and workshops, not the workers at the looms.

Consequences that don't occur to modern readers:

- **From every other polity's perspective, Florence's rulers are the rank of their own valets.** Florence can't run its own army or police, because no soldier will follow a commander without noble blood. They import noble *podestà* one at a time to be chief of police, pay them handsomely after a year's service, then banish them for life on pain of death so they can never use the power they accumulated.
- **Government by sortition from a tower.** Fit merchant names go in a bag; nine drawn at random are locked into a palace-tower for 2–3 months (locked literally, so they can't be bribed or kidnapped) and must reach **consensus** to act. Tyrant-proof by design.
- **Employers as the safety net.** The merchant-guild oligarchy makes political sense because in the patronage system, your employer — not the state — funds your orphans, your disability, your defense attorney. Representing workers via a council of CEOs is legible.
- **When Machiavelli talks about the *popolo*** being armed and respected, and 19th-century translators read it as quasi-democratic, *popolo* means the top ~4% economically: the merchant guildsmen. It's an inch of liberty, not democracy, but the inch mattered enough that people waved banners shouting "libertas" for it.

## "Even when resistance loses, resistance wins"

Palmer's most politically applicable claim: the Medici take over Florence through regulatory capture of the sortition system — first by employing so much of the city that the plurality of random picks statistically work for them, then by bribing the people who pull the names. Cosimo once gets unlucky in the 1430 lottery, is declared a traitor and locked in a tower, bribes his way out for about $1M (calling his guards "the two most foolish men I've ever met — I'd happily have paid tens of millions"), returns in triumph, and tightens his grip: **"It is dangerous to be rich and not powerful."**

But — and this is the load-bearing observation — because the Florentines loved their republic and fought it through, the **tyranny that eventually replaced it was dramatically weaker** than contemporary tyrannies elsewhere. The Medici dukes built the Vasari Corridor (an overhead walkway designed so they could cross the city without being assassinated by their own people). When construction hit the 500-year-old Mannelli family tower, the family said "no, this is our tower" and the duke **detoured** around it, knowing that violating ancient property rights would spark rebellion. Cosimo I wore the republican red robe (*lucco fiorentino*) to costume balls "as if in his heart he longed to dress like a republican."

Meanwhile across the river in Ferrara, Duke Alfonso I d'Este "wandered around buck naked with a sword in one hand and his dick in the other." The d'Este siblings kidnapped each other's musicians and recreationally murdered each other's servants. That is what tyranny looks like when the people never fought it. **Partial victory is not failure.** Palmer applies this directly to anxiety about American democratic backsliding: even if tyranny came, the traditions and structures of resistance would survive and make it weaker.

## The printing-press revolution as a 150-year cascade

The popular framing — Gutenberg invents in 1450, the world transforms — badly misses the mechanism. Palmer's reframe:

- **Gutenberg goes bankrupt in the 1450s.** So does the bank that forecloses on him. So do his apprentices, who flee their debts and leave Germany. The issue isn't the technology — it's that mass-produced books are a commodity in a world with **no distribution network for commodities**. In a small landlocked German town where seven priests are legally allowed to read the Bible, printing 300 Bibles just leaves you with 293 unsellable ones. (Palmer's analogy: e-books technically existed in the 1970s but there was no market until the Kindle.)
- **Venice is the airport hub of the Mediterranean.** If you're sailing A to B you change boats there. Gutenberg's bankrupt apprentices flee to Venice, where you can hand 10 Bibles each to 30 ship captains going to 30 cities. First economically sustainable print. Then book fairs (Frankfurt still exists today) emerge as a trading mechanism: bring a thousand copies of your book, come home with five copies of two hundred books.
- **Printing isn't profitable until the 1490s** — forty years after Gutenberg — and even then it's driven by **upfront paper CAPEX**. Gutenberg borrowed ~$1.5M equivalent just for matched-lot paper. That's why printers invent pamphlets: a second press next to the slow valuable book, cranking out fashion reports from the royal wedding, scandal rags, siege news, sold to intercity news runners.
- **The 1510s are the pamphlet revolution**, and that's the one that reshapes politics. Savonarola's pamphlets in the 1490s only circulated around Florence, Siena, Pisa, took months to move, and his movement was crushed. When Luther publishes the Ninety-five Theses in 1517, they're in print in London **17 days later**. The Reformation is enabled by pamphlets in exactly the same way the Arab Spring is enabled by cell phones.
- **Newspapers then emerge as a response to the pamphlet's unreliability** — serial publication = reputation = accountability. Then *The Gentleman's Magazine* (the first magazine) emerges as a response to newspapers contradicting each other: weekly roundups of what each paper said and where they disagreed. **Each medium is invented to fact-check the last one.** Modern parallel: social media vs. legacy media vs. emerging fact-checking layers.

Palmer's meta-point: the print revolution and the digital revolution are structurally the same thing. "We've had ten tech revolutions in a row" is the wrong framing; it's **one information revolution with successive applications** playing out over ~150 years each. A new "bang" every decade, each reshaping what was possible in communications. ## Why the Industrial Revolution didn't happen in Italy

Italy in 1500 has the fine metalworking for movable type, the milling technology for water and wind power, and the gear technology for watches — all the raw ingredients. But it's also the center of Big Finance, Big Wool, and Big Olive Oil, with olive oil serving triple duty as lamp fuel, cooking oil, *and* the industrial fluid used to process fine wool (which is why a wool suit isn't itchy but an English wool blanket is — England shipped its crude wool to Florence because it couldn't produce enough olive oil to finish it).

Palmer's case:

- **Industrialization happens when you feel you need it.** Italy was already on top economically through agriculture and finance; no felt need. The Industrial Revolution mostly happened in second-tier English towns like Lancaster, not in the already-wealthy centers.
- **Land was more valuable as farms than as mines.** You don't want to rip it up.
- **Political subdivision** — dozens of independent rich cities — meant no central authority could pass the legislation to force transformation. England's centralized crown could.
- **No city wanted to host the industrialization** because it's awful for the host city.
- Recent documents confirm Italians figured out industrial looms **in the 1400s and chose not to build them** — they wanted luxuriant artisanal fabrics, not cheap commodities. The first printed fonts mimic handwritten scripts for the same reason: the market wanted things to look artisanal.

Compare to [[israel-water-miracle]]-style contingency arguments — no such article here yet, but the pattern (technology gets adopted only when there's a local economic pain point) recurs.

## The writing-surface constraint and the real lost library

Palmer argues that **most of what we lost from classical antiquity was lost between 400–600 AD**, not at the Library of Alexandria. The mechanism is boring and material: papyrus (Rome's cheap writing surface, made by slicing reeds into ribbons and sun-drying them) is a warm-weather plant killed by frost. When the Western Empire dissolves, Northern and Western Europe lose access. The papyri in existing libraries start to crumble. Europe's only alternative is parchment — writing on a dead sheep.

Palmer's price analogy is vivid: parchment is to papyrus what a leather jacket is to a head of lettuce. A medieval book costs as much as a house; a pocket book as much as a studio condo; an illuminated Bible as much as a country villa. The University of Paris — *the* library of medieval Europe — has **600 books**. Contemporary sultans in the Middle East have thousands (papyrus). China and Sub-Saharan Africa have thousands (paper).

So monks in 400–600 AD face a choice: a library of 1,000 papyri, only enough leather for 100 copies. Which do you save? They saved what they loved — mostly Saint Augustine. **This is why we have more surviving Augustine than the entirety of pagan classical Latin combined.** The subjective tastes of 6th-century monks functioned as an unintentional, multi-century act of censorship that shaped everything downstream.

Paper reaches Europe in 800 AD — a 400-year famine of cheap writing surfaces. Even then, paper is ~10x cheaper than parchment but distrusted. Europe uses paper for ~400 years before any state document is trusted to it. The Vatican still uses parchment for official records; British Parliament did until ~2015. **Gutenberg is possible in 1450 only because paper had spent 650 years becoming trusted, and because a library-building boom in the 1410s created demand.** Gutenberg was not a random genius: paper surges *before* movable type, and inventors were already trying to make books cheaper. The invention meets the demand.

## Leonardo as saboteur of progress

Palmer's deliberate provocation to her students: **Leonardo da Vinci was not a scientist.** He wrote everything in coded mirror writing so nobody could use his discoveries, including his own students. He wanted unique masterpieces that future generations would marvel at the way he marveled at the Colosseum — "how did he do it? No one can replicate it." Brunelleschi, who built Florence's dome, deliberately burned his notes and schematics. In the community-of-scientists sense, both men were **saboteurs of human progress** — inventors who deliberately cut off the tail of their invention so no one could be their peer.

The switch comes around 1600 with **Francis Bacon's simile of the three insects**:

- **The ant** — the encyclopedist who gathers and piles up knowledge, proud of sitting on the biggest anthill, but from which nothing comes.
- **The spider** — the system-weaver spinning elaborate logical theories out of his own body, hypnotic but spun from nothing real.
- **The honeybee** — gathering from nature, processing through the organ of its own being, producing something sweet and useful for humankind.

The Royal Society founds with Bacon's portrait on the title page, and the standard of achievement flips: "you are not great because you built the dome; you are great because you worked out **how** it can be done and shared that sweet and useful thing with all of humankind." Bacon calls being a scientist **the ultimate act of charity** — giving a gift to every human who will ever live after you. ## The Inquisition accidentally invented peer review

The focus of the Inquisition is badly misunderstood. Of the hundreds of thousands executed, **only 12 trials targeted scientists about science**, only 3 convictions, and **only Giordano Bruno was executed** — and Bruno's condemnation was as much for reincarnation and theological heresies as for his cosmology. About 100 were executed for atheism. Kepler was excommunicated not for planetary motion but for being a Lutheran; his mother was tried for witchcraft for the same reason. The Inquisition's real focus was hundreds of thousands of executions for "Judaizing," paganism, and fine-grained Protestant heresies.

Palmer's favorite claim, from **Daniele Macuglia's dissertation**: the largest and best-funded experimental laboratory in late-17th-century Europe was probably **the one run by Vatican inquisitors**. After the Galileo affair, they decided they had a duty to verify the truth of books sent for censoring. If somebody claimed a mechanical experiment, they had to **repeat the experiment**. So they built a second laboratory, attempting to recreate the results of the first — **which is, structurally, peer review**. Some of them wrote their own scientific treatises at night. "History is never what we expect."

## Censors are always wrong about what to censor

Palmer's history-of-censorship observation (which will be her next non-fiction book): **every generation of censors is wrong, in 20/20 hindsight, about which ideas are actually dangerous.**

- **1540s Index of Banned Books** puts arch-heretic names in ALL CAPS. Machiavelli isn't in all caps. The all-caps authors are minor Protestant theologians — Calvin, Zwingli, Luther, Melanchthon. Inquisitors write to each other about Lucretius: "Only learned people can read it, and they know the false stuff is false — don't bother censoring it. Worry about Protestantism." That "false stuff" is atomism.
- **French Enlightenment raids on clandestine bookshops** find Voltaire, Rousseau, Sade, La Mettrie's materialist atheism, the banned *Encyclopédie* — and they don't care. What drives them to fury is **Jansenist treatises on the nature of the Trinity** (Jansenism: Calvinist-flavored Catholicism with a terrifying authoritarian God). When Rome orders France to burn the *Encyclopédie*, the French love the book (Marie Antoinette is on record enjoying the silk-pantyhose entry); so they ceremonially march the *Encyclopédie* to the pyre and burn Jansenist treatises instead.
- **Milton's *Paradise Lost*** goes through the English censor, who is fine with Satan being a charismatic protagonist parroting anti-monarchical revolutionary pamphlets from the British colonies. The one line they make him change is about astrology.

Palmer's structural reason for this failure: **you cannot censor whatever moves the information fastest, because it will move faster than you can move.** Printers employ the news writers whose job is to move fastest between cities — which means convicted printers always find out about it before the Inquisition can arrive to arrest them. The Inquisition never once successfully arrested a printer. Pamphlets are anonymous, quick, produced overnight. Modern parallel: social media moderation is the exact same problem, and "we just can't keep up" is not a failure of will but a structural property of the fastest medium.

Four-factor model of whether censorship is possible: **law, technology (speed), social will, enforcement cost.** Fail any one and censorship fragments into performance.

## Secondary observations worth keeping

- **History always moves fast**; the 19th-century textbook convention of "long stagnation then modernity" is a lie. "Any time I go to a talk where any historian zooms in on any decade, it feels like it's moving as fast as our present is moving." The *Civilization* video game (the number-one teacher of history since 1991, shipped 70M copies) encodes the lie at its foundation — 50 years per turn in antiquity, 1 year per turn in modernity.
- **Raphael's glowing colors** come from a chain of **eleven major technical revolutions over 120 years** — oil processing → fake gemstones → fake gold painted on tinfoil for picture frames → artists adopting the pigments for glowing effect. Artisanal progress was constant; we just don't notice because we take it for granted.
- **Florence used exile as a diplomatic corps.** Without nobility, it had no legitimate ambassadors. So it exiled people to friendly cities and put them on probation as the city's contact in Bruges, London, or Naples. Machiavelli's exile was *not* this kind — he was sent to a hamlet to rot — and his refusal to break it to take obvious jobs in Rome, Milan, or England is strong evidence of his patriotism, as are his private letters. *The Prince* was circulated in tiny intimate circles, unlike his comic plays and history, which were public. It was a job application: "Bring me home to serve my country. Whatever regime is in power, I will be loyal to *it*."
- **The end justifies the means — only when the end is the survival of your country.** Machiavelli is not a general amoralist. He has seen his city's streets run with blood and believes regime change is almost always worse than enduring tyranny, because civil war is usually not life at all.
- **Cheap writing surfaces enable bureaucracy.** When Egypt and Rome go to war, Egypt periodically stops exporting papyrus; Roman infrastructure falls apart overnight because taxation, record-keeping, and letters stop working.
- **The reception of the New World was muted** because: (a) Europeans initially thought they'd found something "maybe as big as the Canary Islands," (b) 1492 was already dominated by three simultaneous wars, a Spanish takeover of the papacy, and Ottoman invasions of Italy and Hungary, and (c) Luther hits three decades later and swamps the news cycle before Europeans have finished figuring out the Americas are continental-scale. Vatican documents from ~1100–1200 already recognized Vinland via Viking accounts. "Any decade is concerned with its tumults and fails to recognize the importance of what's around it. That's true of every decade."
