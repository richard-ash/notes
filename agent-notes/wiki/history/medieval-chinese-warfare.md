# Medieval Chinese Warfare, 300-900

David A. Graff's *Medieval Chinese Warfare, 300-900* (Routledge, 2002) covers one and a half full cycles of Chinese state formation and dissolution: the final years of the Jin dynasty, the centuries of fragmentation and barbarian rule that followed, the reunification under the Sui, the apogee of the Tang, and the catastrophic An Lushan Rebellion that began the Tang's decline. The period is often called the "Chinese Middle Ages," though it is not contemporaneous with the European Middle Ages — it roughly brackets the fall of the Western Roman Empire.

## The dynastic cycle as recurring collapse

Psmith frames the book through the lens of civilizational collapse. Where Western Europe experienced one or two total breakdowns of order (the Bronze Age collapse, the fall of Rome), China's history is characterized by a recurring cycle: a dynasty consolidates power, governs for centuries, loses the Mandate of Heaven, and the political-economic-moral order disintegrates into decades or centuries of fragmentation. Eventually a new strongman reunifies the empire, and the cycle restarts.

The An Lushan Rebellion (755-763 AD) illustrates the scale: upper-end estimates put the death toll at 36 million people — roughly one-sixth of the world's population at the time. The "Chinese history be like" meme about staggering casualty numbers is not entirely reliable, however. Psmith notes that dramatic census swings may partly reflect the state's inability to count people during periods of disorder, not literal mass death.

## Yellow China vs. Blue China

Graff's period is when the foundational North-South divide in Chinese civilization crystallized. Psmith develops a vivid color-coded framework:

**"Yellow China"** (the North): The Yellow River valley and the North China Plain — wheat agriculture, mass irrigation, hydraulic-empire bureaucracy, centralization, militarism. The stereotypical Northern virtues: plain-spokenness, physical courage, stubbornness. The North is where traditional Chinese civilization began.

**"Blue China"** (the South): The Yangtze and beyond — rugged mountains, dense forests, and above all a fractally crinkled coastline that fostered maritime trade, entrepreneurship, piracy, and cultural exchange. The Chinese diaspora (Cantonese, Hakka, Teochew, Hokkien) is predominantly "blue." Blue China represents independent initiative and innovation, but also corruption, smuggling, and cultural influences from non-Chinese peoples. The concept resonates with Karl Wittfogel's [[liberal-international-order|hydraulic empire]] thesis, linking irrigation-dependent agriculture to centralized despotism.

The divide became structurally important during the post-Jin collapse, when millions of Northern Chinese — aristocrats, literati, and ordinary peasants — fled south across the Yangtze, creating a capital-in-exile. This mass migration made the South genuinely "Chinese" for the first time, but the resulting culture retained a Southern inflection. For two centuries, the Southern dynasties (especially the Chen) maintained military parity with the North, relying on the Yangtze as a natural barrier and superior naval capability.

The stalemate broke when the Sui dynasty founder built a massive fleet in the Sichuan basin and floated it down the Yangtze gorges — five-decked warships carrying 800 men each, equipped with 50-foot spike-bearing booms. He then crushed the South with half a million troops and forcibly relocated the Southern aristocracy northward. But the South was permanently changed.

## The nomad-settler synthesis and the Tang

The second major theme is the blurring of the line between "civilized" Chinese farmer and "barbarian" steppe nomad. Graff argues this was never really a sharp dichotomy — when state capacity weakened, peasants routinely drifted to the periphery and adopted non-sedentary lifestyles, which may account for many of the dramatic population swings in Chinese records.

But the post-Jin period was different in kind: for the first time, the Yellow River heartland was occupied by large numbers of non-Chinese peoples. Genetic evidence suggests substantial Turkic and Mongolic admixture in modern Northern Chinese lineages dating to this period, while modern Southern Chinese are closer to what Northern Chinese were two millennia ago. The literary and material record confirms the hybrid: Chinese armies adopted cavalry, the aristocracy took up archery and falconry, and pants became fashionable.

This hybrid Sino-steppe aristocracy eventually reunified China and produced the Tang dynasty — a fact Psmith finds shocking. The Tang are popularly considered the most quintessentially "Chinese" dynasty ("Tang" remains an archaic ethnonym for Chinese people; overseas Chinatowns are literally "Tang people streets"). But the Western scholarly consensus, which Graff lays out convincingly, is that the Tang ruling elite represented a fusion of Chinese and Turkic traditions.

## Order as the antidote to collapse

The review's most distinctive contribution is its meditation on what actually works when civilization collapses. Psmith highlights the story of Yu Gun, a minor Jin-era official who, when threatened by princely armies in 301 AD, led his community into the highlands and built a self-governing stronghold:

> "In this high and dangerous defile, he blocked the footpaths, erected fortifications, planted defensive hedges, examined merit, made measurements, equalized labor and rest, shared possessions, repaired implements, measured strength and employed the able, making all things correspond to what they should."

Bandits who approached were deterred simply by the sight of Yu's followers deployed in orderly ranks. Psmith reads this through Joseph de Maistre's aphorism that the counter-revolution is not an opposing revolution but the *opposite* of revolution — Yu combats external chaos by creating internal hierarchy and harmony.

This leads Psmith to a critique of contemporary "prepper" culture in three waves. First-wave prepperism was radical individualism — the lone survivor fantasy. Second-wave prepperism absorbed the critique and emphasized community, mutual aid, and social bonds. But Psmith argues this is *still* ahistorical: a community of freely contracting autonomous individuals is just individualism wearing a neighborly mask. What Yu Gun actually demonstrates — and what Tanner Greer argues the American pioneers actually practiced — is that surviving collapse requires genuine hierarchy, leadership, and [[solitude-and-leadership|the willingness to serve in subordinate roles]]. As Psmith puts it:

> One of the great secrets of leadership is that following and leading are actually closely related skills, and that practice at one of them transfers well to the other. This is difficult for we Americans to see, because an aversion to hierarchy is built into our national character, and consequently we operate with impoverished models of what it means to be in a position of authority or of subordination.

The "third-wave prepper," in Psmith's framing, should be practicing not just survival skills or community barbecues, but actual leadership *and* followership within ordered institutions — what Greer calls training for high-stakes service through low-stakes local leadership.

The closing image: in Korean MMORPGs, unlike Western ones, most players are assigned mundane roles — soldiers, porters, blacksmiths — while only a few become heroes. When the stage of history is set, we are far more likely to be cast as Yu Gun's followers standing in orderly ranks than as Yu Gun himself. The poorly-behaved seldom make history.
