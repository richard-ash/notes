# The Liberal International Order and the Predatory Hegemon

The "liberal international order" (sometimes "rules-based international order") is the post-1945 system the United States constructed as global hegemon: free trade, freedom of navigation, foreign aid, reconstruction of defeated enemies, treaty alliances, multilateral institutions, and — at least nominally — a commitment to individual dignity and the dignity of nations as something more than pawns in a great-power game. This article compiles Fareed Zakaria's argument (in an April 2026 Ezra Klein interview pegged to Trump's Iran war) that the order is being dismantled not just procedurally but morally, and that the U.S. is transitioning from *benign hegemon* to *predatory hegemon* — a phrase Zakaria credits to Stephen Walt.

## The benign-hegemon thesis

Zakaria's baseline claim: in the broad continuity of 300–400 years of world history, the U.S. as hegemon has been *qualitatively* different from the Soviet Union, Nazi and Kaiser-era Germany, Imperial France, Britain, and Holland. Differences Zakaria cites:

- After 1945 the U.S. refused reparations from defeated Axis powers and rebuilt them (Marshall Plan, Japan).
- It invented modern foreign aid and provided "public goods" — freedom of the seas, open trade, reserve currency, alliance security — for the whole system rather than extracting tribute.
- Until World War II, *every* victorious power extracted tribute from losers, including Britain and France after WWI. The postwar U.S. broke that pattern.
- Its first military action (1798 quasi-war with France; later the Barbary Wars) was over *freedom of navigation* — a principle it has defended continuously, including warning China that the Strait of Malacca must remain open.

Zakaria is explicit that this is a comparison among hegemons, not a claim of American purity: "You can only compare a hegemon with other hegemons." He concedes extensive mistakes and hypocrisy, including Vietnam ("when you have an ideological conception of your foreign policy and think you have to save Vietnam from these evil Communists, you end up destroying villages to save them"). He rejects the left critique that Trump is merely "America with the mask off"; his argument is structural and comparative.

Franklin Roosevelt is, in Zakaria's telling, the architect. At Casablanca, Roosevelt realized how "savagely the French had ruled" Morocco and resolved that the war would not be fought to restore European colonial rule. He wanted free trade not for narrow efficiency but because "there had to be a way for countries to grow in wealth and grow to feel their power without conquering other countries."

## The predatory turn

Zakaria argues Trump represents a distinct break — not continuity with McKinley-era imperialism (which at least claimed uplift), but a reversion to **18th-century rapacious imperialism** that treats every interaction as a chance to extract short-term rents. The tell: Trump musing about jointly controlling the Strait of Hormuz with Iran and taking a cut of the tolls. This would invert America's founding commitment to freedom of navigation and align the U.S. with the position it has spent 80 years opposing.

Zakaria's ledger on the Trump-initiated Iran war (c. early 2026):

| Before | After |
|---|---|
| Iran's nuclear program "completely obliterated" per Israeli + U.S. intelligence | Iran militarily degraded but its navy/missiles never were the main threat |
| Iran's Strait-of-Hormuz leverage latent | Iran has *demonstrated* the strait as a weapon, may monetize it at $70–90B/year (≈2× its oil revenue) |
| Gulf states in U.S. orbit | Gulf states hedging; China brought into Gulf diplomacy |
| Dollar dominant in energy trade | Payments through Hormuz now settling in yuan and crypto |
| Russia economically constrained | Russia earning $4–5B/month extra from elevated oil prices |
| Western alliance intact | Trump publicly blaming allies; alliance fraying |

The Iranian "10-point plan" Trump described as a workable basis for negotiation includes Iranian control of the strait, accepted enrichment rights, full sanctions relief, and reparations. Zakaria: "If this is a workable basis for negotiation, why the hell didn't we negotiate on this basis two months ago? … Iran would have been comfortable with seven of those demands. There are three that are more demanding than they would have ever been three months ago." The war added demands rather than removing them.

### The moral register

Zakaria's strongest claim is not strategic but moral. Trump's Truth Social posts threatening to destroy Iran's power plants and bridges — "A whole civilization will die tonight, never to be brought back again" — were, Zakaria and Klein both argue, the public threat of a war crime against 93 million civilians. Klein notes the denunciations came not only from the left but from Tucker Carlson ("vile on every level… a promise to commit a war crime"), Megyn Kelly, Marjorie Taylor Greene and Alex Jones (both invoking the 25th Amendment), and Candace Owens ("genocidal lunatic"). Klein's inversion: Trump may have been right that a civilization would die that night — but it was America's, in the sense of America as a *civilized nation* bound by rules of law and basic morality.

Zakaria ties this to the enlightenment project: "At the core of any value system had to be the dignity and life of an individual human being. Those were not pawns in some larger struggle." Trump's innovation, he argues, is not conservative religiosity (Vance's "God of our fathers") nor strategic realism but an **appeal to naked selfishness** — "What's in it for you? Why aren't we getting more out of this?" — which is also why Trump is comfortable with open corruption.

## The post-American world

Zakaria's term (also the title of an earlier book and a recent Zakaria piece) describes a world that routes *around* America rather than through it:

- Other countries treat the U.S. as a problem to cut bilateral deals with while making *their own* free-trade agreements (India–EU, EU–Mercosur, EU–Canada).
- Canada, after decades of betting on tight U.S. integration, is "buying insurance" by deepening ties with China and India.
- India is course-correcting toward strategic autonomy.
- The U.S. has lost its **agenda-setting power** — the ability to define what the global system is arguing about.

A Gallup world survey Klein cites shows approval of Chinese leadership (36%) now exceeding approval of U.S. leadership (31%). Zakaria reads this as a vote *against* the U.S. rather than for China — the Chinese don't want the role, don't have the trust (55 U.S. treaty allies vs. China's one, North Korea, or three counting Russia and Iran), and are content to **free-ride** on the benefits of American hegemony while criticizing it.

Zakaria's worry is that the damage may be *irreparable* in a way the post-Bush-43 / post-Trump-1 damage was not. Bush and the first Trump term were followed by Obama and Biden restorations, and the world happily accepted America back. But the world has changed: China is vastly more powerful than it was in 2003, Russia has oil revenues, and — critically — once other countries start "defecting" (buying insurance, hedging, freelancing), game-theoretic dynamics make it difficult to reverse even if a committed internationalist wins in 2028. *If you think the other guy is going to defect, you defect first.*

## The British Empire parallel

Zakaria's most haunting analogy: the British Empire reached its **maximum territorial extent after WWI**, only 20–30 years before its collapse. The mechanism was **strategic overextension driven by chained dependencies** — Robinson and Gallagher's *Africa and the Victorians* describes the annexation of Fashoda in southern Sudan: Britain needed to control the Suez Canal to protect the route to India, which required controlling Egypt, which required upper Sudan, which required lower Sudan, landing British troops in places no one in London could locate. Meanwhile Germany and America were quietly becoming more productive.

Zakaria's reading: "This is the third Middle Eastern war we've fought in 25 years." The U.S. is pouring moral, political, and financial capital into faraway theaters whose strategic value is unclear while the underlying productive base is neglected. "I don't know whether it's exhaustion or imperial arrogance or a combination. It feels hauntingly reminiscent."

There's a suggestive structural echo with [[why-russia-lost-the-cold-war]]: Paine's analysis of the Soviet collapse emphasizes overextension (militarizing the Chinese border, Afghanistan, client states) combined with economic rot and loss of ideological legitimacy. Zakaria's predatory-hegemon America has different mechanics — the economy is strong, the military intact — but similar symptoms: strategic overreach, credibility collapse, and (most importantly in Paine's framework) the *erosion of ideological/moral legitimacy* that keeps allies attached by consent rather than coercion.

## Why liberalism struggles to defend the order

A substantial middle section of the Klein–Zakaria conversation is a diagnosis of why small-L liberalism is *rhetorically exhausted* at precisely the moment its achievements are being dismantled. Summarized:

1. **Liberalism has won too much.** Emancipation, women's equality, racial equality, religious toleration, child labor laws, universal suffrage — all classical-conservative positions against these have collapsed. The winning movement has become the institutional establishment, which is "fat and corrupt and nonresponsive."
2. **Social democracy was the actual victor.** Per Sheri Berman, the 20th-century contest was not really capitalism vs. communism but social democracy (welfare state + capitalism) vs. everything else. Every Western country adopted the SPD's 1905 platform. Once you've built that, *where do you go?* "Nobody writes poems about expanding Obamacare."
3. **Liberalism is agnostic about the meaning of life.** This is by design — it came out of the religious wars — but leaves a vacancy the right fills with God/family/tradition (Vance's "God of our fathers") and Trump fills with *naked selfishness*.
4. **Defending institutions is a losing message.** Klein's point, which Zakaria partly concedes: liberals defending NATO, the U.N., and the rules-based order are defending procedural tactics, not moral missions. When institutions visibly fail large groups of people, "liberalism really has nothing to say to them." Jerusalem Demsas: "sometimes virtue signaling and hypocrisy are a preferable equilibrium" — *hypocrisy is the homage vice pays to virtue.*

Klein argues the answer requires political movements willing to engage passion and inspiration rather than procedural defense. Zakaria counters with the **principlists** of 1979 Iran as the cautionary tale: people who insist on the "original ideals" against the "terrible pragmatists" often end up in ugly places. "Romanticism in politics is something to be viewed with a certain degree of skepticism." This is an unresolved disagreement that Klein flags as "I'll pick up that thread with you another time."

## Further reading (Zakaria's recommendations)

- **G. John Ikenberry, *A World Safe for Democracy*** — the scholarly account of the rules-based international order.
- **Reinhold Niebuhr, *The Irony of American History*** — on the danger, when powerful, of believing you are virtuous. A call for *Christian realism*: humility at the heart of Christianity "which sometimes we forget when listening to Pete Hegseth."
- **Graham Greene, *The Quiet American*** — Vietnam novel told through a sour British journalist meeting an idealistic American who believes America will deliver peace and justice.
