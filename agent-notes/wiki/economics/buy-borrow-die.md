# Buy, Borrow, Die

The phrase "buy, borrow, die" describes the three-step playbook by which America's wealthiest avoid virtually all federal taxation on their wealth. Tax law professor Ray Madoff argues this is not a loophole but the *de facto* design of the U.S. tax system — one that has effectively written the ultra-wealthy out of the tax base entirely.

## The core mechanism

**1. Buy (accumulate appreciated assets, not salary).** Centibillionaires like Jeff Bezos take nominal salaries ($82,000/year in Bezos's case for 20+ years) and accumulate wealth through stock appreciation instead. Wages face up to 37% income tax plus 15.3% payroll tax; unrealized stock gains face 0%. As Madoff puts it: "Salaries are for suckers."

**2. Borrow (fund lifestyle with loans, not sales).** Selling stock triggers ~23.8% capital gains tax. Instead, the ultra-wealthy borrow against their holdings at favorable rates — lenders face near-zero default risk when the collateral is hundreds of billions in stock. The loan proceeds are not a taxable event. Because consumption doesn't scale anywhere near total wealth (even a $100M/year lifestyle is a rounding error on $200B), borrowers never come close to exhausting their collateral. Lenders are happy to roll loans indefinitely — returning principal just means finding a new borrower.

**3. Die (step-up in basis wipes the slate).** At death, the "angel of death loophole" (step-up in basis) resets the cost basis of inherited assets to fair market value. All unrealized gains accumulated over a lifetime are erased. Heirs can immediately sell at the stepped-up price and pay zero capital gains tax.

### The ProPublica "true tax rate" analysis

In 2021, ProPublica published a landmark investigation based on a trove of leaked IRS records covering 15+ years of tax returns for thousands of the wealthiest Americans. The data — later traced to IRS contractor Charles Littlejohn — provided the first individual-level confirmation of what tax scholars had long theorized.

ProPublica introduced a "true tax rate" metric: federal income taxes paid divided by wealth growth (as estimated by Forbes) over the same period. For the top 25 wealthiest Americans, the collective true tax rate from 2014 to 2018 was **3.4%** — $13.6 billion in taxes on $401 billion in wealth growth. Individual rates were far lower:

| Person | Wealth growth (2014-18) | Taxes paid | True tax rate |
|---|---|---|---|
| Warren Buffett | $24.3B | $23.7M | 0.1% |
| Jeff Bezos | $127B (2006-18) | $1.4B | 1.1% |

For contrast, a median American household in the same period saw net worth grow ~$65,000 (mostly home appreciation) while paying ~$62,000 in federal taxes — a true tax rate approaching 100%.

The Bezos case illustrates the mechanics concretely. In 2007, Amazon's stock more than doubled and his fortune grew $3.8B, yet he reported just $46M in income (interest and dividends from outside investments) and offset it entirely with investment losses and deductions — paying zero federal income tax. In 2011, he reported negative income and claimed a $4,000 child tax credit. Bezos, Elon Musk (zero in 2018), Michael Bloomberg, Carl Icahn, and George Soros (zero for three consecutive years) all achieved zero-tax years through the same structural gap: wealth growth through unrealized appreciation is simply not income under U.S. law.

## The estate tax illusion

The estate tax (40% above a $15M exemption, as of 2025) is theoretically the backstop that catches dynastic wealth. In practice, Madoff argues it has become "a tax in name only." Key evidence:

- Estate tax returns filed: 122,000 (2000) → 6,158 (2021), of which only 2,584 were taxable
- The top 1% held ~$50 trillion in wealth in 2024; the estate tax collected just $30 billion
- In 2025, with full Republican control, estate tax repeal was *not* included in Trump's tax bill — Madoff's interpretation is that the wealthy prefer keeping a symbolic estate tax on the books (which creates the illusion they're taxed) over repealing it (which would spotlight how little income tax they actually pay)

### Estate tax avoidance vehicles

- **Minority discounts:** Splitting a $100M business into three minority stakes (35/35/30%) entitles each to a 30-40% valuation discount, shrinking the taxable estate to $50-60M — then reconsolidating on the other side
- **Dynasty trusts:** Perpetual trusts funded through sales to the trust at below-market interest rates; for income tax purposes, the grantor is treated as dealing with themselves (no tax), but for estate tax purposes, the assets are "out" of the estate
- **Rolling GRATs (Grantor Retained Annuity Trusts):** Cyclically siphon appreciation out of the estate tax-free
- **Charitable vehicles / 501(c)(4)s:** Appreciated stock donated to political-activity-eligible organizations avoids both gift tax and capital gains tax while preserving the donor's control and political influence
- **Life insurance:** A favored vehicle for the super-wealthy; converting wealth into life insurance proceeds makes it nontaxable and unreportable

## Why "the top 1% pay 40% of taxes" is misleading

Madoff argues this statistic conflates the top 1% of *income earners* (high-salaried professionals in law, medicine, finance) with the *wealthiest* Americans (founder-class wealth held in appreciated stock). The latter group is just as likely to be among the 40% of Americans who pay *no* federal income tax as among the top 1% of payers — because they have no taxable income.

Similarly, the "40% of Americans pay no federal income tax" line ignores payroll taxes, which start at dollar one. 80% of Americans pay more in payroll tax than income tax.

## The stock buyback shift

Before 1982, companies distributed profits primarily through dividends (>70% of profits), which were taxed at ordinary income rates. After SEC rule changes legalized buybacks, dividends fell below 20% of profits. Stock buybacks boost share prices instead, converting what was once taxable dividend income into untaxed appreciation. The inflation-adjusted Dow sat around 3,000 from the 1920s through 1982; it is now ~45,000. Buybacks are a significant part of that story — and a significant part of why stockholders no longer generate taxable events from corporate profits.

## Historical precedent: the Tax Reform Act of 1986

Madoff points to Reagan's 1986 tax reform as proof that meaningful change is possible. That act equalized capital gains and ordinary income rates and eliminated the tax shelter industry that had allowed high earners to offset all income. Pre-1986, high marginal rates were largely fictional because of shelters; post-1986, high earners actually pay. The 1986 reform broadened the base by closing avoidance vehicles — the same approach Madoff advocates today for wealth-class avoidance.

## The anti-estate-tax campaign

In the 1990s, 18 of America's wealthiest families (Mars, Koch, Walton, and others) funded a campaign to rebrand the estate tax as the "death tax." Their strategy: make it seem like a universal burden that threatened family farms. Congress struggled to find actual examples of farms lost to the estate tax. Their star witness, Chester Thigpen (a tree farmer and grandchild of slaves), turned out not to be subject to the estate tax at all when he later died — his farm fell well within the exemption.

## Reform proposals

Madoff advocates reforms she considers more durable and constitutionally sound than a wealth tax:

1. **Tax gains on transfer** (the Canadian model): Trigger capital gains tax whenever property is transferred — by sale, gift, or death — not just on sale. This closes the borrow-and-die loop.
2. **Eliminate the capital gains preference:** Tax investment income at ordinary income rates, with an inflation adjustment. Reagan did this in 1986; it can be done again.
3. **Replace the estate tax with an inheritance tax:** Tax recipients on what they receive, just as found money is taxable. Currently, receiving $100B by inheritance does not even have a line on the recipient's tax return.
4. **Close estate planning vehicles:** Eliminate or reform dynasty trusts, GRATs, minority discounts, and the charitable/political loopholes.

### Why not a wealth tax?

Madoff is skeptical of wealth taxes for practical reasons:
- **State level:** People can leave (see: Florida residency-day-counting apps). California's 2026 retroactive one-time 5% wealth tax is a patch, not a solution.
- **Federal level:** Constitutional risk — the Direct Tax Clause may doom it under the current Supreme Court. Valuation of non-public assets (art, partnership interests, crypto) creates enormous administrative burden.
- **Political framing:** A special tax on the rich reads as punitive; Madoff argues the better frame is *everyone should be in the tax system* — a principle, not a punishment.

## Implications

The conversation surfaces a tension between two goals: (1) taxing dynastic wealth for revenue and fairness, and (2) reducing the political power that comes with extreme wealth concentration. Madoff argues that the income-tax-reform path can achieve the first goal but concedes it cannot directly address the second. The top 1% held 32% of national wealth as of 2024 — the highest share since the 1920s.

The deeper structural point: the U.S. tax code creates a two-tier system where wage earners (including very high earners like surgeons at $2M/year who pay >50%) subsidize a system that exempts asset holders. Even Andrew Mellon, the original anti-tax crusader, argued in 1924 that wage income should be taxed *more lightly* than investment income — the exact opposite of today's code.

## IRS enforcement asymmetry

The ProPublica investigation also revealed that years of budget cuts had hobbled the IRS's enforcement capacity, with the wealthiest taxpayers and largest corporations benefiting most from the agency's weakness. Perversely, residents of poor regions were *more* likely to be audited than those in affluent areas — largely because auditing low-income EITC claims is procedurally simpler and cheaper than auditing complex wealth structures. The structural tax advantages described above are thus compounded by an enforcement gap: even when avoidance shades into evasion, the IRS lacks the resources to investigate.
