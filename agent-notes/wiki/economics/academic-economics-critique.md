# Academic Economics: Strengths and Faults

Charlie Munger's 2003 Herb Kay Memorial Lecture at UC Santa Barbara — his most systematic critique of a single academic discipline. Where his 1994 USC talk ([[elementary-worldly-wisdom]]) builds the [[mental-models|latticework of mental models]] and then applies it to investing, this lecture turns the same multidisciplinary lens on economics itself, praising its strengths while diagnosing nine specific defects. The talk is a companion piece: the 1994 lecture says *what* the toolkit is; the 2003 lecture demonstrates *what goes wrong* when a discipline fails to use the full toolkit.

## Why economics deserves praise

Munger opens by crediting economics as the best of the soft sciences. His reasons:

- **Right place, right time.** Economics grew alongside the post-1800 takeoff in per-capita output (~2% annual compounding after millennia near zero), and the collapse of communist economies only reinforced its prestige.
- **More multidisciplinary than peers.** Economics has always grabbed useful ideas from other fields — opportunity cost, incentive theory, the tragedy of the commons (from Hardin's biology/ecology) all made it into Mankiw's textbook.
- **Attracted great writers.** Adam Smith (praised by Kant and Voltaire in his own time), Keynes, and — despite political disagreement — Krugman, whom Munger calls "one of the best essayists alive."
- **Practical engagement.** Economics PhDs like George Shultz and Larry Summers moved between academia and the cabinet in a way no other soft science could match.

## The nine defects

### 1. Fatal unconnectedness ("man with a hammer syndrome")

The overarching critique, borrowed from Alfred North Whitehead: academic disciplines don't learn each other's models. Munger's antidote is the full toolkit used checklist-style — you can't rely on the right model surfacing spontaneously.

A sub-pathology: **overweighing what can be counted.** Because statistical techniques reward measurable factors, practitioners systematically underweight hard-to-measure factors that may matter more. Munger cites Thomas Hunt Morgan banning the Frieden calculator from Caltech's biology department — "as long as I can reach down and pick up big nuggets of gold, I'm not going to let anyone waste scarce resources in placer mining."

### 2. Failure to follow the full attribution ethos of hard science

Hard science demands that you always explain things in the most fundamental way available and explicitly credit the source discipline. Munger argues Mankiw grabs from psychology, biology, and game theory without labeling the grab — "Kipplingism," after Kipling's verse about Homer taking whatever he needed. Better than not grabbing, but worse than grabbing with full attribution, because sloppy filing weakens your ability to retrieve and extend ideas.

The Boltzmann's constant example is vivid: Boltzmann didn't discover the constant, but he derived it from more fundamental physics, and the ethos of hard science renamed it for him. That degree of intellectual ruthlessness, Munger argues, is the correct standard.

### 3. Physics envy

Economics should adopt the ethos of hard science but not its craving for unattainable precision. The hard-form efficient market hypothesis — and its offspring like CAPM — exemplify this pathology. Munger's case study: a McKinsey partner, trained in EMH, told the Washington Post not to buy back its own stock when it was trading at a fifth of obvious asset value. Buffett joined the board, they bought back half the shares, and remaining shareholders gained over a billion dollars.

Munger's prescription: "Everything should be made as simple as possible, but no more simple" (Einstein). And Herb Stein's corollary: "If a thing can't go on forever, it will eventually stop." Formulas with Boltzmann-style precision are simply not available in economics, and pretending otherwise produces worse errors than the rustic legislator who rounded pi to 3.2.

### 4. Too much macro, not enough micro

Munger considers macroeconomics both less fun and less reliable than microeconomics, which he compares to the anatomy and chemistry that underpin medicine. He demonstrates with two case studies:

**Nebraska Furniture Mart (Kansas City):** A new store opened selling at $500M/year, exceeding all competitors. The reasoning chain: instant runaway success in a strange city means low prices; $500M of furniture means enormous floor space; enormous floor space means massive selection; the barrier to entry is the huge capital requirement. Simple checklist reasoning solves a problem that "seems hard."

**Les Schwab Tires:** A 50-year grind in the Northwest, competing first against manufacturer-owned stores, then against Costco and Sam's Club. Munger's algorithm: (a) catch a wave (Japanese tire imports), then (b) execute extremely well across many factors — incentive systems, personnel selection, advertising artistry.

From these he extracts four **algorithms for extreme success:**
1. Extreme maximization or minimization of one or two variables (Costco, NFM)
2. Combining multiple success factors to reach a nonlinear breakpoint ("lollapalooza" effects)
3. Extreme good performance across many factors simultaneously (Toyota, Les Schwab)
4. Catching and riding a big wave (Oracle, Japanese tire imports)

He also stresses **forward-and-backward reasoning** (inversion) — his physicist son solved the "same person won a national championship 65 years apart" puzzle by working backward through what an 85-year-old could still win. Answer: checkers.

### 5. Too little synthesis

Munger tests business school students: "Name instances where raising the price increases volume sold." Almost no one can answer. The obvious cases: price as a quality signal (Beckman Instruments), and — his favorite — raising the price to fund bribing the buyer's purchasing agent (the mutual fund industry's high-commission model, which has driven "at least a trillion dollars" in sales).

The deeper synthesis challenge: Ricardo's comparative advantage and Adam Smith's pin factory are the two most famous ideas in economics. One is the ultimate example of emergent order through trade; the other is the ultimate example of central planning within a firm. Munger argues the interesting question is how they interact — and tells the story of the Hollywood Park Hotel, where a man with a self-contained tour-bus-senior system (pure pin factory) solved Berkshire's failing-hotel problem through what was essentially a market match (pure Ricardo). This interaction between planned specialization within firms and spontaneous matching across firms is a core driver of economic power. (Compare Hayek's argument in [[use-of-knowledge-in-society]] that the price system aggregates dispersed local knowledge that central planning cannot capture — the Ricardo half of Munger's synthesis.)

Munger notes that Max Planck tried economics and gave it up because "the best solution you can get is messy and uncertain." If Planck couldn't get clean answers, nobody else will either.

### 6. Extreme psychological ignorance

The slot machine problem: fifty identical machines, identical payouts, but one earns 25% more. Why? It's programmed to show more near misses (bar-bar-lemon), triggering the [[psychology-of-human-misjudgment|near-miss psychological response]] that drives heavier play. Knowing the psychological checklist makes this easy; not knowing it makes it impossible.

Munger cites a Latin American case where a culture of endemic theft ground an economy to a halt — and psychologists (not economists) fixed it using behavioral interventions. Samuel Johnson's injunction: an academic who maintains an easily removable ignorance commits "treachery."

### 7. Too little attention to second- and higher-order effects

Three examples of the damage from ignoring downstream consequences:

- **Medicare cost forecasts** were off by over 1000% because experts extrapolated past costs without modeling how new incentives would change behavior and spur expensive new treatments.
- **The textile loom trap:** new looms pay for themselves in three years at current prices, but competition ensures the efficiency gains flow to consumers, not to the mill owner. Decades of capital investment yield a perpetual 4% return on capital.
- **Worker's comp gaming:** a truck-trailer manufacturer moved from Texas (30% of payroll in fraudulent claims) to Utah (2% of payroll). The difference isn't intrinsic dishonesty — it's incentive structures that reward gaming. "The people who design easily-gameable systems belong in the lowest circle of hell."

The China trade example cuts deepest: Ricardo proves both sides benefit from free trade in the first order. But second-order, a talented billion-person nation absorbs modern technology and eventually becomes a larger, stronger power. George Shultz's response — if we stop trading, others won't — is itself a tragedy of the commons.

### 8. Febezzlement

Munger's original coinage, built from Galbraith's "bezzle" (the stock of undisclosed embezzlement, which temporarily stimulates the economy because the victim still feels rich while the thief spends). The bezzle reverses when discovered, so it's a minor phenomenon. But the **functional equivalent of embezzlement** — febezzlement — is enormous and durable. Investment management fees are the paradigm case: in a rising market, the client doesn't feel the waste because portfolio values keep climbing, while the advisor treats the fees as earned income. The wealth effect from febezzlement dwarfs the old wealth effect from ordinary asset appreciation.

The deeper methodological point: the fundamental principle of algebra ("if A = B and B = C, then A = C") demands that you systematically search for functional equivalents of any important phenomenon. Economics doesn't do enough of this.

### 9. Not enough attention to virtue and vice effects

Economists are uncomfortable with moral language because it doesn't yield to quantification, but Munger argues the effects are enormous:

**Virtue effects:** Double-entry bookkeeping (Fra Luca de Pacioli) made business more controllable and honest. The cash register "did more for human morality than the congregational church." A reliability ethos — historically instilled through religion — is a genuine economic force multiplier.

**Vice effects:** Financial bubbles (the South Sea Bubble led Parliament to ban publicly traded stock for decades — and England continued to grow). Executive compensation corruption, solvable if directors were unpaid significant shareholders (England's 700-year-old lay magistrate system as proof of concept). And the Navy model: ending your career if your ship runs aground, even if it wasn't your fault, produces greater aggregate justice through total vigilance.

**The paradox of vice:** Mandeville's *Fable of the Bees* showed that envy — condemned in the Ten Commandments — is a powerful driver of spending. Economics has irremovable paradoxes, just as mathematics has irremovable incompleteness (Gödel). Munger's attitude: "When I run into a paradox I think either I'm a total horse's ass to have gotten to this point, or I'm fruitfully near the edge of my discipline."

## The big lesson

"Better roughly right than precisely wrong" (Keynes). The prescription is a bigger multidisciplinary toolkit mastered to fluency, combined with acceptance of irreducible messiness. "If you skillfully follow the multidisciplinary path, you will never wish to come back. It would be like cutting off your hands."

## Temporal notes

This lecture was delivered in 2003 (the fs.blog transcript was published in 2021). Several of Munger's observations have aged notably:

- The China trade analysis now reads as prescient — the second-order effects he warned about became central to U.S. political economy by the 2010s-2020s.
- The EMH/CAPM critique, once heterodox, is now mainstream in behavioral finance.
- Munger's textile loom trap is directly relevant to the contemporary debate about whether AI productivity gains will accrue to firms or be competed away to consumers.
