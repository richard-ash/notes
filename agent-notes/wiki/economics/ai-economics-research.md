# AI and the future of economics research

Lynne Kiesling argues that AI is best understood as a relative-price shock to the academic knowledge economy — not just a faster tool, but a force that reprices which skills, questions, and forms of evidence define scholarly distinction. The essay applies price theory to economics itself, asking: if routine cognitive labor becomes cheap, what becomes relatively more valuable?

## The old equilibrium and its costs

The causal-inference revolution improved economics by imposing evidentiary discipline, but it also narrowed the field's ambition. Credible identification strategies demand narrow treatments, clean variation, and measurable outcomes, so the profession gradually tilted toward questions that fit the available empirical machinery. Kiesling argues this was a rational response to relative scarcities — data construction, technical execution, and credible identification were hard, so the profession rewarded them — but it came at the cost of sidelining deeper questions about institutions, governance, and organizational form.

This is a complementary diagnosis to [[academic-economics-critique|Munger's nine defects]]: where Munger identifies the *intellectual* pathologies (physics envy, psychology-blindness, too little synthesis), Kiesling identifies the *economic* mechanism that produced methodological narrowness — the profession responded rationally to the cost structure of pre-AI research.

## AI as a shock to relative prices

AI cheapens routine cognitive labor: literature search, synthesis, coding, drafting, specification exploration, data cleaning. These tasks are not free or perfect, but dramatically cheaper than two years ago. Once many people can produce plausible literature reviews, competent drafts, and standard empirical analyses, those outputs no longer define scholarly distinction. The floor rises, but the skill premium shifts.

What becomes relatively scarce — and therefore more valuable:

- **Judgment** — deciding what is worth studying, which mechanism is first-order, which assumptions are plausible, which results travel across contexts
- **Evaluation** — filtering the flood of cheap-to-produce manuscripts (Kevin Munger's point: AI lowers the cost of speaking, so the cost of listening rises)
- **Deeper economic reasoning** — the ability to ask the right price-theoretic question: what changed, what became cheaper, what bottleneck moved, what margin now matters?

This mirrors the dynamics described in [[ai-and-the-future-of-work]]: just as AI reshuffles task bundles within jobs broadly, it reshuffles the task bundle within "economist." The routine-execution tasks get automated; the judgment tasks rise in relative value.

## Theory is deeper than formalism

Kiesling distinguishes "theory" (which economics often conflates with formal mathematical modeling) from **economic reasoning** — the disciplined application of principles to understand behavior, tradeoffs, institutions, and constraints. On this understanding, AI doesn't push economics away from theory; it raises the return to deeper reasoning.

She engages Tyler Cowen's argument (Chapter 4 of *The Marginal Revolution*) that ML/AI are weakening the link between economics and intuitive marginalist reasoning. Kiesling concedes the surface point — more empirical work relies on black-box methods that don't map onto chalkboard microeconomics — but argues the conclusion is backward. AI doesn't change whether tradeoffs, substitutions, and shadow prices govern the world; it changes *where* the important margins are. Understanding which scarcities still bind becomes *more* important, not less.

## The Hayekian twist

If AI cheapens formalized information processing, then tacit knowledge, local knowledge, judgment, and institutional understanding rise in relative value. Kiesling connects this directly to [[use-of-knowledge-in-society|Hayek's knowledge problem]]: the more abundant machine-generated outputs become, the more important it becomes to know which facts matter, which contexts are special, and which apparent regularities are misleading. Hayek's insight that knowledge is dispersed, contextual, and difficult to aggregate becomes *more* relevant in an AI-saturated research environment, not less.

## The case for transaction cost economics

Kiesling argues that AI may revive transaction cost economics (TCE) and new institutional economics (NIE) — the tradition of Oliver Williamson on governance, contractual incompleteness, asset specificity, and the comparative logic of markets vs. hierarchies.

The profession absorbed the slice of Williamson's agenda that fit the reigning evidentiary regime (contracting, procurement — domains where governance choices leave observable traces in structured data). But the broader agenda — adaptation under uncertainty, authority within organizations, tacit coordination, relational governance — remained underdeveloped because the phenomena require mixed evidentiary strategies and don't arrive naturally as variables in a panel dataset.

AI changes this cost structure in two ways:
1. It lowers the cost of gathering and synthesizing richer evidence — textual records, contractual materials, case comparisons, archival documents, organizational descriptions
2. It raises the relative value of economists who can reason carefully about institutions even when evidence is messy and incomplete

## The apprenticeship problem

A concern that parallels [[ai-judgment-atrophy]]: economics' apprentice model depends on graduate students learning by doing smaller tasks that build research competence. If AI absorbs those tasks, the profession gains current productivity while weakening the channel through which future judgment is formed. Kiesling frames this as a genuine cost: "the profession will have to rethink how it cultivates judgment when many traditional apprenticeship tasks have been automated."

## What the repriced profession looks like

Kiesling's picture of the near-future economist: someone who still needs empirical skill, but who distinguishes herself by the ability to frame deep institutional questions, decide which mechanisms are first-order, and interpret a changing world with theories that travel across contexts. The key shift is from executing narrow empirical routines to identifying the right institutional margin and integrating multiple kinds of evidence.

She frames this not as economics abandoning its core logic, but as economics rediscovering it — recovering its comparative advantage in disciplined reasoning about choice, institutions, and governance, after a generation in which the profession's reward structure over-indexed on technical execution.
