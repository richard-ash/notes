# Scarce assets

When abundance increases, the value of what cannot be made abundant rises with it. Packy McCormick argues this is the defining investment supercycle of the AI era — and divides the opportunity into two structurally different classes: **Macro Scarce Assets** (structurally fixed supply, bid up by concentrating wealth) and **Micro Scarce Assets** (relationally scarce, valuable only because something adjacent has become abundant).

The unifying claim, attributed to Marc Andreessen as a slogan but with deeper roots in Clayton Christensen's *Law of Conservation of Attractive Profits* and Joel Spolsky's "smart companies commoditize their complements": **when one thing becomes abundant and cheap, another becomes scarce and valuable.**

## The Duveen template

McCormick opens with the Gilded Age art dealer Joseph Duveen, who built his fortune on the line that "Europe had plenty of art and America had plenty of money." Duveen sold Old Masters to Frick, Mellon, and the Vanderbilts at then-absurd prices that became the seed collections of the Frick and the National Gallery of Art.

Duveen biographer Meryle Secrest adds a crucial mechanism McCormick highlights: the Europeans only sold because cheap U.S. agricultural exports had wrecked European farm profits. **Abundance in one market (American grain) forced the sale of scarce assets in another (European art), which were then bid up by the wealth that the first abundance had created.** The supercycle has run before.

The same template applied to titles: by 1915, 454 American "Dollar Princesses" had married into European aristocracy. Cash chased the only thing money couldn't manufacture.

## Macro Scarce Assets

These are structurally scarce: supply is fixed or shrinking while global wealth rises. McCormick's exemplars from the week of 2026-04-24:

- **Thrive Eternal** — Josh Kushner's permanent-capital holding company, anchored by the San Francisco Giants
- **HOF Capital + Bugatti Rimac** — consortium acquiring Porsche's stakes in a 117-year-old hypercar house
- **Gulf-state art** — the rumored MBS purchase of *Salvator Mundi* ($450M, 2017) and the November 2025 sale of Klimt's *Portrait of Elisabeth Lederer* ($236M), reportedly to anchor the Frank Gehry-designed Guggenheim Abu Dhabi
- **Indian Creek Island real estate** — Zuck's $170M house, Bezos's three plots — a 2026 echo of Vanderbilt's 660 Fifth Avenue

The math McCormick puts behind this is what makes the cycle structural rather than cyclical:

- Global GDP roughly 2x since 2000 ($78.7T → $174.3T in 2021 dollars)
- Top 100 wealthiest people: 10x nominal, 4x real ($895B → $7.2T) — twice the global growth rate
- Pension assets 3x to ~$70T
- Private markets ~$0.6–1T → $13–15T
- Sovereign wealth ~$1T → $12–15T

Meanwhile, the supply of MLB franchises, hypercar marques, and Fifth Avenue mansions has not changed, and is in fact *shrinking* as winners take pieces off the market permanently. Frick's art is in The Frick. Mellon's is in the National Gallery. Thrive named its vehicle *Eternal*. **A higher and more concentrated numerator chasing a decreasing denominator.**

McCormick's framing: these are "hyperVeblen Goods — not only are they more desirable the higher the price, the high price *is the entire point*."

## Positional scarcity

McCormick draws on Alex Danco's 2019 essay "Positional Scarcity," whose thesis is that "in conditions of abundance, relative position matters a great deal." Danco taxonomizes positional scarcity into Prestige, Access, Curation, and the overlaps (Proximity, Legitimacy). Macro Scarce Assets sit mostly in Prestige.

The key move from Danco that McCormick adopts: **scarcity isn't a property of the object, it's a property of the position the object occupies relative to other things.** That's what makes the next class — Micro Scarce Assets — investable for non-billionaires.

## Micro Scarce Assets

Micro Scarce Assets are *relationally* scarce — they become valuable because something adjacent becomes abundant. The mechanism is Christensen's Law of Conservation of Attractive Profits: "when modularity and commoditization cause attractive profits to disappear at one stage in the value chain, the opportunity to earn attractive profits with proprietary products will usually emerge at an adjacent stage."

McCormick's metaphor: if Macro is a balloon being inflated (everything gets bigger), Micro is a balloon being squeezed (air moves from one place to another).

Worked examples in the essay:

- **Federico da Montefeltro's library (15th c.)** — As Gutenberg made print abundant, the Duke of Urbino refused to admit a single printed book to his collection, employing 30–40 scribes for 14+ years. His successors had printed books *copied by hand* to fit the rule. Handwritten codices became the scarce form precisely because print made text abundant.
- **Proto-Town (2026)** — Land 30 minutes outside Austin where building is fast and easy. As capital and ideas for hard-tech become abundant, *places where you're allowed to build fast* become scarce.
- **OpenAI buying TBPN** — At ~$852B valuation, OpenAI bought a beloved tech-media show for "low hundreds of millions" and immediately killed its $30M ad revenue. Not the cash flows: the likeability — tech media's scarce asset, complement to ChatGPT's 900M weekly actives.
- **GLP-1s and marathons** — As pharmacological skinniness becomes abundant, sub-2-hour marathon performance becomes the scarce credential. ("You can't fake a sub-2 hour marathon.")
- **AI and rare books** — As generic knowledge collapses to free, top-end rare-book auction prices have spiked: cutoff for the Top 500 jumped from $81K (2023) to ~$120K (2024); million-dollar lots went from 12 to 29. The *median* fell. The top got hot.

This is the same structural argument made in [[ai-and-relational-scarcity]] (Imas on relational goods rising as commodity production collapses), but McCormick generalizes it beyond labor markets to assets, equity, real estate, status goods, and credentials. Imas's relational sector is one specific instance of a Micro Scarce Asset: the human element becomes scarce because AI makes the commodity portion of value abundant.

## The scarce-asset feedback loop

McCormick's most operationally useful claim: **the right Scarce Asset to the right buyer has a scarcity-set floor and an abundance-uncapped upside.**

The Giants example: if Thrive uses the team to recruit AI researchers (whose marginal value Zuck has set in the hundreds of millions), or to win a deal that returns $5B, the Giants are not priced as a sports asset but as a sales tool with infinite optionality. The asset's *floor* is set by the next-highest sports bidder; its *ceiling* is set by what the buyer can do with it.

The OpenAI/TBPN logic, McCormick's "dumb math": at $852B, a 1% lower probability of being regulated out of existence is worth $8.5B. If TBPN delivers a 1–3% chance of moving that needle, hundreds of millions is rational.

## Tickered vs. scarce: SpaceX vs. Navan

McCormick's most counter-intuitive observation: **startups are sexy in the private markets and become boring within months of IPO.** Every U.S. venture-backed non-bio IPO since Figma's July 2025 debut has underperformed the Nasdaq.

His proposed mechanism (separate from valuation arguments): the asset gets *Tickered*. In private markets a great startup is a narrative, secret, and status symbol you had to win your way into. In public markets it becomes a sortable row in a stock screener — one of 248 software companies with 50%+ margins growing 30%+. **The company didn't change; the asset class did.** Its scarcity collapsed.

The SpaceX counter-example: planning to IPO at $1.75–2T despite $15B in revenue, while 12 recently-public peers combine for ~$125B market cap on roughly 2x SpaceX's revenue. Why? "If you want to bet on space, you're going to have to buy the SpaceX IPO." There is no basket. McCormick predicts Anduril will behave like SpaceX, not like Figma — defense modernization has no Nasdaq proxy.

The feedback loop: **business scarcity (rare, defensible operations) → asset scarcity (no substitutes in the portfolio) → lower cost of capital → ability to do more rare, defensible things.** This connects to [[competitive-moats]] (Neumann's structural taxonomy of moats and the scarcity-as-moat thesis) and to [[entrepreneurial-profit]] (the Schumpeterian innovation rent that decays as imitators arrive — except for assets where imitation is structurally impossible).

## Vertical Integrators as scarce-asset factories

McCormick connects this to his Vertical Integrator series: companies that win their category by doing the integrated, hard, multi-stage work in industries no software company can attack (Dandy in dentistry, Flock in law enforcement). The argument is that the *operational difficulty itself* manufactures both micro-scarcity (no near competitors made it through the gauntlet) and macro-scarcity (only one company ends up being the way to bet on its category). One-by-one, each Vertical Integrator becomes a Scarce Asset.

This is also McCormick's mechanism for why "more value will accrue to a smaller number of companies that win their category" — both because the businesses themselves become scarce within their verticals, and because investors treat their equity as scarce.

## The trap

McCormick's closing warning is the part most easily skipped: **the abundance button feels like it works for you specifically, but it works for everyone.** The temptation in this era is to believe great things will be easy because the tooling is easy. They won't be — because the moment something is easy, value shoots elsewhere. "Scarce is hard and hard is scarce, forever and ever, amen."

The implied investment heuristic: do not build, buy, or hold the easily-replicable. Do not pick from someone else's list (his shot at the YC Request for Startups). The interesting question is not "what is scarce now" but "what becomes scarce when the thing I'm building becomes abundant."

## Caveats and confidence

McCormick is making a *prediction* about the next decade dressed in historical examples — the framework is durable but the specific calls (Anduril behaves like SpaceX, SpaceX hits $1.75–2T at IPO, Vertical Integrators become Scarce Assets) are unverified. The "dumb math" valuations of TBPN and the Giants are illustrative, not analytical. The thesis works as a lens for noticing relational-scarcity opportunities but not as a pricing model.

The historical examples are real and well-sourced (Duveen, Federico da Montefeltro, Vanderbilt Costume Ball), but the connection from those to the 2026 sports/art/real-estate purchases is structural-analogy rather than empirical proof of a single mechanism. Two factors are conflated in McCormick's own framing: (1) wealth concentration mechanically bidding up fixed-supply assets (clean econ), and (2) status-seeking and Veblen-good dynamics (squishier behavioral claim). Both are real but operate on different timescales.

Confidence: medium. The Macro/Micro distinction is the durable contribution — the predictions, less so.

## Connections

- [[ai-and-relational-scarcity]] — Imas's narrower, more rigorous version of the same mechanism applied to the labor market: as AI makes commodity production cheap, relational goods (where human presence *is* the product) become scarce. McCormick's Micro Scarce Assets generalizes this to assets, equity, status goods, and credentials.
- [[competitive-moats]] — Neumann's structural taxonomy (state, special know-how, scale, system rigidity) overlaps with what makes a business scarce; McCormick's "scarcity-set floor + abundance-uncapped upside" is a financial restatement of the moat thesis.
- [[entrepreneurial-profit]] — The Schumpeterian innovation rent that scarce assets are designed to capture before imitators arrive.
- [[startup-uncertainty]] — Knight/Neumann's argument that Knightian uncertainty is the only first-day moat; McCormick's "uncertainty as temporary moat" overlaps but Macro Scarce Assets are scarce because of fixed supply rather than uncertainty.
- [[cumulative-advantage]] — Watts/Salganik's social-influence feedback loop is the mechanism by which Veblen-good and status-seeking effects compound, making "Tickered" assets less scarce and "Scarce Assets" more so.
- [[aggregation-theory]] — The flip side: aggregators commoditize their suppliers (driving them toward abundance) and own demand (their own scarce position). McCormick's "smart companies commoditize their complements" (Spolsky) is the same move.
