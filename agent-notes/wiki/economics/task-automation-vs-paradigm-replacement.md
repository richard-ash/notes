# Task automation vs paradigm replacement

A framework for understanding when technology actually displaces workers: automating tasks within an existing paradigm rarely eliminates jobs, but creating a new paradigm that makes the old workflow irrelevant does.

## The ATM parable and its second act

The standard telling, popularized by economists James Bessen, David Autor, and Daron Acemoglu, goes like this: ATMs automated most of what bank tellers did, yet teller employment *rose* from the 1970s through the 2000s. The mechanism was a classic Jevons effect — ATMs reduced the cost of operating a branch, so banks opened more branches (urban branches rose 40%+ from 1988-2004), and tellers were repurposed as "relationship bankers" selling credit cards, loans, and investment products. Task substitution within the existing paradigm of branch-based banking actually increased aggregate demand for the labor it partially replaced.

This became a load-bearing parable in the economics of technological unemployment — cited by J. D. Vance, Eric Schmidt, and economics bloggers as evidence that automation doesn't kill jobs.

But as Oks points out, the story has a second act. After 2010, bank teller employment collapsed: from 332,000 in 2010 to 235,000 in 2016 to just 164,000 in 2022. This wasn't a delayed ATM effect — ATMs had reached full saturation decades earlier. The cause was the iPhone and mobile banking.

## Why the ATM failed to displace but the iPhone succeeded

The ATM operated *within* the paradigm of physical branch banking. It automated the cash-handling task but left the branch itself intact. As long as customers still visited branches, there were labor-shaped holes that demanded human involvement — relationship banking, complex transactions, problem resolution. The ATM was a complementary technology: it made the branch cheaper to run, which meant more branches, which meant more tellers doing different work.

The iPhone didn't automate the teller's tasks — it made the entire branch irrelevant. Mobile banking offered a wholly different way of interacting with a bank, one in which there was no physical location to visit and therefore no role for the teller at all. Bank of America's CEO described the iPhone as a "game changer" that "effectively allowed customers to carry a bank branch in their pockets." Between 2008 and 2025, Bank of America closed roughly 40% of its branches. The number of commercial bank branches per capita peaked in 2009 and has since fallen nearly 30%.

## The general principle

Oks argues that this pattern generalizes: **task automation within an existing paradigm rarely eliminates jobs; paradigm replacement does.** The reason is that existing paradigms are organized around human labor — workflows, org structures, physical spaces, regulatory frameworks are all shaped to accommodate humans. Automating tasks within these structures encounters constant friction: there are always residual tasks, edge cases, and complementary functions that require a human in the loop. The paradigm itself creates labor-shaped holes that resist pure capital substitution.

Paradigm replacement bypasses this entirely. Instead of fitting capital into a labor-shaped hole, it constructs a new system in which the hole doesn't exist.

## Implications for AI

Oks applies this framework to the current AI discourse. The dominant vision of AI as a "drop-in remote worker" — a system that learns an existing workflow and performs it at human level — is, in this framing, an ATM-style strategy: task automation within existing paradigms. Oks expects this to produce disappointing productivity gains and relatively little displacement, because existing workflows are riddled with human-shaped frictions.

The real productivity gains and labor displacement, Oks argues, will come from paradigm replacement: entirely new ways of organizing work that are built around AI's capabilities rather than slotting AI into human-designed workflows. He points to Dwarkesh Patel's concept of the "fully-automated firm" as a paradigm-replacement vision, analogous to mobile banking displacing the branch.

This is a complementary but distinct argument from Andreessen's "task loss, not job loss" framework (see [[ai-and-the-future-of-work]]). Andreessen emphasizes that tasks shift faster than jobs disappear within existing structures. Oks's point is that the structures themselves eventually get replaced — and *that* is when the real displacement happens. The two frameworks are compatible: Andreessen describes what happens in the short run (task reshuffling), while Oks describes what eventually breaks the equilibrium (paradigm shift).

## The electricity analogy

Oks connects this to the well-documented history of electricity and productivity growth. Factories initially used electric motors as drop-in replacements for steam engines, retaining the same floor plans and workflows. Productivity gains were modest. It took a generation for manufacturers to realize that electricity enabled fundamentally different factory designs — single-story layouts with distributed power, assembly lines, and workflows impossible under steam. The full productive force of electricity was unleashed only when the old paradigm was abandoned.

The same pattern — technology → modest gains within old paradigm → new paradigm → transformative gains — may be the template for AI, though Oks notes the gap may close faster because AI, unlike electricity, can actively build the structures that harness its own capabilities.

## The job polarization effect

The transition from branch banking to mobile banking also illustrates job polarization: a "mid-skill" occupation (bank teller — high school diploma, one month of training) was replaced by a thin layer of "high-skill" jobs (software developers building the digital interface) and a larger pool of "low-skill" jobs (customer service representatives). This hollowing out of the middle is a recurring feature of paradigm replacement, not just task automation.
