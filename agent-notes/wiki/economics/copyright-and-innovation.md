# Copyright and Innovation

Tabarrok argues that the standard "copyright incentivizes innovation" justification is largely a pretense for protectionism, and the giveaway is that copyright terms keep getting extended *retroactively* — applied to works that already exist.

## The Tabarrok Curve

Tabarrok's framing is that intellectual property protection has an inverted-U relationship with innovation: too little protection underprovides incentives to create, too much protection chokes off downstream uses, recombination, and competitive entry. Some IP protection encourages innovation; more protection does not necessarily encourage more.

This is structurally identical to the Laffer curve for taxation. The empirical question is which side of the peak the current regime sits on — and Tabarrok's view is that developed-world copyright sits well past it.

## The retroactive-extension giveaway

The most direct argument that current copyright policy is rent-seeking rather than incentive-design is that legislatures repeatedly extend copyright on *works already in existence*. Tabarrok's examples:

- **Walt Disney was long dead** when copyright on Mickey Mouse was extended. No length of term can incentivize a deceased creator to produce more work.
- **2016 EU design copyright extension**: extended copyright on furniture and design objects from 25 years post-death to 70 years post-death. Charles Eames died in 1978, so his designs — replicable in 2003 under the old rule — are now protected until 2048 (and Ray Eames' joint works until 2058).

Retroactive extension reverses the entire logic of copyright:

> The incentive argument for copyright would have to run, *We don't have enough designs* so we should increase the incentive to produce more. The actual argument for copyright runs — *We have lots of popular designs* and we need to keep selling them at a high price.

It is impossible to incentivize past behavior. Any retroactive extension is, by construction, a pure transfer to current rights-holders — typically estates, heirs, and the firms that have acquired back-catalog rights — at the cost of consumers and downstream creators. The only economic function such extensions can serve is rent protection.

## Second-order effects: licensing friction

The 2016 EU rule had a kicker beyond higher prices on replica furniture: design books may need to clear licenses to reproduce photographs of the now-protected objects. Each extension of IP scope creates new transaction costs throughout the surrounding ecosystem — scholarship, criticism, reference works, secondary creators — that did not previously need to clear rights. The visible cost is the price of a chair; the hidden cost is the chilling of derivative work.

## Connection to broader rent-seeking dynamics

Retroactive copyright extension is a textbook case of concentrated benefits and dispersed costs: a small number of rights-holders capture large gains while millions of consumers pay marginally higher prices and lose access to a slowly-expanding public domain. The political economy is straightforward — the beneficiaries lobby; the diffuse losers do not.

This pattern connects to the [[academic-economics-critique|Munger critique]] of how the discipline analyzes regulation: the surface justification ("incentivizing innovation") and the actual function ("protecting incumbent rents") can diverge sharply, and reading regulation only by its stated purpose misses the mechanism.
