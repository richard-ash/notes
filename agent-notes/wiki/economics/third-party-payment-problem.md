# The Third-Party Payment Problem in Healthcare

## The core mechanism

When consumers pay directly for a service, they shop on price. When a third party pays, neither the consumer nor the provider faces meaningful price pressure. This is the central explanation for why U.S. healthcare costs have risen far faster than general inflation — and why sectors with direct consumer payment have not.

Perry argues that the shift from out-of-pocket to third-party payment is the primary driver of healthcare cost inflation in the United States. In 1960, consumers paid 47.6% of healthcare expenditures out-of-pocket. By 2017, that share had fallen to 10.6%. Over the same broad period (1998–2017), medical care services prices rose 105.3%, hospital services rose 189.3%, while overall CPI rose only 50.3%.

The parallel to higher education is notable: college tuition — another sector dominated by third-party payment (federal loans, grants, employer reimbursement) — rose at nearly 6% annually over the same period, tracking hospital costs rather than general inflation.

## The cosmetic surgery natural experiment

Elective cosmetic procedures provide a natural experiment because they operate under fundamentally different payment conditions than standard medical care:

- **100% out-of-pocket payment** — no insurance coverage
- **Transparent pricing** — providers publish prices and compete openly
- **Consumer price sensitivity** — patients shop across dozens of competing providers
- **Reduced bureaucratic overhead** — providers avoid the paperwork burden of third-party billing

Perry presents data from the American Society for Aesthetic Plastic Surgery covering 20 cosmetic procedures available in both 1998 and 2017:

- The unweighted average price increase was **34.2%** (weighted by volume: only **12.6%**) — far below the 50.3% rise in general CPI
- 16 of 20 procedures increased *less* than overall inflation, meaning real prices fell
- Some nonsurgical procedures fell in *nominal* terms: chemical peels dropped 33% ($821 → $545), botox fell ~1%
- The two most popular surgical procedures (breast augmentation and liposuction) rose ~26–28%, roughly half the general CPI increase
- **No cosmetic procedure came close to the 105% increase in medical services or 189% increase in hospital services**

This occurred despite rising demand — botox procedures increased nearly tenfold over the period.

## The incentive structure

The argument follows standard price theory:

1. **Consumer side:** When paying 10 cents on the dollar, consumers have little incentive to compare prices, question necessity, or seek alternatives. When paying the full dollar, they do.
2. **Provider side:** When customers are price-insensitive, providers face weak competitive pressure on pricing. When customers shop, providers must compete.
3. **Bureaucratic overhead:** Third-party payment introduces administrative intermediaries (claims processing, prior authorization, billing codes) that add cost without adding medical value. Cash-pay markets avoid this entirely.

Perry frames this as the fundamental lesson for healthcare reform: "The greater the degree of market competition, price transparency, and out-of-pocket payments, the more constrained prices are." Conversely, "the greater the degree of government intervention, opaque prices, and third-party payments, the less constrained prices are."

## Limitations and context

This analysis, originally published by the American Enterprise Institute, represents a market-oriented perspective on healthcare costs. Several caveats apply:

- **Selection effects:** Cosmetic procedures are elective, non-urgent, and performed on relatively healthy patients. Emergency care, chronic disease management, and complex surgeries involve information asymmetries and urgency that limit the applicability of the competitive market model.
- **The data window ends in 2017.** Subsequent developments in price transparency regulation (the 2021 Hospital Price Transparency Rule and the No Surprises Act) represent policy attempts to introduce some competitive dynamics into third-party-paid care.
- **The article does not address equity concerns.** A system with more out-of-pocket payment may constrain prices but could also reduce access for lower-income patients — the classic efficiency-equity tradeoff in healthcare economics.

The argument is strongest as a diagnosis of *why* healthcare prices rise so fast, and weakest as a complete prescription for reform. It complements structural proposals like [[life-health-insurance-bundling]], which attempt to realign insurer incentives rather than eliminate third-party payment entirely.
