# Economics

Housing markets, monetary policy, labor economics (including Garicano's *Messy Jobs* supply-side bundle/authority framework as the firm-side complement to Imas's demand-side relational scarcity — jobs survive AI because firms buy bundles not tasks and organisations need humans to hold residual decision rights), transportation economics, insurance design, forecasting methodology, economic systems, tax policy (including how inheritance tax shapes firm-level capital extraction and reinvestment behavior, not just wealth transfer), sovereign debt valuation, AI's impact on economics research and on long-term asset prices (Andrews-Farboodi event study showing Treasury yields fall on frontier-model releases, plus the doom-equals-bliss observational equivalence in consumption-based asset pricing), AI-driven structural change, structural transformation, intellectual property / innovation policy, industrial organization (empty-core industries and the structural unprofitability of competitive airlines), the abundance-driven scarcity supercycle (McCormick's Macro vs. Micro Scarce Assets framework: structurally fixed-supply assets bid up by concentrating wealth, plus the relational-scarcity mechanism by which abundance in one good creates scarcity in its complement), cross-country productivity measurement (Bergeaud's pedagogical explanation that constant vs. rolling PPPs answer different questions — productive capacity vs. living standards — and that the Balassa-Samuelson mechanism makes a "cheaper" Europe the mirror image of slower productivity growth; plus Krugman's stylized two-country Cobb-Douglas model of the US-Europe paradox showing how sectoral concentration of fast-productivity-growth tech in the US produces a chained-real-GDP gap with no current-price relative GDP divergence and equal real-wage growth in both countries), and Europe's two-speed political economy (Garicano-Kooi's fixed-effects panel on EU-27 fiscal data showing that the post-2022 Russian shock is mobilizing the converging frontier — Poland, Baltics, Nordics — not Europe's "stuck" western rear of France/Italy/Spain where the Draghi competitiveness agenda is most needed).

- [[us-europe-paradox]] — Krugman's stylized two-country, two-sector Cobb-Douglas model of why chained-real US GDP can pull ahead of EU's while current-price relative GDP and real wages stay equal: sectoral concentration of T-sector productivity growth in the US (with N tradable, so wages equalize via goods-market arbitrage) produces measured US growth of 2𝜏𝜌 vs. zero in EU, but the T price falls at 𝜌 so global consumers capture the gain equally; empirically anchored by the Chicago Fed finding that IT is ~8% of US value added but ~half of US productivity growth, plus the BLS pass-through example (computer manufacturing 14x productivity since 1988 vs flat hospitals, but no wage divergence) and the California-vs-rest-of-US regional analogue showing the same statistical pattern read as "natural specialization" within the US and "European decline" across the Atlantic; PPP GDP-per-worker-hour ratio essentially unchanged at ~86%→~87% (2000→2024); revised piece reframes the policy problem from "European productivity decline" to strategic-autonomy risk of being cut off from US/Chinese frontier tech in a post-rule-bound global system
- [[two-europes-divergence]] — Garicano-Kooi's reframing of the European stagnation debate: the EU is two economies moving in opposite directions (eastern catch-up converging from 27–34% to 53–69% of US per-capita GDP by 2030 vs. France/Italy slipping from 86%/93% to 71%/68%), reconciling Saez-Zucman vs. Aghion as descriptions of different halves; Olson's distributional coalitions and Besley-Persson's state capacity framework applied to Europe (which fits neither — high common-interest capacity but pointed at consumption transfers, not growth); fixed-effects panel on EU-27 fiscal data (2000–2024) showing each 750 km closer to Moscow predicts +0.30 pp defence, +0.39 pp public investment, +0.18 pp education, +0.90 pp bundle post-2022 — the security shock is mobilizing the converging frontier (Poland, Baltics, Nordics), not the stuck rear (France, Italy, Spain) where pension claims are descriptively larger and where Draghi reforms are most needed; the central political-economy claim that nobody has supplied a domestic mechanism to make the rear invest/modernize/grow, with a Macron-Gilets-Jaunes case study and an expectation that fiscal crisis or larger shock is the only remaining catalyst

- [[ai-value-capture]] — Garicano-Saa-Requejo's three theses on AI industrial organization: the model layer is squeezed between switchable customers (no network effects, open-weight ceiling at ~20× cheaper) and monopolistic upstream suppliers (TSMC N3, Nvidia, memory oligopoly capturing the rents); intelligence is not the binding constraint in implementation (HITECH's $27B and the Stanford AI Index 2026 single-digit agent deployment), so surplus flows to the implementation layer where workflow redesign and political authority are scarce; policy prescription for middle powers is an open-weights coalition model held one or two tiers behind the frontier — not to win the race but to keep it competitive — explicitly opposed to the CFG "CERN for AI" version that closes weights as capability grows
