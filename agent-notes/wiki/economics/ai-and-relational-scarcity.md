# AI and relational scarcity

As AI drives the cost of commodity production toward zero, what becomes scarce — and therefore valuable — is human involvement itself. Alex Imas applies the economics of structural change to argue that AI will trigger a macroeconomic shift toward "relational goods": products and services where the human element is intrinsic to the value delivered.

## The commodity form and its limits

The industrial revolution's central innovation was the "commodity form" — detaching products from their makers so they could be standardized, scaled, and traded globally. This was the mechanism of mass prosperity: when production isn't tied to specific people, it can be disaggregated and scaled infinitely.

Imas argues AI represents the culmination of this logic. If AI can produce anything a human can produce — text, images, code, analysis, design — then commodity production becomes near-free. But this doesn't mean everything becomes worthless. It means the *commodity portion* of value collapses, and whatever remains non-commodifiable rises in relative price. The question is what that residual is.

## Structural change: the agricultural analogy

Imas draws on the economics of structural change — the study of how productivity shocks in one sector reshape the entire economy. The canonical example is agriculture: automation reduced U.S. farm employment from 40% (1900) to under 2% today, yet food consumption increased. The mechanism is straightforward:

1. Productivity gains lower prices in the automated sector
2. Lower prices raise consumers' real income
3. Consumers spend additional income on goods with higher **income elasticity** — items whose demand grows faster than income

Agriculture has low income elasticity (people don't eat proportionally more as they get richer). Services have high income elasticity. So the economy structurally shifted from agriculture to services — not because policy directed it, but because that's where rising incomes naturally flow.

Imas cites research showing income effects account for over 75% of observed structural change patterns, dwarfing substitution effects. This matters because it means the shift is demand-driven, not supply-driven — consumers *choose* to spend rising real income on high-elasticity goods.

## Mimetic desire and the exclusivity premium

Imas introduces René Girard's concept of mimetic desire to explain *why* relational goods have high income elasticity. People desire objects not just for intrinsic qualities but because others desire them — especially when access is scarce. This creates demand for exclusivity and status that scales with income.

His experimental evidence is striking: willingness to pay nearly doubles when people learn that access to an identical product will be denied to others. But the exclusivity premium depends on perceived human origin — human-made artwork gained 44% in value from scarcity, while AI-generated work gained only 21%. The human element isn't incidental to the status value; it's constitutive of it.

This connects to [[cumulative-advantage]]: social dynamics create positive feedback loops that amplify demand for goods perceived as scarce and human-made, regardless of whether the "quality" difference is intrinsic.

## The relational sector

Imas defines the "relational sector" as work where human involvement is intrinsic to the value delivered — not incidentally present, but the *reason* someone pays. Examples:

- Teaching, nursing, therapy, childcare
- Hospitality, personal services, clergy
- Artisanal crafts, live performance
- Experience design, community curation

The common property: separating the human from the product destroys the product. A teacher's attention matters *because it's a teacher's attention*, not because the information couldn't be delivered by a machine.

This is a distinct argument from [[task-automation-vs-paradigm-replacement]], which asks *when* automation displaces workers (answer: when paradigms shift, not when tasks are automated). Imas is asking *where* the economy goes after displacement occurs — and his answer is that spending flows toward sectors where displacement is definitionally impossible, because the human presence *is* the product.

## The Starbucks parable

Starbucks — a company selling a standardized product through repeatable processes — reversed its automation strategy after CEO Brian Niccol discovered that "handwritten notes on cups" and hospitality details drove customer satisfaction. The company hired more baristas, not fewer. Even in a commodity business, the relational layer proved to be the margin that mattered.

## Baumol's cost disease as feature, not bug

Baumol's cost disease — the tendency for labor-intensive sectors to become relatively more expensive as productivity rises elsewhere — typically gets framed as a problem. Imas reframes it: in a world where AI collapses commodity costs, Baumol's disease means relational sectors *stay* expensive. This reinforces the structural shift by making relational work the economy's high-value sector, not its cost center.

## The demand collapse escape valve

In earlier work, Imas identified a risk that AI could cause negative economic growth: if automation destroys labor income while capital owners are already satiated, aggregate demand collapses. Mimetic desire provides an escape valve. Because status and exclusivity preferences are comparative and insatiable, demand for relational goods can keep growing even if commodity demand hits a ceiling. The economy reallocates spending rather than contracting.

This is a more optimistic structural argument than [[ai-and-the-future-of-work]], where Andreessen argues AI "arrives just in time" to offset demographic decline. Imas's argument doesn't require demographic crisis to work — it predicts reallocation toward relational goods as a natural consequence of rising productivity in commodity sectors, regardless of population trends.

## Developing world caveat

Imas acknowledges his framework works best for wealthy nations whose consumers have sufficient income for the structural transition. Developing economies built on commodity production for rich countries face "more complicated, and potentially more worrying" prospects — if rich countries no longer need developing-world commodity labor, the income channel that drives structural change may not activate.

## The Trammell objection

Philip Trammell raises a serious challenge: technological progress doesn't just make existing goods cheaper — it creates entirely *new* categories of goods. Medieval peasants couldn't predict 2026's consumption patterns. If AI keeps generating novel commodity goods faster than demand shifts toward relational services, the structural change argument breaks down.

Imas responds that structural change research consistently shows income effects dominate, and points to revealed preference: billionaires, with effectively unlimited commodity access, still spend substantially on relational goods (personal chefs, live performances, bespoke experiences). But Trammell's objection deserves weight — the history of technology is a history of desires nobody anticipated.

## Connections

Imas's framework complements [[ai-economics-research|Kiesling's argument]] about what becomes scarce within economics as a discipline. Kiesling identifies judgment, evaluation, and institutional reasoning as the scarce inputs when routine cognitive labor gets automated. Imas generalizes the same logic economy-wide: human involvement, attention, and relational presence become the scarce inputs when commodity production gets automated. Both are applications of the same price-theoretic intuition — when one input becomes cheap, its complements rise in value — applied at different scales.

The connection to [[use-of-knowledge-in-society|Hayek's knowledge problem]] also runs deep: relational goods are inherently local, contextual, and resistant to standardization — exactly the kind of knowledge Hayek argued could never be centralized.
