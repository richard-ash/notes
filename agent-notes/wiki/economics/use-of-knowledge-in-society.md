# The Use of Knowledge in Society

F. A. Hayek's 1945 paper in the *American Economic Review* is one of the most influential essays in 20th-century economics. It reframes the central problem of economics away from optimal allocation under complete information and toward a deeper question: how can a society make use of knowledge that is never available to any single mind in its totality?

## The knowledge problem

Hayek argues that the economic problem of society is *not* merely one of allocating given resources according to a given set of preferences — a problem that, if all the data were available to a single mind, would be a straightforward exercise in logic. The real problem is that the knowledge required for rational economic decisions is dispersed across millions of individuals, each possessing unique fragments of information about "the particular circumstances of time and place."

This local, practical knowledge — knowing that a machine is underused, that a shipping route has spare capacity, that a surplus stock exists — is, Hayek insists, just as socially valuable as scientific or theoretical knowledge. Yet it is precisely the kind of knowledge that cannot be conveyed to a central authority in statistical form, because statistics necessarily abstract away the local particulars that matter for specific decisions.

## Two kinds of knowledge

Hayek distinguishes between:

1. **Scientific/theoretical knowledge** — general rules and principles that experts can centralize and command. Even here, centralizing it merely shifts the problem to selecting the right experts.
2. **Knowledge of particular circumstances of time and place** — the practical, fleeting, local knowledge possessed by the shipper, the estate agent, the arbitrageur. This knowledge is inherently decentralized and often cannot be articulated, let alone transmitted to a planning board.

Hayek observes that economists and intellectuals tend to dismiss this second type of knowledge, treating commerce and arbitrage with a "kind of contempt" compared to theoretical or technical expertise. This bias leads them to underestimate how much of economic life consists of constant adaptation to small, local changes.

## The price system as information mechanism

The paper's most celebrated argument is that the [[price-mechanism-information|price system]] serves as a mechanism for communicating information. Prices condense vast amounts of dispersed knowledge into a single number that guides individual action without anyone needing to understand the full picture.

Hayek illustrates this with the famous tin example: if tin becomes scarcer (whether from a new use or a supply disruption — it does not matter which), the price rises. Users of tin need only know that it has become relatively more expensive. They economize on tin, switch to substitutes, and this effect ripples outward through the entire chain of substitutes and their substitutes — all without most participants knowing anything about the original cause of the change.

The price system thus achieves what Hayek calls a "marvel": coordinating the actions of millions of people, each acting on their own limited knowledge, as effectively as if the whole system were directed by a single mind possessing all the relevant information. He deliberately uses the word "marvel" to combat the complacency with which economists take this mechanism for granted.

## Against central planning

The paper is an argument in the broader debate about the feasibility of economic planning. Hayek contends that:

- Central planning requires concentrating knowledge that is inherently dispersed and often non-statistical in nature.
- Economic problems arise precisely *because of change* — constant small adaptations to shifting circumstances, not one-time allocation decisions.
- The assumption of "given" data in mathematical economics is misleading: the data are never given to a single mind, and any approach that starts from that assumption "systematically leaves out what is our main task to explain."
- Even in a centrally planned system, the planner must ultimately delegate decisions to "the man on the spot" — which concedes the fundamental point about the need for decentralized decision-making.

Hayek engages with the socialist calculation debate, noting that by 1945 even sympathetic economists had conceded that a price system of some kind was necessary for rational economic calculation in a complex society. The remaining disagreements, he argues, are methodological rather than political.

## Civilization and unconscious coordination

In a broader philosophical point, Hayek quotes Alfred North Whitehead: "Civilization advances by extending the number of important operations which we can perform without thinking about them." The price system is one such formation — a spontaneously evolved institution that coordinates a division of labor based on equally divided knowledge, without anyone having designed it for that purpose.

Hayek argues this has implications beyond economics: the central theoretical problem of all social science is how to coordinate the use of knowledge not given to anyone in its totality. The price system is one solution; language, law, and cultural norms are others.

## Temporal notes

Written in 1945, this paper was an intervention in the socialist calculation debate that had been running since the 1920s. Many of the specific interlocutors Hayek names (Lange, Lerner, Schumpeter) were advocating various forms of market socialism or central planning. The subsequent decades — particularly the collapse of centrally planned economies in 1989-1991 — largely vindicated Hayek's position on the practical impossibility of effective central planning.

The paper's arguments have found renewed relevance in debates about algorithmic planning, platform economics, and AI-based resource allocation. Modern discussions about whether big data and AI could "solve" the knowledge problem by centralizing information are essentially revisiting the question Hayek posed — though the tools available are vastly more powerful than anything he could have imagined.
