# PCSK9 Inhibitors

PCSK9 inhibitors are a class of cholesterol-lowering medicines that biotech investor Alex Carnell (Braidwell) describes as the closest thing to a "free lunch" in modern pharmacology — asymmetrically favorable risk-reward, discovered through human genetics, and potentially more important long-term than GLP-1s for public health impact.

## Discovery: from human genetics to medicine

The PCSK9 story is a textbook case of genetics-led drug discovery. Scientists studying populations around the world identified a group of people with a genetic mutation in their **PCSK9 gene** — they don't produce the PCSK9 protein. Longitudinal studies over 15 years showed these genetically deficient individuals had an **88% reduction** in the risk of ever developing cardiovascular disease. They live long, healthy lives without heart attacks or strokes.

## Mechanism

PCSK9 is a protein our bodies produce that **interferes with LDL cholesterol clearance**. The more PCSK9 you produce, the fewer LDL particles your body can clear from the bloodstream. Over decades, these LDL particles accumulate in the vasculature, slowly building up until they cause a blockage — a heart attack or stroke.

By inhibiting PCSK9, these medicines restore the body's ability to clear LDL cholesterol. Current PCSK9 inhibitors can lower LDL by approximately **50%**, with outcome studies showing:

- **20%+ reduction** in future cardiovascular events for patients who have previously had a heart attack or stroke
- **~25% reduction** in risk for people at high risk of cardiovascular disease
- In the genetically deficient population: **88% reduction** in cardiovascular disease risk over 15 years

## Therapeutic evolution

The drug modality has progressed through three generations:

1. **Monoclonal antibodies** (e.g., evolocumab/Repatha, alirocumab/Praluxa) — injectable biologics that bind to the PCSK9 protein, preventing it from interfering with LDL receptors. Require injection roughly every 2 weeks (26 times/year).

2. **RNA interference (RNAi)** (e.g., inclisiran/Leqvio) — subcutaneous injection that travels to the liver and silences PCSK9 production at the mRNA level. Dramatically longer duration: only **2 injections per year**. A major compliance improvement.

3. **Gene editing** (future) — one-time PCSK9 gene edit to permanently eliminate production. Carnell's colleague Dave Ricks (Eli Lilly CEO) has discussed the pricing paradox this creates: a one-time cure displacing an $8-9K/year chronic therapy needs entirely new reimbursement models (see [[pharma-industry-economics]]).

## The "free lunch" argument

Carnell argues PCSK9 inhibitors are uniquely asymmetric in their risk-reward profile, contrasting them with [[glp1-drugs]]:

**Why it's close to a free lunch:**
- Early concerns about driving LDL to zero proved unfounded — LDL is produced in many places in the body, and even maximal PCSK9 inhibition only reduces LDL by 80-90% (in animal models)
- People with the natural PCSK9 mutation who produce zero PCSK9 protein live long healthy lives with no apparent downsides
- Unlike GLP-1s, which have real toxicity (nausea, vomiting, gallstones, muscle loss), PCSK9 inhibitors have a clean safety profile

**Why it's underperforming commercially despite better risk-reward:**
- LDL accumulation is a **"silent killer"** — you feel nothing while cholesterol builds up. You can be at peak physical shape and drop dead from a heart attack because the buildup was invisible.
- GLP-1s produce visible, immediate effects (weight loss, reduced appetite), making it psychologically easier to stay on them. PCSK9 benefits are invisible — you're preventing a statistical event decades in the future.
- Carnell believes PCSK9 inhibitors should eventually be a larger market than GLP-1s given the asymmetric risk-reward, but this requires solving the behavioral challenge of getting people to take medicine for a disease they can't feel.

## The case for universal adoption

Heart disease remains the **#1 killer in the US**. Most middle-aged adults have a 30-50% probability of having a heart attack or stroke between ages 40 and 80. Carnell argues the combination of PCSK9 inhibitors with other elements of a defensive [[health-stack]] could dramatically reduce that risk to sub-10%.

The key insight: **earlier intervention is dramatically more effective**. LDL accumulates over decades. Starting PCSK9 inhibition earlier — before significant vascular buildup — produces far greater protection than intervening after damage has begun. Carnell considers the risk-reward of PCSK9 inhibitors to be "actually even more favorable than statins in terms of human health."
