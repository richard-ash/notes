# Metformin

Metformin is a biguanide drug approved for type 2 diabetes that has emerged as one of the most promising candidates for pharmacologically targeting aging itself. Originally derived from the French lilac (*Galega officinalis*), it has been in clinical use for over 60 years with an excellent safety record. Its relevance to geroscience extends far beyond glucose control: metformin modulates several core aging pathways — AMPK activation, mTOR inhibition, inflammation reduction, and senescent cell clearance — making it a uniquely broad-spectrum intervention compared to single-pathway drugs like rapamycin.

## Mechanisms of action in aging

Metformin targets multiple pathways implicated in aging, which partly overlap with the pathways activated by caloric restriction:

- **AMPK activation**: metformin activates AMP-activated protein kinase, the cell's central energy sensor, which coordinates downstream effects on metabolism, autophagy, and growth signaling
- **mTOR inhibition**: via AMPK, metformin increases inhibition of the mTOR pathway — the same pathway targeted by rapamycin and caloric restriction. However, metformin achieves this without rapamycin's immunosuppressive side effects
- **Mitochondrial Complex I inhibition**: metformin inhibits Complex I in the electron transport chain, reducing endogenous production of reactive oxygen species (ROS) and shifting the cellular energy balance
- **Anti-inflammatory effects**: metformin reduces chronic systemic inflammation, one of the hallmarks of aging ("inflammaging")
- **Senescent cell clearance**: unlike rapamycin, metformin removes senescent cells through mechanisms that remain unknown. This positions it alongside [[senolytic-car-t-cells]] as a senescence-targeting intervention, though metformin's senolytic mechanism is distinct and not yet characterized
- **IGF-1/insulin signaling reduction**: metformin suppresses the IGF-1 signaling pathway, which has been consistently linked to longevity across species

## Animal model evidence

Metformin extends lifespan and healthspan in multiple model organisms, though results are dose-dependent and sex-specific:

- **C. elegans**: lifespan extension demonstrated
- **Mice**: Martin-Montalvo et al. (2013) showed metformin increased lifespan by **4-6%** across different mouse breeds, with health-span indices (rotarod performance, treadmill distance, glucose/insulin tolerance, cognitive function) improved by **~30%**. Female outbred mice showed ~40% increase in mean lifespan when started early. In a breast cancer model, metformin delayed carcinoma onset and extended lifespan by ~8%
- **Drosophila**: metformin activated AMPK and reduced lipid stores but **did not** extend lifespan — a notable negative result

A critical caveat: the effective dose in mice (1 mM) is well above comparable human doses. A 10-fold increase in the beneficial mouse dose caused increased glucose/lactate and early death, demonstrating a narrow therapeutic window for longevity effects.

## Human observational evidence

The most striking human data comes from observational studies in diabetic populations:

### Cancer reduction
Meta-analyses of epidemiological data show metformin use is associated with a **31% reduction in overall cancer incidence** and **34% reduction in cancer mortality**. The mechanisms likely involve mTOR inhibition, AMPK-mediated lipid synthesis suppression, and insulin sensitization. Studies in Taiwan showed reduced colorectal, liver, and pancreatic cancer incidences in a population-prospective cohort of 800,000 individuals.

### Mortality paradox
Perhaps the most provocative finding: a UK Clinical Practice Research Database study found that metformin-treated diabetic patients had **survival rates comparable to — and among those over 70, better than — age- and sex-matched non-diabetic controls**. This is remarkable because diabetic patients typically have worse outcomes across all domains. Sulfonylurea-treated diabetics, by contrast, had lower survival than matched non-diabetic controls. Long-term UKPDS follow-up confirmed a 36% reduction in all-cause mortality in the metformin group (p = 0.011).

### Cognitive protection
The Singapore Longitudinal Aging Study found metformin use associated with a **51% reduced risk of cognitive impairment** (Mini-Mental Status Exam score ≤ 23), robust to adjustment for vascular and non-vascular risk factors.

## Clinical trial evidence

### Diabetes Prevention Program (DPP)
The landmark DPP trial randomized 3,100+ adults at high diabetes risk to placebo, metformin (850 mg twice daily), or lifestyle modification. Metformin reduced T2D incidence by **31%**, effective across all age groups including those over 60. Metformin also improved cardiovascular risk factors, though at 13-year follow-up it did not significantly reduce cardiovascular events or all-cause mortality.

### UKPDS
The UK Prospective Diabetes Study showed metformin-treated T2D patients had **26% risk reduction for CVD** and **42% reduction in diabetes-related death** compared to conventional treatment — establishing metformin as first-line T2D therapy.

### Negative results
The CAMERA trial (non-diabetic patients with coronary heart disease) found no benefit of 18 months of metformin on carotid intima-media thickness. A post-MI trial found no benefit on left ventricular ejection fraction at 6 months. These negative results in non-diabetic populations underscore that metformin's benefits may be partially mediated through glucoregulation, or that longer treatment durations are needed.

## The TAME trial

TAME (Targeting Aging with Metformin) is the first FDA-approved clinical trial designed to test whether a drug can target **aging itself** as a condition, rather than any single disease. Designed by Barzilai and colleagues, TAME represents a paradigm shift: if successful, it would establish aging as a treatable indication and open the regulatory pathway for geroscience drugs.

### Design
- **Population**: 3,000 subjects, ages 65-79, across ~14 U.S. centers
- **Primary endpoint**: time to new occurrence of a **composite outcome** — cardiovascular events, cancer, dementia, or mortality
- **Secondary endpoints**: functional and geriatric measures
- **Dosing**: up to 2,250 mg/day (plasma half-life ~20 hours)

### Regulatory significance
Rather than testing metformin's effect on each age-related disease separately, TAME uses the composite endpoint to test whether metformin delays the **onset of age-related disease generally**. This is the key innovation: it reframes aging as a modifiable risk factor rather than an inevitable process, creating a template for future geroscience trials.

## Safety profile

Metformin's safety record spans 60+ years. In the DPP/DPPOS follow-up (18,000+ patient-years, with ~25% of participants over 70):
- **No cases** of lactic acidosis or significant hypoglycemia
- Mild anemia in ~12% (vs. 8% placebo)
- Vitamin B12 deficiency in ~7% (vs. 5% placebo) after 13 years, increasing with duration of use
- Lactic acidosis risk is related to renal function, not age, and is considered very low
- GI side effects (diarrhea, nausea) are the most common complaint

## Temporal notes

This paper was published in 2016. As of 2026, the TAME trial has faced significant delays in funding and enrollment. Metformin remains widely discussed in the longevity community but has been somewhat overshadowed by newer interventions: rapamycin analogs, [[senolytic-car-t-cells|senolytic therapies]], [[glp1-drugs|GLP-1 receptor agonists]] (which show their own multi-system benefits), and [[in-vivo-epigenetic-reprogramming|epigenetic reprogramming]] approaches. The observational evidence for metformin's anti-aging effects remains compelling but unconfirmed by a prospective aging-focused trial.

- [[health-stack]] — metformin fits squarely into the "defense" side of the health stack framework as a cardiometabolic and potentially pan-aging intervention, though it is not yet included in Carnell's five-layer model (which focuses on diseases with clearer single-drug solutions)

- [[information-theory-of-aging]] — metformin acts downstream of the pathways described by the ITOA (inflammation, mTOR, senescence) but does not address the upstream cause of epigenetic information loss.
