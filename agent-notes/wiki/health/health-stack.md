# The Health Stack

The "health stack" is a framework articulated by biotech investor Alex Carnell (Braidwell) and Patrick O'Shaughnessy (Invest Like The Best) for thinking about proactive, layered health optimization — borrowing the "stack" metaphor from technology. The central thesis: we already have the medicines to prevent most of the diseases that will kill most of us. The gap is not invention but **impact** — getting these medicines into the hands of people early enough to matter.

## The trillion-dollar thesis

Carnell argues that GLP-1 adoption is not just a once-in-a-decade $100B+ revenue opportunity but "the first commercial proof" that we're ready for a once-in-a-lifetime **trillion-dollar revolution** in public health. That trillion dollars refers not to drug revenue but to a potential **reduction in annual healthcare spend** — the savings from preventing disease rather than reactively treating it.

The evidence: expected lifespan curves have been flat for decades. The last major inflection came from antibiotics, vaccines, and better hygiene. Since then — nothing. Yet the medicines to bend the curve already exist. The bottleneck is adoption.

## Offense and defense

The health stack has two sides:

### Offense (behavioral)
- Proper nutrition and dietary choices
- Strength training and exercise
- Systematic monitoring and testing (blood work, screening)
- Data tracking and longitudinal self-measurement
- AI-assisted interpretation of personal health data (emerging)
- Cancer screening: colonoscopy, stool-based tests (Exact Sciences), blood-based tests (Guardant Health), and emerging multi-cancer early detection panels

Carnell places cancer on the offensive side because there are no broadly preventive medicines for cancer — the strategy is early detection combined with increasingly effective treatments (see below).

### Defense (pharmacological) — the five layers

Each layer has existing medicines that, taken proactively, could collectively add an **extra decade** to expected lifespan:

| Layer | Target | Key medicines | Risk addressed |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Lipid optimization** | LDL cholesterol | Statins, [[pcsk9-inhibitors]] | Heart attack, stroke |
| **2. Cardiometabolic health** | Glycemic environment + visceral fat | [[glp1-drugs]] (semaglutide, tirzepatide) | Diabetes, obesity, cardiovascular disease |
| **3. Neurocognitive health** | Amyloid plaque accumulation | Anti-amyloid antibodies (lecanemab, donanemab) | Alzheimer's disease |
| **4. Inflammatory health** | Chronic systemic inflammation | GLP-1s (via reduced food intake), targeted anti-inflammatories | Atopic dermatitis, Crohn's, colitis, cardiovascular events |
| **5. Blood pressure** | Hypertension | Antihypertensives (various classes) | Stroke, heart disease, kidney damage |

The layers are **force-multiplying**: high LDL + high glycemic environment + chronic inflammation + high blood pressure don't merely add risk — they compound it. Brittle vasculature (from high glucose) plus accumulating lipids plus inflammatory environment plus high pressure is "a ticking time bomb."

## The invention-to-impact gap

Carnell identifies three barriers preventing existing medicines from having their intended population-level impact — the "three C's":

### 1. Complexity
The healthcare system is extraordinarily difficult to navigate. Carnell's personal experience: recognizing a cholesterol problem from a blood test took **two years** to result in access to PCSK9 inhibitors — through specialist referrals, repeated blood draws, insurance approvals, and waiting lists. Many people simply give up. The contrast with consumer tech is stark: you can order toothpaste on Amazon in seconds, but protecting yourself from a heart attack requires "hundreds of phone calls, multiple doctor visits."

### 2. Cost
Chronic preventive medicines are priced like acute treatments but need to be taken for **decades**. The GLP-1 market proved massive price elasticity exists: at $400-500/month (branded injectable), adoption was strong but capped. At $200-250/month (compounded), 15-20% more demand appeared from people willing to accept unregulated products. At $150/month (oral Wegovy), new scripts surged 50% in weeks.

### 3. Compliance
Medicine only works if people take it. The more frequently a medicine must be taken, the harder it is to access, and the more often patients must make the decision to continue, the higher the quit rate. GLP-1s illustrate the compliance unlock: twice daily → once weekly → once monthly → potentially once quarterly. PCSK9 inhibitors went from every 2 weeks (monoclonal antibodies) to twice yearly (RNA interference).

Average chronic medicine adherence: patients take a medicine for about **one year** before quitting. This means the enormous investment in drug discovery produces medicines that never achieve their intended long-term impact.

## Neurocognitive layer: the Alzheimer's parallel

Carnell draws a direct parallel between the Alzheimer's opportunity and the proven GLP-1/PCSK9 playbook: anti-amyloid medicines (from Biogen and Eli Lilly) can slow cognitive decline by ~30% in patients with advanced disease. The thesis is that earlier intervention — before significant plaque accumulation and brain damage — could reduce decline by 40-50%+ and potentially prevent the disease entirely. Lilly data expected in 2026 may validate the early-intervention hypothesis.

The pattern repeats across all five layers: GLP-1s taken early prevent diabetes entirely. PCSK9 inhibitors taken early prevent cardiovascular disease. Anti-amyloid drugs taken early may prevent Alzheimer's. The common principle: **proactive intervention before accumulation of damage is exponentially more effective than reactive treatment after**.

## Cancer: the screening strategy

Cancer sits on the offensive (not defensive) side because no broadly preventive cancer medicine exists. Instead, the strategy is **early detection**:

- **Colonoscopy** remains the gold standard for colorectal cancer — a slow-growing cancer that is highly preventable with regular screening, yet most people avoid it
- **Stool-based tests** (Exact Sciences/Cologuard) — high sensitivity but completion rates limited by logistics
- **Blood-based tests** (Guardant Health/Shield) — learned from stool-test limitations, now inflecting in adoption because a simple blood draw scales far better
- **Multi-cancer early detection** — emerging tests screening for many tumor types simultaneously, expected to mature within 3-5 years

Key diagnostic metrics: **sensitivity** (of 100 cancers, how many does the test catch?) and **specificity** (of 100 cancer-free samples, how many give false positives?). The false positive problem compounds as more dimensions are tested simultaneously.

Carnell is cautious about full-body imaging (Prenuvo-style): more data is valuable but only if you have the **context** to interpret it. Material false positive rates across many dimensions can be "disruptive in life." He personally focuses on tests with high sensitivity and high specificity rather than broad imaging.

Treatment modalities are also advancing: from pills and injectable biologics to **CAR-T cells** (engineered immune cells) that can achieve 100% tumor reduction in 70%+ of patients. Next-generation approaches (e.g., Capstan) deliver CAR-T therapy via IV infusion without needing to extract and engineer a patient's own cells.

## AI and the future of drug discovery

Carnell argues AI will be "one of the biggest unlocks in the history of medicine" by removing two constraints:

1. **Mind constraint** — Human scientists can't retain all relevant literature. Much published research is irreproducible (garbage in, garbage out). AI systems trained on proprietary "science tokens" — novel experimental data generated robotically, not from the existing literature — can generate comprehensive hypothesis sets.

2. **Hands constraint** — Human throughput in running experiments is limited. Agentic systems driving robotic labs (e.g., Laya Sciences' automated labs with robotic arms and automated petri dish handling) can run experiments 24/7 and produce data at unprecedented scale.

Current state (2026): AI can go from **model to molecule in one month** for known targets (vs. years historically). Companies like Laya Sciences and Anaba are already doing this. Novel target discovery by AI has not yet been systematically demonstrated, but Carnell believes the curve is now "inevitable" and follows deterministic scaling laws tied to proprietary data generation.

The moat for AI drug discovery companies: the ability to **generate novel science tokens** that don't exist in the public domain. This is analogous to how LLMs were trained on trillions of novel text tokens — scientific superintelligence requires its own novel training data, produced by automated experimentation.

## The citizen pharmacology movement

Alongside institutional drug development, a grassroots movement of self-experimentation has emerged — particularly around peptides (BPC-157, etc.) and compounded GLP-1s. Carnell notes this reflects the same consumer proactivity driving GLP-1 adoption: people "voting with their feet" to take health into their own hands rather than waiting for traditional FDA pathways. The FDA under commissioner Marty Makary is simultaneously working to reduce regulatory friction — using AI to review filings, challenging whether animal studies are always necessary, and accepting single studies where redundant trials were previously required.
