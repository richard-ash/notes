# Public Problem-Solving Capital

Zack Rosen's operational framework for how private-sector wealth should fund government reform at scale. Where [[abundance-agenda]] is the *policy program* (supply-side liberalism: build housing, energy, transit), this is the *go-to-market manual* for funders — how to actually deploy capital so that reform happens rather than burns. Rosen is the leaked-then-published author (memo originally written 2025 to a single Bay Area funder); he co-founded California YIMBY and Abundance Network and was CEO of Pantheon. The piece is a recruiting document for a "yet-to-be-named" category of philanthropy he calls *public problem-solving at scale*, explicitly analogized to the Progressive Era (1910–1940) when business leaders retrofitted industrial-operations practices — bookkeeping, professional civil service, scaled municipal services — onto a government designed for a 90%-farm economy.

## The central inversion: policy is the product, campaigns are sales & marketing

Rosen's load-bearing claim, aimed at CEOs and VCs entering politics: **you must work backwards from "a government change that improved people's lives" to politics**, not forwards from "elect better people." A political operation without a policy team is "a startup with sales & marketing but no engineers" — and, he says, that broken shape is *standard practice*. Two corollaries he hammers:

1. Electing aligned politicians is a *step toward* a policy outcome, not an outcome itself.
2. You cannot define an "aligned" or "better" politician until you have defined "better" — i.e., the specific government change.

Campaign consultants are framed as Account Executives: they will sell you advertising activity all day (and pocket 5–15% of spend) without ever forcing you to name the goal. This is the same delivery-over-process instinct that animates the broader [[abundance-agenda]], pushed down to the level of how a single check gets written.

## Coalitional power vs. market power

The concept Rosen says private-sector people most consistently fail to grasp: government is `~autocratic` to drive nowhere; power is *hyper-fractured* across interlocking bodies (Supervisor district → municipal code → regional authority like the MTC → state → federal statute), any one of which can simply say "no." In tech, the market winner captures 50%+ and everyone standardizes on them; in politics you must *agglomerate* power by getting interlocking bodies to agree. That agglomeration capability **is** coalitional power. It is "foreign, off-putting, and maddeningly slow" to operators from winner-take-all markets.

## The three styles of political investing

A gambling-table taxonomy of how funders deploy:

1. **Gambling** — donate to an elected, hope for the best. Most "hard money" (ActBlue-style giving) is this.
2. **Card counting** — hire an expert, make risk-adjusted bets. The *default for a new funder*.
3. **The House** — get a ~2-point structural edge and *sustain* it. The only way to win big, even against majority opinion (his example: the decades-long legal campaign to overturn *Roe*, despite it being ~2:1 unpopular).

Abundance, Rosen says, is building "The House" but in pursuit of *majoritarian* goals — a deliberate framing against narrow-interest capture and minoritarianism. The economic logic: narrow interests outspend reformers 10:1 or 100:1, so reform only wins the way startups beat incumbents — focus, speed, smart capital staging, out-execution. He claims a track record of doing exactly that against lopsided spend (in California, foundations backing anti-housing "equity" groups outspent the YIMBY movement $260M to ~$40M).

## The A/B/C funding checklist and the three workback questions

Before greenlighting funding, check three boxes:

- **A** — Do you understand the *policy levers* to pull to create the outcome?
- **B** — Do you have the *political power* to pull them?
- **C** — Do you have the *resources* (talent, money) to *sustain* that power?

A and B are answered by three sequential questions, each of which most funders skip:

1. **What is your goal?** Stated as `Goal: [Action X] to [Change Y]`. (CA YIMBY: "Lobby the California State Legislature to legalize housing." The failure mode: "Buy political ads to elect 'better' politicians.") Rosen also stress-tests goals by asking *who the counterparty is* — a lobbying fight against weak opponents isn't impressive; the strength of the opposition is what makes a win meaningful.
2. **What is the jurisdiction?** Map every governmental authority that controls the thing you want — across all levels simultaneously. This requires real policy expertise "well beyond what almost all political consultants have."
3. **How will you build political power** to influence those bodies? Each jurisdictional body gets its own plan.

The funder's minimum buy-in is **5,000–10,000 hours** of rolling-up-sleeves work to understand how the system actually operates — most funders, having spent 25,000+ hours building wealth, refuse this and instead pair private-sector mental models with consultants and burn money.

## The four-layer land-use model (the worked example)

The California YIMBY case is the proof-of-concept. To revive the housing market you must "bore a giant hole" through **four sequential layers** of land-use policy — and *until all four are cleared, you get almost nothing* (the layers are conjunctive, not additive):

1. **Ministerial Approval** (~85%) — *If your project is legal, can you actually build it, or can a councilmember/activist still veto it?* The hardest political lift; via the CEQA housing exemption + HAA strengthening, California is now "de facto ministerial." Required defeating the "exaction-machine" of equity groups, part of labor, and NIMBYs.
2. **Zoning** (~60%) — *Is it legal to build?* SB79 + RHNA strengthening = "largest up-zoning in U.S. history."
3. **Fees** (0%) — ±$200,000/home in high-demand cities (≈ the all-in cost of a house in Ohio). The 2026 focus.
4. **Implementation** (10%) — building codes, utility hookups, plan-check; a long municipal-level tail Abundance Network expects to absorb.

They have punched through all four *for ADUs* — yielding a $3–4B/year market. Doing the same for multifamily would unlock a $100–200B+ market at 250,000+ homes/year. This is the layered, "drill through every veto" mechanics underneath the [[abundance-agenda]] scorecard's "outcomes: failing" critique — and a concrete answer to the [[abundance-agenda]]'s unresolved *"who decides / voice but no veto"* problem: the YIMBY answer was to *remove* the local veto (ministeriality) entirely rather than design better arbitration. It took 7 years to reach 50%; another 7 to finish, or 3–4 "if the cavalry comes" (HNWI funders, and the corporate government-relations arms of Google/Meta/Apple).

How they did it: (1) professionalized the YIMBY movement; (2) won on the merits *and* nationalized the narrative (getting NYT/Bloomberg to "write the same article every week for years" — California-screwing-up-its-crown-jewel-economy); (3) de-powered NIMBY-aligned narrow interests over time. The meta-lesson: "Work works" — 90% was hands-on execution off a rigorous workback, all of it claimed to be repeatable and scalable.

## Permanent, federated institutions on permanent capital

The structural-design thesis, and the reason one-off giving fails:

- **Federated by design.** The NRA, ACLU, and unions are all architected as federated bodies *because* American power is mapped that way (municipal → state → federal). If you don't build the federated structure in from the start, your ability to shape government is "severely curtailed at scale." Abundance Network is being built as municipal-to-state-to-federal institutional power across all 50 states, starting with housing, energy, transportation.
- **Less capital, sustained, beats a big infusion that fades.** Coalitions form only on *sustained commitments* (NRA↔GOP, pro-choice movement↔Left, public-sector unions in city/state government). Electeds discount donors who can't demonstrate staying power *by ~90%*, because counterparties will be at the table for the elected's entire career and have long memories. The maxim: **institutional political power can only be built on ~permanent capital streams.** This is the funder-side mirror of the [[abundance-agenda]]'s warning that a more powerful state is more dangerous if its funding is porous to donor capture — Rosen's answer is permanence and federation, not a check.

## Product problem, not messaging problem

The political frame: Democrat-controlled states have a *product problem*, not a messaging problem — "the lived experience of residents doesn't match our stated values." Rosen cites Texas out-building California on clean energy, Mississippi's reading "miracle" (Jim Barksdale's civic leadership) going worst-to-#1 while California backslid, and California's highest-in-nation poverty rate under the Supplemental Poverty Measure. The positioning goal: make "Abundance" the leading alternative to MAGA on the right and "The Groups" on the left by 2028 — but the brand must *not* outrun the ability to ship outcomes. Explicit analogy to the conservative movement's 16-year rebuild from Goldwater ('64) to Reagan ('80), with the California conservative faction of '67–'75 as the interstitial step. **"Premature scaling will kill it."** Honest limit Rosen concedes: they know how to change government *via regulation*, but have *no playbook yet* for modernizing human-capital-bound government delivery (police, education, transit, public health) — the hard, unsolved frontier.

## Assessment

Single-source and self-interested (a recruiting memo by a movement co-founder), so claims of efficacy and the percentage-complete figures are the author's own and uncorroborated here. Its durable value is the *framework*, not the scorekeeping: the policy-is-product inversion, the A/B/C gate, the three workback questions, the conjunctive four-layer model, and the permanent-federated-capital thesis are transferable mental tools for evaluating any reform-philanthropy pitch — and a sharp operational complement to the [[abundance-agenda]]'s policy-level argument and to [[political-economy-of-reform]]'s finding that veto points, not social returns, gate reform (the four-layer model is essentially a map of where those veto points sit in land use).
