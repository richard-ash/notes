# Ballot-qualified endorsing organizations

Christopher Elmendorf (UC Davis Law) and David Schleicher (Yale Law), as Elmendorf re-pitched in a May 2026 thread, propose **ballot-qualified endorsing organizations** as an electoral reform for state legislative elections — a structured information layer on the ballot itself that aims to recreate party-like competition in races where actual party labels have lost their disciplining function. They first sketched the idea around 2011.

## The problem they're solving

State legislative races sit in a low-information equilibrium:

1. **Voters know almost nothing about the candidates** beyond party label. Most don't engage at this level — small-donor energy and journalistic attention flow to presidential and congressional races, where the "social media stars" live.
2. **Candidates are desperate for name recognition**, which costs money. The hard money that fills the void comes from unions, business lobbies, and other organized groups that bundle donations and campaign labor.
3. **Party discipline ought to check this** — a legislator who hands the store to organized groups risks her party's majority. But that check is broken in modern American politics, because…
4. **State legislative seats matter mostly for congressional gerrymandering.** A Democrat in California who's furious about her state party's policy positions can't sensibly vote Republican for state legislature — it would put national House control at risk. The same trap holds in reverse for Republicans in Texas. So the within-party check on capture by organized groups disappears: voters can't credibly threaten to defect.

Elmendorf concludes that blue states like California need a way to **recreate party-like competition inside the dominant party**, without depending on a functional minority party to do it. The same logic runs in reverse for red states.

## The mechanism

Combine three pieces:

1. **Top-2 or top-4 nonpartisan primary** (California-style — already in place).
2. **A small ballot-qualified endorser slate.** Per cycle, the state certifies **3 or 4** organizations as ballot-qualified endorsers. Each is entitled to label *one* candidate per race with its brand, directly on the ballot.
3. **Voter petition determines who qualifies.** Orgs compete to get themselves ballot-qualified either by collecting signatures or — Elmendorf's preference — through a Secretary of State online portal where any voter can register their endorser preference. The top 3 or 4 at a cutoff date are certified.

On the ballot, an **"Endorsed by: ___"** line replaces the occupational description currently printed under each candidate's name — structurally parallel to the existing **"Party Preference: ___"** line.

## Why the design choices

**Why so few (3 or 4) endorsers?**
- Cognitive load: voters can't memorize what 20 acronyms stand for.
- It forces organizations toward **issue-bridging brands** rather than narrow single-issue identities. Elmendorf cross-references his Niskanen Center essay urging YIMBYism to become a broader pro-abundance coalition rather than stay a single-issue movement.

**Why voter petition rather than money or party leadership?**
- The interesting screening property: money alone can't buy you in. To become ballot-qualified, an organization has to persuade ordinary voters to actively *ask for* its endorsements on the ballot. Elmendorf's bet: voters won't petition for an org that doesn't truly stand for something.
- This is mechanism design via **demand-side filtering** — different from the *supply-side* filters (campaign-finance disclosure, lobbyist registration) that dominate American election law. The proposal moves the regulatory gate from "what can the group spend?" to "is the group meaningful enough that voters want its label on the ballot?"

**Why on the ballot rather than just in voter guides?**
- The ballot is the only document essentially every voter sees in the booth. Voter guides and endorsement scorecards already exist; the ones that get used are typically the unions' and lobbies' own slate cards. Moving the most credible signals into the ballot itself collapses the asymmetry.

## What it would achieve

Elmendorf claims three payoffs:

1. **Name-recognition path that doesn't go through organized-group capture.** A candidate can become a real player by attracting an endorsement from a popular ballot-qualified org, without owing the org's bundlers anything.
2. **Within-party policy differentiation.** In a top-2 race between two Democrats, the "Endorsed by:" line lets a low-information voter make a meaningful choice on substance.
3. **Faction transparency for accountability.** Engaged voters and journalists can read off which faction controls which legislator — useful both for legislative bargaining and for retrospective accountability.

## Risks Elmendorf flags

- **Heuristic projection.** Broockman, Kaufman & Lenz's finding that voters often wrongly infer from *any* endorsement that the candidate shares their views. Mitigation: keep the slate small and its membership reasonably stable over time, so voters learn what labels signify — but this is in tension with the open-petition design that lets new orgs displace incumbents.
- **Adverse selection on endorser identity.** The same bundling groups that dominate today will try to qualify as endorsers — and some will succeed. Elmendorf's answer is that they'd succeed only by becoming credibly *substantive* to a voting public, which is itself a constraint on what they could be.

## What he's no longer pitching

His original version with Schleicher also envisioned **governors and mayors** as natural ballot-qualified endorsers, on the grounds that they have well-known personal brands. He's now pessimistic on this:

- Governors angling for higher office have political incentives **not to endorse**, leaving the cue mute exactly when it matters.
- Putting chief-executive endorsements on the ballot is **one-sided** — incumbents get the cue, challengers don't. The open-petition design is neutral between incumbent and challenger factions, which makes it the better fit.

## Why this matters beyond California

The proposal targets a structural failure that the [[abundance-agenda]] discussion has so far underspecified — the *who arbitrates between coalitions* problem inside a dominant party. Klein and Thompson argue that institutions are not the point, delivery is; but delivery in a one-party state ultimately runs through *which faction of the dominant party* controls the legislative agenda, and faction-level competition is precisely what's missing when the only voter signal in the booth is the party label everyone in the race already shares. Ballot-qualified endorsers are one concrete answer to "voice but no veto" arbitration at the legislator-selection stage rather than at the project-approval stage.

In the framing of [[political-economy-of-reform]] (Djankov–Glaeser–Shleifer), this is a **structural** reform that creates new political *capacity* rather than redistributing existing rents — the orgs that currently bundle money lose nothing tangible (they can still spend), but their information monopoly over low-attention voters is broken by a competing signal that arrives at the ballot itself. The reform is unusual in that the explicit losers (organized groups) are *not compensated*; the mechanism instead bets that those groups can re-enter the new game on different terms by becoming ballot-qualified endorsers themselves, transmuting their organizational capacity into voter-petition mobilization. Whether that conversion works is the empirical question the proposal cannot answer in advance.

## Open questions Elmendorf doesn't resolve

- **What stops endorsement laundering?** Org A is a credible brand voters petition for. Org A endorses Candidate X who is actually the preferred candidate of opaque Bundler B. The "Endorsed by: A" line on the ballot doesn't disclose B's role. The proposal would presumably need disclosure rules layered on top.
- **What's the marginal voter actually doing in the booth?** The mechanism's payoff depends on the voter glancing at "Endorsed by: ___" and updating. Empirical work on California's ballot-printed party-preference label, occupation descriptors, and voter guide endorsements would constrain how big the effect could be.
- **Federalism / constitutional questions** about state-mandated ballot speech for private organizations get a footnote at best — Elmendorf is a constitutional law professor and presumably has answers, but they aren't in the thread.
