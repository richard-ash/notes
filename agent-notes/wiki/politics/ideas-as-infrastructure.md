# Ideas as Infrastructure

The classical liberal ideas that underpin free markets, innovation, and open societies — free trade, rule of law, property rights, limited government, individual liberty — are not self-sustaining constants. They are infrastructure that requires active intellectual maintenance and investment, just like roads or electricity grids. When that maintenance lapses, the ideas decay, and the prosperity they enabled becomes fragile.

This is the central thesis Rajagopalan advances in her 2025 essay "Who maintains the scaffolding of freedom?" — that business leaders, especially in tech, confused the game (building their companies) with the rules of the game (the institutional and intellectual scaffolding that made building possible in the first place).

## The decay mechanism

Rajagopalan argues the erosion happened through a specific sequence:

1. **The golden period (1980s–2008).** Global trade expanded, skilled immigration got easier, regulatory interference was manageable. The biggest constraint on ambitious builders was their own capability, not the political environment. Entrepreneurs could safely ignore politics.

2. **The intellectual ecosystem atrophied.** Universities moved away from classical liberal values. Think tanks got captured by partisan warfare. The case for free trade, which seemed self-evident in 2000, stopped being defended to people who hadn't lived through the alternative. The mid-century foundations that had funded scholars like [[use-of-knowledge-in-society|Hayek]] and Friedman — the Volker Fund, the Bradley Foundation, the Olin Foundation, the Earhart Foundation, the Scaife Foundation — had no adequate successors.

3. **Successive crises eroded public faith.** Deindustrialization gutted communities in the Midwest and Northern England. The 2008 financial collapse hollowed out entire towns. COVID-19 exposed the brittleness of globalized supply chains. For younger generations, housing costs in startup cities and gig work replacing careers made the market look like a rigged game.

4. **Business leaders finally noticed — and made it worse.** When they recognized that politics mattered, they approached it like a startup: fast, tactical, improvisational. They chased personalities, reacted to news cycles, and lobbied for firm-specific exemptions rather than defending the principles themselves. They mistook firefighting for fireproofing.

## The measurability trap

Rajagopalan identifies a deeper structural reason for the underinvestment: the data revolution. As companies got better at measuring everything — attribution-driven marketing, KPI-focused operations — they developed a mental model that everything worth doing should be measurable.

This framework worked for philanthropy in domains with clear outcomes: medical research, STEM education, environmental initiatives. But it systematically excluded the intellectual infrastructure of liberal democracy, where you cannot draw a straight line from funding a political philosopher to protecting property rights, and where the benefits are diffuse, long-term, and hard to attribute.

The result was a dangerous asymmetry. Business leaders applied venture capital thinking (scalable, measurable impact) to civic engagement, while their ideological opponents invested in narrative, talent identification, and long-term cultural change — things that are hard to measure but ultimately more powerful.

## The historical precedent

Rajagopalan points out that this problem has been solved before. In the post-WWII era, when communism seemed ascendant and Western democracies were reeling from stagflation and industrial decline, a small group of business leaders and philanthropists funded the intellectual ecosystem that nurtured Friedman, Hayek, and the Chicago school. Key foundations:

- **Volker Fund** — seeded mid-century classical liberal scholarship
- **Bradley Foundation** — built networks of think tanks and policy shops
- **Olin Foundation** — invested in law and economics programs reinforcing rule of law
- **Earhart Foundation** — nurtured individual academics in property rights traditions
- **Scaife Foundation** — underwrote skepticism of centralized power
- **Templeton Foundation** — supported inquiry into the moral conditions sustaining liberal society

These were long-term, patient investments in ideas — not projects that would immediately produce more engineers or a smaller chip. The byproducts (decades of innovation and prosperity) were the result of getting the foundations right.

## The call to action

Rajagopalan's prescription: fund talented individuals who understand both markets and policy. Support networks connecting people across government, academia, think tanks, and business. Create fora where policymakers learn from practitioners. Shift from asking "What is the ROI of this grant in twelve months?" to "What foundations will still be standing in fifty years because of this investment?"

She cites Patrick Collison and Tyler Cowen's "progress studies" movement as one modern equivalent — a movement that studies the combination of economic, technological, scientific, cultural, and organizational advances that have improved standards of living, with the goal of understanding how to sustain them.
