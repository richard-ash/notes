# The Abundance Agenda

A political-policy movement in the U.S. centre-left arguing that progressive ends — affordable housing, clean energy, functional public services — are blocked not primarily by lack of will or money but by accumulated procedural rules, veto points, and a post-1960s liberal ethos that prizes input and process over delivery. Named after Klein and Thompson's 2025 book *Abundance*, the movement is closely paralleled by Marc Dunkelman's *Why Nothing Works*, which provides the historical framing.

## The thesis in brief

The core claim has three layers:

1. **Diagnostic**: Blue states and cities can't build housing, transmission lines, transit, or large public projects fast enough. The bottlenecks are largely procedural — zoning, permitting, environmental review, multiple veto points, lawsuit-driven delay — and many were enacted by progressives in the 1960s–70s in reaction to the abuses of mid-century centralized power (Robert Moses, urban renewal, highway clearance).
2. **Historical**: Per Dunkelman, progressivism from ~1900 to 1960 was *centralizing* — TVA, Federal Reserve, Levittowns, the Bureau of Reclamation building dams across the West. After C. Wright Mills's *Power Elite*, the Port Huron Statement, *Silent Spring*, and Caro's *The Power Broker*, progressivism flipped to "speaking truth to power" and pushing decision-making *down* — to neighborhoods, citizen review, lawsuits. Abundance is the first serious attempt since then to revisit the trade-off.
3. **Prescriptive**: The progressive coalition needs a state that can *deliver*, not just regulate. Build more — housing, clean energy, infrastructure, drug approvals — and let the politics of "things working" rebuild faith in government.

## One-year scorecard (Klein/Thompson, April 2026)

Thompson grades the movement on three levels:

- **Vibes** — A+. Newsom on Kimmel literally quotes "a liberalism that builds." Hochul, Pritzker, Mamdani, Sherrill all frame supply-side policy as the answer to housing and energy crises. "Supply side" has displaced "regulate / redistribute" as the default Democratic rhetorical move on these issues.
- **Legislation** — B/B+. California passed the "Abundant and Affordable Homes Near Transit Act"; CEQA was meaningfully reformed in 2025; multiple state-level YIMBY bills. But these come on top of a decade of California housing bills, many billed as "transformative," that didn't move outcomes.
- **Outcomes** — failing. California housing starts haven't budged since the book; the YIMBY movement isn't visible in the FRED data over a 10-year horizon either. Klein and Thompson explicitly conceded their own metric: judge political movements by their outcomes.

Klein himself surfaces a different worry: the **"small-bore" failure mode** — abundance becoming a synonym for "efficiency" or "cutting red tape," a rebranded state-capacity agenda without a positive vision of what abundance is *for*. No 2028 candidate has yet articulated what an abundant future feels like at the level of voter experience.

## The financing/cost critique they conceded

Klein's framework after the tour: housing in America has a **50-year story** (rules — zoning, permitting, NEPA-style review accumulated since the 60s/70s), a **20-year story** (post-2008 the construction industry was decimated; 2010s saw the fewest houses-per-capita of any decade on record), and a **5-year story** (post-pandemic interest rates, inflation, deportation-driven labor scarcity — ~40% of California construction workers are foreign-born).

The book mostly told the 50-year story. Klein concedes a fair critique: even with zoning fixed, projects don't pencil out at current rates and labor costs. Mayors say "we made it legal but the financing doesn't work." Future abundance writing needs to take financing as seriously as it took permitting. (Background: see [[commercial-zoning-costs]] for the welfare-economics evidence on the *upper* bound of what zoning reform alone can deliver — 3–9% output gains from moderate deregulation.)

## The Texas natural experiment

Thompson's headline empirical case for the supply-side framing: **Dallas added a population the size of urban Boston between 2019 and the early 2020s, builds more housing per capita than any other U.S. metro, and home prices have *declined* over the last 3.5 years.** Austin built heavily in the 2010s/early 2020s and rents have fallen for 18–24 months. Texas has billionaires and corporate interests too; what it lacks is the procedural overhang. This is invoked as the cleanest available rebuttal to "housing is a special good immune to supply and demand" and to corporate-power explanations of high blue-state prices.

## Who decides? — the unresolved problem

Dunkelman's central critique of the movement *including his own book*: nobody — neither the populist left nor abundance liberals — has articulated **how a strong state actually arbitrates real trade-offs** between fishermen, ratepayers, neighborhood opponents, environmental groups, and developers when siting a transmission line or offshore wind farm.

The aspiration is "everyone has a voice, no one has a veto, decisions are expeditious and not subject to endless litigation." But the current legal architecture (NEPA, the Administrative Procedures Act, state-level analogs, citizen-suit provisions) was built to do the opposite, and gutting it requires statutory change — slow, filibusterable, and politically thankless. Doge under Trump showed by counter-example that you can move fast by *ignoring* the law, but lose in court and end up unwinding everything ($166B in tariff refunds being processed). A "good Doge" in 2029 has to do this *legally*, which is much harder.

Klein's reframe: this is also a **personnel** problem, not just a rules problem. The Biden administration's failure to spend most of the bipartisan-infrastructure-bill rural broadband and EV-charger money was largely staff coming up with self-imposed delay beyond what statute required. Bring in people who *want* to move fast and find legal ways to do so.

This problem connects to the political-economy-of-reform literature — see [[political-economy-of-reform]] for Djankov–Glaeser–Shleifer's empirical finding that veto points (`N` in their model) are the binding constraint on regulatory reform, and that reforms succeed where compensation efficiency and political capacity exist, not just where social returns are high.

## The procedural fetish (Bagley) and the legitimacy split

Nick Bagley's term "procedure fetish" describes how the Democratic legal mind locates legitimacy in *whether the rules were followed*, while most voters locate legitimacy in *whether the thing got delivered*. Klein extends this: lawyers populate the Democratic Party; lawyers think procedurally; the result is that Democrats internalized "input as a costless virtue" — long comment periods, Tuesday-night city council meetings as the highest expression of democracy, NEPA reviews as a substitute for legislative deliberation.

The abundance position: input is **not** costless. Delay is *itself* corrosive of democratic legitimacy because voters can't feel government in their lives. Mamdani's applause line "time is money" — from a democratic socialist — is the marker that this view has crossed factional lines.

## Power: the populist critique and Klein's response

Warren-style populist critique (steelmanned by Klein):

1. Abundance underweights **corporate power and money in politics**. Billionaires fund the movement and could use it to push Democrats toward business-friendly policy.
2. Abundance "isn't focused on the right enemies."

Thompson's response: the *theory of power* in abundance is liberal, not populist — power can concentrate badly anywhere (corporations, government, public-sector unions, neighborhoods), and there is no safe form of concentration. The neighborhood lady whose horses might be spooked by construction noise is exercising power if she stops the apartment building. The 4×-European union staffing levels that drive NYC's $X-billion-per-mile tunnel costs are an exercise of power. Treating only corporate concentration as power misses the modal blockers in housing, transmission, and transit cases.

Klein's partial concession: a hypothetical Chapter 7 on abundance and money in politics is warranted not because corporate power blocks the typical project, but because **the more powerful you make government, the more dangerous it becomes if the system that staffs and funds it is porous to donor capture**. Abundance is structurally vulnerable to billionaire instrumentalization in a way the book didn't grapple with.

The proposed synthesis ("abundance mullet"): **economic populism in the front, abundance in the back**. Mamdani: rent freeze + 18-to-10-month permitting acceleration. Sherrill: utility price caps + supply-side build-out of solar and storage in NJ. Blue Rose polling found the highest-testing message was the synthesis: "working Americans can't afford the basics because we stopped building them."

## AI, technology, and the "progressive politics of technology"

Klein flags this as the place abundance is going *backwards*. The book's back half tried to outline a center-left politics that is pro-technology — solar-punky, vertical-farm-and-rewilding-coded, building as a moral project. But:

- AI is increasingly read by the public, including by left-leaning audiences, as an *elite project being shoved down throats*, with concentrating effects (Musk, Altman, Amodei) rather than distributive ones. Anthropic and OpenAI are mostly private, so retail investors are excluded from the upside.
- GLP-1s — arguably the most popular drug class in 30 years and potentially transformative for cardiovascular, neurodegenerative, and inflammatory disease — are received with ambivalent or hostile coverage on the left despite enormous demand. (See [[glp1-drugs]].)

Thompson's prescription, adapted from the book's vignette:
1. **Tax frontier-AI profits and redistribute.** A multi-trillion-dollar industry built on aggregated human output has to be a fiscal subject, not just a private one.
2. **Distribute productivity gains as time, not unemployment.** A 4-day work week, not a 20%-displaced labor force. (Connects to [[task-automation-vs-paradigm-replacement]] on why automation within paradigms is rarely employment-destroying when policy chooses otherwise.)
3. **Operation Warp Speed for adjacent technologies** — GLP-1s are the obvious candidate; clinical-trial abundance via AI-assisted trial design is another.

Klein adds the Putnam framing (from *Bowling Alone* / "The Antisocial Century"): too often Americans adopt a technology and absorb *its* values without screening them against *theirs*. Allowing data centers to displace residential land in Wall-Street-Journal-reported zip codes is "AI's values supplanting people's values" — abundance that's not pro-housing isn't abundance.

## Energy: affordability vs. vision

Klein draws a hard distinction between two stances:

- **"Energy affordability"** — the current Democratic byword. Defensive, oriented to the present, framed as "people should be able to pay their utility bill."
- **"Clean energy abundance"** — a vision of dramatically more energy per capita, enabling vertical farming, mass desalination, climate adaptation, exported to poorer countries. A different *world*, not a smaller bill.

Trump's 2025–26 governance has made the affordability case worse on both sides: tariffs on transformer inputs are driving up grid hardware costs (a much bigger driver of rising electricity prices than data-center demand, per energy analysts), and the IRA wind/solar credits have been gutted. But the "absolute disaster" of 2026 energy policy is also instructive — it shows the supply-side opening clearly.

The unresolved tension Dunkelman returns to: the cost revolution in solar and storage is real, but **the binding constraint is transmission**, and transmission lines have to physically pass through *someone's* backyard or forest. His 10-year Maine transmission saga — proposed 2016, built 2026, capable of replacing ~700,000 cars' worth of fossil generation with Canadian hydro — is the canonical case, and not a corporate-power story.

## Institutions vs. delivery (Klein's reframe)

Klein's clearest formulation, articulated for the first time in the conversation:

> *"The institutions are not the point of government. Delivery is the point of government. The point of the institutions is to deliver. If they are not delivering, the institutions are not the thing we defend; the institutions are the thing we upend."*

This requires the Democratic coalition — disproportionately people for whom institutions (schools, courts, agencies, professions) have *worked* — to become much more comfortable with institutional failure as a normal condition needing aggressive remediation, rather than treating institutional defense as identity. The NIH framing exemplifies this:

- **Establishment liberal**: NIH is a $40B jewel; criticism is illegitimate.
- **MAGA**: NIH is captured; cut grants, ban mRNA.
- **Abundance liberal**: NIH-funded scientists report spending up to 40% of their time on paperwork. That is institutional failure. Fix it without destroying the institution.

## The Sanders distinction (and Thompson's pushback)

Bernie Sanders, asked about abundance, drew this line: *"Horrendous bureaucracy is real and terrible — but that's not an ideology, that's common sense. Ideology is taking on the billionaire class."* Sanders himself spent years trying to build two community health clinics in Vermont under ACA funding from 2010 — one is still not built in 2026.

Klein's argument: the inability to build that clinic *is* the question Sanders calls ideology. If you can't deliver the working-class outcomes, the ideology can't hold its coalition. **"Common sense good government" is the precondition for "ideology" to be electorally viable**, not a separate concern. Thompson, more bluntly: the problem isn't a shortage of ideologies, it's a shortage of common-sense governance.

Klein's final synthesis: there are two abundances, and both matter.

- **Abundance as efficiency** — fix what's broken in delivery. The "everyone agrees once you describe it" version (40% paperwork, 18-to-10-month permitting, infrastructure money that doesn't get spent).
- **Abundance as vision** — a world in which a San Francisco firefighter can afford a home in San Francisco; high-speed rail; clean-energy wealth on a scale that changes how life feels. As radical in those cities as Medicare for All is nationally.

The movement's risk in 2026 is collapsing into the first kind only.
