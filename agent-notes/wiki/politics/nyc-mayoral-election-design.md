# NYC mayoral election design

Gaurav Kapadia argues that the chronic weakness of New York City mayoral candidates is downstream of *electoral design choices*, not the talent pool. Three structural features — off-year scheduling, late-June primaries, and closed party primaries — combine to produce an election decided by tens of thousands of votes in a city of 8 million. That outcome is so unusual it deters serious candidates from running and rewards the candidates best at niche organizing rather than broad governance.

## The structural problem in three parts

1. **Off-year election.** NYC holds its mayoral election in odd-numbered years, decoupled from the presidential and midterm cycles. There is a historical reason for the shift, but the consequence is a vastly thinner electorate than would otherwise show up.

2. **June primary, third week.** The Democratic primary lands in the third week of June, after most private schools have let out for summer. Turnout is *very* low. Because NYC is so dominantly Democratic, winning the Democratic primary is effectively winning the mayoralty.

3. **Closed primary.** Only registered Democrats vote in the deciding contest. Independents and members of other parties are filtered out before the structurally decisive moment.

Stacked together, these mean — as Kapadia puts it — "you're able to win the Democratic nomination and therefore the mayoral election with tens of thousands of votes in a city this big. That is absolutely insane."

## Why this depresses candidate quality

The thin electorate creates two compounding effects that select against the candidates Kapadia would want to see:

- **Self-deselection.** Strong potential candidates assume they can't win because NYC is "such a big and great city" — they overestimate the actual barrier and never enter. Recent presidential elections and the current mayoral race have updated the priors: "anyone could win" is now the visible lesson, which Kapadia expects to draw more candidates over time. The Bayesian update is happening — but slowly.

- **One-candidate-locks-it convergence.** In the cycle Kapadia is discussing (the 2025 NYC mayoral race), the conventional wisdom was that one candidate had it locked. Other strong candidates Kapadia personally knew declined to run on that basis. The conventional wisdom turned out to be wrong, but the field was already thinned by the time it became clear.

The structural feature that makes both worse: small electorates mean a candidate who is intensely beloved by 30,000 organized partisans can beat a candidate who is broadly acceptable to 1.5 million New Yorkers who don't show up to a June Democratic primary. The system optimizes for niche enthusiasm rather than governing breadth.

## Kapadia's three proposed fixes

1. **Move the election on-cycle** to even-numbered years, so it ride the presidential and midterm electorates. This is the largest turnout lever.
2. **Move the primary** out of late June to a more normal time of year when school is in session and people are paying attention.
3. **Open the primary** so independents and non-Democrats can vote, expanding the relevant electorate beyond party registrants.

None of these are individually exotic; they are routine features of election systems elsewhere. The cumulative effect is to make the deciding vote a citywide one rather than a partisan-base one.

## What Kapadia is looking for once the field is healthy

His "non-obvious quality" for a mayoral candidate is **optimism** — and he argues it is "extraordinarily scarce." The standard pre-qualifying traits (ability to govern a complicated city, manage many constituents) are necessary but not sufficient. The deciding factor, in his view, is the ability to inspire, and almost all candidates over recent cycles have lacked it, with one exception.

Kapadia's choice for most-underrated NYC mayor is Mike Bloomberg "by a country mile" — popularity quite low in the public memory but impact extremely high, with the long-term initiatives Bloomberg launched now taken for granted as if they would have shown up anyway.

## Where this fits in election-design discourse

The proposal sits alongside several adjacent fixes for low-information / low-turnout elections:

- [[ballot-qualified-endorsers]] — Elmendorf & Schleicher's proposal to certify 3–4 organizations per cycle as ballot-qualified endorsers labeling candidates directly on the ballot. Addresses the *information* problem in low-salience races.
- [[proportional-representation]] — Drutman's case for multi-member-district open-list PR. Addresses the *representation* problem in single-winner gerrymanderable districts.
- [[abundance-agenda]] — Klein/Thompson/Dunkelman's diagnosis that delivery (not institutions) is the point. Kapadia's "ability to inspire" criterion is the candidate-level version of this; the structural fixes are the upstream version.

The Kapadia angle is narrower than any of these — he's not redesigning the institutional voting system, just rescheduling and opening up the existing one. But the underlying diagnosis is the same shape: when an electoral mechanism's effective electorate is much smaller than its formal one, candidate selection drifts away from what the broader public would choose.

## What about Robert Moses?

Tangential but related: Kapadia would push people to like Robert Moses *less* — not because the wielding-power master class wasn't real, but because the long shadow of process-bypassed infrastructure decisions is, fifty and a hundred years later, constraining what NYC can do now. He explicitly frames this as a balance: one person picking the city's taste is bad, but so is unlimited due-process gridlock. The current dial is too far toward the latter for buildable [[induced-demand|infrastructure]] decisions but should not slide all the way back.

The thread connecting Moses-skepticism to election-design is the same one: institutional choices that look fine at the moment of design accumulate distortions over decades. Off-year primaries and Moses-era highway routings are both load-bearing choices that should have been re-examined sooner than they were.
