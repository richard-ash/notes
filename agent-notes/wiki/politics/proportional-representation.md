# Proportional representation

Lee Drutman (New America; author of *Breaking the Two-Party Doom Loop*, 2020) argues for replacing single-member-district House elections with **proportional representation** as the structural fix for two interlocking American pathologies: the post-2019 escalation of gerrymandering into "maximum warfare everywhere all the time," and the collapse of intra-party heterogeneity that traps US politics in a binary demonization spiral. The May 2026 Ezra Klein interview is the most concise live statement of his case.

## The diagnosis: gerrymandering is now a one-way ratchet

Two 2019 and 2026 Supreme Court decisions removed both the federal-constitutional and the statutory ceilings on partisan map-drawing:

- **Rucho v. Common Cause** (2019) — partisan gerrymandering is non-justiciable; the Court will not find a workable standard. Federal courts step out.
- **Calala / Klay** (2026, Drutman's transcript renders the name inconsistently) — gut Section 2 of the Voting Rights Act. Racial gerrymandering now provable only under a near-impossible intentionality standard ("unless you are wearing a KKK mask"); the case was argued as redress for *white* voters allegedly disenfranchised by majority-minority districts.

With both ceilings gone, the equilibrium is the one Hakeem Jeffries calls "maximum warfare." Drutman's roll-call of the 2026 redistricting wars:

- Trump pushes Texas into a mid-cycle redraw → +5 R seats.
- California holds a ballot initiative to suspend its independent commission and counter-gerrymander → +D seats.
- Virginia passes its own override; state court invalidates on a procedural ground; the US Supreme Court will rule.
- Indiana Republicans decline to redraw; Florida (DeSantis) goes from 20–8 R to 24–4 R; South Carolina 7–0 R; Tennessee all-R (Memphis seat eliminated); Louisiana 5–1 R; Mississippi headed for 4–0 R; Alabama uncertain.
- As many as **19 majority-minority Democratic districts** across the South are at risk.

Net 2026 cost to Democrats: **~7–10 seats** depending on the model. The forecast is that this does not stop in 2026 — without intervention, the ratchet continues through 2028 and the post-2030 redistricting.

The downstream tension Drutman flags is **inside** the Democratic coalition: with the VRA dead, the path to maximum counter-gerrymander in blue states runs through breaking up majority-minority districts, which trades minority representation for partisan seat count. Hakeem Jeffries and others have signaled openness to this.

Competitive House seats: ~50 cycles ago, **15** projected in November 2026 out of 435. Gerrymandering explains part; partisan geographic sorting explains the rest.

## The "dummymander" — why this could backfire

The gerrymandering party faces a portfolio choice: draw fewer extremely-safe (60-40) seats, or spread voters across more 53-47 or 55-45 seats. Florida 2026 chose the aggressive version. In a bad year, all the 55-45 seats can flip together — what's known as a *dummymander*. Drutman thinks Florida Republicans overreached.

## The minimalist case for PR: end gerrymandering, re-enfranchise the minority party

The mechanism: replace single-member districts with **multi-member districts** of ~5–9 seats, allocate seats by party vote share via **open-list** ballots (voter picks a candidate from a party's list; their vote counts both for the candidate and for the party's overall share).

Two structural consequences:

1. **You can't gerrymander Louisiana into 6–0 R if Louisiana is a single 6-member district** — there are no lines to draw. In a state like California you still draw lines, but now a 5-member district where Republicans get 40% gives them 2 seats, not zero, so map-drawing's partisan leverage collapses.
2. **It re-enfranchises the structural minority in every state.** A Texas Democrat going from 0% representation to ~35% representation, and a California Republican going from ~5% representation to ~35%, both become voters worth chasing. Drutman flags this as his own primary reason for supporting PR.

Drutman also argues — based on comparative politics — that PR systems have systematically **higher voter turnout** because parties actively seek out underserved groups when every vote matters equally, and that **parties controlling their nominations elevate more diverse candidates** than American candidate-centric primaries, where the loudest and brashest advance.

## The maximalist case: breaking the two-party doom loop

The deeper argument echoes Klein's own *Why We're Polarized*: in 1965, the Voting Rights Act passed with cross-cutting coalitions (liberal Republicans + liberal Democrats vs. conservative Democrats + conservative Republicans). Today both parties have collapsed into ideologically pure, geographically nationalized brands.

Drutman quantifies the collapse via the **House/Senate-to-presidential-vote correlation**:

| Year | Correlation |
|------|-------------|
| 2000 | ~0.20 |
| 2024 | >0.90 (House: 0.98) |

Translation: in 2000, knowing how a state voted for president told you almost nothing about how it voted for Senate. Today it tells you essentially everything. Jim Jeffords (R-VT, ~70% in 2000), Lincoln Chafee (R-RI), Joe Manchin (D-WV), John Tester (D-MT) — the entire archetype of a candidate who outperforms their party brand by 6–8+ points is gone. The most a generational-talent candidate can squeeze out today caps near 6–8 points; the average is the party line.

The doom-loop mechanism: each side holds its heterogeneous coalition together by demonizing the other ("Whatever you think of me, Democrats are fascists / Republicans are evil"), which produces an electorate where ~half believe the other side winning is *illegitimate*, which licenses norm erosion ("they're evil, so whatever we do is justified"), which deepens the demonization. PR breaks the binary by giving disaffected voters in each coalition an exit — a place to vote against their nominal party without electing their existential enemy.

The Brazil/US comparison Drutman draws: Bolsonaro's coalition partners abandoned him after his coup attempt because they were a coalition of distinct parties, not a single tribe. American Republicans could not similarly defect after January 6 because the only available alternative was "help the Democrats."

## How PR would actually be voted

Drutman's preferred design is the **open-list party** system:

- Voter walks into the booth, sees each party's list of (e.g.) 8 candidates in an 8-member district, casts **one vote** for a specific named candidate on a specific party's list.
- That vote counts both for the candidate's personal ranking and for the party's total share.
- Top N candidates from each party (where N = party's proportional share of seats) go to Congress.

Klein presses on the candidate-selection question — who gets *on* the party list? Drutman's answer is conventions or party-member ballots, and that PR "obviates the need for a primary." Klein pushes back: this hands real power to party structures, and it matters enormously whether (e.g.) Brad Lander or Dan Goldman ends up the nominee. Drutman's reply: under PR with a multi-candidate slate, parties have an incentive to run a *balanced* slate (one progressive, one moderate, one bridge candidate) to maximize total vote share, which structurally moderates the slate even without a primary.

This is the weakest link in Drutman's pitch in the interview — Klein flags it explicitly and Drutman doesn't fully answer how the slate gets composed in a way that satisfies American voters' candidate-centric expectations.

## Global comparisons — what PR does and doesn't fix

Klein presses on four contemporary democracies that look unhealthy despite (or because of) their multi-party systems. Drutman's responses:

- **UK** (Labour 33% → 2/3 of seats; Reform potentially 27% → majority). Not PR — first-past-the-post with multi-party fragmentation, "the worst of both worlds." Confirms the case for PR rather than refutes it.
- **Israel** (Knesset = single 120-member district, 3.25% threshold, no constitution). **Too** proportional: 3.25% threshold + no design discipline produces ungovernable fragmentation. The lesson is design parameters matter (a 5–7% threshold would help), and PR alone is not sufficient — Israel lacks a constitution and faces unique external pressures.
- **Germany** (AfD surging, kept out of government). PR with sane thresholds is doing its job: the AfD has been forced into opposition; if they ever become coalition-essential they will have to moderate to govern.
- **Italy** (Meloni). Same story — her far-right party had to moderate to build a governing coalition.

Drutman's broader claim: in the alternative 2016 where Trump lost, a MAGA-as-distinct-party emerges in a PR system and competes openly rather than capturing the Republican Party from within. Klein remains skeptical that this is meaningfully more stable than the gatekeeper-dependent system the US relied on (and which failed in 2016 anyway).

## Senate / presidency: fusion voting as the adjacent mechanism

PR addresses the House only. For Senate, gubernatorial, and presidential races (single-winner by constitutional design), Drutman advocates **fusion voting** — the system still legal in New York where multiple parties can co-endorse a single candidate, with each party's line tallied separately. This lets a Michigan Progressive Party run a Senate candidate jointly with Democrats, build organizational strength via House races, and signal the coalition's composition (if the progressive line gets only 12% of the candidate's total, the candidate updates accordingly).

## The political path to passing it

PR can be done by **statute**, not constitutional amendment. The Uniform Congressional District Act of 1967 mandates single-member districts; Congress could amend it under Article I § 4 of the Elections Clause, which grants Congress broad authority over the manner of federal elections. Rep. Don Beyer has introduced such a bill; co-sponsorship is thin.

The incumbent-incentives problem: California Democrats voting for PR are voting to give up ~35% of their seats to Republicans. Drutman's structural answer: **expand the House** alongside adopting PR. The House has been frozen at 435 since 1911, while the country has more than tripled; current districts average **765,000 constituents** (far higher than any peer democracy). Drutman has co-written with the American Academy of Arts and Sciences proposing a **+150 seat expansion** (~one-third larger). With expansion, no incumbent has to lose their seat to adopt PR — and the math of proportional allocation works in more small states.

The political pitch Drutman makes to Democrats specifically:

1. **The 2030 cycle is going to be brutal** regardless — incumbent-party midterm losses + post-2030 redistricting under maximum-warfare rules. Better to lock in PR while a Democratic trifecta exists than to lose under the current rules anyway.
2. **The "Democratic party vs. Democratic coalition" frame.** Progressive, moderate, and blue-dog Democrats all chafe at being one party. PR lets each faction speak directly to its own voters — bold progressivism in cities, populist center-left in struggling areas, blue-dog conservatism in rural districts — instead of muddling everything into a single national brand that ends up satisfying no one.

The historical-cycles frame (from Drutman's book rec, Sam Huntington's *American Politics: The Promise of Disharmony*): the US reforms its political institutions roughly every 60 years — Revolution, Jacksonian Democracy, Progressive Era, 1960s — and is now due.

## How this relates to other reforms in the agent-notes corpus

- **[[ballot-qualified-endorsers]]** (Elmendorf & Schleicher). Both proposals address a structural failure of party competition. Elmendorf's diagnosis is *within-state-party* — in one-party states, the congressional-gerrymander incentive prevents voters from defecting against their own party at the state legislative level. His mechanism is a small slate of voter-petition-certified endorsing organizations that recreate signal *inside* the dominant party. Drutman's diagnosis is *cross-party* and at the federal level — single-member-district incentives prevent new parties from emerging at all. His mechanism replaces the structure that generates the two-party trap. The two are complements: Elmendorf works under existing constitutional architecture and addresses state legislatures; Drutman requires Congress to legislate but addresses the House. Both share the move of *creating new structured competition where the existing party label has lost its signaling function*.
- **[[political-economy-of-reform]]** (Djankov, Glaeser, Shleigfer). Drutman's path-to-passage analysis fits this frame: reform happens when (a) dissatisfaction is intense, (b) a specific alternative is on the shelf, and (c) incumbent costs are compensable. The House-expansion + PR bundle is engineered specifically to neutralize incumbent costs (no one loses their seat) — a Djankov-style structural reform that creates new political capacity without redistributing existing rents from any individual member.
- **[[abundance-agenda]]** (Klein/Thompson/Dunkelman). The "voice but no veto" arbitration problem in *Abundance* asks who decides between competing claimants inside a coalition. PR pushes part of that arbitration into the electorate by making the coalition's internal composition visible at the ballot: a Democratic Party that's 40% progressive / 40% moderate / 20% blue-dog can be read off the seat distribution rather than negotiated opaquely inside primary fights.

## What the interview undersells

- **The candidate-selection problem under list PR is real and unresolved.** Klein presses on this and Drutman's answer (parties have an incentive to balance their lists) is structural but not satisfying given American voters' deep candidate-centric expectations. Open-list at least lets voters pick the candidate; closed-list systems used in many European democracies do hand pure list-ordering power to party leadership.
- **The "PR fixes polarization" claim is sociologically loaded.** Drutman is right that the post-2000 collapse in candidate-level divergence (0.2 → 0.9 House/Senate-to-president correlation) is structurally tied to nationalized two-party competition. Whether PR mechanically *reverses* that — vs. requiring decades of new institutional habits — is unclear from comparative evidence. Germany has PR and is not immune to AfD-style polarization; the claim is only that PR makes the resulting politics more navigable.
- **The 2030-trifecta-window argument is contingent** on Democrats winning a trifecta they don't currently have, and on a Democratic Party willing to spend trifecta political capital on procedural reform rather than substantive policy. Both are uncertain. The Iowa Rob Sand example (Democrat running on "destroying the two-party system" in a deep-red state) is intriguing but a single data point.
