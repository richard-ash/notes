# Capitalism and Democracy

The standard public-sphere debate frames capitalism and democracy as in tension — either democracy will vote capitalism into the grave (Madison, Mill, Marx, Schumpeter, Caplan) or capitalism's winners will buy democracy's organs (Reich, Polanyi/Kuttner, Sen). Geloso and Tabarrok argue the data refute both: democracy and capitalism are highly correlated, grow together, and tend to form a stable equilibrium when both are present. The relationship is one of **mutual reinforcement** — both are "open-access orders" in the North/Wallis/Weingast sense, and they share growth, peace, and human-development mechanisms that knit them together.

## The three theses

### 1. "Democracy undermines capitalism"

The classical liberal worry from Locke through the Mills. Two flavors, often combined:
- **Cupidity** — voters with below-mean income vote to redistribute (Meltzer–Richard's median-voter formalization, 1981).
- **Stupidity** — voters are rationally ignorant or rationally irrational and vote against their own long-run interests (Caplan, *Myth of the Rational Voter*, 2008). Mill's earlier version: workers don't know that minimum wages, tariffs, and immigration restrictions hurt them.

Marx accepted the classical liberal *prediction* (democracy → socialism) but inverted the *valuation* — for him, democracy was "the first step in the revolution."

### 2. "Capitalism undermines democracy"

A more heterogeneous group:
- **Mild-redistributionists** (Sen, Nussbaum) extend liberty from "negative" (absence of legal barriers) to "positive" (capabilities to actually exercise it). The Great Gatsby curve operationalizes the worry: high inequality reduces intergenerational mobility, freezing class structure.
- **Reich** — capitalism's *winners* (corporations, billionaires) buy political influence. **Kuttner** (with Polanyi) — capitalism's *losers* vote for populist/fascist backlash. Either way, capitalism kills democracy.
- **Socialists** — same diagnosis, harsher remedy: state ownership of the means of production.
- **Conservatives** — capitalism's success erodes the family/church/community institutions that democratic citizenship requires (a Marx-inflected line that mainstream conservatives like George Will sometimes invoke).

### 3. "Capitalism and democracy are mutually reinforcing"

Held by Friedman, Mises, Hayek, and Berger. Mises: "Liberalism must necessarily demand democracy as its political corollary." Berger: he could find no lasting case of "a *political* democracy that has *not* been a market economy." Geloso and Tabarrok extend this thin theoretical tradition with empirics and a richer mechanism story.

## The empirical case

Operationalizing capitalism as the Fraser Institute's Economic Freedom of the World (EFW) index and democracy as V-Dem's liberal democracy index, the authors report:

- **Cross-section (2019)**: ρ = .64 between EFW and liberal democracy. No strong democracies have very low EFW (<5). The only countries with very high EFW (8+) that are not strong democracies are Singapore and Hong Kong, and that cluster shrinks further when EFW is corrected for gender disparity in property rights (Fike's correction).
- **All flavors of democracy** (liberal, participatory, deliberative, egalitarian) correlate similarly with EFW. The egalitarian–EFW correlation is .62 — i.e., even the *equality-emphasizing* concept of democracy is highly correlated with economic freedom.
- **Changes correlate with changes**: a one-standard-deviation increase in democracy is associated with a ~.37 standard deviation increase in EFW (significant at <1%).
- **The Berlin Wall shock** (Albania, Poland, Bulgaria, Hungary, Romania, plus Lithuania and Estonia): democracy increased first, then sustained EFW gains *under democratic regimes*. This refutes the popular claim that big EFW jumps require authoritarianism.
- **Persistence-of-history puzzle**: Alesina–Fuchs-Schündeln (2007) showed East German preferences persisted decades after reunification, which should have made post-communist countries *erode* economic freedom once enfranchised. They did the opposite.
- **The four largest EFW gains 1970–2020** were Peru, Nicaragua, Uganda, and Chile. Mixed regime cases:
  - Peru: EFW *fell* under military junta, *rose* under Fujimori, *stabilized at high levels under restored democracy*. Today's Peru has more EFW than at any point under any of its authoritarian governments since 1970.
  - Nicaragua: EFW collapsed under late Somoza and Sandinistas, rose under post-1990 democracy, collapsed again with Ortega's return in 2006. "Came full circle."
  - Uganda: EFW rose under Museveni (autocracy throughout) — a Hong Kong/Singapore-shaped exception.
  - Chile: the coup *initially reduced* EFW; the rise after 1975 continued and accelerated *after democracy was restored*. Pinochet did not raise Chilean EFW above its democratic-era level.

The pattern: big EFW gains can happen under democracy or autocracy, but autocratic gains typically start lower and end lower, and many are recoveries from prior autocratic destruction. "Anyone seeking economic freedom from behind a veil of ignorance would be well advised to select a democratic government."

## Mechanisms of mutual reinforcement

### Democracies don't want to kill the goose

Capitalism produces growth, and growth is the *only* way a large group can become rich. Redistribution can make a minority rich at the majority's expense, but cannot make the majority rich at a minority's expense. Therefore the only way the median voter can become wealthy is to vote for capitalism. Authoritarian regimes have the opposite incentive structure — Mobutu personally enriched himself by ruling one of the world's poorest countries, because under autocracy you don't need growth to extract wealth.

A capitalist democracy further benefits from a "division of labor": **democracy makes clear that the route to riches is not politics; capitalism makes clear that the route to riches is markets**. Pecuniary self-interest is channeled toward markets where the invisible hand can guide it socially, not toward the state where it produces rent-seeking, monopoly, and stasis. A socialist state has nowhere to channel pecuniary self-interest except toward itself.

### Democracies are a constraint on government — they avoid catastrophes

Democracy is not (per Arrow) an ideal *decision* mechanism — it's a catastrophe-avoidance mechanism. The argument echoes Popper, who saw democracy as a system of peaceful error-correction analogous to the scientific method, and Mises, who defined democracy as the form of government that adapts to the wishes of the governed without violent struggle.

The empirical record:
- **Famines**: Sen's claim that no famine has ever taken place in a functioning democracy is literally false but directionally true. Hunger deaths have fallen ~99% since the 1870s, yet the ten worst famines in *all of human history* are clustered in the same period — and all are byproducts of dictatorships, colonial regimes, or wars involving them. Only dictatorships do famines.
- **Mass murder**: in the twentieth century, hundreds of millions were killed by their own governments — Nazi, Soviet, Maoist, Cambodian, Turkish, North Korean. Democracies are dramatically less likely to murder their own citizens.
- **War**: democracies are less likely to fight each other (the democratic peace) and less likely to engage in civil wars.

These are catastrophe-avoidance properties — and stability is itself a precondition for capitalist accumulation.

### Capitalism prospers with peace and stability

Capitalist growth requires investment, and investment requires peace and stability. Mises: "There can be no lasting economic improvement if the peaceful course of action of affairs is continually interrupted." Even weapons manufacturers prefer domestic peace, since war brings expropriation, price controls, regulation, and possible destruction.

Rich economies are rich because they grew slowly and steadily for many decades, not because they had spurts of 10% growth. Democratic policy is more stable than autocratic policy: it reflects the median voter (lower variance than a single leader), it requires consensus and bargaining, and multi-veto institutions slow change. Slow change is sometimes bad but on net good for long-run growth.

This bears on authoritarian liberalizers. **A dictator's promise to maintain pro-market policies is rarely credible**; the same EFW reform may produce stronger long-run growth under a democracy than under a dictatorship because expectations about future institutions are more stable. Escalante (2024) finds a counterfactual Chile without Pinochet would have grown the same — and that *democratization* is what sped Chilean growth.

### Capitalism and democracy promote human development and social mobility

Economic freedom turns out to be highly correlated with the Human Development Index, life expectancy, education, health, environmental quality, social trust, and life satisfaction — undermining the Sen/Nussbaum worry that economic freedom without "capabilities" is hollow.

The raw correlation between economic freedom and the Gini coefficient is **negative**. More sophisticated analyses are mixed, but the social ills of inequality are demonstrably worse in *unfree* societies, where mobility is constrained. Recent work ties economic freedom to social mobility on net — rulers in unfree countries share rents with the already-rich to maintain power, so *liberalization* tends to send the gains downward, which legitimizes the regime.

### Democracy prospers with growth (creative destruction as politics-fluidizer)

In a zero-sum economy some can become richer only if others become poorer — fractious politics, civil-war potential. In a growing economy, politics can focus on positive-sum games. **Creative destruction** churns interest groups before they can entrench themselves — fewer cement-locked coalitions, more fluid politics. Bjørnskov found that across 212 crises (1993–2010), more economically free societies suffered shallower downturns and faster recoveries, which means capitalism *protects* democracy by minimizing the pressure on politicians to adopt corrosive measures during shocks.

### Doux commerce and doux politique

Montesquieu, Paine, Kant, Cobden, Angell — all argued that commerce moderates manners and reduces war. Aphorism: "When goods don't cross borders, armies will." McDonald and Gartzke find that the **capitalist peace** hypothesis runs ahead of the democratic peace when raced head-to-head — though the authors think the variables are too entangled to distinguish.

Voltaire's London Stock Exchange passage captures the mechanism: market participation forces parties of different identities into reciprocal recognition. Modern experimental evidence (Henrich et al.) confirms that exposure to markets makes people act more pro-socially, altruistically, and universally — the same dispositions democracy requires for compromise. Since most people interact with markets more than with politics, *doux commerce* probably precedes and generates *douce politique*.

## Why socialism is the regime that *is* incompatible with democracy

Democracy is consistent with capitalism. But it is *not* consistent with socialism, defined as extensive state ownership of the means of production. The argument is Dahl's:

> A centrally planned economy issues an outright invitation to government leaders, written in bold letters: **You are free to use all these economic resources to consolidate and maintain your power!**

In a socialist state there is no distinction between politics and economics. So Popper's peaceful-rotation-of-power equilibrium breaks down: every electoral loss carries the threat that the winners will use the apparatus of the economy to dispossess and entrench. Losers therefore have reason not to cede power today. Socialist democracies are bound to be unstable — the regimes degenerate into either authoritarianism or back into market economies.

This implication matters because it relocates the "compatibility" debate. Capitalism and democracy are not in tension; **socialism and democracy are**. The classical liberal worry was about the wrong incompatibility.

## Where mutual reinforcement might fail

The authors are careful to say these are tendencies, not laws. Voters could vote for extreme redistribution and have, in some times and places. Yet income and wealth inequality are *not* notably lower in democracies than in non-democracies, and the most extreme wealth redistributions (e.g., land redistributions) tend to occur in *non-democracies*.

The mechanism the literature underestimates is **ideology**. The Buchanan-via-*Calculus-of-Consent* line: inequality under a constitutional order in which the rules of the game are seen as fair doesn't generate redistributive tension, because the rules are *ipso facto* seen as fair. Liberal democratic ideologies and justifications shield both capitalism and democracy from the extreme ideologies of left and right. Both the pro-capitalism *and* pro-socialism camps in the "democracy undermines capitalism" school assumed a mechanical inequality → redistribution function that is neither evident in the data nor required by theory.

This makes the maintenance of those liberal democratic ideologies non-negotiable — which connects to [[ideas-as-infrastructure|Rajagopalan's argument]] that classical liberal ideas are infrastructure requiring active intellectual maintenance, not constants. The Geloso/Tabarrok empirical case for mutual reinforcement gives the Rajagopalan argument its operational stakes: the equilibrium is stable *only as long as the legitimating ideas hold*.

## Connections to related concepts

- The mechanism that voters in democracies want growth and that growth requires capitalism rests on Hayek's [[use-of-knowledge-in-society|argument that dispersed knowledge cannot be centralized]] and the price system is the only way to coordinate it. Socialist democracy fails on both Dahl's political grounds *and* Hayek's epistemic grounds — the planning state can neither resist consolidating power nor compute what to do with it.
- The "capitalism is an open-access order" framing is North/Wallis/Weingast — the same conceptual lineage as Schumpeter's [[entrepreneurial-profit|entrepreneurial-profit theory of capitalism]], in which creative destruction is the mechanism that prevents both economic and political incumbency.
- Tabarrok's case here pairs naturally with his work on the [[copyright-and-innovation|Tabarrok Curve]] — both argue that the legitimacy of capitalist institutions depends on whether they actually deliver to the broad public rather than being captured by rent-extracting incumbents.
- The capitalist-peace empirics support and complicate the [[liberal-international-order|liberal international order]] literature: trade integration may matter more than democratic peer-status for explaining inter-state peace.
- The empirical case that *socialist democracies are unstable* connects to [[why-russia-lost-the-cold-war|the Soviet Union's collapse]] — the regime's central economic apparatus made every political loss too costly to accept, even before its productive failure became fatal.

## What the chapter does and doesn't do

The chapter is empirically careful (acknowledging that EFW data have weaknesses, that correlation isn't causation, that big regime changes are confounded by recovery dynamics). What it doesn't do:
- It doesn't establish causation in either direction. The authors are explicit that they don't try.
- It doesn't address the **ideology-shift question** seriously: *why* do voters in mature capitalist democracies sometimes turn against the system? The Buchanan answer — perceived fairness of the rules — is gestured at but not unpacked.
- It accepts EFW and V-Dem as adequate operationalizations. Both indexes have well-known critiques (EFW underweights gender; V-Dem subjective-coder issues).
- It doesn't grapple with the modern populist moment as data — the empirical sample largely ends in 2020, before the post-COVID inflation/migration backlash whose democratic-stability implications are still unfolding.

The argument is therefore best read as a strong rebuttal to the *static* claim of incompatibility, not as a forecast that capitalist democracies are guaranteed to persist. As the authors close: "voters must choose capitalism."
