# Public Speaking

Patrick Winston's MIT lecture *How to Speak* (delivered annually for ~40 years through 2018) is the canonical English-language reference on giving talks in technical and academic settings. The thesis is that speaking quality is largely a matter of **knowledge and practice**, not innate talent — Winston's formula is `quality = K + P + t`, with t (talent) deliberately small. The implication: speaking is a learnable craft, and watching effective speakers to extract their techniques into a personal repertoire is the path to competence.

## Why speaking matters

Winston opens with the claim that career success is determined by **(1) ability to speak, (2) ability to write, (3) quality of ideas — in that order**. The ordering is deliberately provocative: ideas matter least because ideas without packaging never reach the audience that could act on them. This echoes Hamming in [[you-and-your-research]], who argues that "selling" your work — formal talks, written reports, informal presentations — is at least 50% of the job and that good ideas are routinely lost because they were poorly presented the first time.

The framing Winston uses: **"Your ideas are like your children. You don't want them to go into the world in rags."** Packaging is therefore a moral obligation to the ideas themselves, not vanity.

## How to start: the empowerment promise

Do **not** open with a joke. The audience is still settling, adjusting to your vocal parameters, putting laptops away — they're not ready to laugh. Jokes told cold fall flat.

Open instead with an **empowerment promise**: tell people what they will know at the end that they don't know now. Winston's model: *"By the end of the next 60 minutes, you'll have been exposed to a lot of ideas, some of which you'll incorporate into your own repertoire, and they will ensure that you get the maximum opportunity to have your ideas valued and accepted by the people you speak with."*

The promise serves three functions: it gives the audience a reason to stay engaged, it commits the speaker to delivering value rather than wandering, and it sets up the contributions slide at the end (which mirrors the promise).

## Four heuristics for keeping the audience

At any given moment, ~20% of any audience is fogged out. The four heuristics below are designed for the inevitability that listeners will check out and need a path back.

### 1. Cycle on the subject

Say it three times. Cycling is the verbal version of "tell them what you'll tell them, tell them, tell them what you told them" — but Winston defends it not as condescension but as redundancy under noisy channels. If 20% are fogged out at any moment, three passes give each person ~99% odds of catching the point at least once.

### 2. Build a fence around your idea

Distinguish your idea explicitly from adjacent ideas it might be confused with. Example: *"My algorithm may seem similar to Jones's, except his is exponential and mine is linear."* Without the fence, your work gets absorbed into the audience's pre-existing categories and loses its distinctive identity — they hear "another version of Jones."

### 3. Verbal punctuation

Provide explicit landmarks ("the first idea is X; the second is Y; now we're on the third…") so that fogged-out listeners can re-board. Numbering and outline-pointing create seams in the talk that function as get-back-on-the-bus stops.

### 4. Ask a question

Pose questions and then **wait** — Winston says seven seconds is the standard maximum (it feels like an eternity, but is the right amount). Question difficulty must be tuned: too obvious and the audience is embarrassed to answer; too hard and silence wins.

## Time and place

- **11 AM** is the optimal lecture time at MIT (everyone's awake, no one has gone back to sleep, not post-meal).
- The room should be **fully lit** — dim rooms signal sleep. Winston's standing demand to A/V people: *"keep the lights full up."* Their counter ("but they'll see slides better") earns the rejoinder: *"It's extremely hard to see slides through closed eyelids."*
- **Case the venue beforehand** — visit it as you would a bank you intended to rob, so there are no surprises.
- The room should be **more than half full**. A hall of 10 in a 200-seat space signals to attendees that something more interesting is happening elsewhere.

## Tools: blackboard, props, slides

Winston draws a sharp distinction between **informing** (teaching, lecturing) and **exposing** (job talks, conference talks).

- **Blackboards are for informing.** Three virtues: graphic quality (you can sketch), speed-matching (writing speed ≈ absorption speed), and a target for your hands (so they don't end up in your pockets, which reads as concealing a weapon).
- **Slides are for exposing.** They're for surfacing ideas to people who already have context, not for teaching them from scratch.
- **Props are for memorability.** Winston's lunatic-fringe hypothesis: props work because of **empathetic mirroring** — mirror neurons let the audience feel themselves doing what you do with the prop, in a way no slide image can replicate. Props are also disproportionately the thing remembered decades later (he cites a colleague who, 20 years on, remembered nothing about a talk except that Winston broke a wooden pointer in half on stage).

### The crimes of slide preparation

Winston's universal critique: *"There are always too many slides, always too many words."* Specific crimes:

- **Reading your slides.** People know how to read; doing it for them is insulting. If the slide says it, you can't say it.
- **Tennis-match staging.** Speaker on one side, slide on the other — audience heads swivel.
- **Background junk, logos, titles.** Strip everything that isn't load-bearing.
- **Font too small.** ~40–50pt is right; ~35pt starts to indicate you're cramming text.
- **Laser pointers.** Using one requires turning your back on the audience, which kills eye contact and engagement. Use on-slide arrows ("look at the guy at the end of arrow number one") instead.
- **Too-heavy slides.** Print the deck and lay it on a table — if it reads as wall-to-wall text without air, the slides are too heavy.
- **Hapax legomenon.** A slide so dense it's incomprehensible (Winston's example: the famous Afghanistan governance complexity slide). You get exactly one of these per talk/paper/book.

The single most important slide rule, supported by an experiment Winston cites: **when slides are dense, the audience remembers what was on the slide and not what the speaker said.** We have one language processor; reading and listening compete for it.

## Informing: inspiration and how to teach thinking

Winston's survey of how people get inspired:

- **Incoming freshmen** were inspired by a high-school teacher who told them they could do it.
- **Senior faculty** were inspired by someone who helped them see a problem in a new way.
- **Everyone** was inspired when someone exhibited **passion** for what they were doing.

On teaching thinking: Winston believes humans are **storytelling animals** — we develop story manipulation skills from fairy tales onward through every professional training. Therefore teaching thinking means giving students stories, the questions to ask of stories, mechanisms for analyzing them, ways of putting them together, and ways of evaluating their reliability.

## Persuading: oral exams and job talks

### Oral exams

The two usual reasons people fail oral exams:

1. **Failure to situate** — talking about your research without the global context of why anyone cares, who else is working on it, and what's at stake.
2. **Failure to practice** — and crucially, practicing with the wrong people. Practicing with your collaborators or your advisor is dangerous because **they hallucinate content into your talk that isn't actually there.** Practice with friends who don't know what you're working on; instruct them: *"if you can't make me cry, I won't value you as a friend anymore."*

### The job-talk five-minute test

In Winston's poll of two senior search-committee members, both said the same thing without coordinating:

- The candidate must show **vision** — a problem someone cares about, plus something new in the approach.
- The candidate must show they've **done something** — best demonstrated by enumerating the steps required to realize the vision (you don't have to have done all of them, but you must articulate them).
- They have **five minutes** to establish both. After that, you've already lost.

The structural recipe for a technical talk is therefore a **sandwich**: vision and approach upfront, body in the middle, **contributions enumerated at the end** (mirroring the steps articulated at the start).

## Getting famous: Winston's star

Winston argues you should care about being remembered because *"you never get used to being ignored."* Five elements (all start with S) make work memorable. He realized in retrospect that his arch-learning PhD thesis became mildly famous because it accidentally had all five:

- **Symbol** — a visual handle. (For Winston: the arch.)
- **Slogan** — a short phrase that becomes the work's tag. (For Winston: "one-shot learning.")
- **Surprise** — a counterintuitive claim. (For Winston: you don't need a million examples; one example used well is enough.)
- **Salient idea** — one idea that sticks out, not "important" but *protruding*. Theses with too many good ideas have no identity. (For Winston: the "near miss" — a non-example that almost qualifies, used as a learning signal.)
- **Story** — a narrative of how you did it, how it works, why it matters. This connects to Winston's storytelling-animal thesis: stories are how humans encode and transmit ideas.

## How to stop: final slide and final words

### The final slide

Bad endings Winston catalogues:

- **Collaborator list.** This belongs on the *first* slide. Putting it at the end suggests no single person owns the work.
- **"The End" / "Questions?" / "Thank You" slide.** It can be on screen for 20 minutes during Q&A — it's a squandered billboard.
- **"Conclusions" slide.** Conclusions are claims that nobody asked for.

The right final slide is labeled **"Contributions"** — a list of what *you* uniquely added to the world. It mirrors the empowerment promise; it's the thing on screen during Q&A and as people file out.

### The final words

- **A joke works at the end** (unlike at the start) because by then the audience has calibrated to your voice. Doug Lenat's secret, per Winston: *"I always finish with a joke, and that way people think they've had fun the whole time."*
- **Do not say "thank you."** It's not a sin, but it's a weak move — it implies the audience stayed out of politeness. Winston shows Bill Clinton visibly biting back the urge to say thank you at a 2012 keynote, substituting a salute and a benediction.
- **Salute the audience** instead — name what was good about the time you spent with them (an honest version of the convention, not platitudes).
